## <a id="1"><b>1. Introduction to Economics</b> </a>

Economics is the study of how societies use scarse resources to produce and consume goods and services.

Each society's objective is to maximise the <b> the well-being </b> of current and future generations, while facing the following basic economics problems:

- Which goods to produce and in what quantities?
- How to produce the goods, which resources and what kind of technology should be used?
- For whom are the produced goods^
- How to produe and consume goods in a sustainable way?

### <b>Basic Principles of Economics (Neoclassical Theory) </b>

1. <b>People face trade-offs</b>: _making decisions requires trading off one goal, with all his benefits and disadvantages, against another_.
2. <b>Opportunity Cost</b>: _decisions require comparing costs and benefits or alternatives: the opportunity cost of an item is what you give up to obtain that item._
3. <b>Rational People think at the Margin</b>: _marginal changes are small, inremental adjustments to an existing plan: the economical corresponding concept of derivative. People make decisions by comparing costs and benefits at the margin._

In comparison to neoclassical microeconomics, <b>behavioral economics </b>considers psychological aspects to better explain people's behavior in the markets, _often assuming that humans are not always rational and don't always maximise their self-interest._

### <b> How people interact? </b>

If people/countries/firms own different resources or good and have different desires, they trade with the goal of increasing the general well-being.
An econmics system in which resources are traded and allocated throught the decisions of different agents is called <b> market economy</b>.

<small> _Adam Smith demostrated that in a market economy characterized by competitive markets, trade between agents leads to the maximization of the well being. This concept is widely famous as the <b>"invisible hand"</b>_ </small>

In a market economy, or more generally, in every society, agents make decisions about both the usage of:
- <b> Inputs (Resources) </b>: everything that the agent uses in the production process.
- <b> Outputs (Goods)</b>: everything that results from the production process.

The <b> Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF)</b> shows all the possible combinations of ouput given the available resources.

In a two goods $X, Y$ economy, it represents all the possible pairs $(X,Y)$ that can be produced or, in other words, the amount of $X$ you have to sacrifie to obtain a certain amounnt of $Y$.

## <a id="2"><b>2. Supply and Demand</b></a>

Supply and demand are the forces that make the market economies work, by determening the quantity produced and price at whith it is sold.

<b> Quantity demanded </b> is the amount of a good that the agents are willing to nuy at a given price.
    - The demand curve is the relationship between the quantity demanded and the price of a good. In formulas: $ Q_d = d(P)$
    - The market demand is the sum of all individuals demands for a good.

Movements in the price of a good can lead to:
- <b> The Income Effect </b>: if the incomes remain costant, then a fall in the price means that customer can afford and will buy more.
- <b> The Substitution Effect </b>: if the price falls, customers will to substitute more expensive goods with the "falling one".

Therefore, we can define relations between demanded goods:
- <b> Substitutes </b>: two goods for which the increase in the price of one leads to an increase in the demand of the other _(example: CocaCola and Pepsi)_.
- <b> Complements </b>: two goods for which the increase in the price of one leads to a decrease in the demand of the other _(example: cars and petrol)_.

<b> Quantity supplied </b> is the amount of a good that sellers are willing and able to sell.
- The <b> Law of Supply </b> states that the quantity supplied rises when the price of the good rises, in other words: $Q_s = s(P) $ is an increasing function.

<b> The Equilibrium Price $P_{ME}$ </b> is the price at which $Q_d = Q_s $.

- Market prices act as signals about how scarse resources are.
- Prices provide information to both producers and buyers:

### <b> Example: The Algebra of Price Equilibrium </b>

Demand: $Q_d = 50 - 3P $

Supply: $Q_s = 10 + 2P $



At the equilibrium: $Q_s = Q_d$. Solving for price leads to:

$$ 50 - 3P_{EQ} = 10 + 2P_{EQ} \to   40 = 5P_{EQ} \to  P_{EQ} = 8 $$

Substituting back in $Q_d$:

$$ Q_{EQ} = Q_s = Q_d = 50 - 3(8) = 50 - 24 = 26 $$

### <b> Controls on Prices </b>
Controls on prices are often put when policymakers believe the market price is unfair. They can result in:
- Price ceiling: _legal maximum_
- Price floor: _legal minimum_

When a policymaker puts a price floor $P_{min}$, the equilibrium is given by:

$$ P_{EM} = \max \{P_{min}, P_{market}\}$$

If $P_{EM} = P_{market}$, we say that thet control is <b> not binding </b> (left), otherwise it is <b> binding </b> (right).

### <b> Shifts in the Demand and Supply Curve </b>

The factors affecting a demand (price) curve can be represented as:

$$D = d(P_n, P_1, P_2, \dots  P_i, Y, T)$$

where $P_n$ is the price of the goods, $P_i$ prices of other goods, $Y$ the incomes and $T$ tastes or fashions. A shift in the demand curve is cause by a change in a factor influencing $f$ <b>other than a change in price $P_n$.</b>

The same applies for the Supply Curve $S = s(P_n, P_1, P_2, \dots  P_i, Y, T)$.
