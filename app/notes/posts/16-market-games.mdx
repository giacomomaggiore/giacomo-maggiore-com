## <a id="1"><b>Introduction</b> </a>

<b>Game Theory</b>: analyzing strategic interactions between agents where the outcome depends on the decisions of other agents.

The key assumptions is that <b>agents act "rationally"</b>, meaning that:
- agents choose actions that they believe is best for them while taking into account information available to them.
- agents anticipate that other agents will also act _rationally/strategically_.

But what is _rationality_?
- Rationality does NOT preclude the possibility of mistakes, errors, non-material values, altruistic or social preferences, social norms and cultures. 

---
<br/>
There are several ways to describe what an _economic model_ is.

- <b>The Standard one</b>: _an economic model is a simplied (usually mathematical) description of reality, designed to yield hypotheses about economic behavior that can be tested_.
- <b>The Fable one</b>: _economic theory formulates thoughts via "models". The word model sounds more scientific that the word fable or tale, but I think we are talking about the same thing._ (Rubinstein, 2009)

In Game Theory, the approach lies between these 2 views:
- we will develop and analyze simplified models that are certainly _"fictional"_ (often with unrealistic assumptoins)
- in most cases, predictions are consistent with intuitions under real-world policy and competition law.

Generally speaking, we can say that _"all modelrs are wrong, but some are useful"_.
- all models are abstractions, i.e. _intentionally neglet or simplify some aspects_
- a model is good if it has the right level of abstraction for a specific goal.

Indeed, <b>unrealistic assumptions</b>allow a theory to focus only on the crucial and relevant elements required to obtaina prediction.
- some research suggests that <b>over-simplified assumptions</b> _(parsimonious economic models)_ deliver better predictions than <b>black box algorithms</b> _(random forest and kernel regressions) when <b>moving across different contexts</b>.

<a href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2202.04796" target="_blanket">Oversimplified models are often superior, source: The transfer performance of economic models, 2022</a>.

## <a id="2"><b>Normal form games and Nash equilibrium</b> </a>

Each game theory's game requires:
- 2 or more individuals _(players, agents)_
- players interascting by making choices that jointly determine an outcome 
- _(usually)_ every player can affect the outcome but no player has full control of the outocme 

A <b>solution</b> is a set of reccomendations about how to play the game, such taht no player will have an incentive to not follow the recomendation.

---

Let's try to formalize it: <b>Formal Form Games with _pure strategies_</b>

An $n$-player normal form game consists in:
- for each player $i = 1,2 \ldots n$, a set of strategies $S_i$
- for each player $i = 1,2 \ldots n$, a utility function $u_i$ that, for each strategy profile $(s_1, s_2 \ldots s_n) \in S_1 \times \ldots \times S_n$ specify a real number: $u_i(s_1 \ldots s_n) \in \R$.

We denote the game by $G = (S_1, \ldots S_n, u_1 \ldots u_n)$.

A <b>Nash Equilibrium </b> is a strategy profile such that no player has an incentive to _unilaterally deviate_ from their corresponding strategy.

Let $S$ be the set of strategy profiles, i.e. $S = S_1 \times \ldots \times S_n$.

Given a $n$-player normal form game $G$, a strategy profile $s^* = (s_1^*, \ldots s_n^*)$ is a <b>Nash Equilibrium</b> of $G$ if for each player $ i = 1, \ldots n$:

$$
u_i(s_1^*, \ldots s_{i-1}^*, s_i^*, s_{i+1}^*, \ldots s_n^*) \ge u_i(s_1^*, \ldots s_{i-1}^*, s_i^, s_{i+1}^*, \ldots s_n^*) \quad \forall s_i \in S_iπ

$$
- _The asterisk $^*$ is often used to denote an equilibrium profile rather than a generic profile._
- When strategy $s_i \neq s_i^*$, it is called <b> _"unilateral deviation"_ </b> from $s^*$.

An alternative definition of <b>Nash Equilibrium</b> is: a strategy profile $(s_1, \ldots s_n$) is NOT a Nash Equilibrium if at least one player has an incentive to unilaterally deviate.

---

We now consider a $n$-player normal form game $G = \{S_1, \ldots S_n; u_1, \ldots u_n \}$ and a strategy profile $(s_1, \ldots s_n)$.

<b>Definitin: Best response in pure strategies</b>

The set of player $i$'s <b>best responses </b> against $(s_1, \ldots, s_{i-1}, s_{i+1}, \ldots s_n)$ is the set of player's strategies that solve:

$$
\max_{s_i' \in S_i} u_i(s_1, \ldots, s_{i-1}, s_i', s_{i+1}, \ldots s_n)
$$

We denote as $R_i(s_1, \ldots, s_{i-1}, s_{i+1}, \ldots s_n)$ the set of player $i$'s best responses against $(s_1, \ldots, s_{i-1}, s_{i+1}, \ldots s_n)$.

<b>Theorem: Nash Equilibrium in pure strategies</b>

A strategy profile $(s_1^*, \ldots s_n^*)$ is a _Nash Equilibrium_  $\iff  \forall i = 1, \ldots n$, we have:

$$
s_i^* \in R_i(s_1^*, \ldots, s_{i-1}^*, s_{i+1}^*, \ldots s_n^*)
$$

In mathematics, we would call this a <b>fixed point</b>.

---

When we are analyzing the strategic interactions in both theoretical and real-world settings, we are often interested in <b>"efficiency"</b>.

- Altought there are many different definition for it, the most standard one is called <b>"Pareto-efficiency"</b>.

An outocome is Pareto-efficient if there is an outcome that makes an individual better off without making another individual worse off. In other words:

<b>Definition: Pareto Efficiency</b>

A strategy profile $s = (s_1, \ldots s_n) \in S$ is Pareto inefficient if there is a strategy profile $s' \in S$ such that:
- all agents are <b>weakly better off</b>: $u_i(s') \ge u_i(s)$ for sall $i$ <b>AND </b> 
- at least one agent $j$ is <b>strictly better off</b>: $u_j'(s) > u_j(s)$.

An outcome that is not pareto <b>inefficient</b> is Pareto efficient.

## <a id="3"><b>Nash equilibrium with mixed strategies</b> </a>

So far, we assumed that players only choose "pure" strategies _(non-random or deterministic)_. But, more generally, we could consider <b>mixed (random) strategies </b>, to ensure that a Nash Equilibrium exists for a broder class of games.

Consider for example the following example. Two players can choose between $H$, $T$ and obtain the respect payoff.

It can be shown that there's no Nash Equilibrium with pure strategies.



<div align = "center">
![Mixed Strategies](/images/notes/market-games/mixed.png)
</div>

So let's suppose that player $1$ chooses $H$ with probability $x$ and $T$ with probability $1-x$. The same applies for player $2$: he chosses $H$ with probability $y$, $T$ with probability $1-y$.

We want to find $x, y \in [0,1]$ such that it is a Nash Equilibrium.

Given that player $1$ chooses $H$ with probabilkty $x$, Player's $2$ payoffs are:
- $\text{Player 2's payoff from  H} = -x + (1-x) = 1 - 2x$
- $\text{Player 2's payoff from  T} = x  + (-1)(1-x) = 2x - 1$

Player $2$ stricly prefers $H$ if:

$$
1-2x > 2x -1 \implies x < 1/2
$$

We can write Player $2$ choices in a more compact way:

$$
R_2(x) = 
\begin{cases} 
y = 1 & \text{if } x < 1/2, \\
0 \le y \le 1 & \text{if } x = 1/2, \\
y = 0 & \text{if } x > 1/2,
\end{cases}

$$

Simirarly, given that Player $2$ chooses $H$ with probability $y$, we can write:
- $\text{Player 1's payoff from  H} = y + (-1)(1 -y) = 2y - 1$
- $\text{Player 1's payoff from  T} = (-1)y + (1)(1 -y) = 1 - 2y$

$$
R_1(y) = 
\begin{cases} 
x = 1 & \text{if } y > 1/2, \\
0 \le x \le 1 & \text{if } y = 1/2, \\
x = 0 & \text{if } y < 1/2,
\end{cases}

$$


A Nash equilibrium occurs when the strategy profile is such every player is playing a best response to the other player’s strategy.

In other words, we have to find $x^*, y^*$ such that:

$$
R_1(y^*) = x^*,\quad R_2(x^*) = y^*
$$

By plotting, we can easily identify them:

<div align = "center">
![Mixed Strategies](/images/notes/market-games/mixed2.png)
</div>

The Nash Equilibrium is then $x^* = y^* = 1/2$.

---
<br/>
<b>Mixed Strategies: formal definition</b>
<br/>

A mixed strategy for a player $i$ is a probability distribution $p_i$ over their set of pure strategies.

The set of all the pure strategies $S_i$ is denoted by $\Delta(S_i)$.



In general, there's _NO always_ a Nash Equilibrium with mixed strategies but, for a broad class of games, there's always a Nash Equilibrium.

<a href="https://www.pnas.org/doi/10.1073/pnas.36.1.48" target="_blank">This result was proven by <b> John F. Nash</b>, Jr. in his seminal 1950 paper “Equilibrium points in $n$-person games”</a>. Nash was awarded 1994 Nobel Prize in Economics for this and other contributions to game theory.

<br/>
---
<br/>

<b>Theorem (Nash, 1950)</b>

Consider then a $n$-player normal form game $G = (S_1 \ldots S_k, u_i \ldots u_k)$, with $n$ finite number and $S_i$ finite for every $i$.<br/>
The game $G$ as at least one Nash equilibrium, possibly involving mixed strategies.

A couple of funny notes:
- The paper is extremley short, just 3 pages!
- There is a book and film about Nash’s life <a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Beautiful_Mind" target="_blank"> “A Beautiful Mind”</a>

<b>Important result #1</b>: When checking for profitable unilaterally decisions, it is necessary and sufficient to look only at pure strategy decisions $p_i = s_i$, _i.e. $p^*$ is a Nash Equilibrium only if:_

$$
U_i(p_1^*, \ldots p_i^*, \ldots p_n^*) > U_i(p_1^*, \ldots s_i, \ldots p_n^*)
$$

for every pure strategy $s_i$.

<b>Important result #2</b>: Suppose $p = (p_1^*, \ldots p_n^*)$ is Nash Equilibrium. If players other than $i$ play according to $(p_1^*, \ldots p_n^*)$, then player $i$ is indifferent between any two pure strategies that they play with strictly positive probability in $p_i^∗$.

<br/>
---
<br/>


<b>Best response: formal definition</b>

The set of player $i$'s best responses against $(p_1, \ldots p_{i-1}, p_{i+1},\ldots p_n)$ is the set of strategies that solve:

$$
\max_{s_i \in \Delta(S_i)} U_i (p_1, \ldots p_{i-1}, p'_i, p_{i+1},\ldots p_n)
$$

We denote by $R_i(p_1, \ldots p_{i-1}, p_{i+1},\ldots p_n)$ the set of best responses against $(p_1, \ldots p_{i-1}, p_{i+1},\ldots p_n)$.



<b>Theorem (N.E.  characterization via best responses)</b>

A strategy profile $(p_1^*, \ldots p_n^*)$ is a Nash Equilibrium if and only if, $\forall i = 1, \ldots n$, we have:

$$
p_i^* \in R_i(p_1^*, \ldots p_{i-1}^*, p_{i+1}^*,\ldots p_n^*)
$$


_Intuition_:
- when looking for Nash Equilibrium:
    - we calculate the player's best response functions 
    - find the intersection(s) of all players' best response functions
    - translate the response functions into strategies.

<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Formal definition: strictly dominated strategies</b>

Given a game $G$, the mixed strategy $p_i$ is <b>strictly dominated by $p_i$ </b> if, for every pure strategy profile of the other players, we have:

$$
U_i(s_i, \ldots s_{i-1}, p_i, s_{i+1}, \ldots s_n) <U_i(s_i, \ldots s_{i-1}, p_i', s_{i+1}, \ldots s_n) 

$$

If a player has a stricly dominated strategy, he will never use it in a Nash Equilibrium.

<b>Theorem:  (Iterated deletion of strictly dominated strategies)</b>

Let G be an $n$-player normal form game such that player $i$ has a strictly dominated strategy in $G$. Let $G′$ be the $n$-player normal form game that is obtained from $G$ by removing such a strictly dominated pure strategy.

Then $G$ and $G′$ have the same set of Nash equilibria.

<b>Formal definition: weakly dominated strategies</b>

_Instead of writing the full strategy profile of the other players as $(s_i, \ldots s_{i-1}, s_{i+1}, \ldots s_n)$, it is convenient to write as $s_{-i}$._

Given a game $G$, the mixed strategy $p_i$ is weakly dominated by $p_i'$ if, for <b>every pure strategy</b> profile of the other players $s_{-i}$, we have:

$$
U_i(s_{-i}, p_i) \le U_i(s_{-i}, p_i')
$$

and there exists at least one pure strategy profile $s'_{i-1}$ such that:

$$
U_i(s_{-i}', p_i) < U_i(s_{-i}', p_i')
$$

## <a id="4"><b>Applications to market competition</b> </a>


We now spend some time to analyze two models of duopoly market competitions:

- <b>Cournout competition</b>: firms compete by choosing to supplky different quantities of the same good
- <b>Betrand competition</b>: firms compete by choosing to set different prices

_A funny example of Bertrand competition: <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2012/03/31/nyregion/in-manhattan-pizza-war-price-of-slice-keeps-dropping.html" target="_blank"> In a Manhattan Pizza War, price of slice keeps dropping (NY Times - 2012)</a>._

---
<br/>
<b>Cournot Model of Duopoly Competition</b>

We start with the following assumptions:
- there are two firms $1,2$ competing in a duopolistic market 
- the firms produce an identical good and choose quantities to produce $q_1, q_2$
- producing $q_1$ units of the good costs $cq_1$ and the same applies for $q_2, with $0 < c < 1$.
- the market clearing per-unit price when $Q = q_1 + q_2$ is defined by the total amount of goods:

$$
p(Q) = 1 - Q
$$

We can now model the game:
- <b>strategy ser for firm $1$:</b> any number $q_1 \ge 0$, i.e $S_1 = [0, \infty)$
- <b>strategy ser for firm $2$:</b> any number $q_2 \ge 0$, i.e $S_2 = [0, \infty)$

The corresponding payoff are:

$$
U_1(q_1, q_2) = p(Q)q_1 - cq_1 = (1 - q_1 - q_2)q_1 - cq_1\\
U_2(q_1, q_2) = p(Q)q_2 - cq_2 = (1 - q_1 - q_2)q_2 - cq_2

$$

<b>Definition: Nash Equilibrium for Cournot Model</b>

A strategy profile $(q_1^*, q_2^*) is a Nash Equilibrium $iff$:

$$
U_1(q_1^*, q_2^*) \ge U_1 (q_1, q_2^*) \quad \forall q_1 \in S_1\\
U_2(q_1^*, q_2^*) \ge U_1 (q_1^*, q_2) \quad \forall q_2 \in S_2
$$

To find the Nash Equilibrium, we start looking at <b>best responses</b>: we fix firm's 2 strategy at some $q_2$ and then we model the best response for firm 1.

Indeed, firm 1 chooses $q_1$ that maximizes:

$$
\begin{align*}
\max_{q_1 \in S_1} U_1 (q_1, q_2)&= \max_{q_1 \in S_1} (1 - q_1 - q_2)q_1 - cq_1 =\\
&= \max_{q_1 \in [0, \infty) } (1 - c - q_2)q_1 - q_1^2

\end{align*}
$$

We proceed with <b>First Order Condition (FOC)</b> since we are maximizing a _concave-down_ quadratic function:

$$
\frac{\partial (1 - c - q_2)q_1 - q_1^2}{\partial q_1} = 0 \iff\\
(1 -c - q_2) - 2q_1 = 0 \iff\\
q_1 = \frac{1}{2}(1 - c - q_2)
$$

we now remember that $q_1 \in S_1 \implies q_1 > 0$, so $q_1$ is feasible if $q_2 \le 1 - c$. In this case, firm 1's best response is:

$$
R_1 (q_2) =
\begin{cases}
 \frac{1}{2}(1 - c - q_2) \quad \text{if } q_2 \le 1 - c \\
 0 \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \text{otherwise}

\end{cases}
$$

If we use the same steps, we can also show that firm's 2 best response function is:

$$
R_2 (q_1) =
\begin{cases}
 \frac{1}{2}(1 - c - q_1) \quad \text{if } q_1 \le 1 - c \\
 0 \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \text{otherwise}

\end{cases}
$$

_Intuition: the firms are identical:_
- they share the same marginal cost and profit function 
- if any of the above were _NOT_ the same, then we should suspect that the best response fuctions will be different!

To find the Nash Equilibium, we look at the intersection of the best response functions.

We assume $q_1 \le 1 - c$ and $q_2 \le 1 - c$, then we equal:

$$
 R_1(q_2^*) = \frac{1}{2}(1 - c - q_2^*) = q_1^* = q_2^* = R_2(q_1^*) = \frac{1}{2}(1 - c - q_1^*) \implies \\
 \implies

 q_1^* = q_2^* = \frac{1 - c}{3}

$$

_Note that the assumptions remain both valid at this solution!_

The equilibrium total quantity is then:

$$
Q^* = q_1^* + q_2 * = \frac{2}{3}(1 - c)
$$

The equilibrium price is then:

$$
p(Q^*) = 1 - Q^* = \frac{1 + 2c}{3}
$$

The equilbrium profits, given $q_1^*, q_2^*$ are:

$$
p(Q^*)q_i^* - cq_i^é = \frac{1 + 2c}{3}\frac{1-c}{3} - c \frac{1-c}{3} = \frac{(1-c)^2}{9} > 0
$$

<b>The Nash Equilibrium is inefficient from firm's perspective</b>:

Both firms can do strictly better by colluding to act together as one profit-maximizing firm and splitting the total profits but:
- as the model stands, doing so it _NOT_ an equilibrium 
- fundamentally, it's a similar game to the _Prisoner's dilemma!_

To see this, suppose the firms cooperate and choose to supply a lower quantity: $q_1 = q_2 = \frac{1-c}{4}$.

This would lead to:

$$
Q = q_1 + q_2 = \frac{1-c}{2}, \quad p(Q) = 1 - Q = \frac{1+c}{2}\\
\text{Profits} = p(Q)(\frac{1-c}{4}) - c \frac{1-c}{4} = \frac{(1-c)^2}{8} > \frac{(1 -c)^2}{9}
$$

_Note that $Q = (1-c)/2$ correspons to what a monopolist would do, therefore $q_1 = q_2 = Q / 2$ corresponds to splitting the optimal monopoly quantity equally._

We now analyze why does collusion allow the firmst to do _strictly better_.

If firm 1 is the _merged monopolist_ (i.e. obtains the profit from both $q_1, q_2$), they solve:

$$
\max_{q_1 \in S_1} (1 - q_1 - q_2)(q_1 + q_2) - c (q_1 + q_2)
$$

The derivative with respect to $q_1$ leads to three effects:

$$

\underbrace{(1 - q_1 - q_2)}{\text{sales effect}}
+\underbrace{-(q_1 + q_2)}{\text{price effect}}
+\underbrace{-c}_{\text{cost of production}}


$$


The optimal solution is obtained setting the previous to $0$. Increasing $q_1$ has 3 effects:
- sales effect: move revnue from selling more $q_1$
- price effect: more $q_1$ means lower price 
- cost of production equal to $-c$

In, on the other hand, firm 1 is not merged, they solve:

$$
\max_{q_1 \in S_1} (1 - q_1 - q_2)(q_1) - c (q_1)
$$

Increasing $q_1$ has the same 3 effects but the _(negative)_ price effect is smaller in magnitude:
- increasing $q_1$ reduces the price for both $q_1, q_2$ sales 
- _unlike the monopoly case, firm 1 doesn't care about $q_2$ sales!
- firm's 1 _doesn't internalize_ the price externality on firm 2

$$\implies$ firm 1 as a duopolist will <b>oversupply</b> $q_1$ relative to a monopoly benchmmark.

Important result from this model:
- Monopoly markets lead to a _lower quantity_ and _higher prices_ than a duopoly _($\implies$ bad for consumers!)_ 
- duopolist have incentives to collude and act like a monopoly! 
    - however, collusion is hard to sustain!
    - firms might also seek to formally combine their companies in a merger 

For these reasons, policymakers are often concerned about monopoly markets. In practise however, mergers are _NOT always rejected_ since there could be some efficiency fains from merging than benefit consumers even if a monopoly forms.

<b>Policy Implications</b>

We now try to incorporate _efficiency gains_ into our model, indeed we modify the m,odel so that after a merger the marginal cost decreases to $c'$, with $0 < c' < c$.

At this point, there's a minimum level of efficiency gain so that the monopoly price is lower than the monopoly price:

$$
\frac{1+c'}{2} < \frac{1 + 2c}{3} \implies (c- c') > \frac{1-c}{3}
$$

<b>Variations of the Cournot Models</b>


- The model can be generalized with more than $N > 2$ firms. In this case, we see that _competition is good for consumers_ and that equilibrium price is decreasing in $N$ and in the limit, as $N \to \infity$, we get a _"perfectly competitive market"_ where the marginal cost equals the price.
- We can consider a market with two goods are complements and we can arrive at a _different conclusion_: merging 2 firms can be better for both firms and consumers.
- We can extend the analysis in a multi-stage interagion, showing that it is possible to sustain collusive relationships between firms.

<br/>
<b>Bertrand Model of Duopoly Competition</b>

In this model firmst compete by setting prices, i.e. each firm chooses a price $p_i \in P_i = [0, \infty)$, for $i = 1,2$.

Products are identical and hence conusmers always buy from the cheapest firm. The demand functions are then:
$$
q_1(p_1,p_2)=
\begin{cases}
1-p_1, & \text{if } p_1<p_2,\\[4pt]
\dfrac{1-p_1}{2}, & \text{if } p_1=p_2,\\[6pt]
0, & \text{if } p_1>p_2.
\end{cases}
$$
$$
q_2(p_1,p_2)=
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{if } p_1<p_2,\\[4pt]
\dfrac{1-p_2}{2}, & \text{if } p_1=p_2,\\[6pt]
1-p_2, & \text{if } p_1>p_2.
\end{cases}
$$
Each firm wants to maximize profits:

$$
U_i(p_i, p_j) = p_i q_i (p_1, p_2)- c q_i (p_1, p_2)
$$

where $c$ is the marginal cost, $0 < c < 1$.

<b>Nash Equilibium of Bertrand Model</b>

A strategy profile $(p_1^*, p_2^*)$ is Nash equilibrium if:
$$
U_1(p_1^*,p_2^*) \ge U_1(p_1, p_2^*)\quad \forall p_1 \in P_1\\
U_2(p_1^*,p_2^*) \ge U_2(p_1^*, p_2)\quad \forall p_2 \in P_2
$$

As before, we find the Nash Equilibium by looking at the best responses.

We fix firm's 2 strategy and we look at firm's 1 best response that maximizes:

$$
\max_{p_1 \in P_1}U_1(p_1, p_2) = \max_{p_1\in P_1}p_1 q_1 (p_1, p_2)- cq_1(p_1, p_2)\\
= \max_{p_1 \in P_1} (p_1 - c)q_1 (p_1, p_2)
$$

In this case, the objective function we want to maximize is not continuous or differentiable anywhere and we cannot apply FOC:
$$

U_1(p_1,p_2)=
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{if } p_1>p_2,\\[6pt]
(p_1-c)\dfrac{1-p_1}{2}, & \text{if } p_1=p_2,\\[8pt]
(p_1-c)(1-p_1), & \text{if } p_1<p_2.
\end{cases}
$$

Let's then proceed with some intuition. _What would happen if we had the monopoly problem and firm 2 does not exist?_  <br/>In this case, the firm's 1 problem rewrites as:
$$
\max{p_1 \in P_1}(p_1 - c)(1 - p_1)
$$


The objective function is _concave-down_ quandatric, so we can apply FOC as:

$$
\frac{\partial (p_1 -c)(1-p_1)}{\partial p_1} = 0 \iff (1 - p_1) + (p_1 - c)(-1) = 0 \iff \\
\iff p^M = \frac{1+c}{2}
$$

We then know that, in absence of competition, firm 1 would like to choose $p_1 = p^M$ like in the monopolist scenario.

We now analyze 3 different sets of $p_2$ values and firm's 1 best response.

1. <b>Suppose</b> $p_2 > p^M$: in this case, firm's 1 best response would still be: $R_1(p_2) = p^M$ because we would still have $p_1 < p_2$ and firm 1 obtains the best-case monopoly outcome.

![Case 1](/images/notes/market-games/case-1.png)
</div>

_Mathematically_, we have:

$$
U_1(p_1,p_2)=
\begin{cases}
0, & \text{if } p_1>p_2,\\[6pt]
(p_2-c)\dfrac{1-p_2}{2}, & \text{if } p_1=p_2,\\[8pt]
(p_1-c)(1-p_1), & \text{if } p_1<p_2.
\end{cases}
$$

2. <b>Suppose</b> $c < p_2 \le p^M$: for firm 1, choosing $p_1 = c$ ensures $p_1 < p_2$ and so the firms gets the whole market, but it also obtains _no profit_ since the price equals the marginal cost:

$$
U_1 (p_1,p_2) = (p_1 - c)q_1(p_1, p_2) = 0 \iff p_1 = c
$$

On the other hand, suppose that firm 1 _"undercuts"_ firm's2 price by a tiny amount $\epsilon > 0$, in this case we would have:
$$
U_1 (p_1,p_2) = (p_1 - c)(1 - p_1) = (p_2 -\epsilon- c)(1 - p_2 + \epsilon)
$$

that, for $\epsilon \approx 0$, is slighly less but close to $(p_2 - c)(1 - p_2)$ and, because $p_2 > c$, we get $U_1(p_1,p_2)>0$.

This is better than $p_1 = p^M$ and $p_1 = c$, so it is firm's 1 best response:

$$
R_1(p_2) = p_2 - \epsilon
$$

<div align = "center">
![Case 1](/images/notes/market-games/case-2.png)
</div>

3. <b>Suppose</b> $p_2 \le c$. In this case, firm 1 cannot _undercut_ $p_2$ by a tiny amount because profits would be negative, so the firm can do better by _not entering the market_. <br/>If $p_2 = c$, firm 1 is indifferent between $p_1 = c$ and $p_1 > p_2 = c$.

So, if we want to summarize firm 1's best responses, we get:

$$
R_1(p_2)=
\begin{cases}
p^M=\dfrac{1+c}{2}, & \text{if } p_2>p^M,\\[8pt]
p_2-\varepsilon, & \text{if } c<p_2\le p^M,\\[8pt]
(p_2,\infty), & \text{if } p_2<c,\\[8pt]
[c,\infty), & \text{if } p_2=c.
\end{cases}

$$
$$
R_2(p_1)=
\begin{cases}
p^M=\dfrac{1+c}{2}, & \text{if } p_1>p^M,\\[8pt]
p_1-\eta, & \text{if } c<p_1\le p^M,\\[8pt]
(p_1,\infty), & \text{if } p_1<c,\\[8pt]
[c,\infty), & \text{if } p_1=c.
\end{cases}
$$

where $\epsilon, \eta > 0$ are very small.

The Nash Equilibrium is $(p_1^*, p_2^*) = (c,c)$ and lies at the intersection:

<div align = "center">
![Case 1](/images/notes/market-games/ne.png)
</div>

<b>Equilibium outcomes</b>:
- rach firm chooses price equal to marginal cost and sells $q_1^* = q_2^* = (1-c)/2$ units 
- firms earn zero profits 
- equilibrium output equals $1 -c$
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Bertrand Paradox</b>

With just 2 firms, we can have a _"perfectly competitive"_ market outcome where price equals marginal cost. Increasing competition beyod 2 firms has _NO_ effects on outcomes, differing drammatically from the Cournot Model.

The paradox can be solved adding more realism to the model:
- relax implicit assuptions _(add capacity constraints or a integer-pricing)_ 
- one of the firms may be more productive 
- product differentiation or geographic differentiation
- dynamic competition or imperfect information
