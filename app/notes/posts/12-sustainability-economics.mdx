## <a id="1"><b>1. Introduction</b> </a>

<b>Economics </b> is the study of how people use scarse resources and respond to incentives.

<b>Sustanability economics</b> tries to understand and manage the long-term relationships between humans and nature so that scare enviromental goods, and their human-made substitues or complements, are being used efficiently.

Sustainability approaches tries to understand what do we want to <b>preserve</b>:
- Income, consumption
- Wealth, capital 
- Utility, social Welfare
- Possibilities, opportunities 
- Nature, ecosystem
- ...

An <b>Outcome-Oriented</b> approach seek to preserve realized human well-being over time _(actual experience)_.
<br/>An <b>Opportunity-Oriented</b> approach seek to preserve _means_ to generate welfare _(potential of the future)_.

In relation to this, we define <b>Capital</b> $K$ as stock that can be used to deliver a flow of services, and we distinguish:
- $K_h$: human capital _(people, education, health)_
- $K_p$: physical capital _(infrastructure, machines, buildings)_
- $K_n$: natural capital _(ecosystem, atmosphere)_

<b>Comprehensive Wealth</b> incluse the total value of all capital stocks. 

- <b> "Weak" sustainability</b> use and invest in natural and human-made capital stock such that comprehensive wealth doesn't decline:

$$
\frac{\partial K (K_h, K_n, K_p)}{\partial t} \ge 0
$$

- <b>"Strong" sustainability</b> use and invest in natural and human-made capital stock such that no stock $i \in \{ h, p, n\}$ falls below a critical level:
$$
K_i \ge \bar K_i
$$

_Example: a strong approach to climate change limits temperature change below critical levels (example: limit warming at 2° no matter of the cost)._ <br/>_A weak approach balances costs and benefits of emissions reductions (impose carbon tax to ensure that fossil fuels are only burned when their benefit exceed social cost)._

## <a id="2"><b>2.1 Economic Growth, Sustainability, Market Failures - Macroeconomic Refresher</b> </a>

<b>GDP</b> (Gross Domestic Product) is the _market value of the final goods and services produced within the borders of a country during a particular time period_.
<br/>It can be measured in 3 ways:
- <b>the production approach </b>
- <b>the expenditure approach </b>
- <b>the income approach</b>
<br/>
<a href="https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/gdp-per-capita-worldbank?tab=line" target="_blank">GDP Stats by Country [Our World in Data]</a>

The idea behind the <b>production approach</b> is to sum up the market value of the final goods and services _produced_ in a year.

<b>Example: Giacomo's Racing Bikes</b><br/>
Giacomo's Land is a small country with one employer (Giacomo's GmbH) that produces 100 racing bikes a year. The market price of an racing bike is $10'000 \text{ CHF}$ _(yes, they are very expensive!)_.
<br/>All Giacomo's Land citizens work in the bike factory, that also owns all the machines, so it onlz needs to hire workers.

To determine the <b>market value of production</b>, we simply do:

$$
\text{Production} = 100 \text{ racing bikes} \times 10'000 \text{ CHF/bike} = 1'000'000 \text{ CHF}
$$

Note that we focus to the final value at tend of the production chain (_value added)_, so we sum the ssales revenues minus the firm's purchases of intermediate products to avoid double-counting.

The <b>expenditure approach</b> looks at the total value of expenditures on goods and services produced in the domestic economy by considering:
- $C$: consumption _(good and servies bought by domestic households)_
- $I$: new physical capital investments from domestic households and domestic firms
- $G$: government expenditures 
- $X$: exports of goods and servies produced domestically and sold abroach 
- $M$: imports of goods and services produced abroad and sold domestically

and calculating GDP ($Y$) as:

$$
Y = C + I + G + X - M
$$

<b>Nominal GDP </b> is the total value of production using _current_ market prices to determine the value of each unit.
- An increase in GDP will record both increases in _actual_ production and increases in the _prices_ of goods and services.

<b>Real GDP</b> is the total value of production using _constant_ market prices_.

Real GDP allows then to compute <b>real changes</b> in output across time since we adjust for the price levels.

In a similar way, <b>PPP (Purchasing Power Parity)</b> adusted income measures adjust for differences in cost of living across locations:

$$
\text{PPP Adjustments} = \frac{\text{Price of consumption basket in location A}}{\text{Price of consumption basket in location  B}}
$$

A very useful tool to visualize PPP adjustments is <a href="https://www.numbeo.com/cost-of-living/" target="_blank">Numbeo - Cost of Living </a>.

If we want to compute PPP Adjustments, we simply do:

$$
\text{PPP Adjustments - Inexpesive Meal} = \frac{15.00 €}{28.59 €} = 0.52
$$

For example, in case we wanto to compare <b>salaries</b>, and we assume the following:
- <b>average pizza-man salary in Naples</b>: $25'000 $€
- <b>average pizza-man salary in Zurich</b>: $60'000 $€
we just have to re-compute the Zurich'one with the PPT adjustments:

$$
\text{PPP-adjusted Salary}_{Zurich} = 60'000 € \times 0.52 = 3120 €
$$

meaning that <b>purhcasing power of Zurich's pizza-men</b> is actually higher.

We recall that the <b>aggregate production function </b> for GDP is:

$$
Y_t = A_t \times F(K_t, L_t \cdot h_t) = A_t \times F(K_t, H_t)
$$

- $K_t$: investment and construction
- $L_t$: labor force participation
- $h_t$: education
- $A_t$: improvements in technology, institutions, management etc...

However, in the _long run_, the only source of sustained GDP per capita growth is the <b>growth in productivity</b> $A_t$. (Note that, _given the right incentives_, market can direct productivity improvements in almost any direction).

Starting from the aggregate production function, the <b>Integraded Assessment Modelds (IAMs)</b> integrade enviromental and resource dynamics into macro-economic analysis.

Firstly, we can account for the role of (fossil) energy $E£ in the GDP production:

$$
Y_t = A_t \times F(K_t, H_t, E)
$$

_Intuition: using more (and cheap) energy today increases GDP today_.

Then, we add climate model to account for effects of fossil use energy on warking $T_t$:

$$
T_t = H(E_{1750, E_{1751}... E_t})
$$

and then we add $A_t$ to the GDP's formula:

$$
Y_t = A_t(E_t) \times F(K_t, H_t, E_t)
$$

_Note that, in reality, energy consumption $E$ affects all the determinants of GDP, not only the productivity (for example, investments depends on depreciation rate, also affected by climate change)._
Most important, climate changes also has strong effects that don't affect GDP directly (_non-market_ impacts such as elderly mortality, biodiversity etc etc...)

## <a id="2.2"><b>2.2 Economic Growth, Sustainability, Market Failures - Microeconomic Refresher</b> </a>

For a better review, please also refer to <a href="https://giacomomaggiore.com/notes/05-microeconomics">my microeconomic notes </a>.

<b>First Fundamental Theorem of Welfare Economics</b> states that, in absence of market failures, free competitive markets lead to the economically efficient outcome.

- _Intuition on the consumer side:_ consumers spend money on a given good until their marginal benefit from another slice is just equal to the marginal cost (the price they pay) of buying an additional unit.
- _Intuition on the producer side:_ firms produce as much of a good until their marginal cost is just equal to the marginal revenue they sell at.

<b>Markets allocate scarse resources</b> efficiently because prices serve as a signal of the value and cost of using the resorces to produce a given good.

A <b>market failure</b> is a problem that causes the free market to deliver an outcome that is inefficient.

- <b>Private marginal cost</b> is the direct cost of producers of producing an additional unit.
- <b>Social marginal cost</b> is the private marginal cost _plus any external costs or benefits_ of producing an additional unit.

_Intuition_: social marginal costs measure the full costs to society.

## <a id="2.3"><b>2.3 Economic Growth, Sustainability, Market Failures - Integrade Assessment Models (IAMs) and the DICE Model</b> </a>

IAMs are integrade models of the economy and the enviroment.<br/>
In macroeconomic, there are the following assumptions and behaviors:
- firms maximize profits 
- households maximise well-being
- agents anticipate the future 
- market equilibrium represents a _solution_.

Interdisciplary and policy IAMs often take these assumptions are given, but they miss the <b>feedback loop</b>: they calculate how growth affects the climate, but they struggle to calculate how much that climate change affects back into the economy.

---

<div align = "center">
![Nordhaus](/images/notes/sust-econ/nordhaus.png)
</div>

The <b>Nordhaus DICE Model</b> (1992) is based on the <b>Ramsey-Cass-Koopmans</b> growth model, whose aggregate production function is:

$$
Y_t^{gross} = A_t \cdot (K_t^{\alpha} L_t^{1 - \alpha})
$$

where $Y_t^{gross}$ is the global gross output, $A_t$ the total factor productivity, $K_t$ the capital stock and $L_t$ the labor. _(Note that many IAms also include energy services as production input)_.

The DICE models also introduce calculates the <b>baseline industrial carbon emissions </b> as:

$$
E_t^{Baseline} = \sigma_t \cdot Y_t
$$

where $\sigma_t$ is the <b>baseline emissions intensity </b> of the economy. _(Note that, in the past $\sigma_t$ showed a linear-decading trend)_.

In addition to this baseline decarbonization trend, society can undertake additional efforts to reduce carbon emissions so, if we define $\mu_t \in [0,1]$ as the fraction of emissions reduced, we can model:

$$
E^{Actual}_t = (1 - \mu_t) E_t^{Baseline}
$$

and then we represent the costs $\Lambda_t$ of emissions reductions as a share of GDP:

$$
\Lambda_t (\mu_t) = \theta_{1t}(\mu_t)^{\theta_2}
$$

The <b>Nordhaus DICE Model</b> also accounts for CO2 emissions and, from the <b>atmospheric carbon concentrations</b> $M_{AT,t}$, it models the <b>increased radiative forcing </b> $F_t$ (_measuring  how much extra heat (in Watts per square meter) is being trapped at any given time $t$._)

$$

F_t = \eta \left[ \log_2 \left( \frac{M_{AT,t}}{M_{AT,1750}} \right) \right] + F^{Abate}_t + F^{Ex}_t

$$
- $\frac{M_{AT,t}}{M_{AT,1750}}$: This ratio compares the current amount of carbon in the atmosphere ($M_{AT,t}$) to the levels before the Industrial Revolution ($1750$).

- The Logarithm represents the diminishing returns of $CO_2$. _Every time you double the concentration, you get a fixed increase in temperature._
- $\eta$ (Eta) is the climate sensitivity parameter
- $F^{Abate}_t$ & $F^{Ex}_t$: These represent forcings from sources other than $CO_2$ (like Methane or Aerosols). 

_Intuition:_ increasing $F_t \to$ increased temperatures.

The model indeed estimated the globa<b>l atmospheric surface temperature change</b> over pre-industrial levels, $T_t$ as a function of the history of CO2 emissions and other forcings:

$$
\begin{align*}

T_t &= f_t(E_0, E_1, \dot E_t) \\
&= f_t((1- \mu_0)\sigma_0 Y_0, (1 - \mu_1)\sigma_1 Y_1, \dot (1 -\mu_t)\sigma_t Y_t)

\end{align*}
$$

Then, the model summarizes the climate change impacts on economy with a <b>damage function</b> $D(T_t)$, which captures the faction of GDP-equivalent loss from warming $T_t$, so we can write:

$$
Y_t^{Net} = (1 - \Lambda_t(\mu_t)) \cdot \Big [ (1 - D(T_t)) \cdot Y_t^{Gross}   \Big]
$$

- reducing emissions (higher $\mu_t$) costs money today but reduces climate damage in the future ($T_t$ decreases)
- macro-level inter-temporal trade-off are analogous to standard consumption/saving decisions.

To understand the inter-temporal tradeoffs, we consider each household's <b>lifetime utility $U$ </b> as:

$$
U = u(c_0) + \sum_{t = 1}^{t = n} \Big[ (\frac{1}{1 + \rho})^t \cdot u(c_t) \Big]
$$

where $u(c_t)$ is concave function of utility of each household at period $t$ and $\rho$ the discount factor.

Recall that the final goal of the DICE model was to estimate the <b>social cost of carbon (SSC)</b>, defined he total damage that one extra ton of carbon emitted today causes to the world for the rest of time.

$$
\begin{align*}
SCC_t &= \frac{\partial W}{\partial E_t}  =\\


&= \sum_{j=0}^{T_{max}} \underbrace{\left( \frac{1}{1 + \rho} \right)^j}_{\text{Discount Factor}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{u'(c_{t+j})}{u'(c_t)}}_{\text{Wealth Effect}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\partial Y_{t+j}}{\partial T_{t+j}}}_{\text{Economic Damage}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\partial T_{t+j}}{\partial E_t}}_{\text{Climate Response}}

\end{align*}
$$

_Intuition:_ $SCC_t$ sums all the discounted future changes in utility _(wealth effect)_, weighted by the effect on future GDP changes _(economic damage)_ and the effect of future temperature changes _(climate damage)_.

![GDP by country](/images/notes/sust-econ/discounting.png)

Furthermore, regarding the DICE model, we can consider 5 <b>different scenarios</b>:

1. <b>Baseline:</b> Current policies + trends
    - _Global carbon price of $6/tCO2 in2020, growing 2.5% per year_
2. <b>Paris Extended:</b>Reach 2030 goals + continue

4. <b>2◦Target:</b> Maximize welfare + temperatur econstraint

Methods to value the enviroment include:
- statistical methods
- experiments 
- structural models 
- structured surveys

<b>Statistical Methods: Correlation vs Causation</b>

If two variables $A,B$ are correlated, it may be the case that:
- $A$ causes $B$
- $B$ causes $A$
- a third factor $C$ causes both $A,B$

The <b>omitted variable bias</b> occurs when the association of $A,B$ gives an iaccurate _(biased)_ impression of the causal impact of $A$ on $B$ _(or viceversa)_ because of an omitted third variable.

<b>Cross-sectional analysis</b> implies comparing outcomes across unit at a given point in time. _(example: evaluating different countries parameters in the same year)_. 

The omitted variable bias is particularly common in the cross-sectiona analyses since it is difficult to isolate effects of a specific variable out of all the other things _(ceteris paribus concept, please refer also to my <a href="https://giacomomaggiore.com/notes/15-econometrics#1-introduction-to-statistics-and-econometrics" target="_blank">Econometrics Notes about that</a>)_.

<b>Time-series analysis</b>, in contrast, compares outcomes across time.

<b>Pane analysis</b> tracks and compares outcomes across _both_ time and units. It casn easily control for _some_ omitted variables such as time-invariant differences across units or shocks that affected everyone.

In general, even with carefully designed analyses of sample data, we cannot guarantee that there are not omitted variables (bias), one way to ensure better accuracy is to couple <b>laboratory experiments</b> in controlled settings with <b>field experiments</b> in real world. _Example: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomized_controlled_trial" target="_blank"> randomized control trials (RCTs)</a>_.


---

<br/>
<b>Structural Models</b>

Experiments and quasi-experimental analyses of sample data can provide _precise insights_ from _specific settings_, but they cannot provide any intelligence regardging:
- interactions/effects not-observed in historical data 
- effects on a larger scale 
- welfare impacts of enviromental changes beyond immediate monetary costs.

<b>Structural models</b> can help us provide a broader measure of economic value of the enviromental capital.

_Strictly related to that_, we can define the <b>Value of Statistical Life (VSL) </b> as the _additional cost that invididuals would be willing to pay for improvements in satefy that, in the aggregate, reduce the expected numbers of fatalies by one_.

$$
\text{VSL} \neq \text{Value of an individual human life}
$$

VLS represents the value of the _mortality risk reduction_.<br/>
_Examples: how much are you willing to pay to travel in airplan with higher safety standards? How much are you willing to pay to go to the doctor for regular health-cancer checks?_

_Lets' consider this example:_ look at the labor market compensation for fatality risk:
- Job $1$: annual fatality risk: $2%$, Salary: $ 60 000 \text{CHF}$
- Job $1$: annual fatality risk: $1%$, Salary: $ 50 000 \text{CHF}$
$ \implies$ workers are paying $10 000 \text{CHF}$ to reduce fatality risk by $1%$.

If we consider 100 workers, 1 life would be saved statistically, so we can derive:
$$
\text{Implied VSL} = 100 \times 10 0000 \text{CHF} = 1 000 000 \text{CHF} 
$$

Structural model, in this context, often rely on VSL to estimate the impacts of a factor.

For example, the <a href="https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Nicholas_Muller/publication/253359043_The_Air_Pollution_Emission_Experiments_and_Policy_Analysis_Model_APEEP_Technical_Appendix/links/02e7e527248d726274000000/The-Air-Pollution-Emission-Experiments-and-Policy-Analysis-Model-APEEP-Technical-Appendix.pdf" target="_blank">APEEP (AP2, AP3) Model</a> analyzed the <b>total PM2.5 mortality health damages</b> caused by each sector in the US economy, with the following steps:
- models pollution emissions from almost 10'000 sources across US 
- models each flow of pollution present in  each county, independently on where the pollution originates 
- combines demographic data for each county with pollution, using a <b>Dose-Response function </b> to estimate how pollution correlates with mortality. _For example_:

$$
\text{Mortality} = \text{Baseline Death Rate} \times \text{Population} \\\times \text{Change in Concentration} \times \text{Response Coefficient}
$$
- translate mortality impacts into monetary terms based on the VSL approach.

A potentially more objective and more directly climate-relevant indicator may be the firm's <b>carbon emissions</b>.
- <b>Scope 1: direct emissions:</b> emissions from the firm's production _(the apprea highly consistent across data providers)_.
- <b>Scope 2: indirect emissions:</b> emissions from purchased heat, electricity, stea, cooling etc _(slightly less but still highly correlated across providers)_.
- <b>Scope 3: indirect emissions from productio of purchased materials, activities and services</b> _(data are more noisy, substantial disagreement)_.

However, often the carbon emissions are not a good indicator of how much a firm is green. <br/>For example, firms with higher carbon emissions and low ESG scores are also the most active _innovators_ in low carbon technologies.

Another potential solution is to measure <b>greenes at the _product_ level</b>.

---
<br/>
<b>Cost of Capital </b>

_How can we distinguish the effects of firm sustainability from potential other factors (such as skilled management or external factors)?_ 

If we collect data on corporate bonds spreads _(measure of borrowing costs or cost of capital)_ and ESG scores, we can estimate the following specification:

$$
\text{Spread}_{ij,t} = \alpha + \beta_1 \text{ESG}_{ij,t} + \beta_2 \text{Firm Control }_{ij,t} + \beta_4 \text{Industry} + \beta_5 \text{Year} \times \text{Year }+\epsilon_{ij,t}
$$

$\beta_1$ is the _"Sustainability" factor_, in other words it is the coefficient that tells you the relationship between the overall spread and the ESG ratings. If $\beta_1$ is negative and statistically significant, it suggests that as ESG scores go up, borrowing costs go down.

<br/>

<b>Carbob Offsets</b>

The _basic intuition_ behind this strategy is that if some other firms can reduce their gas emissions cheaper than a specific firm, that firm could pay the others to reduce its emissions, so _everyone can benefit_.
(Indeed, many companies use carbon offsets to hekp neutralize carbon emissions that are very expensive or impossible to eliminate).

Some possible _negative consequences_:
- <b>Rebound effect</b>: people may increase energy/resources consumètion in response to the offset.
- <b>Lack of additionality</b>: the "paid"/sold emissions would have been reduced even with out carbon offset program payments.
- <b>Carbon Leakage</b>: tree planting in one country may increase emissions inn other countries throguth displacement of agricultural land and increase imports.
<br/>
---

<b>Market Valuation of Firm Sustainability: Insights</b>:

With these challenges and potential problems in mind, let's study 5 general insights on _indirect benefits_ behind sustainability:
1. (Some)<b>Consumers are often willing to pay more for sustainable products</b>. _Intuitively_, this is easy to observe with products that differ only from an enviromental point of view _(same price, dimension, quality etc...)_. Also note that estimating demand curves _(how demand changes depending on sustainability)_ may be very difficult and require lot of data.

_For example_, in Switzerland, organic products have around $12%$ share of the total grocery expenditure, very high compared to the rest of Europe.

![GDP by country](/images/notes/sust-econ/eggs.png)
</div>

2. <b>Consumers are not always willing to pay more</b>, especially for CO2 emissions reductions or offsets. _For example_, considering 64k online bookings at a Swiss Airlin where $82%$ of the buyers could have paid for "carbon tax/offset" for less than $30$ EUR/Ton, only $5%$ actually offset their emissions. The median willingness to pay for offsets as $0$ EUR.

3. <b>Consumers sometimes respond even to unverified claims of greeness</b>, providing incentives for green-washing.

4. <b>Labor recruitment and retention may benefit fro corporate social responsibility</b>, even if evidence is still limited. This is particularly hard to proove since more sustainable firms may also have _better management_ and other reasons why they might have better labor outcomes. However, _experimental evidence_ shows that kobs seekers _(on average)_, give importance to ESG signals.

5. <b>Capital costs are likely lower for more sustainable firms</b>, even if evidence is mixed. In 2021, _for example_, Bolton and Kapcerzyk analyze the associatoin of firm's carbon emissions and stock returns in the US:

$$

_(However, it is not clear what measure for carbon emissions is the best for this use)_.

-  $+1 \text{ std dev}$ Scope 2 Total Emissions $\implies  +2.8%$ annual increase in stock return
-  $+1 \text{ std dev}$ Scope 3 Total Emissions $\implies  +3.6%$ annual increase in stock return

However, the study failed to capture significant effect of carbon emissions intensity _(emissions per unit of sales)_.

## <a id="5"><b>5. Market Value of Firms’ Sustainability Investments</b> </a>

<div align = "center">
![Sustainable Investing](/images/notes/sust-econ/sust-assets.png)
</div>

In the last chapter, we saw tha there's some evidence that _greener firms_ may face lower capital costs.

According to the <a href="https://www.gsi-alliance.org/" target="_blank">Global Sustainable Investment Alliance</a>, in the last years, there has been a increasing trend in sustainable investments:
- global sustainable investment grew $+55\%$ from 2016-2020, reaching around $ 35.3 $ trillion dollar.
- $35\%$ of AuM is made by sustainable investments in 2020.
- _"Green bonds"_, issued to support specific enviromental projects, represent also a growing market _(even if the methods to certificate them is still challenge)_.

So, if some investors prefer _"green securities"_ and there are enough of them, green investments may <b>lower the cost of capital</b>, leading to two potential outcomes:
- cause greener firms to growth relatively more _(growth effect)_ 
- incentivize firms to become greener _(reform effect)_.

Consider thenm the following <b>Partial Equilibium  profit-maximization model</b>.

Consider any firm maximizing profits by choosing capital $K$ with cost $r$, labor $L$ with wage-cost $w$, fossil energy $E$ with energy price $p_e$:

$$
\max p F(K,L,E)- rK - wL - p_e E
$$

Without green investors, if we normalize the price $p$ to $p = 1$ and hold labor constant for an easy rappresentation, we obtain the following simplication:

$$
\max F(K,E) - rK - p_e E
$$

that, if we put the partial derivatives of the profit function equal to zero, brings to the following maximizing conditions:

$$
\frac{\partial F(K,E)}·{\partial K} = r, \quad \frac{\partial F(K,E)}{\partial E} = p_e
$$

That, in ecomomic terms, translates into:

$$
\text{Marginal Product of Capital} = r \quad (\text{Cost of Capital})\\
\text{Marginal Product of Fossil Energy} = p_e \quad (\text{Cost of Fossil Energy})

$$


_(Generally speakiog, we assume that, holding other inputs costant, the marginal product of capital is decreasing)_.

We now introduce green investment, by saying that green investors can lower capital costs. For example, $r$ (cost of capital) may become an _increasing function_ of the energy consumption $E$:

$$
\max F(K, E) - r(E)K - p_e E
$$

In this case, the maximizing conditions become:

$$
\frac{\partial F(K,E)}·{\partial K} = r(E), \quad \frac{\partial F(K,E)}{\partial E} = p_e + \frac{\partial r}{\partial E}K
$$

$$
\text{Marginal Product of Capital} = r \quad (\text{Cost of Capital})\\
\text{Marginal Product of Fossil Energy} = \text{Cost of Fossil Energy} \\+
\text{ Marginal Capital Cost of increase from fossil energy}
$$

If then a firm is green enough to face lower capital cost $r(E) < r$, we would expect to increase its capital investments, resulting in the growth effect:

![Growth Effect](/images/notes/sust-econ/growth-effect.png)
</div>

Furthermore, the new equilibrium coming from the marginal product of fossil energy acts an an _effective tax_ on fossil inputs, incentivizing firms to reduce the use of fossil energy _(reform effect)_.

<div align = "center">
![Reform Effect](/images/notes/sust-econ/reform-effect.png)
</div>

- _2 years after a rating downgrade_ $\implies 13\%$ lower ownership

<b>Levelized Costs</b>

_How can we measure the costs of different electricity generating technologies in a comparable way?_

The <b>levelized cost</b> is the _(constant, real)_ price of power that would equate the net present value of revenue from a plantís output with the net present value of the cost of production.

Assuming that a plant existas for $N$ periods and produces $q_n$ units per period $n$, the present value of costs is:

$$
\sum_n^N \frac{C_n(q_1, ... q_N)}{(1 + r)^n}
$$

were $C_n$ is the cost of producing in period $n$ including also the capital costs.

With the same assumptions, the net present value of revenue is:

$$
\sum_n^N \frac{q_n p_{LCOE}}{(1 + r)^n}
$$

If we equate the expressions we can solve for $p_{LCOE}$:
$$
\sum_n^N \frac{C_n(q_1, ... q_N)}{(1 + r)^n} = \sum_n^N \frac{q_n p_{LCOE}}{(1 + r)^n}

$$
$$
p_{LCOE} = \frac{\sum_n^N \frac{C_n(q_1, ... q_N)}{(1 + r)^n}}{\sum_n^N \frac{q_n }{(1 + r)^n}
}
$$

The LCOE (levelized cost of energy) provides a easy way to compare cost of energy... but, there are two key facts about the electricity market that we must consider:
- it's costly to trasport and store 
- demand is higly volatile in space and time

$\implies$ _.The value of electricity depends critically on the timing, location, and controllability (dispatchability) of its production._

## <a id="8"><b>9. Welfare Economics</b> </a>

<Quote>
<b>Economics</b> studies human behaviour as a relationship between ends and scarce means which have alternative uses.
</Quote>

<Quote>
<b>Sustainability Economics</b> tries to iudnerstand and manage the long-term relationships between humans and nature so that scarse enviromental goods, and their human-made substitues or complements, are being used efficiently for achieving the normative goals of:
1. satisfaction of the needs and wants of individuals.
2. justice, including justice between humans of same and different generations and between humans and non-human nature
</Quote>

The sustainability challenge implies meeting _basic_ needs today while safeguarding opportunities for future generations:

<div align = "center">
![Donut Economics](/images/notes/sust-econ/donut.png)
</div>

<b>First Fundamental Theorem of Welfare Economics</b>

All market equilibria _(in a ideal-utopian market)_ are Pareto-efficient. 

<b>Second Fundamental Theorem of Welfare Economics</b>

Any Pareto efficient allocation can be achieved as a competitive equilibrium through a suitable redistribution of initial endowments, allowing for a separatio of efficiency and distribution concerns.

$ \implies$ _as the conditions for a perfect market are not usually satisfied, governmental actions should be considered_.

In particular, economic policy is both needed to <b>correct market failures</b>:
- too little internalization of external effects 
- too little provision of public goods 
- distorted competition due to market power or information asymetry 

or to enable a "fair" <b>distribution</b> between:
- humans of different generations _(intergenerational justice)_ 
- humans of the same generations _(intragenerational justice)_ 
- humans and the non-human nature _(interspecies justice)_ 

To select and analyze the right economic policy, we firstly need to define a <b>moral compass</b> _(moral theory)_:
- <b>Consequentialism</b>: justed the moral worth of the effects of an action.
- <b>Deontological values</b>: focus on judjing the actions themselves.
- <b>Virtue Ethics</b>: considers as the right action what a virtuout person whould do in that scenario.

However, the relationship between income and utility is not so simple. Assuming all other aspects fixed, common findings say that _utility increases with income at decreasing rate_:

$$
u(y) = \begin{cases}

\frac{y ^{1 - \eta}-1}{1 - \eta} \quad \text{for } 0 < \eta < \infty, \eta \neq = 1 \\
\ln(y) \quad  \text{for } \eta = 1

\end{cases}
$$

where $\eta$ determines the <b>elasticity</b> of marginal utility income:

$$
\epsilon_{u'(y),y} = \frac{\partial u'(y)}{\partial y}\frac{y}{u'(y)} = - \eta
$$

_Intuition: if income increases by by $1 \%$, marginal utility decreases by $\eta \%$._

<b>Utilitarism optimization</b>

In the case of $W^{utilitarism}$ for $n = 2$ in a pure distribution probel ($y_2 = S - y_1$), the maximization of the welfare function brings to:

$$
u'_1(y_1) = u'_2(y_2)
$$

If both individuals have identical preferences, equal incomes are welfare-optimal.

$$

that, if it feasible, to:

$$
u_1(y_1) = u_2 (y_2)
$$

Note that, in this csase, maximin optimization doesn't always bring to equal incomes.

<b>Utilitarianism optimization with unequal weights</b>

If we want to maximize the weighted sum of utilites with $D$ utility discount factor _(useful in the case of 2 generations, but could also work for the intertemporal utility of a single individidual)_, we write:

$$
W^{wu} = u_1 + D u_2, \quad D = \frac{1}{(1 + \delta)^t}
$$

<div align = "center">
![Utilitarianism](/images/notes/sust-econ/change-d.png)
</div>

In this scenario, consider now the utility discount factor $D = \frac{1}{(1 + \delta)^t}$ that, for different values parameters, assumes different meanings:
- $ \delta = 0 \implies D = 1$: <b>Impartial Consequentialism</b> 
- Arguments $\delta > 0 \implies D < 1$:
    - risk of humankind's exinction _(i.e. the change of surviving in the next 100 years)_ brings to:

    $$
    \mathbb{P}(\text{surviving}) = 0.5 = D = \frac{1}{(1 + \delta)^100} \implies \delta \approx 0.69
    $$
    $$
    \mathbb{P}(\text{surviving}) = 0.9 = D = \frac{1}{(1 + \delta)^100} \implies \delta \approx 0.11
    $$

    _Intuition: the greater probability of surviving, the utility discount factor $D$_.

Coming back at the maximization problem, it rewrites as:

$$
W^{wu}(y_1, y_2) = \frac{y_1 ^{1 - \eta}-1}{1 - \eta} + D \frac{y_2 ^{1 - \eta}-1}{1 - \eta}
$$

in the case of maximal distribution of a resource $S$:

$$
\max_{y_1} W^{wu}(y_1, S - y_1) = \frac{y_1 ^{1 - \eta}-1}{1 - \eta} + D \frac{(S - y_1) ^{1 - \eta}-1}{1 - \eta}
$$

that brings to the following welfare maximizing condition:

$$
y ^{-\eta} = D (S - y_1)^{-\eta}\\

S - y_1 = D^{1 / \eta} y_1 \\

y_1 = S / (1 + D^{1 \ \eta})
$$

<b>Critiques of Welfarism</b> 

<a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Rawls" target="_blank">John Rawls</a> gave the following the main key critiques:
- some people derive welfare from _"offensive tastes"_ 
- some people derive welfare from _"expensive tastes"_
- the conception of welfare are so _diverse_ and cannot be measurable 

In 1971, he then propose the <b>Theory of Justice</b>: 

1. each person is to have an equal right to the most extensive basic liberty compatible with a similar liberty ofr others.
2. socio-economic inequalities are to be arranged so that they are _(a)_ reasonably expeted to be everyone's advantage, _(b)_ attached to positions open to all.

_Intuition: equalization of equal liberty must be complete and is prior to the distribution of the other primary goods, then maximize the remaining three primary social goods starting to the lwast well-off_.

In contrast, <a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amartya_Sen" target="_blank">Amartya Sen</a> argued that Rawls went _too far_ in the opposite direction of welfarism since <b>primary goods are not the right maximanda</b>. Instead, the focus should be on <b>what goods to for people</b> _(capability approach)_.

In this debate, if the various institutional primary goods were equally supplied:
- Rawls would call for equalizing the remaining one _(income)_ 
- Sen would call for distributing income in order to equalize the functionoing that people can achieve with that income.
    - Sen defined a <b>person's capacility</b> as the set of vectors of functionings available to him $\implies$ he suggests not to equalize the indec of functionings, but rather for equalizing the capabilities across all people.
    - Also, since there are multiple measures of humand advantage and capability, Sen claims there's no single recipes for justice and proposes and ideal theory of justice to reduce clear inequalities.

## <a id="9"><b>9. Principles of Benefit-Cost Analysis in the Climate Context</b> </a>

A <b>cost-benefit analysis</b> is based on the idea of potential Pareto improvements and the <b>Kaldor-Hicks-Scitovsky</b> compensation criterion.<br/>
In this context, a reallocation _is desirable_ if:
- the winner could compensate the losers and still be better off 
- the losers could not compensate the winners for the reallocation not occuring and still be as well off as they would have been if it did not occur.

_Note that, in realty, these compensation rarely take place and don't necessarily balance out across different projects!_.

<b>CBA in Integraded Assessment Models (DICE)</b>

In simple BCA terms, we want to balance avoided climate damages with<b> mitigation costs</b>, defined as:


$$
\Lambda(t) = \theta_1(t)\mu(t)^{\theta_2}
$$

- $\theta_1(t)$: fraction of economic output that is required to reduce emissions to zero.
- $\mu(t)$:  emission control rate.
- $\theta_2$:  cost convexity parameters

Furthermore, we model the <b>famage function</b> as:

$$
D(t) = \psi T(t)^2
$$

- $\psi$:  aggregate scaling parameter for the damages on consumption via production-damages.

_Note that there's a quadratic Scaling: in the DICE model, damages scale quadratically with $T(t)$ _(change in atmospheric temperature compared to pre-industrial levels)._

As we seen before, the welfare function is then calculate as:

$$
W_0(\tilde{c}_t, L_t) = \sum_{t=0}^{100} L_t \frac{1}{(1+\delta)^{5t}} \frac{\tilde{c}_t^{1-\eta}}{1-\eta}
$$

- $L_t$:  population size in period $t$.
- $\delta$:  rate of pure time preference, it reflects how much less we value the utility of future generations compared to the present.
- $\eta$: the (minus) elasticity of the marginal utility of comprehensive consumption, it represents society's desire for consumption smoothing or inequality aversion.
- $\tilde{c}_t$:  index of generalized _(per capita)_ consumption.
