## <a id="1"><b>1. Introduction</b> </a>

<b>Economics </b> is the study of how people use scarse resources and respond to incentives.

<b>Sustanability economics</b> tries to understand and manage the long-term relationships between humans and nature so that scare enviromental goods, and their human-made substitues or complements, are being used efficiently.

Sustainability approaches tries to understand what do we want to <b>preserve</b>:
- Income, consumption
- Wealth, capital 
- Utility, social Welfare
- Possibilities, opportunities 
- Nature, ecosystem
- ...

An <b>Outcome-Oriented</b> approach seek to preserve realized human well-being over time _(actual experience)_.
<br/>An <b>Opportunity-Oriented</b> approach seek to preserve _means_ to generate welfare _(potential of the future)_.

In relation to this, we define <b>Capital</b> $K$ as stock that can be used to deliver a flow of services, and we distinguish:
- $K_h$: human capital _(people, education, health)_
- $K_p$: physical capital _(infrastructure, machines, buildings)_
- $K_n$: natural capital _(ecosystem, atmosphere)_

<b>Comprehensive Wealth</b> incluse the total value of all capital stocks. 

<b>- "Weak" sustainability</b> use and invest in natural and human-made capital stock such that comprehensive wealth doesn't decline:

$$
\frac{\partial K (K_h, K_n, K_p)}{\partial t} \ge 0
$$

- <b>"Strong" sustainability</b> use and invest in natural and human-made capital stock such that no stock $i \in \{ h, p, n\}$ falls below a critical level:
$$
K_i \ge \bar K_i
$$

_Example: a strong approach to climate change limits temperature change below critical levels (example: limit warming at 2° no matter of the cost)._ <br/>_A weak approach balances costs and benefits of emissions reductions (impose carbon tax to ensure that fossil fuels are only burned when their benefit exceed social cost)._

## <a id="2"><b>2.1 Economic Growth, Sustainability, Market Failures - Macroeconomic Refresher</b> </a>

<b>GDP</b> (Gross Domestic Product) is the _market value of the final goods and services produced within the borders of a country during a particular time period_.
<br/>It can be measured in 3 ways:
- <b>the production approach </b>
- <b>the expenditure approach </b>
- <b>the income approach</b>

<a href="https://ourworldindata.org/grapher/gdp-per-capita-worldbank?tab=line" target="_blank">GDP Stats by Country [Our World in Data]</a>

The idea behind the <b>production approach</b> is to sum up the market value of the final goods and services _produced_ in a year.

<b>Example: Giacomo's Racing Bikes</b><br/>
Giacomo's Land is a small country with one employer (Giacomo's GmbH) that produces 100 racing bikes a year. The market price of an racing bike is $10'000 \text{ CHF}$ _(yes, they are very expensive!)_.
<br/>All Giacomo's Land citizens work in the bike factory, that also owns all the machines, so it onlz needs to hire workers.

To determine the <b>market value of production</b>, we simply do:

$$
\text{Production} = 100 \text{ racing bikes} \times 10'000 \text{ CHF/bike} = 1'000'000 \text{ CHF}
$$

Note that we focus to the final value at tend of the production chain (_value added)_, so we sum the ssales revenues minus the firm's purchases of intermediate products to avoid double-counting.

The <b>expenditure approach</b> looks at the total value of expenditures on goods and services produced in the domestic economy by considering:
- $C$: consumption _(good and servies bought by domestic households)_
- $I$: new physical capital investments from domestic households and domestic firms
- $G$: government expenditures 
- $X$: exports of goods and servies produced domestically and sold abroach 
- $M$: imports of goods and services produced abroad and sold domestically

and calculating GDP ($Y$) as:

$$
Y = C + I + G + X - M
$$

<b>Nominal GDP </b> is the total value of production using _current_ market prices to determine the value of each unit.
- An increase in GDP will record both increases in _actual_ production and increases in the _prices_ of goods and services.

<b>Real GDP</b> is the total value of production using _constant_ market prices_.

Real GDP allows then to compute <b>real changes</b> in output across time since we adjust for the price levels.

In a similar way, <b>PPP (Purchasing Power Parity)</b> adusted income measures adjust for differences in cost of living across locations:

$$
\text{PPP Adjustments} = \frac{\text{Price of consumption basket in location A}{\text{Price of consumption basket in location  B}}}
$$

A very useful tool to visualize PPP adjustments is <a href="https://www.numbeo.com/cost-of-living/" target="_blank">Numbeo - Cost of Living </a>.

For example, <a href="https://www.numbeo.com/cost-of-living/compare_cities.jsp?country1=Italy&country2=Switzerland&city1=Naples&city2=Zurich&tracking=getDispatchComparison" target="_blank"> this comparison between Zurich and Naples</a> shows absolute different in prices.
If we want to compute PPP Adjustments, we simply do:

$$
\text{PPP Adjustments - Inexpesive Meal} = \frac{15.00 €}{28.59 €} = 0.52
$$

For example, in case we wanto to compare <b>salaries</b>, and we assume the following:
- <b>average pizza-man salary in Naples</b>: $25'000 $€
- <b>average pizza-man salary in Zurich</b>: $60'000 $€
we just have to re-compute the Zurich'one with the PPT adjustments:

$$
\text{PPP-adjusted Salary}_{Zurich} = 60'000 € \times 0.52 = 3120 €
$$

meaning that <b>purhcasing power of Zurich's pizza-men</b> is actually higher.

We recall that the <b>aggregate production function </b> for GDP is:

$$
Y_t = A_t \times F(K_t, L_t \cdot h_t) = A_t \times F(K_t, H_t)
$$

- $K_t$: investment and construction
- $L_t$: labor force participation
- $h_t$: education
- $A_t$: improvements in technology, institutions, management etc...

However, in the _long run_, the only source of sustained GDP per capita growth is the <b>growth in productivity</b> $A_t$. (Note that, _given the right incentives_, market can direct productivity improvements in almost any direction).

Starting from the aggregate production function, the <b>Integraded Assessment Modelds (IAMs)</b> integrade enviromental and resource dynamics into macro-economic analysis.

Firstly, we can account for the role of (fossil) energy $E£ in the GDP production:

$$
Y_t = A_t \times F(K_t, H_t, E)
$$

_Intuition: using more (and cheap) energy today increases GDP today_.

Then, we add climate model to account for effectsof fossil use energy on warking $T_t$:

$$
T_t = H(E_{1750, E_{1751}... E_t})
$$

and then we add $A_t$ to the GDP's formula:

$$
Y_t = A_t(E_t) \times F(K_t, H_t, E_t)
$$

_Note that, in reality, energy consumption $E$ affects all the determinants of GDP, not only the productivity (for example, investments depends on depreciation rate, also affected by climate change)._
Most important, climate changes also has strong effects that don't affect GDP directly (_non-market_ impacts such as elderly mortality, biodiversity etc etc...)

## <a id="2.2"><b>2.2 Economic Growth, Sustainability, Market Failures - Microeconomic Refresher</b> </a>

For a better review, please also refer to <a href="https://giacomomaggiore.com/notes/05-microeconomics">my microeconomic notes </a>.

<b>First Fundamental Theorem of Welfare Economics</b> states that, in absence of market failures, free competitive markets lead to the economically efficient outcome.

- _Intuition on the consumer side:_ consumers spend money on a given good until their marginal benefit from another slice is just equal to the marginal cost (the price they pay) of buying an additional unit.
- _Intuition on the producer side:_ firms produce as much of a good until their marginal cost is just equal to the marginal revenue they sell at.

<b>Markets allocate scarse resources</b> efficiently because prices serve as a signal of the value and cost of using the resorces to produce a given good.

A <b>market failure</b> is a problem that causes the free market to deliver an outcome that is inefficient.

- <b>Private marginal cost</b> is the direct cost of producers of producing an additional unit.
- <b>Social marginal cost</b> is the private marginal cost _plus any external costs or benefits_ of producing an additional unit.

_Intuition_: social marginal costs measure the full costs to society.

## <a id="2.3"><b>2.3 Economic Growth, Sustainability, Market Failures - Integrade Assessment Models (IAMs) and the DICE Model</b> </a>

IAMs are integrade models of the economy and the enviroment.<br/>
In macroeconomic, there are the following assumptions and behaviors:
- firms maximize profits 
- households maximise well-being
- agents anticipate the future 
- market equilibrium represents a _solution_.

Interdisciplary and policy IAMs often take these assumptions are given, but they miss the <b>feedback loop</b>: they calculate how growth affects the climate, but they struggle to calculate how much that climate change affects back into the economy.

---

<div align = "center">
![Nordhaus](/images/notes/sust-econ/nordhaus.png)
</div>

The <b>Nordhaus DICE Model</b> (1992) is based on the <b>Ramsey-Cass-Koopmans</b> growth model, whose aggregate production function is:

$$
Y_t^{gross} = A_t \cdot (K_t^{\alpha} L_t^{1 - \alpha})
$$

where $Y_t^{gross}$ is the global gross output, $A_t$ the total factor productivity, $K_t$ the capital stock and $L_t$ the labor. _(Note that many IAms also include energy services as production input)_.

The DICE models also introduce calculates the <b>baseline industrial carbon emissions </b> as:

$$
E_t^{Baseline} = \sigma_t \cdot Y_t
$$

where $\sigma_t$ is the <b>baseline emissions intensity </b> of the economy. _(Note that, in the past $\sigma_t$ showed a linear-decading trend)_.

In addition to this baseline decarbonization trend, society can undertake additional efforts to reduce carbon emissions so, if we define $\mu_t \in [0,1]$ as the fraction of emissions reduced, we can model:

$$
E^{Actual}_t = (1 - \mu_t) E_t^{Baseline}
$$

and then we represent the costs $\Lambda_t$ of emissions reductions as a share of GDP:

$$
\Lambda_t (\mu_t) = \theta_{1t}(\mu_t)^{\theta_2}
$$

The <b>Nordhaus DICE Model</b> also accounts for CO2 emissions and, from the <b>atmospheric carbon concentrations</b> $M_{AT,t}$, it models the <b>increased radiative forcing </b> $F_t$ (_measuring  how much extra heat (in Watts per square meter) is being trapped at any given time $t$._)

$$

F_t = \eta \left[ \log_2 \left( \frac{M_{AT,t}}{M_{AT,1750}} \right) \right] + F^{Abate}_t + F^{Ex}_t

$$
- $\frac{M_{AT,t}}{M_{AT,1750}}$: This ratio compares the current amount of carbon in the atmosphere ($M_{AT,t}$) to the levels before the Industrial Revolution ($1750$).

- The Logarithm represents the diminishing returns of $CO_2$. _Every time you double the concentration, you get a fixed increase in temperature._
- $\eta$ (Eta) is the climate sensitivity parameter
- $F^{Abate}_t$ & $F^{Ex}_t$: These represent forcings from sources other than $CO_2$ (like Methane or Aerosols). 

_Intuition:_ increasing $F_t \to$ increased temperatures.

The model indeed estimated the global atmospheric surface temperature change over pre-industrial levels, $T_t$ as a function of the history of CO2 emissions and other forcings:

$$
\begin{align*}

T_t &= f_t(E_0, E_1, \dot E_t) \\
&= f_t((1- \mu_0)\sigma_0 Y_0, (1 - \mu_1)\sigma_1 Y_1, \dot (1 -\mu_t)\sigma_t Y_t)

\end{align*}
$$

Then, the model summarizes the climate change impacts on economy with a <b>damage function</b> $D(T_t), which captures the faction of GDP-equivalent loss from warming $T_t$, so we can write:

$$
Y_t^{Net} = (1 - \Lambda_t(\mu_t)) \cdot \Big [ (1 - D(T_t)) \cdot Y_t^{Gross}   \Big]
$$

- reducing emissions (higher $\mu_t$) costs money today but reduces climate damage in the future ($T_t$ decreases)
- macro-level inter-temporal trade-off are analogous to standard consumption/saving decisions.

To understand the inter-temporal tradeoffs, we consider each household's <b>lifetime utility $U$ </b> as:

$$
U = u(c_0) + \sum_{t = 1}^{t = n} \Big[ (\frac{1}{1 + \rho})^t \cdot u(c_t) \Big]
$$

where $u(c_t)$ is concave function of utility of each household at period $t$ and $\rho$ the discount factor.

Recall that the final goal of the DICE model was to estimate the <b>social cost of carbon (SSC)</b>, defined he total damage that one extra ton of carbon emitted today causes to the world for the rest of time.

$$
\begin{align*}
SCC_t &= \frac{\partial W}{\partial E_t} \\


&= \sum_{j=0}^{T_{max}} \underbrace{\left( \frac{1}{1 + \rho} \right)^j}_{\text{Discount Factor}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{u'(c_{t+j})}{u'(c_t)}}_{\text{Wealth Effect}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\partial Y_{t+j}}{\partial T_{t+j}}}_{\text{Economic Damage}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{\partial T_{t+j}}{\partial E_t}}_{\text{Climate Response}}

\end{align*}
$$

_Intuition:_ $SCC_t$ sums all the discounted future changes in utility _(wealth effect)_, weighted by the effect on future GDP changes _(economic damage)_ and the effect of future temperature changes _(climate damage)_.
