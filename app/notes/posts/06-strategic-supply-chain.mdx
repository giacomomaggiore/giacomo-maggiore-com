Within a firm, there are several entities that play a role in the supply chain:
- <b>R&D</b> _(can improve internal supply chain, design products to optimize supply chain, design products to improve logistic [IKEA example], decide how often develop new products)_.
- <b>Finance</b> _(manage working capital, invoices and account payable/receivable, analyze tariffs and taxes that depend on supply chain, manage FX rate and hedging)_.
- <b>Operations</b> _(manage all the manufacturing and assemplying processes, key components of the supply chain management)_.

Between all the operators in the supply chain network, there's, _in all directions_, flow of:
- information
- materials
- money
- intellectual property

There are two important views to look at supply chain management:
- <b>The Cycle view</b>: each cycles occurs at the interface between to successive stages, it clearly defines processes and people involved, specifiying roles and responsibilities of each time. _According to this view, the customer order is the trigger for the whole chain._
$$
\text{Customer order} \to \text{Replenishment cycle } \to \text{Manufacturing cycle } \to \text{Purchasing cycle}
$$
- <b>The Pull/Push view</b>: processes belong to one of the following categories depending on the timing of their execution:
    - <b>Pull</b>: execution is started in response to a customer order _(REACTIVE)_
    - <b>Push</b>: execution is started in anticipation _(SPECULATIVE)_

### <b> Modularization & Postponmement</b>

<a href ="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/222558265_Evaluation_of_supply_chain_structures_through_modularization_and_postponement" target="_blanl"> Ernst & Kamrad framework </a> describes supply chain structures based on two levels:
- <b>Modularization</b> refers to the degree to which a product is composed by independent and stardadized parts ("modules"). _It concerns the relationship with suppliers, designers and materials._
- <b>Postponment </b> refers to the delaying and timing of the final differentiation of the product. _It concerns the timing of the assembly, the labelling, the logicist and the distribution._

Note that postponement can be done at different levels:
- <b>time postponement</b>: delaying the final assembly closer to the customer's order
- <b>form postponement</b>: delaying the product differentiation
- <b>place postponement </b>: delaying and separating the plant where the final assembly is done

Intermediaries outside the firm (wholesalelrs and distributors) can be used to facilitate the postponement.


<b>Example: Benetton's Sweater Postponement</b>
- Instead of dyeing cotton and yarn, Benetton makes the sweaters and postpones coloring the inventory.
- The company ships to the stores a limited quantity of sweaters to collect information about customers's tastes.
- The remaining un-colored sweaters is the dyed according to the collected data.

This results in:
- better reaction to customers's demands and trends
- delayed forecasts for specific colors
- ability to still use aggregated demand forecast to start manufacturing early

### <b>4 Phases of Supply Chain Management </b>

SCM can be divided in:
1. Design supply chain
2. Plan supply chain
3. Operate (execute) supply chain
4. Control supply chain

<br/>
<b>1. Design supply chain </b>
- Decisions about the structure and the processes each stage will perform
- Design decisions are _long term_ and _expensive_ to reverse
- Design must support _strategic objectives_ and take into account _market uncertainty_

<small>- Decisions: _locations and capabilities of plants, products to be made and stored in different locations, traportation modes, information systems._</small>

<br />

<br />
<b>2. Plan supply chain </b>
- Contrained by the configuration from the design phase
- Master planning with monthly and weekly time horizon

<small>- Decisions: _customer demand and forecast, markets to be served by each plant, subcontracting, inventory levels and policies, trasportations routes and schedules_ </small>
<br />
<small> - Several additional contraints to be considered: _demand uncertainty, exchange rates, capacity contraints_ ... </small>
<br />
<br />

<b>3. Operate (execute) supply chain </b>
- Contrained by the previous phases
- Time horizon: weekly and daily
- The goal is to implement operating policies as effectively as possible
- Decisions regarding individual customer orders 

<small>- Decisions: _allocation of orders to inventory, production or shipment, setting out order due dates, setting delivery schedules, plan replenishment orders_ ...</small>
<br />
<br />

<b>4. Supply chain control </b>
- Control and performance measurement are essential to keep the plan in line with the objectives
  - Anything measured improves
  - What you measure is what you get 
  - You can't improve if you don't measure!

### <b>Measuring SC performance </b>

Some frequently used KPIs:

$$
\text{Order Accuracy Rate} = \left(  \frac{ \text{Number of Correct Orders} }{\text{Total Orders Delivered}} \right) \times 100
$$
<small>share of orders delivered without any mistakes in product, quantity and quality. </small>
$$
\text{On-time Delivery} = \left(  \frac{\text{Orders Delivered on Time }}{\text{Total Orders Delivered}}  \right) \times 100
$$
<small>share of orders delivered on or before the committed delivery date </small>

$$
\text{Inventory Turnover} = \left(  \frac{\text{Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) }}{\text{Average Inventory Value}} \right) \times 100
$$
<small>how many times inventory is sold and replaced over a pediod </small>

$$
\text{Order Cycle Time} =\left(  \frac{\text{Total Order Processing Time }}{\text{Numbers of Orders Processed}} \right) \times 100
$$

<small>time it takes from receiving an order to fulfilling it </small>

Suppy chain metrics often need to be translated in financial metric in the balance sheet:
- Order Cycle time $\approx$ Cash _(Assets, Balance Sheet)_
- Invoice Accurary $\approx$ Receivable _(Assets, Balance Sheet)_
- Inventory $\approx$ Inventories _(Assets, Balance Sheet)_
- Distribution facilities, equipment and plants $\approx$ Property _(Assets, Balance Sheet)_
- Purchase order quantities $\approx$ Current Liabilities _(Liabilities, Balance Sheet)_
- Financing options for inventory and plants $\approx$ Debt and equity _(Liabilities, Balance Sheet)_

There's <b>consistency</b> and <b>support </b> between competitive strategy, supply chain strategy and other functional strategies.

- <b>competitive strategy </b> defines the set of customer needs to satisfy
- <b>product development strategy </b> defines the portfolio of products to develop
- <b>marketing & sales strategy </b> specifies how to segmenent the market and position the products

### <b>1. Understanding customer needs </b>

Identification of the need of the customer segment, based on individual attributes _(quantity needed, response time tolerated, variety needed, service level required, price, rate of innovation etc...)_

- <b> Demand uncertainty </b> is the uncertainty of customer demand for a product (as a whole)
- <b> Implied demand uncertainty </b> is the resulting uncertainty given the customer attributes and the portion of the demand the supply chain has to handle _(combined metric taking into consideration also the supply chain and the firm: not about how much is sold, but more about how it is sold)_

Some factors that increase _implied demand uncertainty_:
- range of quantity increases $\to$ great variance in demand
- lead time decreases $\to$ less time to fulfill orders
- required service level increases $\to$ the firm has to handle unusual and more problems
- rate of innovation increases $\to$ new products have naturally more demand uncertainty
- number of sale channels or distributors increases $\to$ total customer demand is more disaggregated

### <b>2. Understanding the supply chain </b>

After understanding the customers, next question is: _how does the firm best meet demand?_

The dimension/metric describing best the supply chain is <b>supply chain responsiveness</b>, that is the ability to:
- respond to wide ranges of quantities
- meet short lead times
- handle different products
- design and deliver innovative products 
- provide a very high service level

Note that:

$$
\text{Increasing Responsiveness} \to \text{Higher Costs} \to \text{Reduce SC efficiency}
$$

</div>

<b>Example: Responsive Supply Chain for Fast Fashion"</b>

Zara keeps the decoupling point very early in the process: products leave the fabric undyed and are completed once a trend is spotted _(form postponement)_.

This is an example of <b>high implied demand uncertainty</b>: local manufacturing, express delivery and centralized distributions centers _(both to maintain high visibility and fast lead times)_ are critical in this kind of market.

<b>1. Facilities: role in the supply chain </b>
- The _"where"_ of the supply chain
- Purposes:
  - <b>manufacturing and assemblying facilities </b>
  - <b>warehouses </b>
- They determine the supply chain _long term_
- Key factors to consider: _location (centralization vs decentralizatoin), capacity, manufacturing method (product vs process focused), warehousing methology (cross docking, automated guided vehicle, picking etc...)_.

<b>Cross-docking </b> is common in different industries, _including retail stores, ecommerce companies, manufacturers and logistics service providers (LSPs)_.

Products move right _from the inbound to the outbound dock_ without every staying in the warehouse, achieving faster delivery, less warehouse space, optimized inventory level, lower trasporation and labor cost.


<div align = "center">
![Strategic Scope Supply Chain](/images/notes/supply-chain/cross_dock.png)
</div>

- if responsiveness is a priority $\to$ large number of smaller facilities, decentralizatoin
- if efficiency is a priority $\to$ small number of larger facilities (economies of scale), centralization


<b>2. Inventory: role in the supply chain </b>
- Inventory exists because of a mismatch between supply and demand
- Different types of inventory _(raw materials, WIP, finished goods)_
- The level of inventory has impact on:
  - <b>Material flow time</b>: time between materials entering the sc and exiting the supply chain
  - <b>Throughput:</b> rate at which sales occur in a timeframe _(e.g. 8 units/day)_

There are different components in inventory decisions:
- <b>Cycle inventory</b>: average amount of inventory used to satisfy demand _(depends on lot size)_
- <b>Safety inventory</b>: held in case demand exceeds expectations _(tradeoff needed between its cost and the cost of losing sales)_
- <b>Seasonal inventory</b>: built up to cover predictable variability and seasonality in demand _(tradeoff needed between its cost and the cost of flexible production)_

As before:
- if responsiveness is a priority $\to$ large buffer inventory, locate inventory close to customers
- if efficiency is a priority $\to$ reduce and centralize inventory


<b>3. Trasporation: role in the supply chain </b>
- The _"how"_ of the supply chain
- Purposes:
  - Moving the products between stages
  - Allowing _"disintegratin"_ of the supply chain
- It determines the supply chain at _medium term_

Key factors to consider:
- trasporation mode
- route and network selection
- in-house or outsource

<br/>
- if responsiveness is a priority $\to$ faster trasporation _(express, air cargo)_, given that the customer are willing to pay for it
- if efficiency is a priority $\to$ slower trasporation modes _(sea, road)_, for customers whose priority is price

<br/>
<b>4. Information: role in the supply chain </b>
- Data analysis regarding inventory, trasporation, facilities and connection between different stages $\to$ coordination between stages
- Crucial to daily operations at each stage

Key factors:
- MRP (Manufacturing Resource Planning) $\to$ forecast, aggregate planning _(PUSH system)_According
- Sales and Operations Planning (S&OP)
- Enabling technologies _(blockchain, ML, Gen AI, Radio Frequency Identification, IoT, Cloud...)

Information and data analysis allow supply chain to become <b>more efficient</b> and <b>responsive </b> _at the same time_.

<b>5. Sourcing (Purchasing): role in the supply chain</b>
- Set of business processes required to purchase:
  - goods
  - services
  - logistic services
- sourcing decisions are determinant for:
  - degree of vertical integration (in-house vs outsource)
  - sourcing strategy (single vs multiple)
  - number of suppliers
  - contract with suppliers

$$
\text{Supply Market Research}\to \text{Make-or-buy Decisions }\to \text{Supplier Management}
$$

- if responsiveness is a priority $\to$ supply network focuses on high quality, speed and flexibility _(intensive supplier integration in logistic)_
- if efficiency is a priority $\to$  supply network focueseson "sufficient" quality and cost _(suppliers pool, economies of scale, negotiating power)_

<br/>

<b>Example: Vertical Integration vs Outsourcing </b>

Unlike traditional automotive companies (Ford, VVW) that outsource 80% of the components, Tesla "makes" its own parts _(including seats, batteries, engines and even software and accessories)_ to reduce supply risk and capture.
<br/>
Also, in doing that, Tesla maintains IP and avoid the "double marginalization" effect _(when both supplier and buyer add profit margins, resulting in a higher cost for the final customer)_.

<a href="https://d3.harvard.edu/platform-rctom/submission/tesla-versus-the-world-can-insourcing-beat-outsourcing/" target="_blank">More details here</a>.


<b>6. Pricing: role in the supply chain </b>
- Pricing strategies can be used to:
  - <b>match </b> demand and supply
  - <b> differentiate customer segments </b>

Key Factors to consider:
- pricing and economies of scale
- everyday low-pricing vs high-low pricing
- fixed price vs menu price
- differential prices _(e.g. depending on customer segments, response times)_

### <a id ="3"> <b>3. Demand Forecasting </b> </a>

Different forecasting horizons:
- <b>Strategic</b> (3+ years): _new products planning, facility location, research and development_ 
- <b>Tactical </b>(3 months - 3 years): _sales and production planning, budgeting_
- <b>Operational </b>(Up to 1 year, generally up to 3 months): _purchasing, job scheduling, workforce and production levels, job assignments_

<Quote>
Demand forecasting is calculating or predicting future demand of a product or service, usually as a  result of study and analysis of available historical data.
</Quote>

Consequences of bad forecasting:
- <b>Major financial impact</b> $\to$ excess recourses, unsold products or lost profits and customers
- <b>Misallocation of resources </b>

### <b>Overview of Forecasting Methods</b>




<div align = "center">
![Forecasting](/images/notes/supply-chain/forecast.png)
</div>

<b>The Delphi Method</b>
This method is a structured communication technique, _originally developed as a systematic, interactive forecasting relying on a panel of experts._

<div align = "center">
![Delphi Method](/images/notes/supply-chain/delphi_method.png)
</div>


<b>Causal model </b>
This method predicts one parameter as a function of others
$$
\text{Demand} = f(\text{interest rates, GDP, etc...}) = f(X_1, X_2, X_3 \dots X_n)
$$
Often, we assume a _linear model_:
$$
\text{Demand} = b_0 + b_1 X_1 + b_2 X_2 + b_3 X_3 \dots b_n X_n
$$

<b>Time-Seies Method </b>

$$
\text{Oberved Quantity (O)} = \text{Systematic Component (S)} + \text{Random Componend (R)}
$$

- $S$ is the expected value of demand (influenced by level, trend and seasonality)
- $R$ is the part of the forecast that deviates from systematic component

The <b>forecast error </b> is the difference between the forecast value and the actual value

$$
e_t = F_t - D_t
$$

A <b>time series </b> is a collection of past (historical) data from which we want to isolate patterns and use them to <b>predict</b> future data.

Some time-series models:

<b>Moving Averages</b>

Level in period $t$ is the average demand over the last $N$ values:

$$
L_t = (D_t + D_{t-1} + \dots +D_{t-N+1}) / N
$$
$$
F_{t+1 } = L_t
$$


- easily understood and computed
- provides a stable forecast
- lags a trend
- ignore complex relationship in data

<b>Simple Exponential Smoothing </b>

It is used when demand has no observable trend or seasonality.

- Start with a initial estimation of level $L$, assumed to be the average of historical data:

$$
L_t = (D_t + D_{t-1} + \dots +D_{t-N+1}) / N
$$
$$
F_{t+1 } = L_t
$$
After observing demand $D_{t+1}$, revise the estimates of level by making a <b>weighted </b> adjustment:

$$
L_{t+1 } = \alpha D_{t+1} + (1 - \alpha)L_t
$$
with $0 \le \alpha \le 1$
$$
F_{t+2 } = L_{t+1}
$$

-  _large number of periods_ taken into consideration $\to$  _small_  $\alpha$
- Low $\alpha (.0.5- .2)$ when data are fairly stable
- High $\alpha (0.2 - 0.4)$ when data are unstable

### <a id ="4"> <b>4. Supply Chain Network Design </b> </a>

Three main distribution strategies:

- <b>Intensive</b>: a product is distributed by wholesales/retailer through as many channels as possible _(coca cola, mars)_
- <b>Exclusive</b>: a wholesaler/retailer receives the exclusive right to sell the product for a geographical area _(franchises: burger king, mcdonald)_
- <b>Selective</b>: a company uses a limited number of selected wholesalers/retailers who whill give specific attention to the product _(swatch, ralph lauren)_ 

Retail channels of distribution and sales:
- <b>Online</b>
- <b>Brick-and-mortar</b>: business with physical presence

### <b>Basic models for supply chain network design/Optimization </b>

<b>Minimum Cost Flow Optimization Problems</b>

Assumptions and properties:
- at least one node is a supply node, at least one node is a demand node
- flow only possible in one direction (the arrow)
- each source has a fixed supply, each demand has a fixed demand
- the cost of the flow is _proportional_ to the amount of that flow 

The goal is to <b>minimize </b> the <b>objective function</b> (the total cost of all flow).
The solution is feasible if and only if the <b>sum of the supplies </b> equals the <b>sum of the demands </b>.

$$
\min T = \sum_i \sum_j c_{ij} x_{ij}
$$

with the following constraints:
- $\sum_j x_{ij} = s_i $ (supply)
- $\sum_j x_{ij} = d_j $ (demand)
- $\sum_i s_i = \sum_j d_j $ (supply equals demand)
- $x_{ij} > 0$

$c_{ij}$ denotes the cost of shipping from $i$ to $j$ and $x_{ij}$ is the decision variable _(the quantity shipped from $i$ to $j$),

For transporation costs, the optimization rewrites as following.
We assume $i = 1 \dots n$ supplier warehouse, $j = 1 \dots m$ customer regions, the the objective function is:

$$
\min T = \sum_i^n \sum_j^m c_{ij} x_{ij}
$$

- $\sum_j x_{ij} = s_i$ for each origin $i$ (all supply must be distributed)
- $\sum_j x_{ij} = d_j $ for each destination $j$ (all demand must be distributed) 
- $x_{ij} > 0$

Some special case modifications to the transportation problem:
- <b>Maximum shipping capacity </b>from $i$ to $j$: $x_{ij} < L_{ij}$
- <b>Minimum shipping guarantee </b>from $i$ to $j$: $x_{ij} > L_{ij}$
- if <b>supply > demand</b>: add a dummy destination with zero cost and its demand equals to the remaining supply
- if a rouce it not accessible: remove the corresponding decision variable

<b>Assignment problems</b>

Given a set of agents, a set of tasks and a cost table, the goal is to <b>match agents to tasks </b> so that all tasks are performed at the minimum possible ost.

_Note that_ an assignment problem is a special case of a minimum-cost flow problem in which <b>all suppliers and all demands are equal to 1</b>.

Some initial assumptions needed:
- the numbers of agents and tasks are the same ($n$)
- each agent is assigned to exactly one task and each task is performed by exactly one agent
- all agents are assigned and all tasks are performed

$$
\min T = \sum_i^n \sum_j^n c_{ij} x_{ij}
$$

- $\sum_j x_{ij} = 1 $ for each task $i$ (each task is done by 1 agent)
- $\sum_j x_{ij} = 1 $ for each agent $j$ (each agent is assigned at 1 task) 
- $x_{ij} > 0$
- $x_{ij}$ integer

Special case mofifications to the assignment problem:
- <b>Number of agents exceeds the number of tasks:</b> $\sum_j x_{ij} \le 1$
- <b>An agent can work one multiple tasks ($a$ tasks):</b> $\sum_j x_{ij} \le a$
- <b>Number of tasks exceeds the number of agents</b>: add enough dummy agents
- <b>An assignment is unacceptable</b>: remove the corresponding decision variable

<b>Transhipment Problems </b>

It is a special case of a transporation problem where the freight moves through intermediate nodes before reaching the final destination.
$$
\min T = \sum_i \sum_j c_{ij} x_{ij}
$$
- $\sum_j x_{ij} = s_i $for all supply nodes
- $\sum_j x_{ij} - \sum_j x_{}ij = 0 $ for all transshipment nodes
- $\sum_j x_{ij} = d_j $ for all demand nodes
- $x_{ij} > 0$

in a more general version, constraints can be written as:

$$
\sum_{j=1}^n x_{ij} - \sum_{j=1}^n x_{ji} = b_i 
$$

$$
b_i = \begin{cases}
= s_i \text{ for i is a supply node}\\
= 0 \text{ for i is a transshipment node}\\
= -d_i \text { for i is a demand node}
 \end{cases}
$$


$$
0 \le x_{ij} < u_{ij}
$$

_The first sum represents the outflow, the second the inflow and the third on the right the net flow._
_$u_{ij}$ represents the arc capacity_

<b>Maximum Flow Problems</b>
The goal is to maximise the amount flowing through the network rather than minimizing the cot of the flow.
In maximum flow problems, the source and the sink do not have fixed supplies and demands.

Some initial assumptions:
- all flow starts at one node (source $s$) and terminates at one other node (sink $t$)
- all other nodes are transshipment nodes
- flow is only allowed in one direction


$$
\max \omega
$$

such that
$$
\sum_j x_{ij} - \sum_j x_{ji} = \begin{cases}\omega 
\text{ for i = s }\\
- \omega \text{ for i = t}\\
0 \text{ for the remaining ones}

 \end{cases} 
$$

$$
l_{ij} \le x_{ij} \le u_{ij}
$$

<b>Shortest path problems </b>

The goal is to find the shortest path (with the minimum total length) from the origin ($i$) to the destination $s$.

$$
\min T = \sum_i \sum_j c_{ij} x_{ij}
$$
such that
$$
\sum_{j = 1}^n x_{ij} - \sum_{jk = 1}^n x_{ji} = \begin{cases}
1  \text{ for i = s}\\
-1 \text{ for i = t}\\
0 \text{ in the remaining cases}


\end{cases}
$$

### <a id ="6"> <b>6. Advanced Supply CHain Network Design and Optimization </b> </a>
<b>Capacitated Plant Location Models </b>

In this model, we assume $n$ plants with capacity $K_i$ serving $m$ regions with demand $D_j$, each of them with fixed ($f_i$), variable ($c_{ij}$) costs
$y_i$ is the decision variable indicating wheter plan $i$ should be open, $x_{ij}$ denotes the quantity transported from $i$ to $j$.

$$
\min \sum_j^n f_i y_i + \sum_i^n \sum_j^m c_{ij} x_{ij}
$$
such that

$$
K_i y_i - \sum_j^m x_{ij} \ge 0
$$

$$
D_j - \sum_j^n x_{ij} = 0
$$

$$
y_i = 0/1
$$

$$
x_{ij} \ge 0
$$

<b>Capacitated Plant Location Models with Single Sourcing </b>

The main different from the previous model is that now each region can be served _only by one plant_ (but a plant can serve multiple regions).
The optimization problem rewrites as follows:
$$
\min \sum_j^n f_i y_i + \sum_i^n \sum_j^m D_j c_{ij} x_{ij}
$$
such that

$$
K_i y_i - \sum_j^m x_{ij} \ge 0
$$

$$
D_j - \sum_j^n x_{ij} = 0
$$

$$
y_i = 0/1
$$

$$
x_{ij} \ge 0
$$

$$
x_{ij} = 0/1
$$

<b>Gravity Location Model </b>

First of all, in order to calculate distances, we need to transform geographical coordinates latitude, longitude and height, to cartesian cordinates $x,y,z$.
Then:
- the gravity location model assumes the customer are located as grid points on a planned
- every grid point point is a potential plant location/solution
- we look for the spot $(x,y)$ that represents the gravity of all customers.

Given the coordinates of each customer: $x_n$, $y_n$:
- we compute the distance from the center: $d_n = \sqrt{(x-x_n)^2 + (y- y_n)^2}$
- we set the quantity $Q_n$ to be shipped at each customer $D_n$
- we compute to each customer $F_n$ and the minimze the total cost:

$$
\min \sum_n^k d_n D_n F_n
$$

_Note that, since the objective function is not linear, there is no analytical solution for the problem, but only a iterative algorithm_.

<b>Minimum Spannin Tree Problems </b>

The goal is to minimize the  total lenght of links inserted in a network.

Starting with only the nodes for a network, the problem now is to design the network by deciding which links to add.

<b>Prim's algorithm</b>:
- Start with any node _(often the one with the cheapest edge)_
- Grwo the tree by repeatetly adding the cheapest edge connecting another node outside the tree
- repeat until all nodes are included
- break ties arbitrarly

### <b>The Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) Model </b>

<b>Cycle inventory</b>: the average inventory that builds up in the supply chain because a stage produces or purchases in lots.

$$
\text{Cycle Inventory} = Q / 2
 $$
 
 given $Q$ the quantity/dimension of each lot and assuming that demand is stable, replenishment is costant and indipendent of demand.

$$
\text{Avg. Flow Time } = \text{Avg. Inventory} / \text{Avg. Flow Rate}
$$

$$
\text{Avg. Flow Time from Cycle Inventory} = \text{Cycle inventory} / \text{Demand} = \frac{Q}{2D}
$$

Lower cycle inventory is better because:
- average flow time is lower
- working capital requirements are lower
- lower inventory holding costs

we then define:
- $C$ as the average price paid per unic purchased 
- $S$ as a fixed ordering/setup cost occurring eath time an order is placed.
- $H$ as the holding cost in the inventory _(including cost of capital, physical warehouse, products becoming obsolete etc...)_
(sometimes $H = hC$ as a fractoin of the unit cost of product)

we then assume the followings:
- production is istantaneous
- delivery is immediate and deterministic (no uncertainty)
- demand is costant over time
- each order implies a costant setup cost
- products can be ordered and analyzed individually 

Given $D$, $C$, $S$, $h$, we want to find $Q$ (lot size) and sn$ (order frequency / time between orders) that minimize the total annual cost.

- $CD$ annual material cost
- $\frac{D}{Q}$ number of orders per year
- $\frac{D}{Q}S$ annual ordering cost
- $\frac{Q}{2}H = \frac{Q}{2}hC $ annual holding cost

then the total annual cost is
$$
TC = CD + \frac{D}{Q}S + \frac{Q}{2}hC
$$

to minimize TC, we take the first derivative with respect to $Q$ and we set it equal to $0$:

$$
0 = 0 + \frac{-SD}{Q^2} + \frac{hC}{2}
$$
$$
 \frac{SD}{Q^2} = + \frac{hC}{2}
$$

$$
 \frac{2DS}{hC} = Q^2
$$

Rearraging we find the optimal lot size, also defined as the <b>economic order quantity</b>:

$$
Q^* = \sqrt{  \frac{2DS}{hC} }
$$

the order frequency $n$ is then computed as:

$$
n^* = \frac{D}{Q^*} = \sqrt{ \frac{DhC}{2S}}
$$

and the cycle time in years when demand is in years (how much time between orders) as:

$$
T^* = \frac{Q^*}{D}
$$

To sum up, the EOQ model:
- easy to compute
- does not require data hard to obain
- policies are ronust
- assumptions can be relaxed
- it gives a good overall idea and can be the starting point for more complicated models

### <b>Economic Production Quantity (EPQ) </b>

It is an extension of the EOQ that takes into account that the _inventory arrives in increments_ at $P$ (production rate).

Therefore the inventory builds up at rate $P - D$ when production is on and depleted at rate $D$ when production is off.

<div align = "center">
![EPQ](/images/notes/supply-chain/epq.png)
</div>

In this case, the total cost is adjusted and given by:

$$
TC_{EPQ} = \left ( \frac{D}{Q}S \right) + \left(  \frac{I_{max}}{2}H  \right)
$$

where $I_{max}$ is the maximum inventory and it is given by 
$$
I_{max} = Q \left( 1 - \frac{D}{P} \right)
$$

Following the same derivation of the EOQ, we obtain the following optimal lot size:

$$
Q^P = \sqrt{ \frac{2DS}{(1 - D/P)hC}}
$$

### <b>The Newsvendor Model </b>
The classical newsvendor problem occurs when:
- short selling period
- only one procurement/order opportunity _(every specific day for the newsvendor)_a
- stochastic/random demand
- products loose value after the period
leading to lost sales, leftovers and no value for any remaining unsold product.

We then define the associated costs:
- $C_0$: holding (overage) cost per unit remaining at the end of the period 
$C_0 = \text{Purchase Cost } - \text{Salvage Value}$
- $C_u$: shortage (underage cost) per unit at the end of the season
$C_u = \text{Sales Price} - \text{Purchase Cost}$

The decision variable is $Q$: the order quantity to maximize profit

$$
\mathbb{E} [\text{Cost } f(Q)] = C_0 \mathbb{E} [\text{Overage}(Q)] + C_u \mathbb{E} [\text{Underage}(Q)]
$$

To obtain the optimal $Q$, the following methods are possible:
- <b>Brute force enumeration: </b> Calculate $f(Q)$ for all possible $Q$
- <b>Compute </b> $\frac{\partial f(Q)}{\partial Q}$
- <b>Incremental Analysis </b>

Incremental analysis focuses on the cost/benefit of ordering one additional unit $(Q \to Q + 1)$
- if the extra unit is sold, you make additiona $C_u$ in profits.

This event happens with $\mathbb{P}(D > Q)$, therefore the expected benefit is:
$$
\mathbb{P}(D > Q) C_u
$$

- if the extra unit is not used, you wasted $C_0$.

This event happens with $\mathbb{P}(D \le Q)$, therefore the expected loss is:
$$
\mathbb{P}(D \le Q)C_u
$$

At the optimal $Q$, benefits and loss should balance each other:

$$
\mathbb{P}(D > Q) C_u = \mathbb{P}(D \le Q) C_u
$$

Collecting the terms, we obtain the optimal condition (also known as the <b>Critical Ratio)</b>:

$$
\mathbb{P}(D \le Q) = CR =  \frac{C_u}{C_u + C_0} 
$$

<div align = "center">
![Critical Ratio](/images/notes/supply-chain/cr.png)
</div>

For normally distributed demands, $Q^*$ is computed using the corresponding z-value:

$$
Q^* = \mu + z \sigma
$$

### <a id ="8"> <b>8. Coordination in the Supply Chain </b> </a>

Supply chain coordination means working together with the aim of improving performance, aligning plans and objectives.

To do that, each stage should take into account the effects of its actions on other stages.

Lack of coordination reduces total supply chain profits with different consequences in the "world": a famous example is the <b>bullwhip effect </b>(BWE).

The bullwhip effect happens when _small changes in consumer demand_ get amplified into  larger swings in orders and inventory as you move upstream close to the manufacturer.

<div align = "center">
![BWE](/images/notes/supply-chain/bwe.png)
</div>

- shortage/gamig and panic buying are classic cause of bullwhip effect
- stores impose limits on the maximum quantity people can purchase to address this

Different reasons behind the bullwhip effect:
- companies place <b>periodic orders</b>
- the demand forecast is based on <b>orders received </b> (often by retailers or intermediaries), not on the <b>end user real demand</b>
- the supply is allocated as a % of the amount ordered
- <b>promotional pricing </b> causes distortions in demand

This has negative effects on the whole supply chain:
- increased manufacturing cost
- increased inventory cost
- increased replenishment lead time
- increased transporation cost
- increased labor cost for shipping and delivering
- decreased availability of products
- worse relationship across the supply chain

resulting in a whole supply chain that moves away from the <b>efficient fronties </b> due to _higher costs and lower responsiveness_.

<b>Example: the Bullwhip effect in post-pandemic retail </b>

During covid, demand for <a href="https://www.onepeloton.com/" target="_blank">Peloton products </a> exploded, the company received a lot of orders and therefore ordered massive amounts of inventory.
When then demand stabilized, they were left with _inventoru excess_.

This is classic _information driver_ failure: the lack of real time data and the delay of orders led to an excess of stock.

In this cases, <b>Vendor Managed Inventory </b> or <b>collaborative planning and forecasting</b> to share real time data with suppliers can reduce bullwhip effects.

To compute levelness (or bullwhip effect), we firstly compute:
- the order variance ratio (OVR) at $I$ (manufacturer/ wholesaler level)

$$
OVR_I = \frac{\sigma_k ^2 (I_{outgoing})}{\sigma_k^2(I_{incoming})}
$$

where $\sigma_k ^2 (I_{outgoing})$ is the variance of outcoming orders at $I$ over $k$ intervals.

- the order variance ratio at $H$ (retailer/consumer level):
$$
OVR_H = \frac{\sigma_k ^2 (H_{outgoing})}{\sigma_k^2(H_{incoming})}
$$

BEW is said to prevail if:

$$
OVR_I > OVR_H 
$$

(the upstream $OVR$ exceeds downstream $OVR$)

To compute <b>stability</b> we firstly need to:
- define the schedule change at the level (echelon) in consideration at one poin in time:

$$
\text{schedule change}_{Ei} = \frac{\text{Maximum} - \text{Minimum}}{\text{Mean}}
$$

for each forecast for a particular time period.

- then the overall stabilit/instability <b>across $k$ periods</b> is defined as:

$$
\text{Instability}_{Ei} = \frac {\sum_1^k \text{schedule change}_{Ei}}{k}
$$

Instabilities are sait to increase _upstream_ when

$$
\text{Instability}_{Ei+1} >\text{Instability}_{Ei+1}
$$

### <b>Obstacles to coordination and remedial actions</b>

1. Incentive obstacles $\to$ _aligning goals and incentives_
2. Information and processing obstacles $\to$ _improving information accuracy_
3. Operational obstacles $\to$ _improving operational performance_
4. Pricing obstacles $\to$ _designing pricing strategies to stabilize orders_
5. Behavioral obstacles $\to$ _building strategic partnerships with trust_

Supply chain partnerships can be divided in 3 clusters:
- Type $I$: coordinationan on a limited basis, short term focus, involves one functional area of the firm.
- Type $II$: integration and integration of activities,  long term focus, multiple divisions and functions involved.
- Type $III$: significant level of integration, each firm views sees the other as a _exentension_ of their own firm, typically no "end-date" approach.
