## <a id="1"><b>1. Introduction to Economics</b> </a>

Economics is the study of how societies use scarse resources to produce and consume goods and services.

Each society's objective is to maximise the <b> the well-being </b> of current and future generations, while facing the following basic economics problems:

- Which goods to produce and in what quantities?
- How to produce the goods, which resources and what kind of technology should be used?
- For whom are the produced goods?
- How to produe and consume goods in a sustainable way?

### <b>Basic Principles of Economics (Neoclassical Theory) </b>

1. <b>People face trade-offs</b>: _making decisions requires trading off one goal, with all his benefits and disadvantages, against another_.
2. <b>Opportunity Cost</b>: _decisions require comparing costs and benefits or alternatives: the opportunity cost of an item is what you give up to obtain that item._
3. <b>Rational People think at the Margin</b>: _marginal changes are small, incremental adjustments to an existing plan: the economical corresponding concept of derivative. People make decisions by comparing costs and benefits at the margin._

In comparison to neoclassical microeconomics, <b>behavioral economics </b>considers psychological aspects to better explain people's behavior in the markets, _often assuming that humans are not always rational and don't always maximise their self-interest._

### <b> How people interact? </b>

If people/countries/firms own different resources or good and have different desires, they trade with the goal of increasing the general well-being.
An econmics system in which resources are traded and allocated throught the decisions of different agents is called <b> market economy</b>.

<small> _Adam Smith demostrated that in a market economy characterized by competitive markets, trade between agents leads to the maximization of the well being. This concept is widely famous as the <b>"invisible hand".</b>_ </small>

In a market economy, or more generally, in every society, agents make decisions about both the usage of:
- <b> Inputs (Resources) </b>: everything that the agent uses in the production process.
- <b> Outputs (Goods)</b>: everything that results from the production process.

The <b> Production Possibilities Frontier (PPF)</b> shows all the possible combinations of ouput given the available resources.

In a two goods $X, Y$ economy, it represents all the possible pairs $(X,Y)$ that can be produced or, in other words, the amount of $X$ you have to sacrifie to obtain a certain amount of $Y$.

## <a id="2"><b>2. Supply and Demand</b></a>

Supply and demand are the forces that make the market economies work, by determining the quantity produced and price at whith it is sold.

<b> Quantity demanded </b> is the amount of a good that the agents are willing to nuy at a given price.
    - The demand curve is the relationship between the quantity demanded and the price of a good. In formulas: $ Q_d = d(P)$
    - The market demand is the sum of all individuals demands for a good.

Movements in the price of a good can lead to:
- <b> The Income Effect</b>: if the incomes remain costant, then a fall in the price means that customer can afford and will buy more.
- <b> The Substitution Effect</b>: if the price falls, customers will to substitute more expensive goods with the "falling one".

Therefore, we can define relations between demanded goods:
- <b> Substitutes</b>: two goods for which the increase in the price of one leads to an increase in the demand of the other _(example: CocaCola and Pepsi)_.
- <b> Complements</b>: two goods for which the increase in the price of one leads to a decrease in the demand of the other _(example: petrol and cars)_.

<b> Quantity supplied </b> is the amount of a good that sellers are willing and able to sell.
- The <b> Law of Supply </b> states that the quantity supplied rises when the price of the good rises, in other words: $Q_s = s(P) $ is an increasing function.

<b> The Equilibrium Price $P_{ME}$ </b> is the price at which $Q_d = Q_s $.

    - _A high price tells that a product is demanded, a low price that the good is oveproduced._

### <b> Controls on Prices </b>
Controls on prices are often put when policymakers believe the market price is unfair. They can result in:
- Price ceiling: _legal maximum_
- Price floor: _legal minimum_

When a policymaker puts a price floor $P_{min}$, the equilibrium is given by:

$$P_{EM} = \max \{P_{min}, P_{market}\} $$

If $P_{EM} \le P_{market}$, we say that thet control is <b> not binding </b> (left), otherwise it is <b> binding </b> (right).

The same applies for the Supply Curve $S = s(P_n, P_1, P_2, \dots  P_i, Y, T)$.

_Note that, for simplicity, we can say: price causes a movement on the supply/demand curve, everything else cause a shift._

### <b> Elasticity </b>
Elasticity is a measure of how much buyers and sellers respond to changes in market conditions.

<b>Price elasticity of demand</b>, for example, measures the change in prices due to a given change in quantity, in formulas:

- <b>Point Elasticity</b>: _measures elasticity at a specific point in the curve._

$$
E_p = \frac {\partial Q}{\partial P} \frac{P}{Q} 
 $$ 

- <b>Arc Elasticity</b>: _measures elasticity over a range of prices._

$$
\bar{E_p} = \frac {\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \frac {\bar{P}}{\bar{Q}} 
$$

with $\bar{Q}, \bar{P}$ averages on the range.

A similar definition can be given to <b> Income Elasticity </b> that measures the changes in the quantity bought depending on the income:


$$
IE_{Q_b} = \frac {\partial Q_b}{\partial I}  \frac {I}{Q_b}
$$

- Normal goods: $IE_{Q_b} > 0$
- Luxury Goods: $IE_{Q_b} > 1$
- Inferior Goods: $IE_{Q_b} < 0$

Usually, we also have that products with more substitutes have the more price elastic demand _(more substitues => lower switching cost)_
Also, if a unit change price doesn't have any effect on the market, we usually have a _perfectly inelastic demand_ with a vertical demand curve _(same applies for supply)_<br/>

 with vertical supply curve.
### <b> Cross Price Elasticity </b>

Given two goods $b$, $m$, cross price elastiity is defined as:

$$
E_{Q_b P_m} = \frac {\Delta Q_b}{\Delta P_m} \frac {P_m}{Q_b}
$$

- Complements: $ E_{Q_b P_m} < 0 $
- Substitutes: $ E_{Q_b P_m} > 0$
- Indipendents: $ E_{Q_b P_m} \approx 0$

<b> Example: The Algebra of Price and Cross-Price Elasticity </b>

Given the demand and supply quantity $Q_s, Q_d$, the price $P$ and the product substitute price $P_s = 2$, find the absolute value of price elasticity of demand at the market equilibrium.

$$
Q_s = 8P - 8
$$

$$
Q_d = 10 - 2P + P_s
$$

We firstly compute equilibrium:

$$
8P - 8 = 10 - 2P + P_s \to 10P = 18 + P_s => P^* = \frac{18 + P_s}{10}
$$

$$
Q^* = Q_s(P^*) = 8P^* - 8 = \frac{64 + 8P_s}{10}
$$

We now compute elasticity as:

$$
|e_d| = |\frac{\partial Q_d}{\partial P} \frac{P^*}{Q^*} |=| -2 \frac{(18 + P_s)/10}{(64 + 8 P_s )/10}| = 2 \frac{18 + P_s}{64 + P_s} 
$$

## <a id="3"><b>3. Elasticity & Theory of Consumer Demand: Neoclassical Perspective</b></a>

The consumption of a certain good/service provides satisfaction to the consumer. <b> Utility </b> measures the level of satisfaction associated.

- <b> Early neo-classical economists </b> believed that utility can be measured numerically _just like weight, temperature or distance.

- <b> Modern consumer theory </b> states that utility cannot be measured numerically because is a subjective phenomenon.

Utility can also be measured in people's <b>willigness to pay </b> (WTP). The demands curve relflects the overall WTP for a good.

---

### <b> Budget Constraints </b>

If we assume that buyers are rational and whant to maximize their utility _(more is preferred to less)_, preferences and utilities cannot explain consumer behavior completely, reason why we need some budget costraints (limits on consumption bundles that a consumer can afford).

Given two goods $X, Y$, the <b> budget line </b> shows all the combinations $(X, Y)$ which can be bought with a certain income $I$:

$$
P_x Q_x + P_y Q_y = I
$$
$$
Q_x = \frac{I}{P_x} - \frac{P_y}{P_x}Q_x
$$

Of course, an increase in the income (corresponding in a budget line shift to the right) means that the consumer can buy more goods of both $X, Y$, assuming that prices remain the same.



### <b> Indifference Curvers </b>

Given two goods $X,Y$, an indifference curve represents all the consumption bundles $(X,Y)$ that bring the same level of satisfaction (utility).



<div align = "center">
![Indifference Curve](/images/notes/microeconomics/indiff_curve.png)
</div>

- Higher indifference curves (top-right) are preferred to lower ones.
- Indifference curves slope downward from left to right and are bowed inward.
- Indifference curves do not cross.

Also, we can recall the income e substitution effects:
- the <b>income effect</b> after a change in price moves the consumer to _higher or lower indifference curve_.
- the <b>substitution effect</b> after a change in price moves the consumer _along an indifference curve_.

Perfect substitutes and complements show <b> particular indifference curves</b>.
<div align = "center">
![Indifference Curve](/images/notes/microeconomics/substitutes_complements.png)
</div>


<b>Example: income and substitution effects with two goods $A,B$</b>

Initially, prices and quantities consumed by Giacomo are the following:

$$
P_A^1 = 10, Q_A^1 = 3, P_B^1 = 10, Q_B^1 = 7
$$

In a second period, price $B$ drops, resulting in:

$$
P_A^2 = 10, Q_A^2 = 2.5, P_B^2 = 5, Q_B^2 = 15
$$

We then know that, if Giacom could change its income to maintain the same utility of the first period, quantities consumed would be:

$$
Q_A^* = 1.5, Q_B^* = 9
$$

The substitution effect measures the change in consumption caused just by the changes in price, keeping utility costant: $Q_B^* - Q_B^1 = 9 - 7 = 2$. _(Note that with the substitution effect Giacomo is moving along the same indifference curve $\to$ same utility)_.

The income effect measures the change in consumption caused by the increase of purchasing power (for the price drop): $Q_B^2 - Q_B^* = 15 - 9 = 6$. _(Note that with the income effect the price change pushes Giacomo on a different level of utility)_.

---

The <b> marginal rate of substitution </b> (MRS) is rate at which consumer is willing to trade one good for another.
- It is equal in magnitude to the slope of the indifference curve
- High MRS represents an higher importance of the new good.

To represent the satisfaction gained from the consumption of a bundle $(X,Y)$, we use a <b> utility function </b> $u(X,Y)$.
- $u(X,Y)$ assigns a number to each bundles of goods so that more preferred ones lead to higher numbers.
- Numbers don't have a intrinsic meaning, therefore we cannot compare two different utilites function.

<b>Marginal utility </b> is the additional level of satisfaction obtained from extra unit of good.

$$
MU_X = \frac {\partial U}{\partial X}
$$

Usually, microeconomics assume that all utilities function have diminishing <b>marginal utility</b>.

Given two goods $(X,Y)$, the optimal consumption is obtained at:

$$
MRS_{Y,X} = \frac {MU_X}{MU_Y}
$$

## <a id="4"><b>4. The Cost of Production</b></a>

<b> The production function </b> is a simplied mathematical representation of the production process.

$$
Q = f(L, C)
$$
where, in this case $L,C$ repreent labor and capital.
$Q$ indicates the _highest output_ that a firm can produce for every specific combination of inputs.

Normally, we distinguish between two periods:
- the short run, with variable $L$ and fixed $\bar{C}$
- the long run, with both $L, C$ variable.

The <b> total product </b> measures the total amount of output produced in physical units of measurement (tons, kWh...)

The <b> marginal product of a production factor </b> (MP) is the extra ouput produced as one input increased by one unit:

$$
MP_i = \frac {\partial Q}{\partial F_i}
$$

where $F_i$ is a production factor. 
<div align = "center">
![Total and Marginal Product](/images/notes/microeconomics/marginal_product.png)
</div>

The diminishing (decreasing) marginal product property affirms that the marginal product of an input declines as the quantity of input decreases ($MP_i$ is decreasing function in $F_i$). _Of course, it's not a law of nature, just an empirical principle_.

---

### <b> Cobb Douglas Production function </b>

A very widely used production function in economics is the <b> Cobb Doublas Production Function</b>, defined as:

$$
Q = z C^{\alpha}L^{\beta}
$$

where $z, \alpha, \beta$ are costants.
We also get:

$$
MP_L = z \beta C^{\alpha}L^{\beta -1}
$$
$$
MP_C = z \alpha C^{\alpha - 1}L^{\beta}
$$

<b>Example: cost minimization problem with the Cobb Doublas Production function </b>

The firm _Jack Inc_  produces commodity $Q = 10$ using inputs labor $L$ $(P_L = 2)$ and capital $K$ $(P_K = 2)$, with the following production function:

$$
Q(K,L) = K^{0.7}L^{0.3}
$$

To solve the minization problem and obtain the optimal $K^*$, we first need to solve the production function for $L$:

$$
L^{0.3} = Q K^{-0.7} \to L = Q^{10/3}K^{-7/3}
$$

we then substitute $L, K$ in the cost function:

$$
C = P_L L + P_K K = P_L Q^{10/3}K^{-7/3} + P_K K
$$

we determine the cost-minimizing $K^$ by taking the first derivative:

$$
\frac{\partial C}{\partial K} = P_K + P_L Q^{10/3} \frac{-7}{3}K^{-10/3} = 0
$$

that brings to:

$$
K^* = (7/3)^{3/10} Q \left ( \frac{P_K}{P_L}\right)^{-3/10}
$$

### <b> Productivity Indicators </b>

A <b> partial productivity indicator </b> measures the firm's output in relation to a single production factor.
For istance, <b> labor productivity </b> can be defined as $Q/L$.

The <b> total factor productivity </b> (TFP) can be defined as the ratio of total ouput to an index of total input:

$$
TFP = \frac{\text{Total  Output}}{\text{Total Input Index}}
$$

TFP is generally used as a relative measured between different firms (countries) or the same firm (country) in different periods.
The total input index is often defined as a linear combination of different production factors or other indeces.

The <b> Tornqvist Index </b> provides, using the TFP, an indication of the overall growth rate:

$$
\text {Tornqvist Index} = \ln(\frac{TFP_t}{TFP_s}) = ln (\frac{\text{Output Index}_st}{\text{Input Index}_st})= ln(\text{Output Index}_st) - ln (\text{Input Index}_st) =
$$

$$
= \frac{1}{2} \sum^N_{i = 1}(v_{is}+v_{it})(\ln y_{it}-\ln y_{is}) - \frac{1}{2} \sum^N_{i = 1}(\omega_{is}+\omega_{it})(\ln x_{it}-\ln x_{is})
$$
where  $v_i$ is the revenue share, $y_i$ the output, $w_i$ the cost share and $x_i$ the cost share.

<small> _More about this index can be found <a target = "_blank" href = "https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indice_di_Törnqvist"> here </a>._ </small>

### <b> Costs, Revenues, Economic and Accounting Profit </b>

Generally, the economic goal of a firm is to maximize economic profit:

$$
\text{Profit} = \text{Total Revenue} - \text{Total Cost}
$$


The cost of production also includes <b> opportunity cost </b>, that is the benefit or value of the most valuable good forgone or something the firm has to forgone in order to operate.

Cost of production can includes both:
- <b> explicit costs</b>: input costs that require directly paymnent of money
- <b> implicit costs </b>: they do't require outflow of money and they are not recorded in the accounting system. _Opportunity cost, external and enviromental costs lie in this category_

The distinction between explicit and implicit costs highlights another important difference between economic and accounting profit.

$$
\text{Economic Profit} = \text{Total Revenue } - [\text{Explicit Cost}+\text{Implicit Cost}]
$$
$$
\text{Accounting Profit} = \text{Total Revenue } - \text{Explicit Cost}
$$

Other distinctions between the economic and accounting view are represented by deprecitation, sunk cost and costs related to negative externalities.

Cost of production can be further divided into:
- <b> Fixed Costs</b>: not determined by the quantity of output.
- <b> Variable Costs </b>: dependent on the quantity of output.
$$
TC = TFC + TVC
$$
_Note that, for many firms, the distinction between fixed and variable depends on the time horizon being considered: in the long run, fixed costs become also variable._


<div align = "center">
![Total Costs](/images/notes/microeconomics/total_cost.png)
</div>

We can then define:

$$
\text{Average Fixed Cost}: \text{AFC} = \frac {\text{Fixed Cost}}{\text{Quantity}} = \frac{FC}{Q}
$$

$$
\text{Average Variable Cost}: \text{AVC} = \frac {\text{Variable Cost}}{\text{Quantity}} = \frac{VC}{Q}
$$

$$
\text{Average Total Cost}: \text{ATC} = \frac {\text{Total Cost}}{\text{Quantity}} = \frac{TC}{Q}
$$

$$
\text{Marginal  Cost}: \text{MC} = \frac {\partial \text{TC}}{\partial Q}
$$

### <b> Economies of Scale </b>

- Economies of scale refer to the property for which the long-run average total cost falls, as the quantity increases due to:
    - Large initial investments in production capacity.
    - Transaction cost does not increase with output.
    - If the firm operates at scale, workers can specialize where they are more productive.
- Diseconomies of scale refer to the property for which the long-run average total cost rises.
- Costant returns to scale (CRS) refers to the property for which the long-run average total cost remains costant.

<div align = "center">
![Economies of Scale](/images/notes/microeconomics/economies_scale.png)
</div>

We use $ES$ to measure the previous attributes:

$$
\text{ES} = \frac {1}{\text{Cost Elasticity}} = \frac {1}{ \frac {\partial TC Q}{\partial Q TC}}
$$

- $ES > 1$: economies of scale
- $ES < 1$: diseconomies of scale
- $ES \approx 1$: CRS

### <b> Economies of Scope </b>
If the joint production of a single firm is greater than the output it could be achieved to different firms, each producing a single product, we speak of economies of scope.

In other words, if the cost of producing different output by one firm is lower than the aggregated cost or producing the same output in separated firms, we speak of economies of scope.

Economies of scope works because joint production allows to alloca resources in a more productive way, there's a better load factor on machine (_higher saturation_) and labor and there's risk diversification in R&D.

### <b> Isoquants </b>

A isoquant is a function representing all possible combinations of factor inputs (such as labor and capital) that can be used to produce a given level of output.
Perfect substitutes and complements show particular isoquants curve.

$$
\text{MRTS} = \frac{\text{MP}_L}{\text{MP}_C} = \frac{\frac{\partial Q}{L}} {\frac{\partial Q}{K}}
$$

### <b> Inefficienties and the Principal Agent Problem</b>

The previous derivation is based on the assumption that prices were always fair and there are not inefficienites, which in realy there are.

## <a id="7"><b>7. Consumer, Producer Surplus & Welfare Analysis</b></a>

Consumer and producer surpluses are used to measure the change in welfare due to a change in the market conditions.

The <b> consumer surplus</b> is the buyer's willigness to pay minus the amount the buyer actually pays; _it measures the extra amount of value in $ the consumer receives._<br/>
It increases as the price decreases.

_Consumer surplus is calculated as the area below the demand curve and above the price._

<div align = "center">
![Consumer Surplus ](/images/notes/microeconomics/consumer_surplus.png)
</div>

The consumer surplus may be useful to evaluate the benefits in the evalutation of a public investment (or similar) since it measures the increase of value for the final user based on the price paid.
Think, for example, at the cost-benefit analysis of the construction of a tunnel for a railroad.
<div align = "center">
![Consumer Surplus ](/images/notes/microeconomics/consumer_surplus.png)
</div>


The <b>producer surplus </b> is the amount a seller is paid for a good minimum the seller's cost or, in other words, _it is the excess of revenues over long run cost of production._<br/>It decreases as price decreases.

_Producer surplus is calculated as the area above the supply curve and below the price._

<div align = "center">
![Producer Surplus ](/images/notes/microeconomics/producer_surplus.png)
</div>

<b> Total surplus </b> (TS) is defined as the sum of both of them.
$$
\text{TS} = \text{Consumer Surplus} + \text{Producer Surplus}
$$
$$
\text{TS} = \text{Value to buyers} - \text{Cost to sellers }
$$

<div align = "center">
![Total Surplus ](/images/notes/microeconomics/total_surplus.png)
</div>

<b>Deadweight loss </b> is instead the loss to the society because the market is not producing at its most efficient level.

It is found as follow:
- compute the initial equilibrium $Q^*$
- find the new equilibrium given by an efficient $Q^{inefficient} < Q^*$
- the <b>deadweight loss</b> is calculated as the area of the triangle obtained by:
    - the demand curve _(top side)
    - the supply curve _(bottom side)
    - the vertica line $Q = Q^{inefficient}$.

This area is _almost always_ a triangle/arrow pointing to the initial/optimal equilibrium.

## <a id="9"><b>9. Firms in Competitive Markets</b></a>

A <b>perfectly competitive</b> market has the following properties:
- many buyers and sellers 
- firms are price-takers _(firms have no power to influence the price, they accept the equilibrium price given by supply and demand)_
- homogeneous goods
- firms can freely enter or exit the market
- no externalities
- perfect and complete information
- economic agents are rational

A market like that results in <b>perfect equilibrium</b> and <b>maximum welfare</b>.

A <b>Competitive firm</b>'s goal is to maximise its profit, producing in a efficient way to minimize costs and with a long term visition to maximise the market value of the firm.
$$
\text{Total Revenue (TR)} = P  Q
$$

$$
\text{Average Revenue (ATR)} = \frac{\text{TR}}{Q} = P
$$

$$
\text{Marginal Revenue (MR)} = \frac{\partial  TR}{\partial Q} = P
$$

The following charts show the equilibrium conditinos:

</div>

<div align = "center">

The <b>profit maximizing</b> condition is:

$$
\text{Marginal Cost (MC)} = \text{Marginal Revenue (MR)}
$$

and, in a competitive market, we have: $\text{MR} = \text{Price} $

Therefore, the profit-maximizing condition rewrites as follow:

$$
\text{Marginal Cost (MC)} = \text{Marginal Revenue (MR)} = \text{Price (P)}
$$

This conditions implies that a firm's marginal cost curve describes also it's <b>supply curve</b>.

![Equilibrium in the short run](/images/notes/microeconomics/short_run_supply.png)
</div>

As long as revenues minus variable costs ($TR - AVC$) cover the fixed cost partly, the firm should produce until $MR = P = MC$.

If instead the prices such that $TR < AVC$, the firms minimizes its loss shutthing down the production.

The critical market price (<b>Shutdown point</b>) is the point such that:
$$
P = AVC
$$

## <a id="11"><b>11. Monopolistic Competition</b></a>

Monopolisitc competition refers to the scenario in which many firms sell products that are <b>similar but not identical.</b>
- many sellers competing for the same customers
- each firm produces a product that is _slightly_ different from the others, each firm faces a downward demad curve
- free entry and exit
- the cross-price elasticites are large 

In the <b>short run</b>, momopolistic competition and monopoly behave similarly.

Short run economic profits (given by $P > MR = MC$) encourage new firms to enter the market, leading to:
- increased number of firms
- reduced demand faced by firms already in the market
- old monopolisitc firms' demand curve shift to the left
- demand for old monopolisit firms fall and their profits loss

The <b>short-run economic loss encourages </b>the firms to exit the market, leading to:
- decreased numbers of products offered
- increased demand faced by a single firm
- shift of the remaining firms' demand curve _to the right_
- increased profits for the remaining firms

There's an important difference between monopolistic and perfect competition:
- _in perfect competition_, each firm produces the optimum quantity at the optimum price ($P = MC$)

- in _monopolistic competition_, price exceeds marginal cost ($P > MC$), leading to:
    - a <b>mark up</b> on prices _(firms have unique products and then they can charge about marginal cost)_
    - an <b>excess capacity</b> _(the quantity produced is less than the most efficient one at scale because this leads to higher profits)_.

### <b>Examples: Competitive Market vs Cournot, Bertrand and Stackelberg Models in a duopoly </b>
Suppose a town with two firms, $A, B$ producing potable water.
Suppose, in a competitive market,  a demand curve given by: $P = 120 - Q$ and $MC = 0$.

Total revenues are given by:
%%
TR = PQ = 120Q - Q^2
%%

Differentiating to obtain $MR$:

$$
MR = \frac{\partial TR}{\partial Q} = 120 - 2Q
$$
We then set $MR = MC = 0$ to find the equilibrium quantity $Q^*$:

$$
0 = 120 - 2Q^* \to 120 = 2Q^* \to Q^* = 60
$$

In a monopoly market therefore, we would obtain $Q^*=60$ and, substituting $Q^*$ in the demand and revenue function:
$$
P^* = 60
$$

$$
TR = 120Q^* - (Q^*)^2 
$$

---

In a <b>Cournout Duopoly </b>, if we define $Q_A, Q_B$ quantities produced by firms and we set their marginal cost to zero again, we can compute again the market demand can be rewritten as follows:

$$
P = 120 - Q = 120 - (Q_A + Q_B)
$$

The profit functions are:
$$
\pi_A = PQ_A = (120 - (Q_A + Q_B))Q_A = 120Q_A - Q_A^2 - Q_A Q_B
$$

$$
\pi_B = PQ_B = (120 - (Q_A + Q_B))Q_B = 120Q_B - Q_B^2 - Q_A Q_B
$$

Each firm maximizes profits by setting the derivative to zero. 
$$
\frac{\partial \pi_A}{\partial Q_A} = 120 - 2Q_A - Q_B = 0 \to Q_A = \frac {120 - Q_B}{2}
$$
$$
\frac{\partial \pi_B}{\partial Q_B} = 120 - 2Q_B - Q_A = 0 \to Q_B = \frac {120 - Q_A}{2}
$$

Since the firms are identical, we set $Q_A = Q_B = Q^*$, that brings to:

$$
Q^* = \frac{120 - Q^*}{2} \to Q^* = Q_A = Q_B = 40.
$$

- The total output $Q$ is then $Q = Q_A + Q_B = 80$
- The market price is $P = 120 - Q = 40$
- Profits per firm are $\pi = PQ = 1600$

_(Confirming what we said before about quantity produced and price set in a oligopoly)_

---

In the <b>Stackelberg model </b>; we keep the same startgin assumptions, $P = 120 - Q$, with $Q = Q_A + Q_B$, and $MC = 0$.
We then define firm $A$ as the <b>first mover</b>, while firm $B$ <b>follows</b>.

To solve the equilibrium, we start from the end: firm $B$ observs quantity produced by $A$ and maximises his profits _(same as Cournot)_:
$$
\pi_B = PQ_B = (120 - (Q_A + Q_B))Q_B = 120Q_B - Q_B^2 - Q_A Q_B
$$
Differentiating and setting to zero:
$$
\frac{\partial \pi_B}{\partial Q_B} = 120 - 2Q_B - Q_A = 0 \to Q_B = \frac {120 - Q_A}{2} 
$$
Leading to firm $B$'s <b> reaction function </b> $Q_B = \frac {120 - Q_A}{2} $.

<b>Unlike in the Cournot model </b>, firm $A$ know how the follower $B$ will react, so $A$ moves in advance substituting $B$'s reaction function in his profit function:


$$
\pi_A = PQ_A = (120 - (Q_A + Q_B))Q_A = 120Q_A - Q_A^2 - Q_A \frac {120 - Q_A}{2}

$$

$$
\pi_A = 60Q_A - 0.5Q_A^2
$$

Now firm $A$ can maximize his profit setting the derivative to zero:

$$
\frac{\partial pi_A}{\partial Q_A} = 60 - 2(0.5)Q_A = 0 \to Q_A^* = 60
$$

We can now find quantity produced by firm $B$ by substitugin $Q_A^*$ in $B$^s reaction function:

$$
Q_B^* = \frac {120 - Q_A^*}{2} = 30
$$

That leads to a market price of:

$$
P^* = 120 - (60 + 30) = 30
$$

- The total output $Q$ is then $Q = Q_A^* + Q_B^* = 90$
- The market price is $P = 120 - Q = 30$
- Profits per firm $A$ are $\pi = PQ_A^* = 1800$
- Profits per firm $B$ are $\pi = PQ_A^* = 900$

We can easily notice that, being the first-mover, firm $A$ has significan higher profits than $B$ and that, since $P > MC = 0$, there are still some inefficienties in the market.

---

In a <b>Betrand competition model </b>, firms compete on price.
We them start from the initial starting assumptions: $P = 120 - Q$ (that can be rewritten as $Q = 120 - P$) and $MC = 0$ for both firms.

The logic behind Betrand competition is that:
- if $P_A < P_B$: firm $A$ captures all the demand: $Q_A = 120 - P_A$ and $Q_B = 0$ (the opposite also applies)
- if $P_A = P_B$: firms equally split the market: $Q_A = Q_B = \frac{120 - P}{2}$

Therefore, the profit function $A$ is:
$$

\pi_A(P_A, P_B) = 
\begin{cases} 
P_A(120 - P_A) & \text{if } P_A < P_B \\
P_A \left( \frac{120 - P_A}{2} \right) & \text{if } P_A = P_B \\
0 & \text{if } P_A > P_B 
\end{cases}
$$

In this scenario, each firm has an incentive to cut prices to capture the market until $P_A = P_B = MC$ ($= 0$ in this case).
We can then conclude:

- The market price is $P = MC = 0$
- The total quantity produced is $Q = 120 - P = 120$, with Q_A = Q_B = Q/2 = 60
- Total profits are $\pi_A = \pi_B = 0$

This leads to a situation with total market efficiency.

### <a id="13"> <b>International Trade </b> </a>

When countries own <b>different resources/goods</b> and have <b>different skills/utilieis </b>, trade can be possible and beneficiary to all partecipants.

<b>The theory of comprative advantage </b> states that countries should specialize in producing goods with the <b>lower opportunity cost</b>, produce a surplus of those goods nd them trade with other countries.

In general, _allowing some trade_ is bettern than _not allowing any_.
But this doesn't mean that everything that happend in the international economy make everyone better _(some agents gain, some others may loose)_.

Old theories (example: <b>The Ricardian Model</b>) models trade based on comparative advantage, starting from the assumption of _costant return to scale_, homogeneous goods and perfect competition.

The <b>New Trade Theory </b> by <b>Paul Krugman</b> assumes _increasing return to scale_, _network effect_, _product differentiation_, _love of variety_, _transport costs, enviromental costs_, _imperfect competition_ etc...

From this theories, <b> welfare conclusions </b> on the effects of trade policies can be different.

<Quote>

These new models call into doubt the extent to which actual trade can be
explained by comparative advantage; they also open the possibility that
government intervention in trade via import restrictions, export subsidies, and so
on, may under some circumstances be in the national interest after all.
</Quote>

The effects of free trade are shown comparing the <b> domestic price </b> and the <b>globalx price </b> (the price that prevails in the world market):
- if a country has a comparative advantage, then $P_{domestic} < P_{world}$ and the country will be an <b>exporter</b>.

<div align = "center">
![Exporting Country](/images/notes/microeconomics/exporting_country.png)
</div>
- if a country doesn't have any comparative advantage, then $P_{domestic} > P_{world}$ and the country will be an <b>importer</b>.
<div align = "center">
![Importing Country](/images/notes/microeconomics/importing_country.png)
</div>

In some cases, with international trade the gains of the winners exceed the losses of the losers, so the winners can compensate the losers and still be better of.
In reality, <b>compensation is rare </b>.

Without compensation, international trade expands the size of the _economic cake_, potentially leaving some participants worse off.

Possible trade policies are:
- <b> tariffs</b>: tax on goods produced abroad and sold domestically that raise the price of imported goods

<div align = "center">
![Tariffs](/images/notes/microeconomics/effects_tariffs.png)
</div>
- <b>import quota</b>: a limit on the quantity of a good that can be produced abroad and sold domestically
<div align = "center">
![Quota](/images/notes/microeconomics/effects_quota.png)
</div>

<b>Arguments for restricting trade</b>:
- loss of jobs
- national security & increased dependence on other countries
- infant industires face difficulties to emerge
- unfair competition
- enviromental aspects

While some of there have some merit, economicsts generally believe that <b>free trade is usually the best policy</b>.
However, free trade strategies shoyld be correctly designed and implemented to be sustainable and reduce the enviromental impact as much as possible.

### <b> E8. Equilibria in Competitive Markets </b>

The industry of ketchup (considered an highly competitive market)  is composed of identical firms with total cost:

$$
C(q) = 2 q_i^2 + 6q_i + 18
$$

for $q_i > 0$ and $C(0) = 0$, where $q_i$ is the firm's production of ketchup.

Suppose there are 100 ketchup producers. What is the short-run supply curve $Q_{SR}$ for the kecthup industry (expressing it as a function of the price)?

<b>Solution</b>

From the total cost function of a firm, we can find:

- the variable cost: $VC = 2q_i^2 + 6q_i$
- the marginal cost: $MC = \partial C(q) / \partial q_i = 4q_i + 6
- the average variable cost: $AVC = VC / q_i = 2q_i + 6$

In a perfectly competitive market: $P = MC$, provided that the price is above the minimum average variable cost.

We can indeed see that, if $q_i = 0$, $AVC = 6$, so a firm sells ketchup at $P = MC$ as long as $P > \min \{ AVC \} = 6$. (This is also called the <b>Shutdown Price</b>).

If we then set $P = MC$, we obtain:

$$
P = 4q_i + 6 \to 4q_i = P - 6 \to q_i = \frac{P - 6}{4}
$$

The ketchup industry is composed by 100 identical firms. 
The industry supply curve $Q_{SR}$ is the horizontal sum of all individual supply curves:

$$
Q_{SR} = 100 q_i = 100 \frac{P - 6}{4}
$$

So we can conclude that:

$$
Q_{SR}(P) = \begin{cases} 25P - 150 & \text{if } P \ge 6 \\ 0 & \text{if } P < 6 \end{cases}
$$


<b>Part 2:</b> What is the long-run supply curve  $S_{LR}$ with free entry? (Assume constant-cost industry).

<b>Solution</b>

The long run supply chain curve is the one supplying ketchup at the efficient primce $P$, where economic profits are driven down to zero.

This happens when $ATC$ (average total cost) is the minimum one.

To find it, we equal $ATC$ and $MC$:


$$
ATC = 4q_i + 6 = \frac{C(q)}{qi} \\

4q_i + 6 = 2q_i + 6 + \frac{18}{q_i} \\

2q_i^2 = 18 \to q_i^2 = 9 \to q_i = 3

$$

We now substitute $q_i = 3$ in the price equation to find both the price and the average total cost.

$$
P = ATC =  4(3) + 6 = 18
$$

<b>Part 3></b>

Suppose that the demand is $Q_D = 660 - 20P$. What is the long-run demand quantity?
Determine also how many ketchup producers there will be in the market.

<b>Solution</b>

If we substitue the equilibrium price $P = 18$, we find:

$$
Q_{LR} = 660 - 20(18) = 300
$$

Since each firm produces the efficient quantity $q_i = 3$, we will have $N = q_{LR} / q_i = 300/3 = 100$ producers of ketchup in the market.

### <b>E10. Competitive Market: Cournot Model</b>

Suppose a town with two firms, $A, B$ producing potable water.
Suppose, in a competitive market,  a demand curve given by: $P = 120 - Q$ and $MC = 0$.

Total revenues are given by:

$$
TR = PQ = 120Q - Q^2
$$

Differentiating to obtain $MR$:

$$
MR = \frac{\partial TR}{\partial Q} = 120 - 2Q
$$
We then set $MR = MC = 0$ to find the equilibrium quantity $Q^*$:

$$
0 = 120 - 2Q^* \to 120 = 2Q^* \to Q^* = 60
$$

In a monopoly market therefore, we would obtain $Q^*=60$ and, substituting $Q^*$ in the demand and revenue function:
$$
P^* = 60
$$

$$
TR = 120Q^* - (Q^*)^2 
$$


In a <b>Cournout Duopoly </b>, if we define $Q_A, Q_B$ quantities produced by firms and we set their marginal cost to zero again, we can compute again the market demand can be rewritten as follows:

$$
P = 120 - Q = 120 - (Q_A + Q_B)
$$

The profit functions are:
$$
\pi_A = PQ_A = (120 - (Q_A + Q_B))Q_A = 120Q_A - Q_A^2 - Q_A Q_B
$$
$$
\pi_B = PQ_B = (120 - (Q_A + Q_B))Q_B = 120Q_B - Q_B^2 - Q_A Q_B
$$

Each firm maximizes profits by setting the derivative to zero. 
$$
\frac{\partial \pi_A}{\partial Q_A} = 120 - 2Q_A - Q_B = 0 \to Q_A = \frac {120 - Q_B}{2}
$$
$$
\frac{\partial \pi_B}{\partial Q_B} = 120 - 2Q_B - Q_A = 0 \to Q_B = \frac {120 - Q_A}{2}
$$

Since the firms are identical, we set $Q_A = Q_B = Q^*$, that brings to:

$$
Q^* = \frac{120 - Q^*}{2} \to Q^* = Q_A = Q_B = 40.
$$

- The total output $Q$ is then $Q = Q_A + Q_B = 80$
- The market price is $P = 120 - Q = 40$
- Profits per firm are $\pi = PQ = 1600$

_(Confirming what we said before about quantity produced and price set in a oligopoly)_

### <b>E11. Competitive Markets: Stackelberg Model</b>


In the <b>Stackelberg model</b>; we keep the same starting assumptions, $P = 120 - Q$, with $Q = Q_A + Q_B$, and $MC = 0$.
We then define firm $A$ as the <b>first mover</b>, while firm $B$ <b>follows</b>.

To solve the equilibrium, we start from the end: firm $B$ observs quantity produced by $A$ and maximises his profits _(same as Cournot)_:
$$
\pi_B = PQ_B = (120 - (Q_A + Q_B))Q_B = 120Q_B - Q_B^2 - Q_A Q_B
$$
Differentiating and setting to zero:
$$
\frac{\partial \pi_B}{\partial Q_B} = 120 - 2Q_B - Q_A = 0 \to Q_B = \frac {120 - Q_A}{2} 
$$
Leading to firm $B$'s <b> reaction function </b> $Q_B = \frac {120 - Q_A}{2} $.

<b>Unlike in the Cournot model</b>, firm $A$ know how the follower $B$ will react, so $A$ moves in advance substituting $B$'s reaction function in his profit function:


$$
\pi_A = PQ_A = (120 - (Q_A + Q_B))Q_A = 120Q_A - Q_A^2 - Q_A \frac {120 - Q_A}{2}

$$

$$
\pi_A = 60Q_A - 0.5Q_A^2
$$

Now firm $A$ can maximize his profit setting the derivative to zero:

$$
\frac{\partial pi_A}{\partial Q_A} = 60 - 2(0.5)Q_A = 0 \to Q_A^* = 60
$$

We can now find quantity produced by firm $B$ by substitugin $Q_A^*$ in $B$'s reaction function:

$$
Q_B^* = \frac {120 - Q_A^*}{2} = 30
$$

That leads to a market price of:

$$
P^* = 120 - (60 + 30) = 30
$$

- The total output $Q$ is then $Q = Q_A^* + Q_B^* = 90$
- The market price is $P = 120 - Q = 30$
- Profits per firm $A$ are $\pi = PQ_A^* = 1800$
- Profits per firm $B$ are $\pi = PQ_A^* = 900$

We can easily notice that, being the first-mover, firm $A$ has significan higher profits than $B$ and that, since $P > MC = 0$, there are still some inefficienties in the market.

### <b>E12. Competitive Markets: Bertrand Competition</b>

In a <b>Betrand competition model</b>, firms compete on price.
We them start from the initial starting assumptions: $P = 120 - Q$ (that can be rewritten as $Q = 120 - P$) and $MC = 0$ for both firms.

The logic behind Betrand competition is that:
- if $P_A < P_B$: firm $A$ captures all the demand: $Q_A = 120 - P_A$ and $Q_B = 0$ (the opposite also applies)
- if $P_A = P_B$: firms equally split the market: $Q_A = Q_B = \frac{120 - P}{2}$

Therefore, the profit function $A$ is:
$$

\pi_A(P_A, P_B) = 
\begin{cases} 
P_A(120 - P_A) & \text{if } P_A < P_B \\
P_A \left( \frac{120 - P_A}{2} \right) & \text{if } P_A = P_B \\
0 & \text{if } P_A > P_B 
\end{cases}
$$

In this scenario, each firm has an incentive to cut prices to capture the market until $P_A = P_B = MC$ ($= 0$ in this case).
We can then conclude:

- The market price is $P = MC = 0$
- The total quantity produced is $Q = 120 - P = 120$, with $Q_A = Q_B = Q/2 = 60$
- Total profits are $\pi_A = \pi_B = 0$

This leads to a situation with total market efficiency.

Suppose there are 4 firms in the industry in the short-run. Calculate the profit maximizing price $P^*_{SR}, Q^*_{SR}, \pi*_{SR}$.

<b>Solution</b>

When $N = 4$, the demand curve is: $Q = 25 - P \to P = 25 - Q$.<br/> We can then calculate the marginal revenue $MR$ and set it equal to the marginal cost $MC$.

$$
MR = \frac{\partial (PQ)}{\partial Q} =  \frac{\partial (25Q - Q^2)}{\partial Q} = 25 - 2Q
 $$

$$
MC = 2Q = 25 - 2Q = MR \to \\
\to 4Q = 25 \to Q^* = 25/4 = 6.25
$$

This also gives: $P^* = 25 - Q^* = 18.75$.

<b>Part 2:</b> Monopolistically competitive markets are characterized by free entry and exit.<br/>
As more firms come into the market, each firm’s share of overall market demand will fall - that is, the typical firm’s demand curve will shift leftward. <br/>
Entry and the resultant leftward shift in firms’ demand curves will cease when firms make zero economic profit.<br/>
Given this process, what is the profit-maximizing output and price and how many firms will exist in the industry of this exercise in the long-run?

To find the profit-maximizing output and prie, as well the final $N$, we set two conditions:

- $MR = MC$ (as before)
- $P = ATC$ (price equals average total cost)

we know that:

$$
Q = 100/N - P \to P = 100/N - Q
$$

we can compute $MR$ as

$$
MR = \frac{\partial (PQ)}{\partial Q} = 100/N - 2Q
$$

and set $MR$ equal to $MC$:
$$
100/N - 2Q = 2Q \to 4Q = 100/N \to Q = 25/N
$$

we then set $P = ATC$:

$$
100/N - Q = 50/Q + Q
$$

if we substitute in the last equation $Q = 25/N$, we obtain:

$$
100/N - 25/N = 50N/25 + 25/N \\

50/N = 50N/25 \to 1/N = N/25 \to N^2 = 25 \to N = 5
$$
