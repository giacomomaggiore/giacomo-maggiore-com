## Autoregressive (AR) Models — Notes for Time Series and Macroeconomic Forecasting

## 1. Introduction

An **Autoregressive model (AR)** is one of the simplest and most fundamental models in time series analysis.  
It expresses the current value of a variable as a linear combination of its own past values and a random shock.

The AR model is particularly useful in **macroeconomics**, where many variables (like GDP growth or inflation) display persistence — meaning the past influences the present.

---

## 2. General Definition

An AR model of order $ p $, denoted AR($p$), is written as:

$$
y_t = c + \phi_1 y_{t-1} + \phi_2 y_{t-2} + \dots + \phi_p y_{t-p} + \varepsilon_t,
$$

where:
- $ y_t $ = value of the variable at time $ t $,
- $ c $ = intercept or drift term,
- $ \phi_i $ = autoregressive coefficients,
- $ \varepsilon_t $ = white noise error term, $ \varepsilon_t \sim (0, \sigma^2) $.

The model implies that current observations depend linearly on a finite number of past observations.

---

## 3. The AR(1) Case

The simplest case, AR(1), is:

$$
y_t = c + \phi y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t.
$$

- If $ |\phi| < 1 $: the process is **stationary** and mean-reverting.  
- If $ \phi = 1 $: the process has a **unit root** (random walk).  
- If $ |\phi| > 1 $: the process is **explosive**.

The mean and variance of a stationary AR(1) are:

$$
E[y_t] = \frac{c}{1 - \phi}, \quad
Var(y_t) = \frac{\sigma^2}{1 - \phi^2}.
$$

---

## 4. Stationarity Conditions

For the AR($p$) process to be **covariance-stationary**, all roots of the **characteristic polynomial** must lie outside the unit circle:

$$
1 - \phi_1 z - \phi_2 z^2 - \dots - \phi_p z^p = 0, \quad |z| > 1.
$$

Stationarity implies that the series has:
- Constant mean and variance,
- Autocovariances depending only on the lag, not on time.

If not stationary, we often **difference** the series to induce stationarity (as in ARIMA models).

---

## 5. Estimation

The parameters $ \phi_i $ and $ c $ can be estimated by **Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)** since the model is linear in parameters:

$$
\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} = (Y'Y)^{-1} Y'y,
$$

where $ Y $ contains lagged values $ [y_{t-1}, y_{t-2}, \dots, y_{t-p}] $.  
Alternatively, one can use **Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)** under Gaussian errors.

For small samples, Yule–Walker equations can also be used, based on sample autocorrelations.

---

## 6. Forecasting with AR Models

### One-step-ahead forecast

For AR(1):
$$
\hat{y}_{t+1|t} = c + \phi y_t.
$$

### Multi-step-ahead forecast

For horizon $ h $:
$$
\hat{y}_{t+h|t} = \mu (1 - \phi^h) + \phi^h y_t, \quad \text{where } \mu = \frac{c}{1 - \phi}.
$$

The forecast **converges to the long-run mean** $ \mu $ as $ h \to \infty $.  
Forecast uncertainty grows with $ h $ as errors accumulate.

---

## 7. Autocorrelation Function (ACF)

For a stationary AR(1):

$$
\rho(k) = \phi^k,
$$

meaning autocorrelation decays **geometrically** with the lag $ k $.  
This property makes AR models suitable for data that exhibit exponential decay in correlation.

---

## 8. Economic Interpretation

In macroeconomics, AR models are often used as **benchmark forecasting tools** for individual variables:

- GDP growth, inflation, or unemployment rates are often modeled as AR(1) or AR(2) processes.
- The **persistence** parameter $ \phi $ captures how strongly the past influences the present.
- Shocks $ \varepsilon_t $ represent **unexpected innovations** or disturbances (e.g., policy, technology, or demand shocks).

Even though AR models ignore cross-variable interactions, they remain valuable for **baseline forecasts** and for **testing richer models** like VARs or BVARs.

---

## 9. Relation to Moving Average (MA) and ARMA Models

- **MA(q)**: depends on past shocks instead of past values.
  $$
  y_t = c + \varepsilon_t + \theta_1 \varepsilon_{t-1} + \dots + \theta_q \varepsilon_{t-q}.
  $$
- **ARMA(p, q)**: combines AR and MA parts.
  $$
  y_t = c + \phi_1 y_{t-1} + \dots + \phi_p y_{t-p} + \varepsilon_t + \theta_1 \varepsilon_{t-1} + \dots + \theta_q \varepsilon_{t-q}.
  $$
AR models are thus a special case of ARMA with $ q = 0 $.

---

## 10. Advantages

- Simple and interpretable.
- Requires minimal data and assumptions.
- Good short-run forecasting performance for persistent series.
- Easy to estimate and compute analytically.

---

## 11. Limitations

- Ignores relationships between multiple variables (univariate only).
- Cannot capture structural changes or regime shifts.
- Overly simplistic for complex macroeconomic dynamics.
- Non-stationary series require differencing or transformation.

---

## 12. Example: AR(2) for GDP Growth

Suppose quarterly GDP growth $ g_t $ follows:

$$
g_t = 0.5 + 0.7 g_{t-1} - 0.2 g_{t-2} + \varepsilon_t.
$$

Interpretation:
- The positive $ \phi_1 = 0.7 $ shows persistence.
- The negative $ \phi_2 = -0.2 $ introduces a mean-reverting correction.
- The process fluctuates around the long-run mean $ \mu = \frac{0.5}{1 - 0.7 + 0.2} = 1.0 $.

---

## 13. Extensions

- **ARIMA**: integrates differencing for non-stationary series.
- **SARIMA**: includes seasonal components.
- **ARX / ARIMAX**: includes exogenous regressors.
- **VAR**: multivariate extension allowing interactions across variables.
- **BVAR**: Bayesian extension with prior shrinkage.

### Vector Autoregressive (VAR) Models — Notes for Macroeconomic Forecasting

## 1. Introduction

A **Vector Autoregressive model (VAR)** is a multivariate generalization of the univariate autoregressive model (AR).  
It captures **dynamic interdependencies** among multiple time series variables, such as GDP growth, inflation, and interest rates — all influencing each other over time.

Formally, for an $ n $-dimensional vector of variables:

$$
y_t = 
\begin{bmatrix}
y_{1t} \\
y_{2t} \\
\vdots \\
y_{nt}
\end{bmatrix},
$$

a VAR of order $ p $ (denoted VAR($p$)) is defined as:

$$
y_t = c + A_1 y_{t-1} + A_2 y_{t-2} + \dots + A_p y_{t-p} + u_t,
$$

where:
- $ y_t \in \mathbb{R}^n $ is the vector of endogenous variables,
- $ c \in \mathbb{R}^n $ is a vector of intercepts,
- $ A_i \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n} $ are coefficient matrices,
- $ u_t \sim (0, \Sigma_u) $ is a white-noise innovation vector with covariance matrix $ \Sigma_u $.

---

## 2. Intuition and Economic Interpretation

The VAR assumes that **each variable depends on its own lags and the lags of all other variables**.  
In macroeconomics, this is useful because most variables co-move and react to common shocks.

Example: a 3-variable VAR might include
$$
y_t = [ \text{GDP growth}_t, \text{Inflation}_t, \text{Interest rate}_t ]'
$$
where:
- GDP reacts to its own past, inflation, and interest rates.
- Inflation reacts to GDP and policy rates.
- Interest rates react to inflation and GDP dynamics.

This **endogenous system** avoids imposing strong a priori causal restrictions — allowing the data to “speak” dynamically.

---

## 3. Stationarity and Stability

A VAR($p$) is **covariance-stationary** if all roots of the **characteristic equation** lie outside the unit circle.

$$
\det(I_n - A_1 z - A_2 z^2 - \dots - A_p z^p) \neq 0, \quad \text{for } |z| \le 1.
$$

If the process is stationary:
- The unconditional mean is
  $$
  \mu_y = (I_n - A_1 - A_2 - \dots - A_p)^{-1} c.
  $$
- The system has finite variances and autocovariances.

If not stationary (unit roots present), one often uses **differenced VARs** or **Vector Error Correction Models (VECMs)** for cointegrated data.

---

## 4. Estimation

Each equation in the VAR can be estimated **by OLS** independently because all right-hand-side variables are the same in each equation.

Let:
$$
Y = [y_p, y_{p+1}, \dots, y_T]', \quad
X = [1, y_{t-1}', y_{t-2}', \dots, y_{t-p}'].
$$
Then the model can be written compactly as:
$$
Y = X B + U,
$$
where:
- $ B = [c, A_1, A_2, \dots, A_p]' $ is the matrix of coefficients,
- $ U $ is the matrix of residuals.

The OLS estimator is:
$$
\hat{B} = (X'X)^{-1} X'Y.
$$

---

## 5. Forecasting with VAR

Once estimated, a VAR model can generate **multi-step forecasts** recursively.

For $ h $-step ahead forecasts:
$$
\hat{y}_{t+h|t} = c + A_1 \hat{y}_{t+h-1|t} + \dots + A_p \hat{y}_{t+h-p|t}.
$$

### Forecast Error Variance Decomposition (FEVD)
Shows the proportion of the forecast error variance of a variable that is due to shocks in each variable in the system — revealing **sources of volatility**.

### Impulse Response Functions (IRFs)
Measure the **dynamic reaction** of each variable to a one-time shock in another variable, while holding all other shocks constant.

Both FEVD and IRF are central tools in **macroeconomic analysis** — used to interpret the transmission of monetary or fiscal shocks.

---

## 6. Identification of Structural Shocks (SVAR)

In macroeconomics, we often want to interpret shocks economically (e.g. a *monetary policy shock*).  
The reduced-form residuals $ u_t $ are **correlated**, so they cannot be directly interpreted as structural shocks.

We assume a **structural representation**:
$$
A_0 y_t = A_1^* y_{t-1} + \dots + A_p^* y_{t-p} + \varepsilon_t,
$$
where $ \varepsilon_t $ are **structural shocks** (orthogonal, $ E[\varepsilon_t \varepsilon_t'] = I $).

The relationship between reduced-form and structural form is:
$$
u_t = A_0^{-1} \varepsilon_t, \quad \Sigma_u = A_0^{-1} (A_0^{-1})'.
$$

Identifying $ A_0 $ requires **restrictions** (based on theory):
- **Cholesky decomposition** (recursive identification),
- **Sign restrictions** (modern approach),
- **Long-run restrictions** (Blanchard–Quah).

---

## 7. Advantages for Macroeconomic Forecasting

- **Flexibility**: captures rich dynamics among macro variables without strong theoretical constraints.
- **Empirical accuracy**: often outperforms large-scale structural models for short- to medium-term forecasts.
- **Transparency**: model specification and estimation are straightforward.
- **Scenario analysis**: via impulse responses and FEVDs, policymakers can explore "what if" scenarios.

---

## 8. Limitations

- **Overparameterization**: the number of parameters grows rapidly with $ n^2 p $.
- **Data-hungry**: requires long and stable macro time series.
- **No structural interpretation by default**: reduced-form VARs lack economic meaning without identification.
- **Non-stationarity**: macro variables often exhibit unit roots and cointegration — requiring adaptations (VECM).

---

## 9. Typical Macroeconomic VAR Example

Let:
$$
y_t =
\begin{bmatrix}
\text{GDP growth}_t \\
\text{Inflation}_t \\
\text{Interest rate}_t
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

A VAR(2) model:
$$
y_t = c + A_1 y_{t-1} + A_2 y_{t-2} + u_t.
$$

Forecasts of GDP growth depend on its own lags, past inflation, and past interest rates — and vice versa for the other equations.  
Shocks to the interest rate can then be propagated to inflation and GDP through impulse responses.

---

## 10. Extensions

- **Structural VAR (SVAR)** — economic identification of shocks.
- **Bayesian VAR (BVAR)** — introduces priors to shrink estimates, solving overfitting problems.
- **Time-varying parameter VAR (TVP-VAR)** — allows coefficients to evolve over time.
- **Factor-Augmented VAR (FAVAR)** — includes latent factors summarizing large information sets.
