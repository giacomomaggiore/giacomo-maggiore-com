## <b>Introduction to AR, VAR, and BVAR Models </b>


Time series models are essential tools in <b>macroeconomics </b>, where variables evolve over time and influence one another.  
To forecast them, most widely used are the <b>Autoregressive (AR) </b>, <b>Vector Autoregressive (VAR)</b>, and <b>Bayesian Vector Autoregressive (BVAR)</b> models.  

- <b>AR</b> is the simplest one and captures persistence in a single variable: the intituition is that its past values affect the current one.  

- <b>VAR</b> extends this idea to multiple variables, capturing the influence of each on the others.

- <b>BVAR </b>applies <a href="https://kiwidamien.github.io/shrinkage-and-empirical-bayes-to-improve-inference.html" blank="_blank"> Bayesian shrinkage  </a> to VARs, improving forecasts when data are limited or when there are many variables (reducing overfitting).

Below, you will find a more detailed overview of these methods.  

---

### <b>Autoregressive Model (AR) </b>

An AR model of order $ p $, denoted AR($p$), is written as:

$$
y_t = c + \phi_1 y_{t-1} + \phi_2 y_{t-2} + \dots + \phi_p y_{t-p} + \varepsilon_t,
$$

where:
- $ y_t $ = value of the variable at time $ t $,
- $ c $ = intercept or drift term,
- $ \phi_i $ = autoregressive coefficients,
- $ \varepsilon_t $ = white noise error term, $ \varepsilon_t \sim (0, \sigma^2) $.

The model implies that current observations depend linearly on a finite number of past observations.


The simplest case, AR(1), is:

$$
y_t = c + \phi y_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t.
$$

- If $ |\phi| < 1 $: the process is <b>stationary </b> and mean-reverting.  
- If $ \phi = 1 $: the process has a <b>unit root </b> (random walk).  
- If $ |\phi| > 1 $: the process is <b>explosive</b>.

The mean and variance of a stationary AR(1) are:

$$
E[y_t] = \frac{c}{1 - \phi}, \quad
Var(y_t) = \frac{\sigma^2}{1 - \phi^2}.
$$


For the AR($p$) process to be <b>covariance-stationary</b>, all roots of the <b>characteristic polynomial</b> must lie outside the unit circle:

$$
1 - \phi_1 z - \phi_2 z^2 - \dots - \phi_p z^p = 0, \quad |z| > 1.
$$

Stationarity implies that the series has:
- Constant mean and variance,
- Autocovariances depending only on the lag, not on time.

If not stationary, we often <b>difference</b> the series to induce stationarity.

 
## <b>Estimation and Forecasting</b>

The parameters $ \phi_i $ and $ c $ can be estimated by <a href="https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/machine-learning/ordinary-least-squares-ols/" target="_blank"> <b>Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)</b></a> since the model is linear in parameters:

$$
\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}} = (Y'Y)^{-1} Y'y,
$$

where $ Y $ contains lagged values $ [y_{t-1}, y_{t-2}, \dots, y_{t-p}] $.  
Alternatively, one can use <b>Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)</b> under Gaussian errors.



The one-step ahead forecast can be computed as:
$$
\hat{y}_{t+1|t} = c + \phi y_t.
$$

and the multi-step ahead for an horizon $h$ as:

$$
\hat{y}_{t+h|t} = \mu (1 - \phi^h) + \phi^h y_t, \quad \text{where } \mu = \frac{c}{1 - \phi}.
$$

Note that the forecast <b>converges to the long-run mean</b> $ \mu $ as $ h \to \infty $, but forecast uncertainty grows with $ h $ as errors accumulate.


We now introducte the <b>Autocorellation Function ACF </b> that, for a stationary AR(1) is defined as:
For a stationary AR(1), :

$$
\rho(k) = \phi^k,
$$

Clearly, autocorrelation decays <b>geometrically </b> with the lag $ k $.  
This property makes AR models suitable for data that exhibit exponential decay in correlation.



## <b>Economic Interpretation </b>

In macroeconomics, AR models are often used as <b>benchmark forecasting tools </b> for individual variables:

- GDP growth, inflation, or unemployment rates are often modeled as AR(1) or AR(2) processes.
- The <b>persistence</b> parameter $ \phi $ captures how strongly the past influences the present.
- Shocks $ \varepsilon_t $ represent <b>unexpected innovations</b> or disturbances (e.g., policy, technology, or demand shocks).

Even though AR models ignore cross-variable interactions, they remain valuable for <b>baseline forecasts</b> and for <b>testing more complex models</b> like VARs or BVARs.
But... AR comes with some limitations:

- Ignores relationships between multiple variables (univariate only).
- Cannot capture structural changes or regime shifts.
- Non-stationary series require differencing or transformation.

## <b> Vector Autoregressive (VAR) </b>

A **Vector Autoregressive model (VAR)** is a multivariate generalization of the univariate autoregressive model (AR).  
It captures <b>dynamic interdependencies* </b> among multiple time series variables, all influencing each other over time.

Formally, for an $ n $-dimensional vector of variables:

$$
y_t = 
\begin{bmatrix}
y_{1t} \\
y_{2t} \\
\vdots \\
y_{nt}
\end{bmatrix},
$$

a VAR of order $ p $ (denoted VAR($p$)) is defined as:

$$
y_t = c + A_1 y_{t-1} + A_2 y_{t-2} + \dots + A_p y_{t-p} + u_t,
$$

where:
- $ y_t \in \mathbb{R}^n $ is the vector of endogenous variables,
- $ c \in \mathbb{R}^n $ is a vector of intercepts,
- $ A_i \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n} $ are coefficient matrices,
- $ u_t \sim (0, \Sigma_u) $ is a white-noise innovation vector with covariance matrix $ \Sigma_u $, representing the unpredictable part of the process.


A VAR($p$) is <b>covariance-stationary</b> if all roots of the <b>characteristic equation</b> lie outside the unit circle.

$$
\det(I_n - A_1 z - A_2 z^2 - \dots - A_p z^p) \neq 0, \quad \text{for } |z| \le 1.
$$

If the process is stationary:
- The unconditional mean is
  $$
  \mu_y = (I_n - A_1 - A_2 - \dots - A_p)^{-1} c.
  $$
- The system has finite variances and autocovariances.

If not stationary (unit roots present), one often uses <b>differenced VARs</b> or <b>Vector Error Correction Models (VECMs)</b> for cointegrated data.


## <b>Estimation and Forecasting </b>

Each equation in the VAR can be estimated <a href="https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/machine-learning/ordinary-least-squares-ols/" target="_blank"> <b>OLS</b></a> independently because all right-hand-side variables are the same in each equation.

Let:
$$
Y = [y_p, y_{p+1}, \dots, y_T]', \quad
X = [1, y_{t-1}', y_{t-2}', \dots, y_{t-p}'].
$$
Then the model can be written compactly as:
$$
Y = X B + U,
$$
where:
- $ B = [c, A_1, A_2, \dots, A_p]' $ is the matrix of coefficients,
- $ U $ is the matrix of residuals.

The OLS estimator is:
$$
\hat{B} = (X'X)^{-1} X'Y.
$$


Once estimated, a VAR model can generate <b>multi-step forecasts</b> recursively.

For $ h $-step ahead forecasts:
$$
\hat{y}_{t+h|t} = c + A_1 \hat{y}_{t+h-1|t} + \dots + A_p \hat{y}_{t+h-p|t}.
$$

### Forecast Error Variance Decomposition (FEVD)
Shows the proportion of the forecast error variance of a variable that is due to shocks in each variable in the system — revealing **sources of volatility**.

### Impulse Response Functions (IRFs)
Measure the **dynamic reaction** of each variable to a one-time shock in another variable, while holding all other shocks constant.

Both FEVD and IRF are central tools in **macroeconomic analysis** — used to interpret the transmission of monetary or fiscal shocks.

## 6. Identification of Structural Shocks (SVAR)

In macroeconomics, we often want to interpret shocks economically (e.g. a *monetary policy shock*).  
The reduced-form residuals $ u_t $ are **correlated**, so they cannot be directly interpreted as structural shocks.

We assume a **structural representation**:
$$
A_0 y_t = A_1^* y_{t-1} + \dots + A_p^* y_{t-p} + \varepsilon_t,
$$
where $ \varepsilon_t $ are **structural shocks** (orthogonal, $ E[\varepsilon_t \varepsilon_t'] = I $).

The relationship between reduced-form and structural form is:
$$
u_t = A_0^{-1} \varepsilon_t, \quad \Sigma_u = A_0^{-1} (A_0^{-1})'.
$$

Identifying $ A_0 $ requires **restrictions** (based on theory):
- **Cholesky decomposition** (recursive identification),
- **Sign restrictions** (modern approach),
- **Long-run restrictions** (Blanchard–Quah).

---

## 7. Advantages for Macroeconomic Forecasting

- **Flexibility**: captures rich dynamics among macro variables without strong theoretical constraints.
- **Empirical accuracy**: often outperforms large-scale structural models for short- to medium-term forecasts.
- **Transparency**: model specification and estimation are straightforward.
- **Scenario analysis**: via impulse responses and FEVDs, policymakers can explore "what if" scenarios.

---

## 8. Limitations

- **Overparameterization**: the number of parameters grows rapidly with $ n^2 p $.
- **Data-hungry**: requires long and stable macro time series.
- **No structural interpretation by default**: reduced-form VARs lack economic meaning without identification.
- **Non-stationarity**: macro variables often exhibit unit roots and cointegration — requiring adaptations (VECM).

## 9. Typical Macroeconomic VAR Example

Let:
$$
y_t =
\begin{bmatrix}
\text{GDP growth}_t \\
\text{Inflation}_t \\
\text{Interest rate}_t
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

A VAR(2) model:
$$
y_t = c + A_1 y_{t-1} + A_2 y_{t-2} + u_t.
$$

Forecasts of GDP growth depend on its own lags, past inflation, and past interest rates — and vice versa for the other equations.  
Shocks to the interest rate can then be propagated to inflation and GDP through impulse responses.

## 10. Extensions

- **Structural VAR (SVAR)** — economic identification of shocks.
- **Bayesian VAR (BVAR)** — introduces priors to shrink estimates, solving overfitting problems.
- **Time-varying parameter VAR (TVP-VAR)** — allows coefficients to evolve over time.
- **Factor-Augmented VAR (FAVAR)** — includes latent factors summarizing large information sets.

A <b>Bayesian Vector AutoRegression (BVAR)</b> is the Bayesian extension of the classical <b>Vector AutoRegression (VAR)</b> model.  
It is widely used in macroeconomics to study and forecast the joint evolution of multiple interrelated time series _(GDP, inflation, and exchange rates)_ under the assumption that each variable depends both on <b>its own past values</b> and on the <b>past values of all others</b>.



## <b>Basic Structure of a VAR(p) </b>

Suppose we have $ K $ variables and consider $ p $ lags.  
The general VAR(p) model is written as:

$$
y_t = A_1 y_{t-1} + A_2 y_{t-2} + \dots + A_p y_{t-p} + \varepsilon_t
$$

where:


- $ y_t $ is a $ K \times 1 $ vector of endogenous variables (e.g., [GDP, Inflation, Exchange Rate]),
- $ A_i $ are $ K \times K $ coefficient matrices,
- $ \varepsilon_t $ is a vector of the error terms with covariance matrix $ \Sigma $.

The goal is to estimate:
$$
\hat{A}_1, \dots, \hat{A}_p, \hat{\Sigma}
$$

typically made through <a href="https://www.geeksforgeeks.org/machine-learning/ordinary-least-squares-ols/" target="_blank"><b>Ordinary Least Squares (OLS)</b></a> or <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximum_likelihood_estimation" target="_blank"><b>Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)</b> </a>.

- Parameters are **fixed**, not random.  
- Output: **point estimates** like $ \hat{A}_{ij} = 0.35 $.  
- The model provides a single best estimate of each coefficient.

The classical VAR thereofore assumes that <b>true parameter values exist</b>, and the task is to estimate them as accurately as possible from the sample.


The main problem of classical VAR is that, as the number of variables $ K $ and lags $ p $ increases, the number of parameters to estimate grows <b>quadratically</b>.  
For example, a VAR with 10 variables and 4 lags already has 400 coefficients to estimate, and that’s not even counting intercepts or covariances.

This leads to several practical issues:

- <b>Overfitting:</b> the model adapts too much to the sample data.  
- <b>Parameter instability:</b> small sample size leads to unreliable estimates.  

leading traditional VAR models become <b>unmanageable</b> and <b>statistically weak</b> when the dataset is small but the system is large.

---

## <b>The Bayesian Solution: Introducing Priors </b>

A <b>BVAR </b> mitigates this dimensionality problem by introducing prior information _(the priors)_ about the model parameters.  

Instead of estimating the coefficients only from the data, a BVAR combines:

1. <b>The likelihood </b> (what the data tell us)
2. <b>he prior distribution </b>(what we believe about the parameters *before* observing the data)

This combination yields a <b>posterior distribution</b> that balances data-driven evidence and prior beliefs.

In a BVAR, parameters are treated as random variables that have their own <b>probability distributions</b>.  
This reflects the idea that we are uncertain about the true values.

## <b>The Role of Priors (Prior Distributions) and the Estimation Mechanism </b>

The estimation procedure of a BVAR relies on the principles of <b>Bayesian inference</b>, where parameter uncertainty is explicitly modeled rather than ignored.

Let $ Y = (y_1, y_2, \dots, y_T) $ be the observed data and let the model parameters be $ \Theta = (A_1, \dots, A_p, \Sigma) $.  
The goal is to determine the posterior distribution $ p(\Theta \mid Y) $, which represents the updated beliefs about the parameters after observing the data.

According to <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bayes%27_theorem" target="_blamk"> Bayes’ theorem </a>:

$$
p(\Theta \mid Y) = \frac{p(Y \mid \Theta) \, p(\Theta)}{p(Y)}
$$

where:

- $ p(Y \mid \Theta) $: <b>Likelihood function </b>, capturing the probability of the observed data given the parameters.  
- $ p(\Theta) $: <b>Prior distribution</b>, giving information the parameters before observing the data.  
- $ p(Y)$: <b>Marginal likelihood</b> or evidence, serving as a normalizing constant.


Combining prior and likelihood yields the <b>posterior</b>:

$$
p(A, \Sigma \mid Y) \propto p(Y \mid A, \Sigma) \, p(A, \Sigma)
$$

So, in the BVAR, we obtain the followings

- Parameters are <b>random variables</b>.  
- Output is composed by a <b>posterior distributions </b> for each coefficient $ p(A_{ij} \mid Y) $.  
- Uncertainty is <b>intrinsic</b> to the model and explicitly quantified.  
- Forecasts integrate over parameter uncertainty:
  $$
  p(y_{T+h} \mid Y) = \int p(y_{T+h} \mid A, \Sigma, Y) \, p(A, \Sigma \mid Y) \, dA \, d\Sigma
  $$
- Estimation can produce full distributions, means, medians, or credible intervals.

In a BVAR, we are not looking for the true values of fixed parameters, but rather a probabilistic belief about them.

In many standard priors (e.g., Normal-Inverse Wishart), the posterior distributions remain in the same conjugate family, which allows closed-form analytical solutions for posterior means and variances.

When conjugacy does not hold, simulation-based methods (Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) or Gibbs sampling) are employed to approximate the posterior.
