## <a id="1"><b>1 Introduction to Statistics and Econometrics</b> </a>

<b>Econometrics</b> is the use of statistical methods to analyze economi data, starting _typically_ from <b>non-experimental data</b>.

Common goals of econometric analysis are:
- estimating relationships between variables 
- testing economic theories and hypoteses
- evaluating and implementing government and business policy

<b>Structure of Econometric Data</b>

- Cross sectional data 
    - observations that represent individuals, firms, cantons, countries _(normally, but non necessarily, at one point in time)_.
    - observations are drawn randomly from a population (if not, we have a sample-selection problem)
- Time Series Data
    - observations represents periods in time
    - observations are consecutive and hence not random 
- Pool Cross-Sections
    - at least two cross sections are combined in one data sectional
    - cross sections are drawn independently of each other 
    - can often be treaded similar to a normal cross section 
- Panel (or longitudinal data)
    - cross-sectional units followed over time 
    - panel data have a cross-sectional and time series dimension 
    - useful to account for time-invariant unbobservables and for model lagged responses 
<br/>
<b>Causality</b>

The definition of _causal effect_ of $x$ on $Y$ is: _"how does variable $y$ if variable $x$ is changed, but <u>all other factors are held costants?</u>_
<br/>(This concept is called <b>Ceteris Paribus</b>).

Simply establishing a relationship _(correlation)_ netweem variables can be misleading _(important distincition between correlation and causation!)_

There are multiple types of experiments that allow answering causal questions:
- <b>Randomized controlled trials (RCT)</b>
- <b>Natural experiments</b>
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Probability Review: Distributions</b>

- <b>Chi-Square Distribution</b>

Let $Z_i$ be $n$ independented random variables with $Z_i \sim N(0,1)$, then

$$
X = \sum^n_{i = 1} Z_i^2
$$

has a chi-square distribution with $n$ degrees of freedom and we write $X \sim \chi^2_n.

- <b>t-Distribution</b>

Let $Z \sim N(0,1)$ and $X \sim \chi^2_n$, then 
$$

T = Z / \sqrt(X/n)
$$

has a t-distribution with $n$ degrees of freedom and we write $T \sim t_n$.

- <b>F-Distribution</b>

Let $X \sim \chi_k^2$ and $Y \sim \chi_l^2$ and assume $X,Y$ independent, then:

$$
F = \frac{X}{k}\frac{Y}{l}
$$

has a F-distribution with $(k,l)$ degrees of freedom and we write $F \sim F_{k,l}$.

<b>Central Limit Theorem</b>

The standardized average $Z$ of any population with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$ is _asymptotically_ $N(0,1)$ distributed, or, in other words:

$$
Z = (\bar y - \mu)(\sigma / \sqrt(n)) \sim N(0,2)
$$

<b>Law of Large Numbers</b>

Let $y_1 \dot y_n$ be independent, identically distributed random variables with mean $\mu$, then:

$$
\lim_{n \to \infty}(\frac{1}{n} \sum^n_i y_i) = \mu
$$

## <a id="2"><b>2 Simple Regression Analysis</b> </a>

_How can we use the data to describe economic reltions or behaviors?_

$$
y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \mu
$$

- $y$: dependent variable _(outcome variable)_
- $x$: independent variable _(regressor, covariate, control variable, "the cause")_
- $\mu$: error term _(disturbance)_
    - it represents strictly _unpredictable_ random behaviors, _unspecified_ or _unobserved_ factors or an _approximation error_ if the relation is not perfectly linear.
- $\beta_0$: intercept parameter 
- $\beta_1$: slope parameter 

We start now with <b>Population Modelling</b> and consider the following assumptions:

- <b>SLR.1: Linear parameters</b>
    - in the population model the following relation holds: $y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \mu$

- <b>SLR.2: Random sampling</b> 
    - we have a ranbdom sample of dize $n$ of random variables $(x,y)$

- <b>SLR.3: Sample variation in the explanatory variable</b>
    - the sample outcome on $x$ are not all the same value

- <b>SLR.4: Zero Conditional Mean </b>
$$
\mathbb{E}[\mu | x] = \mathbb{E}[\mu] = 0
$$
or, _in other words_, for every slices of population determined by $x$, the average of $\mu$ i eaul to the population average $\mu$ (which is zero).
<br/>This also implies:

$$
\mathbb{E}[y | x] = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x
$$

also called the <b>Populatio Regression Function</b>.

We now derive the <b>Ordinary Least Squares</b> (OLS).

_Intuition:_ we estimate the population parameters from a <b>data sample</b> of two random variables.

- Let $\{(x_i, y_i)  \} : \quad i = 1 \dot n$ denonte a random sample of size $n$
- scatter plot in a $(x,y)$ system
- the regression equation $y_i = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_i + \mu_i$ allocates to each $x_i$ a value $y_i$ including a disturbance term $\mu_i$.

But _how can we derive the estimates for $\beta_0, \beta_1$

We firstly rely on the <b>SLR.4</b> assumption to derive two equations:
$$
\begin{align*}
\mathbb{E}[\mu] &= 0 \quad  \implies \mathbb{E}[y - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x] = 0 \\

\mathbb{E}[u | x] &=0 \quad \implies \mathrm{Cov}[x,u] = \mathbb{E}[x \mu] - \mathbb{E}[x]\mathbb{E}[\mu] =\\
&= \mathbb{E}[x \mu] = \mathbb{E}[ x ( y - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x)] = 0

\end{align*}
$$

These are called <b>populatio moment restrictions</b>

Using then the <b>sample moments</b> from the population, we can get an estimates of the population moments.

Recall:
- $\sum_i (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_i)/n = 0\quad \quad  (1)$
- $\sum_i x_i((y_i - \beta_0) - \beta_1 x_i)/n = 0 \quad (2)$

we retwrite $(1$) as: $\quad b_0 = \bar y - b_1 \bar x $, then we then plug into $(2)$ and solve for $b_1$:

$$

b_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})(y_i - \bar{y})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2}

$$

Finally, we can easily put back $b_1$ into the first equation and derive $b_0$.

_Intuition_: the slope estimate is the <b>sample covariance </b> $x$ and $y$, divided by the sample variance $x$.
- if $x,y$ are positively correlated, the slope will be positive.
- if $x,y$ are negatively correlated, the slope will be negative.

_Moreover, intuitively_, the OLS is fitting a line through the sample points such that the <b>sum of squared residuals is as small as possible</b>.

<b>Algebraic Properties of OLS</b>:
- the sum of of the OLS residuals is zero.
- the sample average of the OLS residuals is zero.
- the sample covariance between the regressors and the OLS residuals is zero.
- the OLS regression line alwyas goes through the mean of the sample.

Moreover, we notice that _each observation $y_i$_ is made up of an explained $\hat y_i$ and unexplained part $\hat u_i$ ($y_i = \hat y_i + \hat u_i$).

Using this terminology, we can define:
- $\text{Sum of Squares Total (SST)} = \sum_i (y_i - \bar y)^2$
- $\text{Sum of Squares Explained (SST)} = \sum_i (\hat y_i - \bar y)^2$
- $\text{Sum of Squares Residuals (SST)} = \sum_i (\cap \mu_i)^2$

To understand how well does the sample fit the regression line, we define: <span id="R_squared"></span>

$$
R^2 = \frac{\text{SSE}}{\text{SST}} = 1 - \frac{\text{SSR}}{\text{SST}}
$$

---

Be aware that, the term <b>linear </b> in a OLS model does not mean a linear relationship between the variables, but a model in which the parameters enter the model in a linear way. <br/>The following are all linear models (in the parameters):
- $y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 \ln(x) + \mu$
- $\ln(y) = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x + \mu$
- $y = \beta_0 + \beta_1(\frac{1}{x}) + \mu$
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Implication of the Simple Linear Regression</b>

1. OLS is unbiased _(the proof depend on the 4 assumptions, if any fails, the OLS is not necessarily unbiased)_
2. The sampling distibution of our estimates is centered around the true parameter 

But, following the second implication, how likely is it that the true slope is slightly larger, smaller or zero?

This question can be translated into another assumption:
- <b>SLR.5 Homoskedasticity</b>: assume $\text{Var}[\mu | x] = \sigma^2$

Using then the _Homoskedasticity assumption_, we can derive the variance of $b_1$.

$$
\text{Var}[\mu | x] = \mathbb{E}[\mu^2 | x] - \mathbb{E}[\mu | x]^2 = \mathbb{\mu^2 | x} =\\

\mathbb{E}[\mu^2] = \text{Var}[\mu] = \sigma^2
$$

Hence, $\sigma^2$ is also the <b>unconditional variance</b>, called the <b>error variance</b>.

We can then use this formula to find the variance of $b_1$:

$$
\text{Var}(b_1) = \frac{\sigma^2}{\sum_{i=1}^{n} (x_i - \bar{x})^2} =  \frac{\sigma^2}{SST_x}
$$

- the larger the error variance $\sigma^2£, the larger the variance of the slope estimate.
- the large the variability in the $x$ ($SST_x$), the smaller the variance of the slope estimate.

Finally, starting from the residuals $\hat u_i$, we can form an <b> unbiased estimate of the error variance</b> (often called the <b>mean square error</b> (MSE)), denoted by $s^2$:

$$
s^2 = (n-2)^{-1} \sum_i \hat u_i^2 = \frac{SSR}{n-2}
$$

_Intuition_: $\sigma^2$ is the truth; $s^2$ is our best guess based on the sample we have.

Moreover, we divide by $(n-2)$ because, estimating $b_0, b_1$, we lost 2 degrees of freedom.

## <a id="3"><b>3 Multiple Regression Analysis: Basics</b> </a>

The key problem with simple linear regression is that the assumption $\mathbb{E}[u | x] = \mathbb{E}[u] =0 $is _often problematic_.

Consider, for example, that the _true population model_ is:

$$
\begin{align*}
\text{wage} &= \beta_0 + \beta_1 \text{education} + \beta_2 \text{IQ} + \mu \\

\text{wage} &=\beta_0 + \beta_1 \text{education} + v

\end{align*}
$$

The previous assumption states that the error term $v = \beta_2 \text{IQ} + \mu$ has $0$ (zero) expected value _(no trend)_ or, in other words, the \text{IQ} <b>does not</b> correlate with the $\text{wage}$.

This causes $\beta_1$ to be <b>biased</b> compared to the "true parameter", so it _doesn't measure_ the causal effect of education to wage.

Thus, instead of assuming that multiple variables are _uncorrelated_ with the output variable, the <b>multiple regression model </b> allows to include them directly in the model.

$$

y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 \ldots \beta_k x_k + \mu
$$

- $\beta_0$ is still the <b>intercept</b>
- $\beta_0 \ldots \beta_k$ are the <b>slope parameters</b> 
- $\mu$ is the error term.

We still need a <b>zero conditional mean</b>: $\mathbb{E}[u | x_1 \ldots x_k] = 0$, that means, in other words, that all the factors that influence the outcome are included in the model.

---

In order to estimate the parameters, we still use the <b>Ordinary Least Squares </b> method and we minimize the residuals:

$$
\min_{\beta_0 \ldots \beta_k} \sum_i (y_i - \beta_0 - \beta_1 x_1 \ldots \beta_k x_k -\mu)^2
$$

leading to $k + 1$ conditions to derive $k + 1$ parameters.

The <b>estimate model</b> $\hat y = b_0 + b_1 x_1 + b_2 x_2 \ldots b_k x_k + \mu$ alloqs a <b>ceteris paribus</b> interpretation, that means that a change in $x_1$, $\Delta x_1$, leads to a change in $y$ given by $b_1 \Delta x_1$, keeping all the other $x_i$ fixed.

---

<b>Frisch-Waugh-Lovell (FWL) Theorem</b>

Given a multiple (dual for example) regression model y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2  + \mu$, the coefficient $\beta_1$ can be found in 2 steps:

1. Regress $x_1$ on $x_2$:

$$
x_1 = \alpha_0 + \alpha_1 x_2 + u
$$

Save then the <b>residuals</b> $\hat r_1 = x_1 - \hat x_1$. This represents the part of $x_1$ _not correlated_ with $x_2$.

2. Regress $y$ on the residuals:

$$
y = \gamma \hat{r}_1 + e
$$

The resulting coefficient $\gamma$ will be identical $b_1$ of the original model.

---

The <a href="#R_squared"> previous definition of $R^2$ is still valid</a>, but we can add the following remarks:

- $R^2$ is the <b>squared correlation coefficient </b> between $y$ and the predicted $\hat y$.
- $R^2$ <b>never decreases</b>  when adding independent variables to a regression _(usually will increase)_.
- Because it increases changing the number of parameters, it <b>NOT</b> a good measure to compare different models.

As in the simple regression model, we can formalize the assumptions for the multiple one:

- **MLR.1: Linear in parameters**  
  in the population model the following relation holds:
  $$
  y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k + \mu
  $$

- **MLR.2: Random sampling**  
  we have a random sample of size $n$:
  $$
  \{(y_i, x_{i1}, \ldots, x_{ik})\}_{i=1}^n
  $$

- **MLR.3: No perfect collinearity (sample variation in explanatory variables)**  
  no explanatory variable is an exact linear combination of the others, and each regressor has variation in the sample.

- **MLR.4: Zero Conditional Mean**  
  $$
  \mathbb{E}[\mu \mid x_1,\ldots,x_k] = 0
  $$
  or, for every slice of population determined by $(x_1,\ldots,x_k)$, the average of $\mu$ is zero.

  This also implies:
  $$
  \mathbb{E}[y \mid x_1,\ldots,x_k] = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \cdots + \beta_k x_k
  $$
  also called the **Population Regression Function**.

Then, we can derive the following:

<b>Implication 1: Unbiasedness of OLS</b>

Under the previous assumptions, the OSL estimator is <b>unbiased</b>: $\mathbb{E}[b_i] = \beta_i$.

- If we include _irrelevant variables_ in our model, the OLS estimator remains still <b>unbiased</b>.
- If we exclude _relevant variables_, OLS will usually be <b>biased</b>.

Let's suppose the true model is:

$$
y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 x_1 + \beta_2 x_2 + \mu 
$$

but our model is:

$$
y = \gamma_0 + \gamam x_\ + v
$$

and we actually estimate:  
$$
y = c_0 + c_1 x_1.
$$

The slope parameter will then be:

$$

c_1 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_i-\bar{x})^2}
$$

Recall that the numerator is:

$$
\begin{align*}

& \sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)(y_i-\bar{y}) =\\

= &\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)\left(
\beta_0+\beta_1 x_{i1}+\beta_2 x_{i2}+u_i
-\beta_0-\beta_1\bar{x}_1-\beta_2\bar{x}_2
\right)
\\
 =&
\beta_1\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2
+\beta_2\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)(x_{i2}-\bar{x}_2)
+\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)u_i
\end{align*}
$$

Since $\mathbb{E}[\mu_i] = 0$, it implies that:

$$
\mathbb{E}[c_1]
=
\beta_1
+
\beta_2
\frac{
\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)(x_{i2}-\bar{x}_2)
}{
\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2
}
$$

Note that the bart after $\beta_2$ is the slope from the regression of $x_2$ on $x_1$:

$$
d_1
=
\frac{
\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)(x_{i2}-\bar{x}_2)
}{
\sum_{i=1}^{n}(x_{i1}-\bar{x}_1)^2
}
$$

so we have:

$$


\mathbb{E}[c_1] = \beta_1 + \beta_2 d_1


$$

Then we can consider two corner cases:
- $\beta_2 = 0$: $x_2$ doesn't affect $y$:
- $d_1 = 0$, so $x_1, x_2$ uncorrelated in the sample.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Assumption 2: Efficiency of OLS Estimator</b>

Once we know that the estimate is centered around the true parameter, we want to understand how it is distributed.

If we add a fifth assumption (<b>MLR.5 Heteroskedasticity</b>), we know that:
$$
\text{Var}[\mu | x_1 \ldots x_k] = \mathbb{E}[\mu^2 | x_1 \ldots x_k] - \mathbb{E}[\mu | x_1 \ldots x_k]^2 = \sigma^2
$$

we also know/derive that:

$$
\text{Var}[y | x_1 \ldots x_k] = \mathbb{E}[y^2 | x_1 \ldots x_k] - \mathbb{E}[y | x_1 \ldots x_k]^2 = \sigma^2

$$

<br/>
---
<br/>

<b>Theorem: Sampling Variances of the OLS Slope Estimators</b>

Given the assumptions from MLR.1 to MLR.5,
$$
\text{Var}[b_i] = \frac{\sigma^2}{SST_i (1 - R^i)}
$$

where $SST_i = \sum_i (x_{i,j} - \bar x_j)^2$ and $R_j^2$ is the $R^2$ from the regression $x_j$ from all other $x_i$.

- a larger $\sigma^2$ leads to larger variance in the estimarors.
- a smaller $SST_i$ implies a smaller variance in the estimators.
- a larger $R_j^2$ _(linear dependence between the variables)_ implies larger variance in the estimators.
    - this is called <b>multicollinearity problem</b>.

Let's analyze now what happens if we mis-specify the model. Consider again the true and the mis-specified models:

$$
y = c_0 + c_1 x_1 \\
\bar y = b_0 + b_1 x_1 + b_2 x_2
$$

In this case the estimated variance equals $\text{Var}[c_1] = \sigma^2 / SST_1$.

On the other hand, the variance using the _true model_ equals: $\text{Var}[b_1] = \frac{ \sigma^2 }{SST_1 ( 1 - R_1^2)}$

So, unless $x_1, x_2$ are uncorrelated:

$$
\text{Var}[c_1] < \text{Var}[b_1]
$$

_Intuition:_ 
- the variance of the estimaor is smaller in the mis-specified model_.
- the mis-specified model is biased 
- As the sample size grows, the variance of each estimator shrinks to zero, making the variance difference less important

---

<b>Estimating the error</b>

- Estimate of the errorr variance

$$
s^2 = \hat{\sigma}^2
= \frac{SSR}{df}
= \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\hat u_i^2}{n-(k+1)},
\qquad
df = n-(k+1)
$$

where $df$ represents the degrees of freedom.

- Standard deviation of $b_j$:
$$
\operatorname{sd}(b_j)
= \sqrt{\operatorname{Var}(b_j\mid X)}
= \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{SST_j(1-R_j^2)}}
$$

- Standard error of $b_j$:

$$
\operatorname{se}(b_j)
= \frac{s}{\sqrt{SST_j(1-R_j^2)}}

$$

<br/>
---
<br/>

<b>Theorem: Unbiased Estimation of $\sigma^2$:</b>:

Under the assumptions MLR.1 to MLR.5, the $s^2$ estimator is unbiased, $\mathbb{E}[s^2] = \sigma^2$.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Theorem: Gauss-Markov Theorem </b>

Under the assumptions MLR.1 to MLR.5, the OLS estimators $b_0 \ldots b_k$ are the <b>best linear unbiased estimators (BLUEs)</b> of $\beta_0 \ldots \beta_k$:
- <b>Best</b>: estimators have the lowest possible variance 
- <b>Linear</b>: estimators are a linear function of $y$
- <b>Unbiased</b>: expected value equala the population paramters.

---

<b>Testing ore complex hypotheses - Linear Combination</b> 

Suppose we want to test if $b_1$ is equal to another parameter, that is: $H_0: b_1 = b_2$. Then the statistic test is $ t = (b_1 - b_2)/se(b_1 - b_2)$. 

But, if we expand the formula:
$$

se(b_1 - b_2) = \sqrt{\text{Var}(b_1 - b_2)} =\sqrt{\text{Var}(b_1)+\text{Var}(b_2)-2\text{CoV}(b_1,b_2)} =\\
=\sqrt{se(b_1)^2 + se(b_2)^2-2s_{12}}
$$

So we need $s_{12}, which we don't usually have.

To avoid this, we can use the following _"trick"_.

We set $H_0: q_1 = b_1 - b_2 = 0$. To do that, we also have to substitute $q_1 = b_1 - b_2$ in our model.

So, for example, we can consider:

$$
y = b_0 + b_1 x_1 + b_2 x_2 + \mu \implies \\

y = b_0 + q_1 x_1 + b_2 (x_1 + x_2) + \mu
$$

<b>Multiple Linear Restrictions</b>

So far we tested a single linear restriction ($b_1 = 0, b_1 = b_2$). Now we want to jointly test multiply hypotesis about the parameters.

A typical example is testing _"exclusion restrictions"_, that means knowing if a group of parameters are equal to zero.

Then the null hypotesis might be something like: $H_0: b_{k-q+1} = 0, ... b_k = 0$, but we cannot check each statistic separately because we want to know fi the $q$ parameters are jointly significant each other.
<br/>We instead need to estimate the _restricted model_ without all the $x_{k-q+1}..x_k$ as the _un-restricted model_ with all the $x$ included.

_Intuition: we want to know if the change in SSR is bigh enough_.

This is called $F$-statistic, defined as: 

$$
 F =\frac{ (SSR_r - SSR_{ur})}{/SSR_{ur}}\frac{  (n-k-1)}{q}
$$

where $r$ is the restricted, $ur$ the un-restricted. _Note that the $F$ statistic is always positive_.

_Intuition: the $F$ statistic is measuring the relative increase in the SSR when moving from the unrestricted to the restricted model.
-$q = df_r - dr_{ur}, quad df_{ur} = n - k - 1$.

To decide if the increases in SSR is _"big enough"_, to reject the exclusions, we compare if with the $F$ distribution, indeed we know that: $F \sim F_{q, n-k-1} where:
-  $q$ is the numerator degrees of freedom 
- $n-k-q$ is the denominator degrees od freedom.
