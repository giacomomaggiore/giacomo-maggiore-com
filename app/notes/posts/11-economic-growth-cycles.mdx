## <a id="1"><b>1.1 Introduction</b> </a>

Macroeconomics tries to answer some key questions:
- What determines key macroeconomic variables? _(GDP, unemployment, inflation, credit, consumption, interest rate...)
- How can economic policy influence these variables? _(government spending, tax policies, monetary policy, "wage policy"...)
- How can macroeconomic policy and market regulation operate together?
- Do macroeconomic processes show inherent dynamics? _(cycles, trend and instabilities)_
- Should economic policy focus mainly on long-run growth or on stabilization of the business cycle?

To start answering them, we firstly start with some <b>stylized facts</b> of Macroeconomics:
<br/>_Note that, if a variable is <b>procyclical</b>, it moves in the same direction as the overall economy._

Some additional <b>common rules of macroeconomic policy</b>, based on consensus, argue that:
- in case of <b>liquidity problems</b>, the central bank should provide liquidity _by raising money supply_.
- Government spending and investments of infrastructure should <b>not be lowered</b> during recession periods.
- In downturns automatic stabilizer _(first responders" to an economic crisis)_ should work.
- In the long run monetary policy has to focus on price stability _(inflation around 2% a year)_.
- Real wage increases should <b>not exceed productivity</b> growth whem at the sime time the number of employees remain constant.

<b>The Kenynesian Branch</b>
- <b>Keynesians</b>: focus on the _short-run_. They argue that markets don't always clear and that government intervention is necessary to manage the business cycle.
- <b>New Kenynesians</b>: they introduce <b>microfoundations</b> _(explaining how people behanve and spend) and <b>nominal rigidities</b> _(prices and wages don't adjust instantly, which justifies government intervention)._
<br/>

<b>The Classical Branch</b>
- <b>Classic</b>: they argue that markets are self-correcting and the <b>invisible hand</b> ensures optimal employment in the long run.
- <b>New Classic</b>: Emerged in 1970, they introduced <b>rational expectations</b>, suggesting that because people anticipate government policy, those policies often become ineffective.
<br/>

<b>The Monetarist & RBC Branch</b>
- <b>Monetarists</b>: <a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milton_Friedman" target="_blank">led by Milton Friedman,</a> they argued that changes in the money supply are the main driver of economic fluctuations.
- <b>RBC Economics (Real Business Cycle):</b> they are a subset of New Classical thought. They argue that "cycles" aren't failures of the market, but rather efficient responses to technological shocks or changes in productivity.

## <a id="2"><b>2.1. IS-LM Model in Closed Economy</b> </a>

In the long run:
- prices are flexible
- output is ddetermined by factors of production and technology
- unemployment equals its natural rate

In the short run (_IS-LM setting)_:
- prices are <b>fixed</b> (or change slowly)
- oputput is determined by <b>aggregate demand</b>
<br/>

<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IS%E2%80%93LM_model" target="_blank">The IS-LM Model [Wikipedia]</a>

---
<br/>
<b>The LM-curve (positive relationship between real interest rate and output)</b>

In equilibrium, money supply equals money demand:

$$
M^s = M^d = p L (i, Y)
$$

where $L(i,Y)$ is a function representing  the liquitidy preferences, $i$ is the interest rate and $p$ the price level.

If we consider:

$$

L(i, Y) = kY - hi
$$

 where:
- $\alpha$: Sensitivity of money demand to changes in income ($\frac{\partial L}{\partial Y} > 0$).
- $\beta$: Sensitivity of money demand to changes in the interest rate ($\frac{\partial L}{\partial i} < 0$).

If we write $M^s = p (\alpha Y - \beta i)$ and solve for $i$:

$$
i = \frac{\alpha}{\beta}Y - \frac{1}{\beta}\left(\frac{M^s}{P}\right)
$$

This yields an <b>upward-sloping LM curve</b>, which represents the positive relation between the interest rate and the output.


In this model:
- the Central Bank (CB) controls money supply 
- if output increases, the demand for money increases at any $i$. 
- for a fixed supply of money, $i$ must increase to lower the demand for money and maintain equilibrium.


<div align = "center">
![Macroeconomic Schools](/images/notes/economic-growth/lm.png)
</div>
<br/>

It is also important to distinguish between:
- <b>Movements along the curve:</b> caused by a change in output ($Y$).
- <b>Shifts of the curve:</b> caused by changes in Monetary Policy (the Money Supply).

Indeed, increasing money suppòly leads to:
- increase in  demand for money to maintain equilibrium $\to i$ must decrease
- for any level of output $Y$, the corresponding $i$ is lower.

The result is that an increase in the money supply leads the LM curve to shift down:

- $\Delta M^s \to \Delta i = - \frac{\Delta M^s}{p \beta} \quad$ or, equivalently: $\quad \frac{\partial i}{\partial M^s} = - \frac{1}{\beta p}$
- $\Delta i \to \Delta M^ s = - p \beta \Delta i\quad$ or, equivalently: $\quad \frac{\partial M^s}{\partial i} = -\beta p$.

<div align = "center">
![Macroeconomic Schools](/images/notes/economic-growth/lm2.png)
</div>

---
<br/>
<b>The IS-curve (negative relation between real interest rate and output)</b>

Assumptions:
- there is only one good that can be used for consumption or investment
- all demanded goods will be supplied _(no rationing of demand)_.

We then model planned expenditures $E$:

$$
E = C(Y) + I(i) + G
$$

where $C$ is the consumption, $I$ physical investments (<b>not financials!</b>) and $G$ the public spending.

When $E = Y$ planned and effective expenditures are equal and we obtain the <b>IS-curve</b>.

If we consider: $E = Y = cY + I_0 - \gamma i + G$, we obtain the following:

$$
Y = \frac{1}{1 - c} [I_0 - \gamma i + G]
$$

$$
i = \frac{(c-1)Y + I_0 + G}{\gamma}
$$

Even though the IS-curve is often referred to as the goods market equilibrium condition, this is misleading.
<br/>In microeconomics, a market equilibrium balances demand and supply via price adjustments _(market clearing condition)_.
<br/>The IS-curve does not reflect endogenous price levels.

---
<br/>
<b>The Multiplicator-Process in IS-LM Model</b>


Intiuitively, if we consider increase in public spending $G$:
- increase in public spending moves the IS-curve to the right, $Y$ increases.
- the LM-curve remains unchanged
- in the short run, interest rate and outputincrease.

<div align = "center">
![Macroeconomic Schools](/images/notes/economic-growth/increase_g.png)
</div>


In formulas, if we consider $\Delta G$ and we solve both IS and LM equations simultaneously, from the LM-curve we get:

$$
i = \frac{\alpha}{\beta}Y - \frac{1}{\beta}\frac{M}{P}
$$

if we substitute this into the IS-expression:

$$
Y = cY + I_0 - \gamma \left[ \frac{\alpha}{\beta}Y - \frac{1}{\beta}\left(\frac{M}{P}\right) \right] + G
$$

$$
Y = cY + I_0 - \frac{\gamma \alpha}{\beta}Y + \frac{\gamma}{\beta}\left(\frac{M}{P}\right) + G
$$

$$
Y - cY + \frac{\gamma \alpha}{\beta}Y = I_0 + \frac{\gamma}{\beta}\left(\frac{M}{P}\right) + G
$$

$$
Y \left[ (1 - c) + \frac{\alpha\gamma}{\beta} \right] = I_0 + \frac{\gamma}{\beta}\left(\frac{M}{P}\right) + G
$$

If we consider $\Delta G$ or, equivalently, we take its partial derivative:

$$
\Delta Y \left[ (1 - c) + \frac{\alpha\gamma}{\beta} \right] = \Delta G
$$

Dividing both sides gives us the<b> Fiscal Policy Multiplier:</b>

$$
\frac{\Delta Y}{\Delta G} = \frac{1}{(1 - c) + \frac{\alpha\gamma}{\beta}}
$$

- $(1 - c)$: represents the how much goes into savings _(smaller this is, the bigger the multiplier)_.
- $\alpha\gamma / \beta$: is the <b>monetary drag.</b>
    - If $\alpha$ is high, income growth creates massive money demand.
    - If $\gamma$ is high, investment is very sensitive to interest rates.
    - If $\beta$ is low, it takes a larger increase in $i$ to balance the money market.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Easing of monetary policy</b>

- Central Bank increases money supply by setting lower interest rate at given $Y$.
- The LM-curve shifts downward
- The IS-curve remains unchanged
- $Y$ increases (with $G$ unchanged because exogenous, $I$ increases and $C$ increases) and $i$ declines

<div align = "center">
![Macroeconomic Schools](/images/notes/economic-growth/easing.png)
</div>

---

<br/>
<b>Declining consumer confidence</b>

- It may be caused by financial crisis, wars, terror attacks or whatever
- Households consume less and save more of given income
- the IS-curve odes leftward
- LM-curve remains unchanged assuming, _for simplicity_, that the demand for money does not change
- $Y$ and $i$ decline.

Possible counter-measures to stabilize $Y$:
- Expansive fiscal policy can shift the IS-curve to initial position 
- Easing of monetary policy implies downard shift of LM-curve

## <a id="2.2"><b>2.2. Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply Curves</b> </a>

So far we've been using the IS-LM model to analyze the short-run dynamics. However, a change in price level would shift the LM-curve and therefore affects the output.
<br/>The (aggregate demand) AD-curve describes the relationship between price and output.

<div align = "center">
![AD-curve](/images/notes/economic-growth/ad_curve.png)
</div>

On the other hand, we can model the <b>short-run </b> aggregate supply curve with an upward slope _(given by sticky wages and sticky costs)_.

<div align = "center">
![AD-curve](/images/notes/economic-growth/short_run_supply.png)
</div>

We now give an example of <b>complete adjustment </b> and we see what happend with a reduction of the government spending.

Firstly, a reduction in $G$ shifts both IS and AD-curves left.

<div align = "center">
![AD-curve](/images/notes/economic-growth/complete_adjustment.png)
</div>

In the short-equilibrium, $Y$ is lower than the initial-natural value $\bar Y$.

Thefore, aggregate supply moves down and we see a gradual adjustment of pcises, leading to a downward-shift of the LM-curve that, finally, brings the system back to $\bar Y$.

<div align = "center">
![AD-curve](/images/notes/economic-growth/complete_adjustment_2.png)
</div>

_In reality, downward price and wage adjustments are difficult (see for example, the Greek crisis in 2010-2016)_:
- _very long wage contract make them hard to re-negotiate_
- _resistance of particular groups of employees_.

In the case of Greece:
- _costs were also partly determined by international markets due to the import_
- _governments has to rely too much on increasing valued added-takex that leads to an increase in prices_.

## <a id="2.3"><b>2.3. Control of Interest Rates and Taylor Rule</b> </a>

Models with LM-curve assume that the CB uses the money supply as an instrument, therefore $i$ will determined endogenously by the equilibrium.
<br/>In reality, CB uses short-term interest rates as their policy instruments _(rather than the money supply)_.

The main advantage of targeting interest rating versus money supply is that, in the second case, the difference between fluctuating demand and stable siupply would lead to strongly functuating interest rates.
<br/>Nowadays CB counterbalance fluctuations in money demand by adapting the money supply to maintain the short-term interest rate at the targeted level.

CB influences short-terminterest rates $i_0, i_1, i_2...$.

A risk-neutral investors must be indifferent between:
- make a long-term investment with interest rate $i_L$
- make short-term investments in each period $t$ at interest rate $i_t$ (with $i^e_t$ denoting the expected interest rate)

$$
\begin{align*}
1 + i_L &= [(i + i_0)(1 + i_1^e)(1 + i_2^e)...(i + i_n^e)]\\
i_L & \approx i_0^e + i_1^e + i_2^e ... i_n^e
\end{align*}
$$

But, in practice:

$$
i_L \approx i_0 + i_1^e + \tau_{0,1}^P + i_2^e + \tau_{1,2}^P + .... i_n^e + \tau_{n-1,n}^P
$$

where $ \tau_{n-1,n}^P $ denotes the <b>positive term premium</b>.

In this way, by influencing the short-term nominal interest rates, the CB can also influence the long-term ones.

<b>Real Business Cycle (RBC) Theory</b>

The following basic RBC-model starts with the following assumptions:
- an infinitely long-living household/agent maximizes the expected value of 
$$
U = \sum_{t = 0}^{\infty} \delta ^t u_t
$$

with respect to the intertemporal budget restriction, where $u_t$ represents the utility in period $t$ and $0 < \delta < 1$ the discount factor.
- a representative firm maximizes profits, given its production function.
- there exist no frictions _(no price rigidities, no externalities, no asymmetric info, no public goods...)_


The aggregate <b>production function</b> is then:
$$
Y_t = K_t^{\alpha} (A_t L_t)^{1-\alpha}, \quad \quad 0 < \alpha < 1
$$

- $Y_t$ aggregate output
- $A_t$ measure for the efficeny of labor (stochastic), example: $\ln A_t = \bar A + \rho \epsilon_{t-1}+ \epsilon_t$, with $\epsilon \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$, where $\rho$ describes the persistence of the shock.
- $K_t$ capital
- $L_t$ labor input
- $\alpha$ share of physical capital


_Intuition: the stochastic component $A_t$ induce business cycles_.

$A_t$ is then a measure fo the <b>efficiency of labor</b> over time and relative to capital. It's a catch-all variable that includes levels of health, skill, education and technological knoweldge.

<b>$1^{st}$ Welfare Theorem</b>

Under technical assumptions - (i) local non-satiation of preferences, (ii) compete markets with no frictions and no externalities, (iii) price taking behaviors - any allocation that forms a competitive equilibrium is Pareto-efficient.

However, the RBC model received several critiques:
- there's no broad evidence that strong technology shocs always affect the economy 
- there's no adeguate explanation of lasting recessions
- in typical RBC models, employment fluctuactions comesfrom the household'ìs intertemporal substitution of labor, empirical studies, in reality, show little evidence about that.
- RBC models do not consider monetary shocks, but only technology shocks.
- the assumption of a unique representative agent is not accurate, small heterogenity in utilities can have a strong influence on the model.

<b>Monetarism</b>

Early Monetarism started around 1920 when <a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irving_Fisher" target="_blank">Irving Fisher</a> applied the <b>quantity theory of money</b> as a tool for quantitative analysis of prices, inflation and interest rates.
The key concept behind is that the amount of money circulating in an economy is the primary driver of its health, inflation, and growth.

<br/>The <b>quantity equation</b> is the central and formal definition of this model:

$$
M \cdot V = P \cdot Y
$$

- $M$: stock of money
- $V$: velocity of money 
- $P$: price level
- $Y$: real GDP
<br/>
<a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pArvddxqPUM&t=47s" target="_blank">Quantity Theory of Money - Fisher Equation [Video]</a>

The <b>Quantity Theory</b> _(coming from the Early Monetarism)_ is an interpretation of the previous equation that assumes that $V$ is costant: if people’s spending habits don't change, then any change in $M$ (Money) must lead to a proportional change in $P$ 
In its extreme form, it predicts the <b>neutrality of money</b>: an exogenous increase of the stock of money is followed by a proportional increase of the price level, without any effect on real variables like consumption, output and investment.

_Recall that, the velocity of money is defined as:_

$$
V := \frac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{stock of Money M}}
$$


In the 50ies, <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Monetary_History_of_the_United_States" target="_blank"> Milton Friedman and Anna Schwartz</a> argued that money might be neutral in the long run, but it has real effects in the short run.
<br/>They linked the previous crisis with their theories: before Friedman, most people thought the <b>Great Depression</b> was a failure of capitalism. Friedman and Schwartz proposed that it was actually a failure of the Federal Reserve because Fed didn't put money back into the system in moments of need, resulting in a strong <a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stretta_del_credito" target="_blank">credit crunch</a> and deflation.

If compared with Keynesians, Monetarists show a different approach:

- <b>constant-money growth rule</b>: central banks should increase annually 3% the amount of money supplied
- <b>avoid interest rate targeting</b> and too low _(below the natural level)_
- <b>distrust of fiscal policy</b>: governemtn spending "crowds out" (reduce) investment and has little impact in the long run.

However, in 1980, monetarism lost his appeal for different reasons:
- the assumption on $V$ was not realistic
- it was hard to understand the definition of money stock _(cash, liquid investments, cash equivalents...?)_
- money growth was not a good indicator of demand

<b>Neoclassical Economics</b>

The model, based on Lucas (1972) and Phelps (1970),  starts from the following assumptions:
- the economy is populated by a laege number of farmers indexed by $i$
- each farmer produces a specific good
- each farmer selles its good in a competitive market at individual price $P_i$ and market price $P$
- revenues allow each farmer to consume a bundle of goods
- there are two <b>2 possible shocks</b>:
    - prefrerence shock 
    - monetary shock 

For simplicity, we also add the assumption that there's perfect information. We then model each farmer's production as:
$$
Y_i = N_i
$$

where $Y_i$ is the output and $N_i$ his amount of labour.

The budget constraint for each individual with consumption $C_i$ is:

$$
P C_i = P_i Y_i
$$

The utility function of each individual is then:

$$
U_i(C_i, N_i) = C_i - \frac{1}{\theta}N_i^{\theta} \quad \quad \theta > 1
$$

Since there's perfect information, each individual knows the aggregate price level $P$, by substituting $C_i =   \frac{Y_i}{P}P_i =  \frac{P_i}{P}N_i$, each individual faces the following optimization problem:

$$
\max_{N_i} U_i = \Big ( \frac{P_i}{P} \Big ) N_i -\frac{1}{\theta}N_i^{\theta}
$$

that leads to:

$$
N_i = \Big ( \frac{P_i}{P} \Big )^{1/(\theta -1)}
$$

if we consider $n_i = \ln N_i$ and $y_i = \ln Y_i$, we can rewrite the first-order condition as:

$$
n_i = y_i = \frac{1}{\theta - 1}(p_i - p)
$$

_Intuition: a farmer increases his production whenever the relative price of his product increases._

If we then assume that the demand function depends on the <b>good's relative price </b>, on <b>real income</b> and on a <b>real preference shock </b>$z_i$, we can write the <b>log-demand</b> as:

$$
y_i^d = y + z_i - \eta (p_i - p)
$$

where $\eta$ is the elasticity of demand between goods.

Therefore, the aggregate output is given by $y = \int_0^1 y_i d_i$ and the aggregate price level by $p = \int_0^1 p_i d_i$.
<br/>_(Note that the preference shock is assumed to have zero mean: $\int_0^1 z_i d_i = 0$)_

We then consider <b>aggregate demand</b>, given by: $M = P Y$ _(intuition: similar to the quantity equation with $V = 1$).
<br/> In log-terms, this rewrites as:

$$
y = m - p
$$

with $m$ assumed to be stochastic $m \sim N(0, \sigma^2)$, representing the monetary shock _(central bank's policy is unpredictable, creating random fluctuations in demand)_.

In equilibrium, the market has to clear, i.g. $y_i = y_i^d$:

$$
\frac{1}{\theta - 1}(p_i - p) = y + z_i - \eta(p_i - p)
$$

solving for $p_i$ yields to:

$$
p_i = \frac{\theta - 1}{1 + \eta(\theta - 1)} (y + z_i) + p
$$

if we then take the integral over all goods, we get:

$$
p = \frac{\theta - 1}{1 + \eta(\theta - 1)} y + p
$$

Hence, we obtain the equilibrium as: $y = 0$ _(remember that $y = \ln Y \to Y = 1$)_ and $m = p$. <br/>
For the indidual good, this means:

$$
y_i = \frac{1}{\theta - 1}(p_i - p) = \frac{z_i}{1 + (\theta - 1)\eta}
$$

_Intution:_
- money is neutral and can only affect the price level, output is fixed at the full employment rate $Y = 1$.
(<b>Classical dichotomy</b>: monetary policy cuase nominal prices and wages to change, leaving the real equilibrium unchanged).
- the equilibrium $y_i$ depends only on the relative price $p_i$ which depends on $z_i$.

<br/>
---

We now consider the same model with <b>imperfect information</b>, so each farmer only observes the price $p_i$ of his produced good  but not the market price/price index $p$.

In this way, a monetary shock has real effects on output, raising aggregate demand. As the unpredicted increase of money supply generates price movements, individuals $i$ may misinterpret this partially as an increase of relative price index of a good.

In this model, output is given by the <b>Lucas Supply Function</b>:

$$
y = \int_0^1 n_i d_i = b(p - E(p))
$$

_Intuition_:
- deviation of $y$ from its natural level is an increasing function of the surprise in the price level
- if we make use of the aggregate demand function $y = m - p$, we can solve Lucas Supply Function for $p$ and $y$:

$$
p = \frac{1}{1 + b}m + \frac{b}{1+b}E(p)

$$
$$
y = p =\frac{b}{1+b}m - \frac{b}{b+1}E(p)
$$

This model argues that there's a statistical output-inflation relation: however, if policy markers attempt to take advantage of this _(raising output by raising the money growth rate above the trend level)_, they may cause this relationsbip to break down.

However, models based on Neoclassical economy (like Lucas Supply function) are appropriate to study:
- if monetary policy has large effect if it surprises the public 
- if trying to create repeatedly surprises will be futile

<b>New Kenyesians Economics</b>

The main assumption behind is that <b>prices are not completely flexible</b> _(Some models also consider wage rigidities)_.

Firm's profit maximization yields to the following <b>New Keynesian Phillips Curve</b>:

$$
\pi_t = \kappa y_t + \beta \mathbb{E} [\pi_{t+1}]
$$

where $\pi_t$ represents the inflation, $y_t$ the output, $\kappa > 0$ and $0 < \beta < 1$.

- an increasing output level leads to higher inflation 
- high expected inflation tomorrow yields to higher inflation today. _(If firms expect higher prices, they set higher prices today)_.

On the other hand, households's intertemporal consumption optimization probel yields to the following IS-cuve:

$$
y_t = - \gamma (i_t - \mathbb{E} [\pi_{t+1}] ) + \mathbb{E} [\pi_{t+1}] \quad \quad \gamma > 0
$$

The IS-curve states that output $y_t$ depends negatively on the real interest rate and positively on the expected future output.
<br/>Also, if the real interest rate is expected to rise in the future, savings become more attractive and consumption declines.

A forward iteration of the IS-curve and the Philipps curve brings to:

$$
\begin{align*}
y_t &= \mathbb{E}_t \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} [ - \gamma (i_{t+1} - \pi_{t+1+i})] \\

\pi_t &= \kappa y_t + \beta \mathbb{E}[\tau_{t+1}] \\

&= \kappa \mathbb{E}_t \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} [\beta^i y_{t+1}]
\end{align*}
$$

_Intuition: firms set their nominal prices based on the expectations of future output_:
- output depends on all expected future real interest rates
- infation depends on all expected output levels.

For the analysis of the _optimal monetary policy_, we also add supply shocks $\xi_t$ and demand shocks $\sigma_t$, both described by an AR(1) process.

$$
\begin{align*}

\sigma_t &= \rho_1 \sigma_{t-1} + u_t \quad \mu \sim N(0, \sigma_1^2) \\
\xi_t &= \rho_2 \xi_{t-1} + \epsilon_t \quad \epsilon \sim N(0, \sigma_2^2)

\end{align*}
$$

with $\rho_1, \rho_2 \in (0,1)$ measure the persistence of the shocks.

The forward iteration of the IS-curve and the Phillips curve with shocks leads to:

$$
\begin{align*}
y_t &= \mathbb{E}_t \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} [ - \gamma (i_{t+1} - \pi_{t+1+i})] \\

\pi_t &=  \mathbb{E}_t \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} [\beta^i(\kappa  y_{t+1} + \xi_{t+i})]
\end{align*}
$$

Since output depends now also on expected future demand shocks, shocks on the IS-curve can be completely accomodated. Indeed, at the optimum, the policy maker ajusts the interest rate such that the shocks are offset.
<br/>On the other hand, cost-push shocks $\xi_t$ (supply shocks) generate a trade-off between output and inflation ($\to$ efficient frontier).

Criticism:
- there's no delay in the response of inflatio to shocks 
- inflation is just forward-looking, past inflantion is irrilevant
- in the cases of demand shocks, disinflation can be achieved costlessly and may even generate a boom if anticipated. _However, disinflations have historically entailed signficant output losses_.

## <a id="4"><b>4. Consumption and Investment</b> </a>

<b>The Keynesian Consumption Function</b>

The Keynesian consumption function has the form:

$$
C_t = \bar C + c Y_t, \quad \quad \bar C > 0, 0 < c < 1.
$$

We immediately notice that consumption depends only on <b>current income</b>.

We then define the <b>Marginal. Propensity of Consumption </b>(MPC), that lies between $0$ and $1$ as:

$$
MPC \equiv \frac{\partial C_t}{\partial Y_t} = c
$$

Furthermore, the <b>Average Propensity of consumption</b> (APC), that dereases with an increasing income, is defined as:

$$
APC \equiv \frac{C_t}{Y_t} \equiv \frac{\bar C}{Y_t} + c
$$

_Intuition: savings are luxuries and can only be afforted with higher income_.

However, empirial observation don't proove this result: APC decreases with increasing income in the short term but, as the income increases over a long-term horizon, the APC remains constant.

---
<br/>
<b>The Life Cycle and Permanent Income Hypotheses</b>

The Life Cycle and Permanent Income hypoteses try to solve the previous inconsistency.<br/>
<a href="https://www.investopedia.com/terms/l/life-cycle-hypothesis.asp" target="_blank" >The Life Cycle Hypotesis</a> was proposed by Ando, Brumberg and Mofigliani in the 1950s

The core idea is that households pursue <b>consumption-smoothing</b>, i.e. they want to spend eqial amount of money in each period of their life _(so they borrow when they earn less and save in the opposite situation)_.

As a result, wealth is <b>increasing until retirement</b>, and is gradually used for consumption afterwards. 
<br/>Savings, on the other hand, do not depend nuch on current income, but on total lifetime income.

<a href="https://www.investopedia.com/terms/p/permanent-income-hypothesis.asp" target="_blank">The Permanent Income Hypotesis</a>, proposed by Milton Friedman in 1971, states that income $Y$ is a sum of permament income $Y_p$ and transitory insome $Y_t$:

$$
Y = Y_p + Y_t
$$
- Permanent income is the long-term/average income 
$$Y_p \approx r \cdot \sum_{s=0}^{\infty} \frac{E[Y_{t+s}]}{(1+r)^s}
$$
- Transitory income is the one coming from surprises and fluctuates, with $\mathbb{E}[Y_t] = 0$.

_Consider the permanent income as your salary for your work contract, hopefully increasing with seniority; and the transitory income as some unexpected bonus, lottery wins, monetary heritage etc..._.

The theory suggests that, since people want to avoid fluctuactions in consumption, current consumption will mainly depend on permanent income. Moreover, people will use borrowings and savings to smooth their consumption over time.

At the limit, transitory income becomes _irrilevant_: $C = \alpha Y_p$ with $\alpha \in (0,1)$.

In this model, the APC can also be expressed as: 
$$
APC = \frac{C_t}{Y_t} = \alpha \frac{Y_p}{Y}
$$


As a result, for a given level of permament income, people with high levels of transitory income will have APCs lower than average.
<br/>Also, over the long-period, the APC should be independent on the transitory income, since $\mathbb{E}[Y_t] = 0$ _(shocks and fluctuactions cancel each other)_.

According to this theory then, economic policy can affect consumption only if it affects permanent income _(example: tax cuts can increase consumption only if they are not perceived as transitory)_.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>The Fisher Approach to Life Cycle Hypotesis </b>

We now consider a simple formal model of optimal consumption/saving.

- Consider an agent that lives for $2$ periods.
- The agent has a period utility $u(C_t)$ with a discount factor $\delta$ with $0 < \delta < 1$. <br/>We also assume $u'(.)>0$ and $u''(.)<0$.
- The agents faces the following budget constraints:
    1. Period: $S = Y_1 - C$
    2. Period: $C_2 = S(1+r) + Y_2$
    where $r$ denotes the real interest rate.

So, if we plug toghether, we end up with the following <b>intertemporal-budget constraint</b>:

$$
C_1 + \frac{C_2}{1 + r} = Y_1 + \frac{Y_2}{1 + r}
$$

The agent then faces the following optimization problem:

$$
\max_{C_1,C_2} \big [ u(C_1) + \delta u (C_2)\big ]\\

s.t. \quad C_1 + \frac{C_2}{1 + r} = Y_1 + \frac{Y_2}{1 + r}

$$

At the optimum, the <b>Euler/Tancency condition</b> _(the point where a consumer has perfectly balanced their budget to get the maximum possible satisfaction)_ must hold:

$$
u'(C_1) = \delta(1 + r) u'(C_2)
$$

_Intuition_:
- if $u'(C_1)>\delta (1 + r)u'(C_2)$: the agent should consume more on the first period and save less.
- if $u'(C_1)>\delta (1 + r)u'(C_2)$: the agent should shift consumption to the second period and save more.
The model has then these implications:
- Consumption depends on the (expected) discounted income over both periods _(in contrast with the Keynesian approach when consumption depends only on current income)_
- Consumption typically depend on the real interest rate _(changes in $r$ lead to income and substitution effect)_.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Intertemporal Budget Constraint of the Government</b>

In a 2-period model, the government levies taxes $T_t$ and spends $G_t$ in public goods (non rival and non-excludable).
- In the first period, the government can end up in a fiscal defitic (or surplus if $D<0$): 
$$
D_1 := G_1 - T_1
$$
- in the second period, the government has to repay the deficit plus the interests:
$$
T_2 = D_1(1 + r) + G_2
$$

If we combine the two equationsm,we obtain the <b>Intertemporal Budget Constraint</b>:

$$
T_2 = G_2 + (1+r)(G_1 - T_1) \implies \\

T_1 + \frac{T_2}{1 + r} = G_1 + \frac{G_2}{1 + r}
$$

_Intuition: the present value of governemtn spending equals the present values of tax incomes._

As a consequence:
- if the governement decides to lower taxes in the first period by $\Delta T$ keeping spending constant
- then, in the second period, it has to raise taxes by $\Delta T (1 + r)
- the tax cut increases the income of the houdeholds by $\Delta T$ in period 1 and decreases it in period 2 by $\Delta T (1 + r)$
- lifetime income is given by $Y_1 + Y_2/(1 + r)$, so, after the changes in taxation, it becomes:

$$
(Y_1 + \Delta T) + \frac{Y_2 - (1+r)\Delta T}{1+r} = Y_1 + \frac{Y_2}{1+r}
$$

- according to the <b>Fisher model</b>, in each period, the households consume the _same amount as before the tax policy_, since lifetime income was NOT affected.

This is called <b>Ricardian Equivalence Theorem</b>.

<a href="https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/ricardianequivalence.asp" target="_blank">The Ricardian Equivalence Theorem in detail [Investopedia]</a>

_Intuition: househols have more money in the first period, but they know they will have less in the second, so they save to pay higher taxes in the future_.

Despite this theorem, governments often try to simulate consumption by reducing taxes and keeping spending costant. These policies are NOT completely futile, since, in practice, the Ricardian Equivalent Theorem doesnt hold perfectly. _(The increase in households' current income increases their consumptoion by some positive fraction of the additional income)_.
<br/>This comes from the fact that, in most cases, household are myope: they notice an increase in income, but they are unable to predict a future increase in taxation _(households are not 100% rational and follow simple rules)_.

However, even in theory, there are some possible deviations from the <b>Ricardian Equivalence</b>:
- <b>Limited lifetime</b>: the government can take long periods of times to repay its debt, shifting the tax burden to future generation and redistributing consumption from a generation to another.
    - For this reason, if households took the utility of future generations into account, they should save m,ore and NOT expand consumption for the expected future tax burden.
- <b>Distortionary taxation</b>: not always who benefit the tax reduction will also repay the tax burden _(the governement can levy taxes from different parts of the population)_

## <a id="5"><b>5. The Solow Growth Model</b> </a>

<Quote>
(Productivity) growth isn't everything, but in the long run it's almost everything. <a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Krugman" target="_blank">Paul Krugman, 1990</a>
</Quote>

Real GDP per capita is often considered the most important indicator for average standard of living.

_(Is this really the case? Luciano Canova <a href="https://www.amazon.it/metro-della-felicità-Luciano-Canova-ebook/dp/B07P6GKJPY" target="_blank"> in this book answers this</a>: is the PIL the right measure to measure happiness?)_

More in detail:
- <b>Economic growth </b> is the increase in value of all goods and services produced by an economy. It is conventionally measured as the percent rate of increase in <b>real gross domestic product</b>.
- <b>GDP</b> is defined as the total market valoue of all final goods and servied produced in a country in a gived time period. It's defined by the <b>National Income Identity</b>:
$$
\text{GDP} = \text{Consumption}+\text{}+\text{Gross Investing} + \text{Gov. Spending} + \text{Exports}- \text{Imports}
$$

---
<br/>
<b>The Solow Model - General Assumptions</b>

- A closed economy _(no trade)_
- One good that can be consumed or used as capital 
- Houseolds save a _costant exogenous fraction_ $s$ of their income _(we are not intered in why they save $s$, we simply put that value into the model)_
- Rate of technological progress is _exogenous_ 
- No taxes, no subsidies _($\approx$ no governement)
- Discrete time $t = 0,1,2...$

Output $Y_t$ is produced according to the <b>aggregate production function:</b>

$$
Y_t = F(A_t L_t, K_t)
$$

- $A_t$: efficiency of labor 
- $L_t$: size of the labor force $\implies A_t L_t$ is the <b>effective labor</b>
- $K_t$: stock of capital 

Further assumptions:
- constant return to scale (CRS): $F(\lambda A_t L_t, K_t) = \lambda F(A_t L_t, K_t)$
- $F' > 0, F'' < 0$ for each argument

We model technological progress as $(A_{t+1}-A_t)/A_t$ and we consider as exogenous.

Most of the times, in the Solow Model, we assume a <b>Cobb-Douglas</b> production function:

$$
F(A_t L_t, K_t) = (A_t L_t)^{1 - \alpha} K_t ^{\alpha}, \quad \alpha \in (0,1)
$$

_(it can be easily checked that this function respectes all the assumptios)_.

Because of the costan return to scale, the productionm function can be written in <b>intestive form</b>:

$$
\frac{Y_t}{(A_t L_t)} = F\Big (1, \frac{K_t}{L_t A_t} \Big)
$$

if we now introduce:
- $f(x) := F(1,x)$ 
- $k := K_t / (A_t L_t)$
- $y_t := Y_t / (A_t L_t)$

we obtain:

$$
y_t = f(k_t) = k_t ^{\alpha}.
$$
_(This makes easier to solve the model since we only have one argument)_.

We now introduce some assumptions on the population growth and the technological progress:
- population _(coinciding with labor force)_ at $t = 0$ is $L_0$
- population grows at a costant rate $n$: 
$$
L_{t+1} =L_t + n L_t \implies L_t = L_0 (1 + n)^t
$$
- technological level at $t = 0$ is $A_0$ 
- technological level grows at a costant rate $g$: $A_t = A_0 (1 + g)^t$

The <b>capital stock</b> increases by investment $I$ and decreases by depreciation at a costant rate $\delta$.

We then obtain the <b>foundamental law of motion</b> of the Solow Model:

$$
K_{t+1} = K_t + I_t - \delta K_t = (1 - \delta) K_t + I
$$

In a closed economy without government spending:

$$
Y_t = C_t + I_t, \quad I_t = S_t.
$$

If we also assume that $S_t = s Y_t, s \in (0,1)$ is costant over time, this implies:

$$
C_t = (1 -s) Y_t \\

K_{t+1} = (1 - \delta)K_t + sY_t
$$

---


We now take some time to describe the variable. Given the ones in absolute form ($K_t, L_t, A_t, Y_t, C_t)$, we have the <b>Per Capita Values</b> _(simply dividing by population)_ as:
- $\bar k_t := \frac{K_t}{L_t}$
- $\bar y_t := \frac{Y_t}{L_t}$
- $\bar c_t := \frac{C_t}{L_t}$

and the <b>Intensive Form</b> _(simply dividing by effective labor)_ as:
- $ k_t := \frac{K_t}{L_t A_t}$
- $ y_t := \frac{K_t}{L_t A_t }$
- $ c_t := \frac{K_t}{L_t A_t}$


If we divide the foundamental law of motion by the effective labor too, we obtain the <b>Capital Accumulation Equation</b> in intensive form:

$$
\begin{align*}
K_{t+1}/(A_t L_t) &= (1 - \delta) \frac{K_t}{A_t L_t} + s \frac{Y_t}{A_t L_t}\\

k_{t+1}(1 + g)(1 + n) &= (1 - \delta)k_t + s y_t \\

(k_{t+1} -k_t)(1 + n)(1 + g) &= s f(k_t) - (\delta + n + g + n g)k_t\\

(k_{t+1} -k_t)(1 + n)(1 + g) &= s (k_t)^{\alpha} - (\delta + n + g + n g)k_t\\


\end{align*}
$$
Where the last equality is true if we consider the Cobb-Douglas function and it represent the <b>Foundamental Law of motion</b> in intensive form.

$(\delta + n + g + ng)k_t$ is the <b>break-even</b> investment _(the min investment needed to prevent the capital stock in intensive form from shrinking)_. If actual investment $s k_t ^{\alpha}$ is equal to the break-even investment, $k_t$ is costant over time.

A _diminishing marginal product_ of capital implies that $k_t$ converges to where actual investment equals break-even investment.
<br/>In the long run, $k_t$ is costant over time, reaching a <b>steady state</b>
$$
k_{t+1} = k_t = k^*
$$

<div align = "center">
![Steady State in the Solow Model](/images/notes/economic-growth/solow.png)
</div>
<div align = "center">
![Steady State in the Solow Model](/images/notes/economic-growth/solow2.png)
</div>

The long-run equilibrium ($k_{t+1} = k_t = k^*$) implies the following mathematical solution:

$$
0 = (k_{t+1} -k_t)(1 + n)(1 + g) = s k_t^{\alpha}- (\delta + n + g + n g)k_t \implies \\ 

0 = s(k^*)^{\alpha}- (\delta + n + g + n g)k^* \implies \\ 

k^* = \Big (  \frac{s}{\delta + n +g + ng} \Big) ^{1 /( 1 - \alpha)}
$$

that brings the following properties:
- $y^*$, the ratio of output fo effective labor, is costant:

$$
y^* = (k^*)^{\alpha} =  \Big (  \frac{s}{\delta + n +g + ng} \Big) ^{\alpha /( 1 - \alpha)}
$$
- $c^*$ is costant 

$$
c^* = (1 - s)y^* = (1 - s) \Big (  \frac{s}{\delta + n +g + ng} \Big) ^{\alpha /( 1 - \alpha)}
$$

- $k^*, c^*, y^*$ do NOT depend on the initial capital $k_0$.
- Capital per capita $\bar k$, output per capita $\bar y$ and consumptio per capita $\bar c$ grow at rate $g$ of technological progress:

$$
\frac{\bar x_{t+1}-x_t}{\bar x_t} = \frac{x^* (A_{t+1}-A_t)}{x^* A_t} = \frac{x^* (A_t(1 + g)- A_t)}{x^* A_t} = g
$$

- $K, Y, C$ grow at rate $g + n + gn \approx g + n$ for $g,n << 1$:

$$
\frac{X_{t+1}-X_t}{X_t} = \frac{x?* (A_{t+1}L_{t+1} - A_t L_t)}{x^* A_t L_t} = \frac{x^* A_t L_t ((1 + g)(1 +g)-1)}{x^* A_t L_t} = \\
= (1 + g)(1 + n)-1 = g+ n + gn
$$

We can also se that changes in $s, n, \delta, \alpha$ have only effects on per-capita steady-state value.
<br/>A change in $g$ has growth effects on per-capital steady-state values.
<br/>A shock to the capital stock has temporary (but no long-run) effects.

_Example: the Second World brought a shock to the capital stock:_

<div align = "center">
![Steady State in the Solow Model](/images/notes/economic-growth/ww2.png)
</div>

We now ask which saving rate $s^*$ maximizes steady-state consumption $c^*$:

$$
\max _s c^* (s) = (1 - s) f(k^*(s)) = f((k^*)) - (\delta + n + g + ng)k^*(s)
$$

whoose solution is characterized by:

$$
f'(k_{gold}) = n + \delta + g + ng
$$

which is called the <b>Golden Rule of Capital Accumulation</b>.

_Note that, for the Cobb-Douglas production function, $s_{gold} = \alpha$_ and that an economy with $s > s_{gold}$ is <b>dynamically inefficient</b>.

<b>Implications of the Solow Model</b>:
- long-run growth per-capita is only driven by technological progress 
- in the long-run, capital accumulation is a consequence of growth in technology 
- in the short run, growth is faster for countries far away that their steady state.

A bank trasfer is _essentialy_: a <b>message</b> $+$ a <b>balance sheet update</b>

- Settlement: how are obligations discharged _(with what asset)?_

_In the two-tier system, interbank settlement uses central bank reserves._

2. Bank A <b>debits Alice’s deposit account </b> by 10.
3. Bank A sends<b> the interbank payment instruction</b> into the payment system.
4. <b>At the central bank layer,</b> Bank A’s reserve account is debited by 10 and Bank B’s reserve account is credited by 10.
5. Bank B <b>credits Bob’s deposit account </b> by 10.
6. The payment is complete once the interbank obligation has been settled in central bank money.

<b>How do banks acquire reserves?</b>
- Incoming payments from other banks
- Borrowing from other banks or the central bank _(a bank borrows from another in the interbank market, a bank borrows from the central bank through central bank lending operations against collateral)_.
- Selling assets.

_Banks can redistribute reserves among themselves, but only the central bank can create reserves for the system as a whole_.

<b>How is money created?</b>
- Central bank money

- Commercial Bank Money (deposits)
    - Created electronically when banks expand their balance sheets and credit deposit accounts _(main case: banks make loans, some other options include when banks buy assets from non-banks)_.

The <b>Bank balance sheet</b> can be simplified in:
- assets: _loands, securities, reserves_ 
- liabilities: _depotis, wholesale funding, equity_.

## <a id="8"><b>8. IS-LM Model and Open Economy</b> </a>

The <b>monetary policy goals</b> can be summarized, as in the standard US textbook by <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederic_Mishkin" target="_blank">Frederic Mishkin</a>, in:
- low unemployment (high GDP)
- economic growth 
- price stability 
- interest rate stability 
- financial market stability

The problem is that this multitude of goals often anno be reached simultaneously, requiring some trade-offs.

We speak by of <b>discretionary policy</b> when the central bank can choose its policy period by period.<br/>We speak of <b>rule-based monetary policy</b> when policy is _constrained_ by a previously announced rule or commitment.

At first look, a discretionary policy seems superior to a _less-flexible_ rule based, however, when current policy affects private expectatios for the future, discretion may lead to inferior outcomes.

_(Since the early 1990s, many countries have moved towards a more rule-based monetary system, largely independent by the government)_.

On the other side, a dicretionary-policy has some major drawbacks and the most important is the _so-called_ <b>Time Inconsistency Problem</b>.

<b>The Time Inconsistency Problem</b>

The classic analysis of this problmes comes from Kydlan and Prescott, that, for this achievement, received <a href="https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2004/press-release/" target="_blank">the Nobel Prize</a>.

A plan is time inconsistent if it is judjed optimal at $t_0$, but at $t_1$ the same decision maker prefers to revise the plan, even though no new relevant information is available and the current enviroment has not yet changed.

_(This problem is also linked with credibility: announcements are no longer credible if one knows that they will later changed)._

<b>The TI Problem in Monetary Policy</b>

When a central bank knows that it cannot influencer real variables _in the long run_ but it can influence inflation rate, it will _ex-ante_ formulate its poity in a such a way that it results in a optimal inflation. 

However, if the central bank can influence real-variables _in the short run_, it will be tempted to deviate from the previous policy.

Therefore, it is no longer time-consistent.

The equilibrium is thus an inflation rate which is <b>higher than socially desired</b>, but without changing output in the long run.

_(The solution would be a independent CB with rule-based monetary policy)_.

---
<br/>
<b>The Barro-Gordon (1983) Model I</b>

The model a CB that bases in actions on a <b>social welfare function</b> _(discrete-monetary policy)_.

The CB can directly coose an infòation rate $\pi$ for the short-run.

The private sector is the second player and it is described by rational expectations, we then model a strategic game with simultaneous decisions.

The social welfare funtion can be defined as:

$$
S = - \alpha(\pi - \tilde \pi)^2 - (y - \tilde y)^2
$$

or as a <b>loss function</b> as:

$$
L = - S =   \alpha(\pi - \tilde \pi)^2 + (y - \tilde y)^2
$$

with:
- $\alpha >0$: costant
- $\pi$: inflation rate 
- $y$: output
- $\tilde \pi$: target inflation _(we assume here $\tilde \pi = 0$)
- $\tilde y$: target production.

The Philipps curve summarizes the interplay between real and nominal variables:

$$
y = y^N + \beta (\pi - \pi_E)
$$

with:
- $y^N <\tilde y$: natural output (\tilde y = \lambda y^N$ with $\lambda > 1$)
- $\beta > 0$: costant 
- $\pi_E$: expected inflcation

The prive sector has rational expectations, that is:

$$
\pi_E = \mathbb{E}(\pi) = \pi
$$

This immediately im,plies that _(via the Philipps Curve)_ in equilibrium $y$ must satisfy:

$$
y = y^N \quad \pi = 0
$$

_Unfortunately,_ with discrete-monetary policyt, this is not what we get in equilibrium!

So let's go back to the loss function and let's substitute the Phillips curve:

$$
L =   \alpha(\pi - \tilde \pi)^2 + (y - \tilde y)^2
\\
y = y^N + \beta ( \pi - \pi_E) \implies \\

L = \alpha \pi^2 + [( 1 - \lambda)y^N + \beta (\pi - \pi_E)]^2
$$

We now minimize this function with respect to $\pi$:
$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \pi} = 2 \alpha \pi + 2 \beta [(1 - \lambda)y^N + \beta (\pi - \pi_E)] = 0 \implies 
$$

that leads to the <b>Monetary policy reaction function</b>:
$$
\pi = \frac{\beta}{\alpha + \beta^2}[\beta \pi_E + (\lambda -1)y^N]
$$

Together with the reaction function _(expecations)_ of the private sector, $ \pi_E = \pi$, the equilibrium is:

$$
\pi = \frac{\beta (\lambda -1)}{\alpha}y^N \quad \pi > 0 \forall \lambda > 1
$$

The equilibrium ouput is then: $y = y^N$.

Therefore, the optimal scenario ($\pi = 0, y = y^N$) is not reached in equilibrium, but rahter a solution that is _inferior_.

- The inferior solution is time-consistent, while announging $\pi = 0$ is cleary not.
- There's a suboptimal high inflation is the equilibrium, called <b>inflation-bias</b>
- _Intuition_: a discretionary policy provides an inferior result and a monetary policy dependent on _ad-hoc_ governement decisions is therefore not optimal.

For these reasons, sine discretionary policy does not provide the desired results, it is _socially-optimal_ to delegate monetary policy to an independent institution, that it then credibily announce $\pi = 0$.

Along these lines the constitutions of the ECB was drafted.

<b>Possible extensions of the Barro-Gordon Model</b>:

- Look at repeated games instead of a one-shot game
- Include information imperfecions 
- Take supply-shocks into account 
- Model wage settings

$\implies$ also with these changes, the results presented here remain qualitatively very robust.

## <a id="9"><b>Bank Runs $ The Diamon-Dybvig Model</b> </a>

Banks hold only a fractio of their deposits as cash and reserves. The remainder of banks' assets is _illiquid_. $\implies$ this makes banks prone to <b>bank runs</b>

A bank run is often characterized by a sequence of events:
- depositors lose trust in a bank's viability 
- depositors withdraw demand deposits 
- the bank must <b>liquidate its long-term assets</b> to serve its short-term liabilities 
- the bank <b>defaults</b> if the assets' liquidation value is too small for the bank to serve all withdrawals

a _"not so healty"_ bank can fail due to a bank run:
- a bank runs out of liquidity due to unexpected liquidity shock 
- yet, he market value of its _(illiquid)_ assets exceeds its liabilities
- the bank defaults if it must <b>fire-sell</b> its assets at low prices.

Note the distinction between:
- <b>solvency risk</b>: long-term risk on the balance sheet, given by excessive debt, declining asset values 
- <b>liquidity risk</b>: short-term risk on the cash flow _(availability of liquid assets)_ caused by a poor cash and timing manageent 

Therefore:
- A company _(bank)_ can  be <b>solvent but not liquid </b> _(if it has enough assets but lacks the cash to meet immediate obligatoins)_.
- A company _(bank)_ can be <b>liquid but not solvent</b> _(if it has enough cash to meet short-term obligations but not enough assets compared to long-term liabilities)_  
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>The Diamond-Dybvig Model</b>

This model formalizes bank runs in with two important features:
- banks offer deposit contracts, which provide <b>risk sharing and liquidity insurance</b> in case of shocks on consuption needs 
- deposits contracts can have _underisarble_ <b>bank run equilibria</b> in which all depositors panic and withdraw immediately, _including the ones who would prefer to consume late_. 
<br/>
<b>The Consumer's perspective</b>

We start with a _slightly simplified version_ of the enviroment.
- there are three dates, $t = 0,1,2$ 
- there is a mass of <b>ex-ante identical, risk-averse agents</b>
- there are two types of investment technologies:
    - <b>storage</b> yields $1$ at $t = 1$
    - <b>long-term investment</b> yields $R > 1$ at $t = 2$ but only $0.8$ it it must be liquidated early at $t = 1$ _(fire sale)_ $\implies$ <b>early liquidation is costly</b>!

At $t =0$, all agents are identical. They don't know in advance whem they will need to consume. 

At $t = 1$, each agent learns their type _(which is a private information)_:
- with probability $\pi$ they will need liquidity early ($t = 1$)
- with probability $1 - \pi$ they can wait and consume later ($t = 2$)

_(we can interpret $\pi$ as the probability to be hit by a liquidity shock)_ 

Suppose now that one unit of the good is available at you at $t = 0$. There are two ways to use it:
- <b>storage</b> (liquid asset)
- <b>long-term investment</b> offering a return while being illiquid

Suppose that each person acts alone, _without a bank_. Then each individual must deal with the possibility of needing early consumption by itself, creating a difficult chice: _keep in storage and be liquid or invest for the long-term and risk a early-liquidation loss_.

$\implies$ <b>Without a bank, each person bears their own liquidity risk</b>.

$\implies$ <b>Optimal allocation</b> for an individual depends on $R, \pi$ and his degree of <b>risk-aversion</b>.

<b>Pooling </b> helps because, even if each individual is uncertain about their own liquidity needs_, in a large group, the fraction of people who need early consumption is often fairly predictable $\implies$ _by the Law of Large Numbers_, a <b>fraction</b> $\pi$ will need liquidity early at $t = 1$.

_Economic intuition (<b>Liquidity Insurance</b>): if many people pool their funds, some can withdraw early, while the rest of the resources can remain invested long term_.

<b>The Bank's perspective</b>

Banks collect deposits from many households. They can then invest a share $1 -\pi$ of these funds in long-term assets and keep a share $\pi$ as liquid.

- all depositors are then offered the possibility of withdrawing on demand 
- they partecipate in the returns of the asset portfolios held by banks throught interest earned on their deposits 
- Banks perfom <b>maturity transformation</b>:
    - they hold <b>relatively illiquid-long-term assets</b>
    - they issue <b>relatively liquid-short-term liabilities</b>
<br/>_(This mechanism allow society to invest in productive long-term assets while agents with unexpected early consumption needs can still access fund at any $t$)_ 

As a consequence: 
- more resources can remain invested in high-return long-term projects. 
- depositors partecipate in these returns with interest on deposits 
- early consumers gain access to liquidity when they need it 

But, _at the same time_, this structure also creates <b>fragility</b>: if too many depositors demand liquidity at once, the bank can run out into troubles.

In particular, this mechanism becomes fragile if a larger fraction $> \pi$ tries to withdraw early since a bank can promise liquidity to each depositor individually, but not to all depositors simultaneously _(the bank is liquid only against "normal" withdrawal demand, not against everyone withdrawing at once)_

<b>The panic-scenario</b> 

Now suppose that many depositors try to withdrawal at $t = 1$, including those whou could actually wait. In this case, the bank must liquidate a large amount of its long-term assets early _(remember that early liquidation is costly)_, so <b>panic destroys value</b>.

$\implies$ if too many depositors demand liquidity at once, the bank may be forced to sell long-term assets at a loss and may be unable to satisfy everyone in full.

For a depositor who does _not_ need funds early:
- if they expect the others to remain calm/rationale, it is best to wait until $t = 2$
- if they expect many others to run, <b>waiting becomes dangerous</b>:
    - the bank may run out of liquidity 
    - long-term assets may be liquidated at a loss 
    - late withdrawers may receive less/zero 

<br/>
<b>The first-mover advantage</b>

Banks typically serve withdrawal requests in the order in which they arrive <br/> $\implies$
those who arrive _early_ are more likely to be paid in full 

In a panic, it can be <b>individually rational</b> to withdraw early simply because others may do so first <br/>$\implies$
this <b>first-mover advantage</b> is the central ingredient of the Diamon-Dybvig logic.

If everyone expects a run, the running becomes <b>individually rational</b> and so the run occurs. 

---
<br/>
<b>Liquidity Crisis vs Solvency Crisis</b>

A bank can fail even when it is solvent $\implies$ the prblem may be of liquidity, not necessarily of solvency
- if the assets could be held to maturity, they might be sufficient to cover the bank's obligations 
- if the bank is forced to liquidate them early under stress, it may realize losses and fail 

_In general,_ a <b>liquidity crisis</b> arises because fund are needed now, a <b>solvency crisis</b> arises because assets are worth too little<br/> $\implies$ 
The Diamond-Dybvig model is a story about a <b>liquidity crisis that can turn into a solvency crisis</b>

Note that crsis can also begin in the opposite direction: <b>doubts about solvency can trigger a liquidity crisis</b>.

<br/>
<b>From classic bank runs to modern digital banks</b>

In this model, depositors try to withdraw cash at the same time. However, _in modern banking systems,_ this often no longer means queuing up physically at the bank. Instead, the run can be even more frictionless and consist in:
- rapid digital transfers to other banks 
- shift into money market funds or Treasury Bills 

A modern bank run is often not a run <b>into cash</b>, but a run <b>out of a bank's liabilities</b> and into alternative stores of value.

<b>Policy, interpretation and limits</b>

The previous model doesn't state that banks are undesiderable, banks are indeed _socially useful_ because they provide liquidity insurance. The policy is therefore not to eliminate banking, but the challenge is to preserve the <b>good equilibrium</b> while ruling out the run equilibrium.

<b>Suspension of Convertibility</b>

One classic solution is a <b>suspension of convertibilitiy</b>. When cash reserves are depleted in $t = 1$, this prevents runs:
- the bank stops withdrawals in $t = 1$ after first 20 depositors in queue withdrew $\implies$ it closes down when initial cash reserves are depleted 
- this ensures that loans can mature without _fire-sales_.

In this case, this action <b>restores confidence</b>:
- late types know they are safe and there's no reason to withdrawal early 
- early types can each withdraw successfully at $t = 1$ because late types have no incentive to run 

But this not _necessarily_ work well if $\pi$ is unknown or understimated.

<b>Deposit Insurance and Lender of Last Resort (LOLR)</b>

Another solution is <b>deposit insurance</b>:
- if depositors know their funds are protected even it the bank comes under pressure, then the incentive to run is reduced 
- in this case patient depositors no longer need to runsh to run early 

A further solution is a <b>Lender of Last Resort</b>:
- the central bank can provide liquidity to a foundamentally healty but illiquid bank
- this prevent inefficient fire-sales and disorders that can lead to failure 

$\implies$ but these solutions can raise other issues, _such as moral hazard_:
- deositors may pay less attention to bank risks 
- banks may have stronger incentives to take risk 

$\implies$ it downside risks are partly absorbed by the <b>public sector</b>, private actors may take on more risk than is <b>socially desiderable</b>.

This is why modern banking systems do not realy on deposit insurance or LOLR, they also provide a <b>prudential framework</b> to limit risk-taking in the first place. Two core elements are:
- <b>capital regulation</b>: banks must have enough loss-absorbing equity 
- <b>liquidity regulation</b>: banks must hold enough liquid assets

$\implies$ a stable system requires both crisis-management tools and ex-ante regulation.

## <a id="10"><b>10. Crises in Market Economies</b> </a>

Crises can be either single _(as standalone events: financial market, real estate, currency, public debt, banking, private debt etc...)_  or the combination of two or more aspects at the same time.

<b>2008 Banking Crisis</b>

_For example_, during the banking crisis in 2008, house prices in the US dropped by about 20-30% $\implies$ many households suddenly saw the value of their houses fall below the amount they still owed to the bank _(this is called negative equity)_ $\implies$ they could no longer refinance their mortgage or sell the house to repay the loan $\implies$ many borrowers became stressed or defaulted $\implies$ <b>private debt crisis</b>.

In simple terms: when the value of the house falls a lot, but the debt stays the same, the borrower is trapped. If many households face this situation at the same time, the problem spreads from the housing market to the whole economy.

<a href="https://www.netflix.com/ch-en/title/80075560" target="_blank">More about: The Big Short</a>.

Banks, in particular, had losses on <b>Mortgages Backed Securities (MBS)</b> when private borrowers defaulted.

A MBS is a <b>security backed by mortgages</b>, it represents an interest in a pool of mortgage loans).

Unlike a bond, in the MBS case the cash flows are not fixed, they depend on mortgage repayments, prepayments _(borrowers can refinance or pay early)_, higher default risk and the discount rate used by the market. 

For many agency MBS, people often use an <b>option-adjusted spread approach</b> _(extra yield added to a benchmark so the bond's model price matches the market price accounting for embedded optioins)_, that means that the bond is priced as the present value of the cash flows after adjusting for the prepayment.

$$
P = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{\mathbb{E}[CF_t]}{(1+r_t+\text{OAS})^t}
$$

Banks had very little equity _(in the extreme case: UBS and some other around 2% of total assets)_ so:
- some banks could not absorb losses and started to move toward default 
- healthy banks were affected through interbank loans 
<br/>$\implies$ many more banks moved to default $\implies$ danger of banking system collapse.

Since a collapse of the banking system would have cause <b>large losses</b> (in GDP, employment, panic etc...), the <b>government rescued banks</b>.

As a consequence, in some countries public debt increases so much that a <b>public debt crisis occurred</b>.

_What could have been done to prevent such a crisis?_ 
- increase equity ratios in banks so they can absorb losses (<b>banking crisis</b>)
- reduce public debt over time (<b>public debt crisis</b>)
- require at least 20% of house value as equity investment (<b>private debt crisis</b>)

The criteria to choose a good instrument usually are: 
- is the criteria effective?
- does it have negative consequences for the economu today or in the future? 
- does it burden taxpayers excessively? 

From a <b>crisis-management</b> point of view, the goal is simple:
- stop panic in the short run
- avoid a collapse of credit to households and firms
- stabilize output and employment.

The main actors and tools are:

1. <b>Central Bank (liquidity manager)</b>
    - provides emergency liquidity to banks _(typically short-term loans against collateral)_
    - lowers policy rates to reduce funding stress
    - can expand its balance sheet to calm markets.

2. <b>Government / Treasury (solvency backstop)</b>
    - recapitalizes weak banks _(injecting public capital and becoming shareholder if needed)_
    - supports restructuring or temporary public control of failing institutions
    - can purchase or ring-fence distressed assets to reduce uncertainty
    - can provide targeted fiscal support to households and firms.

3. <b>Public Guarantees (confidence tools)</b>
    - stronger deposit guarantees to prevent retail bank runs
    - guarantees on interbank funding to restart lending between banks
    - guarantees on loans to firms to avoid a credit freeze in the real economy.

4. <b>Standard macro policies (demand stabilization)</b>
    - monetary easing: lower short-term interest rates
    - fiscal expansion: higher government spending and, in some cases, temporary tax relief.

5. <b>Direct government/CB interventions:</b>
    - providing loans to firms by government 
    - buying up toxic asset by CB in the market.

_Intuition:_ the central bank mainly addresses <b>liquidity</b>, while the government addresses <b>solvency</b> and supports demand. Using both together is often necessary in systemic crises.

Main trade-off: stronger support reduces the probability of collapse today, but can increase public debt and moral hazard tomorrow.

In 2008, there should have been injected new funds to become equity holders, increased depotis guarantees and interbank loans and lowered the short term interest rate.

Note that measures 4 and 5, at least alone, _do not resolve a banking crisis!_.

---
<br/>
<b>Public Debt Crisis</b>

The most common reasons for a <b>public debt crisis</b> are:
- <b>Persistent deficits over many years</b>: governments spend more than they collect in taxes, not only in recessions but often also in normal times.
- <b>Large extraordinary shocks</b>: wars, natural disasters, banking rescues, and major infrastructure programs can suddenly raise debt.
- <b>Higher interest costs</b>: if interest rates rise while debt is already high, debt dynamics can deteriorate quickly.

In theory, a Keynesian approach suggests:<b> deficits in recessions, surpluses in expansions.</b> In practice, many governments do the first part, but not the second one.

The <b>common pool problem</b> means that many political actors push for spending that benefits their own group, while the tax cost is spread across all taxpayers and often shifted to the future. As a result, total spending can become too high.

Typical political mechanisms behind debt accumulation are:
- <b>Political budget cycles</b>: before elections, policymakers may increase spending or cut taxes to gain support.
- <b>Delayed stabilization</b>: even when adjustment is needed, political conflict can postpone decisions, so debt keeps rising.
- <b>Fiscal illusion</b>: voters may see the immediate benefit of spending, but underestimate the future cost of taxes and debt service.

$\implies$ The outcome is often asymmetric policy: debt rises in bad times, but is not reduced enough in good times.

To address this, countries use different <b>fiscal rules</b>:
- <b>Balanced-budget rules</b>: budgets should be close to balance over the cycle.
- <b>Debt brakes</b>: if debt rises too much, automatic correction is triggered in later years.
- <b>Stabilization reserves</b>: governments save in good times, then use those buffers in recessions.
- <b>Expenditure rules</b>: spending growth is capped to avoid permanent expansion of public spending.
- <b>Institutional accountability</b>: stronger monitoring, fiscal councils, and political penalties for repeated violations.

<b>Deep dive: Maastricht criteria (EU)</b>

The Maastricht framework introduced two famous reference values:
- annual public deficit should not exceed <b>3% of GDP</b>
- public debt should not exceed <b>60% of GDP</b>, or should move clearly toward that level over time.

_The economic intuition is simple:_
- the <b>3%</b> limit tries to avoid persistent large yearly deficits
- the <b>60%</b> limit tries to keep total debt at a sustainable level.

Over time, this framework evolved through <b>the Stability and Growth Pact:</b>
- a <b>preventive arm</b>: _countries should keep their structural budget close to a medium-term objective_
- a <b>corrective arm</b>: _if deficit/debt limits are breached, an excessive deficit procedure can be opened._

In practice, there are also <b>escape clauses</b> for severe recessions or extraordinary shocks _(for example systemic crises)_, because strict consolidation during a deep downturn can worsen the recession.

_Main strengths:_
- creates common criteria for fiscal discipline
- improves comparability across countries
- reduces the risk that one country's fiscal instability influence others.

_Main weaknesses:_
- one rule cannot fit all countries all the times
- enforcement can become political and uneven
- complex rules are harder for voters to monitor.

So there is a clear trade-off:
- very <b>rigid</b> rules increase discipline, but can be too harsh in crises
- very <b>flexible</b> rules help stabilization, but may fail to stop debt accumulation.

So the main policy challenge is to combine both $\implies$ credible long-run debt reduction with enough short-run flexibility in major shocks.

A new approach to limit public debt accumulation is a <b>debt-sensitive majority rule</b>:
- the majority needed to approve the budget is 50% when there is no deficit, but the required majority increases as the planned deficit increases.
<br/>$\implies$ for instance, a deficit of 1% of GDP may require 55% of votes, while a deficit of 2% may require 60%, and so on.

_According to this rule_: the higher the planned public debt, the higher the parliamentary majority needed to approve it.

## <a id="11"><b>11. Macroeconomic Forecasting</b> </a>

Macroeconomic forecasting refers to the process of <b>predicting future realizations </b> of economic aggregates _(GDP, indlation, unemployment, interest rate etc...)_.

- <b>Forecasting</b>: the process of looking ahead using historical data to predict values at a specific points in the future.
    - _Idea: Predict $Y_{T+h}$ where $h > 0$ (horizon), given all information available up to to $T$._
- <b>Nowcasting</b>:  the prediction of the present, the very near future, or even the very recent past where $h$ is very small.
- <b>Backcasting</b>: can have separate meanings:
    - _Estimating missing data from the past before your data collection began._
    - _Imagining a specific future and working backward to determine what needs to happen to make it happen._

Several approchaes possible to forecasting:
- judgental/expert forecasting, based on opinions, inution, visual data inspection and prior economic theory. 
    - strengths: _flexiblility to incorporate current events of breaks, useful when data is limited or unreliable_
    - weaknesses: _subjective to bias, hard to replicate_
- time-series model 
- machine learning models that learns complex, often non linear patterns from data with minimal prior structur.
    - main difference from time-series: _ML mainly focused on large unstructured data, it emphazises flexbible and data drive prediction, while TS models rely on explicit statistical structure and assumptions._
- structursal macroeconomic models 
    - dynamic stochastic general equilibrium (DSGE) models: they model the behavior and the interdependencies between agents based on micro-economic theory $\implies$ they provide a set of equations describing how the economy works. 
    - Structural model an be either calibrated or estimated _(based on macro-economic time-series data)_ 
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Time-series Basics</b>

A time series is a sequence $y_1, y_2... y_T$. We define the forecast horizon as $h \in [1...H]$ and we forecast:

$$
\mathbb{E}_t [y_{t+h}] \quad \text{or} \mathbb{E}_{t-h} [y_{t}]
$$

to obtain a forecast series:

$$
\mathbb{E}_t [y_1] = \hat y_1,\mathbb{E}_t [y_2] = \hat y_2 ... \mathbb{E}_t [y_T] = \hat y_T

$$

We define the forecast error as:

$$
e_t = \mathbb{E}_{t-h}[y_1 ]- y_t
$$


The <b>Loss Function</b> $L = F(e_1, e_2 ... e_T)$ defines the criterium for forecast accuracy. Some common examples are:
- <b>Root mean squared forecast error (RMSFE)</b>:

$$
L = \frac{1}{T}\sum_i^T e_t^2
$$

- <b>Mean absolute forecast error (MAFE)</b>:

$$
L = \frac{1}{T}\sum_i^T | e_t |
$$

---
<br/>
<b>The Random Walk forecast model</b>

We consider the simple case in which we want to forecast $y_{t+1}$ at period $t$ _(one-step ahead)_. 

The random walk model is simply:

$$
y_t = y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t, \quad \epsilon_t \sim N(0, \sigma^2)
$$

Conditioning on the information set at time $t$ and using $\mathbb{E}_t[\epsilon_{t+1}] = 0$:

$$
\mathbb{E}_t[y_{t+1}] = \mathbb{E}_t[y_t + \epsilon_{t+1}] = y_t
$$

So the one-step forecast is:

$$
\hat y_{t+1} = y_t
$$

The corresponding forecast error is:

$$
e_{t+1|t} = y_{t+1} - \hat y_{t+1} = \epsilon_{t+1}, \quad \mathbb{V}_t(e_{t+1|t}) = \sigma^2
$$

For horizon $h$:

$$
y_{t+h} = y_t + \sum_{j=1}^{h}\epsilon_{t+j}
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
\hat y_{t+h|t} = y_t,
\quad
\mathbb{V}_t(e_{t+h|t}) = h\sigma^2
$$

_Intuition: the best prediction is the latest observed value, while uncertainty increases linearly with the horizon._

<b> AR(1) forecast model</b>

The autoregressive model of order 1 can be defined as:

$$
y_t = \phi y_{t-1} + \epsilon_t
$$

where $\phi$ is the <b>autoregressive parameter</b>.

The one-step-ahead conditional expectation is:

$$
\mathbb{E}_t[y_{t+1}] = \mathbb{E}_t[\phi y_t + \epsilon_{t+1}] = \phi y_t
$$

In practice, replacing $\phi$ with its estimate $\hat\phi$, we get:

$$
\hat y_{t+1 } = \hat \phi y_t
$$

For $h$ steps ahead:

$$
\hat y_{t+h|t} = \hat\phi^{h}y_t
$$

and the forecast error can be written as:

$$
e_{t+h|t} = \sum_{j=0}^{h-1}\phi^j\epsilon_{t+h-j}
$$

If $|\phi|<1$ _(stationary case)_, then $\hat y_{t+h|t} \to 0$ as $h \to \infty$.

_Intuition_:
- if $\phi \approx 1$, dynamics are highly persistent and close to a random walk.
- if $0<\phi<1$, shocks fade out gradually (mean reversion).
- if $\phi<0$, the process tends to alternate signs around its mean.

<b>Dynamic factor model (DFM) framework </b>

Let the variation in the data be captured by $q$ high-frequency factors. 

We consider the <b>measurement equation</b> for the dynamic factors $f_t$:

$$
y_t = \Lambda f_t + e_t
$$

and the <b>state equation</b> for the dynamic factors:

$$
f_t = \Phi f_{t-1} + v_t
$$

Firstly, we forecast the factors using the state equation:

$$
\hat f_{t+1 | t} = \hat \Phi \hat f_t
$$

Then, we use the measurement equation to forecast observables:

$$
\hat y_{t+1|t} = \hat \Lambda \hat f_{f+1|t}
$$
