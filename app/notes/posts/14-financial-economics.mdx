## <a id="0.1"><b>0.1 Introduction - The Fama-Schiller controversy</b></a>

In 2013, Eugene Fama, Bob Shiller and Lars Hansen jointly received the <a href="https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2013/summary/" target="_blank">Novel Prize in economics</a> for their economic analysis of asset prices but, ironically, Fama and Shiller represent a interesting and controversial case for the subject.

<a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_Fama" target="_blank">Eugene Fama</a> is most often considered as the father of the <b>Efficient Market Hypotesis</b> (EFH) which supports the <b>Capital Asset Pricing Model </b>(CAPM), which states that stock's returns over time should be commensurate with its riskiness in relation to the overall market.

In formulas: :

$$
E(R_i) = R_f + \beta_i (E(R_m) - R_f)
$$

Where $R_f$ is the risk-free rate, $\beta_i$ (Beta) the sensitivity of the asset to market,$E(R_m) - R_f$ the risk-premium _(the extra return investors demand for picking risky assets over the risk-free)._

Moreover, Fama argued that any test of EMH is actually testing two things at once:

- That the market is efficient.
- That the model is a correct way to measure risk.

<a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Shiller" target="_blank">Bob Shiller</a>, on the other hand, published in 1981 the paper <a href="https://www.aeaweb.org/aer/top20/71.3.421-436.pdf" target="_blank">Do stock prices move too much to be justified by subsequent changed in dividends?</a> that, answering _Yes!_ to the question, claims that the EMH was one of the _remarkable errors_ in the history of economics.

Regarding Fama's theories, Shiller opinion was something like...
- _I think that maybe he has a cognitive dissonance_
- _It's like being a Catholic priest and then discovering that God doesn't exist [...], you can't deal with that, you've got to somehow rationalise it._

In particular, Shiller’s empirical result is that stock prices move way more than the dividends.<br/>
If a stock price is truly the _"discounted value of all future dividends",_ it should be stable because dividends don't change that much, but Shille showed the opposite using the <b>Cyclically Adjusted Price-to-Earnings</b>(CAPE) ratio.

In the following yeats, empirical data <b>gave credit to Shiller's critic.</b>

But what does the CAPM actually say?
- how the market should price financial assets in function of the risk 
- it shows that a complete market _(perfect information and full pricing)_ will lead to a market equilibrium with optimal risk allocation

However, the CAPM relies on very heavy and simplifying assumptions, some are technical and could be relaxed, others are foundamental and not often realistic:
- <b>the complete market assumption</b>:
    - it would require infinitely many assets
    - it would require complex products _(derivatives)_
    - complexity implies expertise and potential moral hazard 
- <b>other assumptions are unrealistic</b>:
    - investors can lend and borrown unlimited money under risk free rates 
    - all assets are divisible and liquid 
    - all agents have identical beliefs

_So, was Fama simply wrong?_
- Not really, he didn't give up and he refined the model, abandoning CAPM and replacing it with a <b>multi-factor risk model</b>
- Even Shiller still endorses a _loose_ verion of it.
- For Shiller, asset prices can _overshoot_ in the short term (due to emotion and irrationality), but they show _reversion to the mean_ in the long period.
- Additionally, according to Shiller markets are still the best tools we have for aggregating information.

To sum up, the Fama-Shiller case is typical from a social science and economics: two theories can contradict each other and <b>still retain intellectual value</b>.
<br/>_"All Models are wrong, but some are usesul"_ applies perfectly here: Fama gave an excellent structure to think the financial markets and, even if its work is imperfect, it's yet a solid starting point.

## <a id="0.2"><b>0.2 Introduction to finance and investment planning</b></a>

A <b>financial system</b> is a set of institutions and markets that have the primary purpose of allowing the _desynchronization_ of income and consumptio.

A financial system allows to match different financial needs of different agents in _two dimensions_:
- <b>Time</b> (borrow and save):
    - wish of continuousu consumption vs discrete income stram 
    - smooth consumption of time/the life-cycle
- <b>Risk</b> (diversify, insure, hedge):
    - smooth consumption across state of nature and possible market scenarios.

<b>Asset</b>: something valuable with well-defined property rights _(a contract, a good)_.

<b>Real Asset</b>: something that has an _intrinsic_ value due to its substance and properties.

<b>Financial Asset</b>: something that has _NO intrinsitic material value_ but which can be traded.

_(Note that the distinction between financial and non-financial asset is not binary and well defined: for example, _is gold a financial or physical/real asset?_)

Financial assets can be caterogized as follows:
- <b>Riskless assets</b>: assets whose future values can be known with certainty _(a government bond that yields 100 CHF in 5 years, assuming no default)_
- <b>Risky assets</b>: assets whose future value may depend on some events _(insurance, stocks, options)_blank
- <b>Derivatives</b> are risky assets whose future values depend on the price of other assets.

A <b>bond</b> is often considered a riskless asset _(depending on the issuer)_.

A <b>zero-coupon (ZC) bond with maturity $t$</b> yields no payment before period $t$ and pays $1$ in period $t$.<br/>They can vary _only_ in terms of <b>maturity</b>.

Denote by $p_{0,t}$ the price of a ZC bond with maturity $t$ at time $0$ _(usually the first subscript is current time, the second the maturity)_. In absence of arbitrage, we have:

$$
p_{0,0} = 1
\\

p_{0,t} = \frac{p_{0,t-1}}{(1 + r_t)}

$$


where $r_t$ is the <b>discrete per period forward interest rate</b> between $t-1$ and $t$. By iterationm we have:

$$
p_{0,t} = \frac{1}{\prod^t_{i = 1}(1 + r_i)}
$$

In relation to this, we can distinguish:
- <b>Discounting</b>: the process of determining the _prevent valiue_ of a future payment $x_t$ at time $t > 0$:

$$
PV(x_t) =  \frac{x_t}{\prod^t_{i = 1}(1 + r_i)}
$$
- <b>Compounding</b>: the process of determining the current value at time $t$ _(in the future)_ of a monetary amount $x_0$ invested at $t = 0$
$$

CV_t(x_0) = \prod_{i = 1}^t (1 +r_i) \cdot x_0
$$

The <b>discrete sport interest rate</b> $\bar r_t$, for a zerou coupon bond with maturity $t$ such that:

$$
p_{0,t} = \frac{p_{0,0}}{(1 + \bar r_t)^t}
$$

Or, in other words, the spot rate is the _geometric average_ of the per period forward rate of interest:

$$
\frac{1}{(1 + \bar r_t)^t} = \frac{1}{\prod^t_{i = 1}(1 + r_i)} \implies 1 + \bar r_t = \Big[\frac{1}{\prod^t_{i = 1}(1 + r_i)}  \Big]^{1/t}
$$


We now consider a bond with price $p_0$ in $t = 0$ that yields a series of positive <b>coupon payments</b> $a_t$ until the maturity date, so for $t = 1, \dot T$, plus the final payment of <b>par vaue</b> in $t = T$.

Its <b>yield to maturity</b>(YTM) is the unique rate $y$ for which the present value of these payments is equal to $p_0$:

$$
p_0 = \sum_{t = 1}^T \frac{a_t}{(1 + y)^t} + \frac{Par}{(1 + y)^T}
$$





---

The same analysis can be formulated in continuous time, in this case the <b>instantaneous forward interest rate </b> is:

$$
p(t + dt) \approx \frac{p(t)}{1 + r(t)dt}
$$

or, equivalently:

$$
\frac{p(t + dt) - p(t)}{p(t)} \approx - r(t)dt
$$

By integration, we get:

$$
p(t) = p(0)e^{-\int^t_0 r(\tau)d \tau} = e^{-\int_0^t r(\tau) d\tau}
$$

This interest rate $r(\tau)$ is called _"instantanenous"_ since it represents the evolution of the bond's price process for a infinitesimally small period of time $dt$.

Moreover, in continuous time we can define:

$$
PV(x) = \int_0^{\infty} e^ {-\int_0^t r(\tau) d \tau} x(t)dt
$$

The <b>continuously compounended forward interest rate </b> between $t_1, t_2$, denoted by $\bar r_{t_1}(t_2)$ is defined as:

$$
p(t_2) = p(t_1) e^{-(t_2 - t_1)\bar r_{t_1}(t_2)}
$$

$$
\bar r_{t_1}(t_2) = \frac{1}{t_2 - t_1} \log \Big [ \frac{p(t_1)}{p(t_2) }\Big] = \frac{1}{t_2 - t_1} \int^{t_2}_{t_1}r(\tau) d\tau

$$


The <b>continuously compounded spot interest rate </b> for a bond with maturity $$ is:

$$
\bar r_0 (t) = \frac{1}{t} \int^t_0 r(\tau) d\tau
$$

which is the _arithmetic average_ of the instantaneous forward interest rates between $0,t$.

The function $t \to \bar r_0(t)$ provides the <b>yield curve</b>.

---

We now consider <b>risky assets</b>. To evalute an <b>uncertain stream </b> of future payments, we still used an additive process:

$$
\mathbb{E}[PV(x)] = \sum_{t = 1}^{+ \infty} \frac{\mathbb{E}[x_t]}{ \prod^t_{i = 1}(1 +r_i)} = \sum_{t = 1}^{+ \infty} \frac{\mathbb{E}[x_t]}{ \prod^t_{i = 1}(1 +r_t^f + \pi_t)}
$$

where the second formulation is needed if we want risk to be taken into account, and we write $r_i = r^r_t + \pi_t$, where $r^f_t$ is the risk-free rate and $\pi_t$ the risk premium.

## <a id="1"><b>1. Options and financial strategies</b></a>

<b>Derivatives</b> are assets whose values mechanically depend on the values of other financial assets (the <b>underlying</b>). <br/>For example:
- <b>Forward contracts</b>:  _OBLIGATION_ to purchase _(long position)_ or sell _(short position)_ the underlying at a specified future price at a specified delivery date.
- <b>Options contracts</b>: _RIGHT_ to purchase or sell a specified amount of the underlying at a specified _exercise price_ at or before a specified _expiration date_.

Options offer an advantage: the transaction does not have to occur if it not profitable for the owner of the option. This advantage comes at a price:
- Forwards are entered at _NO cost_
- Options are purchased or sold at positive price that the represents the _cost of the right_ to buy/sell.
- Selling (or _writing_) an option implies an obligation (if the option is exericed, the seller must sell or buy the stock and incurs a loss) \to the seller receives a compensation.

Derivatives can help shaping the risk exposure:
- <b>Hedging</b> means insuring against market price volatility
- <b>Speculation</b> means expoiting market price volatility

---

<b>Forward Contract Payoff</b>

- $X$ agree delivery price
- $S_t$ spot market prie of the underlying at maturity $T$

Profits at maturity $T$ are:
- Payoff long position $ = S_t - X$
- Payoff short position $ = X - S_t$

For this reason, forwards are great for <b>speculation</b>. At a goiven time $t$, an unerlying has a price $S_t$, with <b>no clear evolution</b> for the future. If a speculator expectes:
- $S_T > S_t \implies$ long forward with $X < S_T$
- $S_T < S_t \implies$ short forward with $X > S_T$

On the other hand, forwards are used in hedging to <b>avoid risks</b>.<br/>.
Suppore, _for example_, that Giacomo's GmbH needs to sell an activity (_example: some running shoes_) at maturity $T$, with value $S_t$ at given time $t$.
<br/>Giacomo faces then a risk management problem: how can he insure against price fluctuations of the running shoes?

Solution: use the forward contract to construct a <b>hedged portfolio</b> that fixes the profits _from the furue sale_ at a desired level $\pi^*$:

$$
\pi^* = S_t + (X - S_T) = X
$$

To fix the profits at $\pi^* = X$, Giacomo can open a <b>short position</b> on the forward contract with delivery price $X$.

_(The opposite - long forward contract - applies if you need to buy and you want to avoid risk)_.

<b>Options Payoff</b>

An options is a right to purchase/sell a certain amount of the underlying at/before future expiration $T$ at exercise price _(strike)_ $X$.<br/>Options differ by:
- Position
    - <b>Long</b>: option holder _(right to exercise)_ 
    - <b>Short</b>: option writer _(obligation to facilitate the option's exercise)_ 
- Type of right:
    - <b>Put</b>: long position has the right to sell an asset for $X$ 
    - <b>Call</b>: long position has the right to purchase an asset for $X$
- Possibility of early exercise: 
    - <b>American option</b>: exercise at or before expiration 
    - <b>European option</b>: exercise only at expiration.

Consider now:
- $X$ exericse price of an option 
- $S_T$ market price of the underlying at expiration $T$
- <b>Premium</b>: purchase price of an option _(market value)_:
    - $C_0$: premium for a <b>call</b> at $t = 0$
    - $P_0$: premium for a <b>put</b> at $t = 0$.

## <a id="2"><b>2. Option valuation</b></a>

In this chapter we focus on <b>European options</b>, please refer to the above sections for formal definition.

We also use the following notation:
- $S_0$: price of the underlying asset at toime $t = 0$
- $C_0, P_0$: prices (premia) of the call and the put option at time $t = 0$.
- $r_f$: rate of the risk-free asset.

An <b>Arbitrage Opportunity</b> is defined as a <b>financial strategy</b> that yields a (sure) cash flow $A_0 \ge 0$ at time $0$ and a cash-flow $A_t \ge 0$ at time $T$ and there is at least one stat of the word where one inequality is strict.

- $A_0 > 0, A_T >ge$ almost surely is an abirtrage 
- $A_0 \ge 0, A_t \ge 0$ almot surely with $\text{Prob}[A_T > 0]>0$ is an arbitrage

In this chapter, we assume that the market is arbitrage-free _(the market clears out arbitrage opportunities)_.

<b>Price Bounds on call options (European, no dividend)</b>
- Upper Bound: $C_0 \le S_0$

_Proof:_ <br/>
Consider two portfolios:
- Portfolio $A$: $1$ call + cash $PV(X)
    - Price in $t = 0$: $ [C_0 + PV(X)]
    - Value in $T$: $X + \max [S_t. X, 0] = \max [S_t, X]

- Portoflio $B$: $1$ stock $S_0$
    - Price in $t = 0: S_0$
    - Value in $T$: $S_T$
<br/>
Portfolio $A$ is never less valuable than portfolio in $T$, so $A$ cannot be less expensive in $t = 0$, otherwise we have an arbitrage opportunity, therefore we conclude:

$$
C_0 + PV(X) \ge S_0
$$

and $C_0 \ge 0$ since the call is an option.

- Lower Boud:  $C_0 \max [S_0 - PV(X), 0]$ where $PV$ is the present value operator. 


<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Price Bounds on put options (European, no dividend)</b>
- Upper boud: $P_0 \le PV(X)
- Lower Bound: $P_0 \ge \max [PV(X) - S_0, 0]$

_Proof:_<br/>
Consider two portfolios:
- Portfoio $C$: buy $1$ put + $1$ stock
    - Price in  $t = 0$: $C_0 + S_0$
    - Value in $T$: $\max(X - S_T, 0) + S_T = \max [S_t, X$] 
- Portfolio $D$: cash $PV(X)
    - Price in $t = 0$: $PV(X)$ 
    - Value  in $T$: $X$ 

$C$ is never less valuable than $D$, so $C$ cannot be less expensive in $t = 0$, otherwise we have an arbitrage opportunity, therefore:

$$
P_0 + S_0 \ge PV(X)
$$

and we add $P_0 \ge 0$ since the put is an option.

---

<br/>
<b>The Put-Call Parity Theorem</b>

$$
S_0 + P_0 = \frac{X}{(1 + r_f)^T} + C_0
$$

To understand this, consider the following:
- Portfolio $A$: $1$ stock and $1$ put 
- Portfolio $B$: $\frac{X}{(1 + r_f)^T}$ bonds and $1$ call

the values of the two portfolio are equal in $t =T$, therefore they have to be equal also in $t = 0$, leading to the Put-Call parity formula.

---

<b>Option Valutation</b>

Determinants of the value _(price/premium)_ of a call option $C_0$ _(European, no dividend)_:
- stock price $S_0$ (+), exercise price $K$ (-)
- volatility of underlying stock (+) _(intuition: volatility increases the probability that the stock value at expiration is greater than $K$, increasing potential gains)_.
- time to expiration $T-t$ (+) _(intuition: the more time to expiration, the higher the range od stock price increases, similar to volatility),  also, the present value of exercise price falls)_
- interest rate (+) _(intuition: present value of exercise price falls)_.

---

<b>Two-state Option Valuation Model </b>

Consider a market with:
- a stock that currently trades at $S_0$, and which value in $t = 1$ can be either $S_1^u, S_1^d$, with $S_1^u > S_1^d$
- call optio on the stock with exercise price $X \in (S_1^d, S_1^u)$ and expiration date $T = 1$ 
- a risk free asset with yearly interest rate $r_f$

Then it is possible to replicate the payoffs of the call using a combination of the risk-free asset and the stock. The replicating strategy can be found as:

$$
\begin{cases}
\alpha S_1^u + \beta (1 + r_f) = S_1^u - X \\
\alpha S_1^d + \beta (1 + r_f)= 0


\end{cases}
$$
which gives:
$$
\alpha = \frac{S_1^u - X}{S_1^u - S_1^d}, \quad \beta = \frac{(S_1^u - X)S_1^d}{(1 + r_f)(S_1^d - S_1^u)}
$$
