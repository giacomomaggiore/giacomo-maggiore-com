## <a id="0.1"><b>0.1 Introduction - The Fama-Shiller controversy</b></a>

In 2013, Eugene Fama, Bob Shiller and Lars Hansen jointly received the <a href="https://www.nobelprize.org/prizes/economic-sciences/2013/summary/" target="_blank">Nobel Prize in economics</a> for their economic analysis of asset prices but, _ironically,_ Fama and Shiller represent an interesting and controversial case for the subject.

<a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_Fama" target="_blank">Eugene Fama</a> is most often considered as the father of the <b>Efficient Market Hypothesis</b> (EMH) which supports the <b>Capital Asset Pricing Model</b> (CAPM), which states that a stock's returns over time should be commensurate with its riskiness in relation to the overall market.

In formulas:

$$
E(R_i) = R_f + \beta_i (E(R_m) - R_f)
$$

Where $R_f$ is the risk-free rate, $\beta_i$ (Beta) the sensitivity of the asset to the market, and $E(R_m) - R_f$ the risk-premium _(the extra return investors demand for picking risky assets over the risk-free)._

Moreover, Fama argued that any test of EMH is actually testing two things at once:

- That the market is efficient.
- That the model is a correct way to measure risk.

<a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Shiller" target="_blank">Bob Shiller</a>, on the other hand, published in 1981 the paper <a href="https://www.aeaweb.org/aer/top20/71.3.421-436.pdf" target="_blank">Do stock prices move too much to be justified by subsequent changed in dividends?</a> that, answering _Yes!_ to the question, claims that the EMH was one of the _remarkable errors_ in the history of economics.

Regarding Fama's theories, Shiller opinion was something like...
- _I think that maybe he has a cognitive dissonance_
- _It's like being a Catholic priest and then discovering that God doesn't exist [...], you can't deal with that, you've got to somehow rationalise it._

In particular, Shiller’s empirical result is that stock prices move way more than the dividends.<br/>
If a stock price is truly the _"discounted value of all future dividends",_ it should be stable because dividends don't change that much, but Shiller showed the opposite using the <b>Cyclically Adjusted Price-to-Earnings</b>(CAPE) ratio.

In the following years, empirical data <b>gave credit to Shiller's critic.</b>

But what does the CAPM actually say?
- how the market should price financial assets in function of the risk 
- it shows that a complete market _(perfect information and full pricing)_ will lead to a market equilibrium with optimal risk allocation

However, the CAPM relies on very heavy and simplifying assumptions, some are technical and could be relaxed, others are foundamental and not often realistic:
- <b>the complete market assumption</b>:
    - it would require infinitely many assets
    - it would require complex products _(derivatives)_
    - complexity implies expertise and potential moral hazard 
- <b>other assumptions are unrealistic</b>:
    - investors can lend and borrow unlimited money under risk free rates 
    - all assets are divisible and liquid 
    - all agents have identical beliefs

_So, was Fama simply wrong?_
- Not really, he didn't give up and he refined the model, abandoning CAPM and replacing it with a <b>multi-factor risk model</b>
- Even Shiller still endorses a _loose_ version of it.
- For Shiller, asset prices can _overshoot_ in the short term (due to emotion and irrationality), but they show _reversion to the mean_ in the long period.
- Additionally, according to Shiller markets are still the best tools we have for aggregating information.

To sum up, the Fama-Shiller case is typical from a social science and economics: two theories can contradict each other and <b>still retain intellectual value</b>.
<br/>_"All Models are wrong, but some are usesul"_ applies perfectly here: Fama gave an excellent structure to think the financial markets and, even if its work is imperfect, it's yet a solid starting point.

## <a id="0.2"><b>0.2 Introduction to finance and investment planning</b></a>

A <b>financial system</b> is a set of institutions and markets that have the primary purpose of allowing the _desynchronization_ of income and consumption.

A financial system allows to match different financial needs of different agents in _two dimensions_:
- <b>Time</b> (borrow and save):
    - wish of continuousu consumption vs discrete income stream 
    - smooth consumption of time/the life-cycle
- <b>Risk</b> (diversify, insure, hedge):
    - smooth consumption across state of nature and possible market scenarios.

<b>Asset</b>: something valuable with well-defined property rights _(a contract, a good)_.

<b>Real Asset</b>: something that has an _intrinsic_ value due to its substance and properties.

<b>Financial Asset</b>: something that has _NO intrinsic material value_ but which can be traded.

_(Note that the distinction between financial and non-financial asset is not binary and well defined: for example, "is gold a financial or physical/real asset?")_

Financial assets can be caterogized as follows:
- <b>Riskless assets</b>: assets whose future values can be known with certainty _(a government bond that yields 100 CHF in 5 years, assuming no default)_
- <b>Risky assets</b>: assets whose future value may depend on some events _(insurance, stocks, options)_
- <b>Derivatives</b> are risky assets whose future values depend on the price of other assets.

A <b>bond</b> is often considered a riskless asset _(depending on the issuer)_.

A <b>zero-coupon (ZC) bond with maturity $t$</b> yields no payment before period $t$ and pays $1$ in period $t$.<br/>They can vary _only_ in terms of <b>maturity</b>.

Denote by $p_{0,t}$ the price of a ZC bond with maturity $t$ at time $0$ _(usually the first subscript is current time, the second the maturity)_. In absence of arbitrage, we have:

$$
p_{0,0} = 1
\\

p_{0,t} = \frac{p_{0,t-1}}{(1 + r_t)}

$$


where $r_t$ is the <b>discrete per period forward interest rate</b> between $t-1$ and $t$. By iteration we have:

$$
p_{0,t} = \frac{1}{\prod^t_{i = 1}(1 + r_i)}
$$

In relation to this, we can distinguish:
- <b>Discounting</b>: the process of determining the _prevent value_ of a future payment $x_t$ at time $t > 0$:

$$
PV(x_t) =  \frac{x_t}{\prod^t_{i = 1}(1 + r_i)}
$$
- <b>Compounding</b>: the process of determining the current value at time $t$ _(in the future)_ of a monetary amount $x_0$ invested at $t = 0$
$$

CV_t(x_0) = \prod_{i = 1}^t (1 +r_i) \cdot x_0
$$

The <b>discrete sport interest rate</b> $\bar r_t$, for a zerou coupon bond with maturity $t$ such that:

$$
p_{0,t} = \frac{p_{0,0}}{(1 + \bar r_t)^t}
$$

Or, in other words, the spot rate is the _geometric average_ of the per period forward rate of interest:

$$
\frac{1}{(1 + \bar r_t)^t} = \frac{1}{\prod^t_{i = 1}(1 + r_i)} \implies 1 + \bar r_t = \Big[\prod^t_{i = 1}(1 + r_i)\Big]^{1/t}
$$


We now consider a bond with price $p_0$ in $t = 0$ that yields a series of positive <b>coupon payments</b> $a_t$ until the maturity date, so for $t = 1, \dot T$, plus the final payment of <b>par value</b> in $t = T$.

Its <b>yield to maturity</b>(YTM) is the unique rate $y$ for which the present value of these payments is equal to $p_0$:

$$
p_0 = \sum_{t = 1}^T \frac{a_t}{(1 + y)^t} + \frac{Par}{(1 + y)^T}
$$





---

The same analysis can be formulated in continuous time, in this case the <b>instantaneous forward interest rate </b> is:

$$
p(t + dt) \approx \frac{p(t)}{1 + r(t)dt}
$$

or, equivalently:

$$
\frac{p(t + dt) - p(t)}{p(t)} \approx - r(t)dt
$$

By integration, we get:

$$
p(t) = p(0)e^{-\int^t_0 r(\tau)d \tau} = e^{-\int_0^t r(\tau) d\tau}
$$

This interest rate $r(\tau)$ is called _"instantanenous"_ since it represents the evolution of the bond's price process for a infinitesimally small period of time $dt$.

Moreover, in continuous time we can define:

$$
PV(x) = \int_0^{\infty} e^ {-\int_0^t r(\tau) d \tau} x(t)dt
$$

The <b>continuously compounended forward interest rate </b> between $t_1, t_2$, denoted by $\bar r_{t_1}(t_2)$ is defined as:

$$
p(t_2) = p(t_1) e^{-(t_2 - t_1)\bar r_{t_1}(t_2)}
$$

$$
\bar r_{t_1}(t_2) = \frac{1}{t_2 - t_1} \log \Big [ \frac{p(t_1)}{p(t_2) }\Big] = \frac{1}{t_2 - t_1} \int^{t_2}_{t_1}r(\tau) d\tau

$$


The <b>continuously compounded spot interest rate </b> for a bond with maturity $T$ is:

$$
\bar r_0 (t) = \frac{1}{t} \int^t_0 r(\tau) d\tau
$$

which is the _arithmetic average_ of the instantaneous forward interest rates between $0,t$.

The function $t \to \bar r_0(t)$ provides the <b>yield curve</b>.

---

We now consider <b>risky assets</b>. To evalute an <b>uncertain stream </b> of future payments, we still used an additive process:

$$
\mathbb{E}[PV(x)] = \sum_{t = 1}^{+ \infty} \frac{\mathbb{E}[x_t]}{ \prod^t_{i = 1}(1 +r_i)} = \sum_{t = 1}^{+ \infty} \frac{\mathbb{E}[x_t]}{ \prod^t_{i = 1}(1 +r_t^f + \pi_t)}
$$

where the second formulation is needed if we want risk to be taken into account, and we write $r_i = r^r_t + \pi_t$, where $r^f_t$ is the risk-free rate and $\pi_t$ the risk premium.

## <a id="1"><b>1. Options and financial strategies</b></a>

<b>Derivatives</b> are assets whose values mechanically depend on the values of other financial assets (the <b>underlying</b>). <br/>For example:
- <b>Forward contracts</b>:  _OBLIGATION_ to purchase _(long position)_ or sell _(short position)_ the underlying at a specified future price at a specified delivery date.
- <b>Options contracts</b>: _RIGHT_ to purchase or sell a specified amount of the underlying at a specified _exercise price_ at or before a specified _expiration date_.

Options offer an advantage: the transaction does not have to occur if it not profitable for the owner of the option. This advantage comes at a price:
- Forwards are entered at _NO cost_
- Options are purchased or sold at positive price that the represents the _cost of the right_ to buy/sell.
- Selling (or _writing_) an option implies an obligation (if the option is exericed, the seller must sell or buy the stock and incurs a loss) $\to$ the seller receives a compensation.

Derivatives can help shaping the risk exposure:
- <b>Hedging</b> means insuring against market price volatility
- <b>Speculation</b> means expoiting market price volatility
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Forward Contract Payoff</b>

- $X$ agreed delivery price
- $S_t$ spot market price of the underlying at maturity $T$

Profits at maturity $T$ are:
- Payoff long position $ = S_t - X$
- Payoff short position $ = X - S_t$

For this reason, forwards are great for <b>speculation</b>. At a goiven time $t$, an unerlying has a price $S_t$, with <b>no clear evolution</b> for the future. If a speculator expectes:
- $S_T > S_t \implies$ long forward with $X < S_T$
- $S_T < S_t \implies$ short forward with $X > S_T$

On the other hand, forwards are used in hedging to <b>avoid risks</b>.<br/>
Suppore, _for example_, that Giacomo's GmbH needs to sell an activity (_example: some running shoes_) at maturity $T$, with value $S_T$ at given time $T$.
<br/>Giacomo faces then a risk management problem: how can he insure against price fluctuations of the running shoes?

Solution: use the forward contract to construct a <b>hedged portfolio</b> that fixes the profits _from the future sale_ at a desired level $\pi^*$:

$$
\pi^* = S_T + (X - S_T) = X
$$

To fix the profits at $\pi^* = X$, Giacomo can open a <b>short position</b> on the forward contract with delivery price $X$.

_(The opposite - long forward contract - applies if you need to buy and you want to avoid risk)_.

## <a id="2"><b>2. Option valuation</b></a>

In this chapter we focus on <b>European options</b>, please refer to the above sections for formal definition.

We also use the following notation:
- $S_0$: price of the underlying asset at toime $t = 0$
- $C_0, P_0$: prices (premia) of the call and the put option at time $t = 0$.
- $r_f$: rate of the risk-free asset.

An <b>Arbitrage Opportunity</b> is defined as a <b>financial strategy</b> that yields a (sure) cash flow $A_0 \ge 0$ at time $0$ and a cash-flow $A_T \ge 0$ at time $T$ and there is at least one stat of the word where one inequality is strict.

- $A_0 > 0, A_T \ge$ almost surely is an abirtrage 
- $A_0 \ge 0, A_t \ge 0$ almot surely with $\text{Prob}[A_T > 0]>0$ is an arbitrage

In this chapter, we assume that the market is arbitrage-free _(the market clears out arbitrage opportunities)_.

---
<br/>
<b>Price Bounds on call options (European, no dividend)</b>
- Upper Bound: $C_0 \le S_0$

_Proof:_ <br/>
Consider two portfolios:
- Portfolio $A$: $1$ call + cash $PV(X)
    - Price in $t = 0$: $ [C_0 + PV(X)]
    - Value in $T$: $X + \max [S_t. X, 0] = \max [S_t, X]

- Portoflio $B$: $1$ stock $S_0$
    - Price in $t = 0: S_0$
    - Value in $T$: $S_T$
<br/>
Portfolio $A$ is never less valuable than portfolio in $T$, so $A$ cannot be less expensive in $t = 0$, otherwise we have an arbitrage opportunity, therefore we conclude:

$$
C_0 + PV(X) \ge S_0
$$

and $C_0 \ge 0$ since the call is an option.

- Lower Boud:  $C_0 \max [S_0 - PV(X), 0]$ where $PV$ is the present value operator. 


<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Price Bounds on put options (European, no dividend)</b>
<br/>
- Upper boud: $P_0 \le PV(X)$
- Lower Bound: $P_0 \ge \max [PV(X) - S_0, 0]$

_Proof:_<br/>
Consider two portfolios:
- Portfoio $C$: buy $1$ put + $1$ stock
    - Price in  $t = 0$: $C_0 + S_0$
    - Value in $T$: $\max(X - S_T, 0) + S_T = \max [S_t, X$] 
- Portfolio $D$: cash $PV(X)
    - Price in $t = 0$: $PV(X)$ 
    - Value  in $T$: $X$ 

$C$ is never less valuable than $D$, so $C$ cannot be less expensive in $t = 0$, otherwise we have an arbitrage opportunity, therefore:

$$
P_0 + S_0 \ge PV(X)
$$

and we add $P_0 \ge 0$ since the put is an option.

---

<br/>
<b>The Put-Call Parity Theorem</b>

$$
S_0 + P_0 = \frac{X}{(1 + r_f)^T} + C_0
$$

To understand this, consider the following:
- Portfolio $A$: $1$ stock and $1$ put 
- Portfolio $B$: $\frac{X}{(1 + r_f)^T}$ bonds and $1$ call

the values of the two portfolio are equal in $t =T$, therefore they have to be equal also in $t = 0$, leading to the Put-Call parity formula.

---
<br/>
<b>Option Valutation</b>

Determinants of the value _(price/premium)_ of a call option $C_0$ _(European, no dividend)_:
- stock price $S_0$ (+), exercise price $K$ (-)
- volatility of underlying stock (+) _(intuition: volatility increases the probability that the stock value at expiration is greater than $K$, increasing potential gains)_.
- time to expiration $T-t$ (+) _(intuition: the more time to expiration, the higher the range od stock price increases, similar to volatility),  also, the present value of exercise price falls)_
- interest rate (+) _(intuition: present value of exercise price falls)_.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Two-state Option Valuation Model </b>

Consider a market with:
- a stock that currently trades at $S_0$, and which value in $t = 1$ can be either $S_1^u, S_1^d$, with $S_1^u > S_1^d$
- call option on the stock with exercise price $X \in (S_1^d, S_1^u)$ and expiration date $T = 1$ 
- a risk free asset with yearly interest rate $r_f$

Then it is possible to replicate the payoffs of the call using a combination of the risk-free asset and the stock. The replicating strategy can be found as:

$$
\begin{cases}
\alpha S_1^u + \beta (1 + r_f) = S_1^u - X \\
\alpha S_1^d + \beta (1 + r_f)= 0


\end{cases}
$$
which gives:
$$
\alpha = \frac{S_1^u - X}{S_1^u - S_1^d}, \quad \beta = \frac{(S_1^u - X)S_1^d}{(1 + r_f)(S_1^d - S_1^u)}
$$

In absence of arbitrage, we then conclude: $C_0 = \alpha S_0 + \beta$.

We can now apply the same reasoning in <b>continuous-time</b>. Considering a give time range $T$, we split the time range in _(infinitely many)_ periods $dt$ and we assume that in any period the stock price is multiplied by either $(1 +\alpha dt), (1 + \beta dt)$.
<br/>We assume the distribution of the stock price at time $T$ is <b>log-normal</b>. <br/> Applying _(infinitely many times)_ the two state valuation model makes it possible to compute the value of the call.

---
<br/>
<b>Black-Scholes Formula</b>

Formally, the value of the stock at time $t$ denoted $S(t)$ is assume to vary as follows:

$$
dS(t) = \mu S(dt) + \sigma S(t) dB(t)
$$

where $B(t)$ is a standard <b>Brownian Motion</b>, so for any $y$ the distribution of $S(t)$ is log-normal.

We then assume:
- constant risk-free interest rate 
- no arbitrage 

and we get the <b>famous</b> Black-Scholes formula:

$$
C_0 = S_0 N(d_1) - X e^{-rT} N (d_2)
$$

- $d_1 = \frac{\ln (S_0 / X) + (r + \sigma^2/2)T}{\sigma \sqrt(T)} $

- $d_2 = d_1- \sigma \sqrt(T)$

- $N(d)$ probability that a random draw from a standard normal distribution will be less than $d$
- $r$: risk free interest rate, _(annualized continuously compounded rate with same maurity as the expiration o the option, note that is different from $r_f$)_,
- $\sigma$: standard deviation of the annualized continuously compounded rate of return of the stock.

_Intuition: $N(d)$ can be interpreted as the risk-adjusted probabilities that the call option will expisre in-the-money ($X < S_T$)_

## <a id="3"><b>3. Pricing by arbitrage</b></a>

We consider two periods: _ex-ante, ex-post_, denoted by $t = 0,1$ and $\Omega$ contingent states at time $t = 1: \omega \in {1, ... \Omega}$.
<br/>There are $K$ assets available at time $t = 0: k \in {1, ... K}$.
- $a_k^{\omega}$ is the value of asset $k$ in state $\omega$ at time $t = 1$
- we denote $p_k$ the price of asset $k$ at time $t = 0$ 
- a <b>market </b> is the data: $(a_k^{\omega}, p_k)$

A <b>portfolio</b> $(z_k)$ is a vector of asset quantities: $z_k$ is the quantit of asset $k$ in the portfolio.
- the price of portolio is: $\sum_k p_k z_k$
- the value of the portfolio in state $w$ is: $\sum_k a_k{\omega} z_k$

Given now a market with:

$$

a = \begin{pmatrix}
a_1^1 & \dots & a_1^\Omega \\
\vdots & & \vdots \\
a_K^1 & \dots & a_K^\Omega
\end{pmatrix} \text{ and prices } p = \begin{pmatrix}
p_1 \\
\vdots \\
p_K
\end{pmatrix}

$$

a portfolio with asset quantities $(z_1, ... z_k)$ has price:

$$
(z_1, \dots, z_K) \begin{pmatrix}
p_1 \\
\vdots \\
p_K
\end{pmatrix} 
$$
and values 
$$
 (z_1, \dots, z_K) \begin{pmatrix}
a_1^1 & \dots & a_1^\Omega \\
\vdots & & \vdots \\
a_K^1 & \dots & a_K^\Omega
\end{pmatrix}.
$$

We then introduce the followig definitions:
- an asset is <b>risk-free</b> if $a_k^{\omega}$ is independent of $w$ 
- an asset $k$ can be replicated by a subset of assets, indexed $i \in S$, if there exists a costant $(z_i)$ such that:

$$
a_k^{\omega} = \sum_{i \in S, i \neq k} z_i a_i^{w} \quad \forall w
$$

_(in such cas the asset $k$ is called <b>redudant</b>)_.

A market $(a_l^{\omega}, p_k)$ is <b>complete</b> if for any asset $b^{\omega}$ there exists a costant $(z_k)$ such that:

$$
b^{\omega} = \sum_k z_k a_k^{\omega}
$$

On the other hand, markets are <b>complete</b> when all revenue configurations are replicable through some portfolio.

<b>Consequences of complete markets</b>:
- there are at least as many assets at the states: $| \Omega| \le K$
- a market is complete if and only if the payoff matrix $a$ ith dimension $(K \times \Omega)$ has rank $\Omega$.
- if a marekt is complete with $K > \Omega$, there are $\Omega$ independent assets, then we can eliminate $K - \Omega$ assets which are linear combinations of the independent ones.
<br/>
---
<br/>
An <b>arbitrage portfolio</b> is a portfolio $(z_k)$ such that:

$$
\sum_k a_k^{w} z_k \ge 0\quad \forall \omega
\\
\sum_k p_k z_k \le 0
$$
at least one of these $\Omega + 1$ inequalities being strict.

A market is <b>arbitrage free</b> if there is no arbitrage portfolio.

A marekt $(a_k^{w}, p_k)$ without arbitrage opportunity satisfies the <b>Law of One Price:</b><br/>
_If two assets $i,j$ satisfy $a_i^{\omega} = a_j^{\omega}$ for every state $\omega$, then the two assets have the same price $p_i = p_j$._

<b>Proof</b>

Assume that $p_i > p_j$, then the portfolio such that $z_i = -1, z_j = 1, z_k = 0$ for $k \neq i,j$ satisfies the following:
$$
\sum_k a_k^{w}z_k = 0\\

\sum_k p_k z_k = p_j - p_i < 0
$$

that is an arbitrage portfolio.

---
<br/>
<b>The No-Arbitrage Theorem</b>

A market $(a_k^{w}, p_k)$ is arbitrage-free is and only if there exists a vector $q = (q_1 ... q_{\Omega}) \in \R^{\Omega}$ such that:

- $q^{\omega} > 0$ for every state 
- $p_k = \sum_{\omega} a_k^{\omega} q_{\omega}$ for every assets 

$q$ is then called <b>state-price vector</b>.

In matrix form, the state-price vector with positive components solves:

$$
\begin{pmatrix} p_1 \\ .\\.\\ p_k\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1^1 \quad ... \quad a_1^{\Omega}\\.\\.\\ a_K^1 \quad ... \quad a_K^{\Omega}\end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}  q_1 \\.\\.\\ q_{\Omega}\end{pmatrix}
$$

_There may exists several state price vectors in a market_.

_Intuition_:
- the state price ($q_\omega$) is simply the price you must pay today to receive that $1$ in state $\omega$ tomorrow _(regardless of the asset)_.
- the state-price vector is just the collection of these prices for every possible future state.
- $q$ doesnt tell you  which assets to buy to receive $1$
- once you have the state-price vector $q$, you can price any asset $k$ using the sum above _(the price of an asset $k$ equals the sum of its payoff in each state weighted by the state prices)_ 


<br/>

---

<br/>
<b>Risk Neutral Probability</b>

Assume a state price vector $(q_{\omega})$.

Denote $\pi^*_{\omega} = \frac{q_w}{\sum_{\omega} q_{\omega}} > 0$, with $\sum_{\omega} \pi^*_{\omega} = 1$. 
<br/>Therefore $\pi^*_{\omega}$ can be interpreted as a probability. In particular, $(\pi^*_{\omega})$ is called <b>the risk neutral probability.</b>
- if a market admits several state price vectors, it admits also as many risk neutral probabilities.

If we define: $r_k^{\omega} = \frac{a_k^{\omega}}{p_k}$ the return of asset $k$ in state $\omega$, using $p_k = \sum_{\omega} a_k^{\omega}q_{\omega}$:


$$
E^*[r_{k\omega}] = \sum_{\omega} \pi_\omega^* r_{k\omega}
$$

If we substitute the definition of $\pi_\omega^* = \frac{q_\omega}{\sum_j q_j}$:

$$
E^*[r_{k\omega}] = \sum_{\omega} \left( \frac{q_\omega}{\sum_j q_j} \right) \frac{a_{k\omega}}{p_k}
$$

If we extract  the constants ($p_k$ and the total sum of state prices) out of the summation:

$$

E^*[r_{k\omega}] = \frac{1}{p_k \sum_{\omega} q_{\omega}} \sum_{\omega} q_\omega a_{k\omega}
$$

Since $\sum_{\omega} q_\omega a_{k\omega}$ is exactly the definition of $p_k$, the terms cancel out:

$$
E^*[r_{k\omega}] = \frac{1}{\sum_{\omega} q_{\omega}}

$$

$$
\sum_{\omega}  \pi^*_{\omega}  r_k^{\omega} = \mathbb{E}^*_{\omega} [r_k^{\omega}] = \frac{1}{\sum_{\omega}q_{\omega}}

$$

<b>All asset returns have the same-risk neutral expectation.</b>. _(This does not imply that all assets have the same returns expectactions according on physical probability)_.

---


For any state $\omega_0$, the corresponding <b>Arrow-Debreu</b> security, denoted by $a_{\omega_0}^{\omega}% is the asset with values given, for all $\omega$, by:

$$
a_{\omega_0}^{\omega} = 1 _{\omega = \omega_0}
$$

- If the market is arbitrage free and $q$ is a state-price vectoro, then the price of $a_{\omega_0}$ is $q_0$.
- A market that contains all Arrow-Debreu securities is complete.

_Intuition: a contract that pays exactly $1$ if a specific state of the world and $0$ in every other possible scenario._

Assume now that a market includes a risk-free asset that pays $1$ in all state of the world and denote by $p_0$ its price ($p_0 = \sum q_{\omega}$) and $r_f = 1 / p_0$, then:
- for all assets $k$: $\mathbb{E}_{\pi^*}[r_k^{\omega}] = r_f$
- if the market contains the Arrow-Debreu security $a_{\omega_0}$, its price is $p_0 \pi^*_{\omega_0}$
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Arbitrage Bounds</b>

Given an arbitrage-free market $M$, define $Q_M$ the set of state price vectors for this market:
- $Q_M$ is a convex set 
- $Q_M$ empty $\iff M$ offers arbitrage opportunities 
- $Q_M$ is reduced to a point $\iff$ M is complete and arbitrage-free 
- if $q, q' \in Q_M$, then $q - q' \perp M$, that means:

$$
\sum_{\omega} (q_{\omega} - q'_{\omega}) a_k^{\omega} = 0 \quad \forall k
$$

---
<br/>

<b>Theorem (Arbitrage Bounds)</b>

For amy claim $c = (c_1, ... c_{\Omega})$, _not necessarily replicable_ in $M$, the set of prices $p_c$ for such a claim to not generage arbitrage opportunities is:

$$
\{ p_c \quad|\quad  p_c = \sum_{\omega} q_{\omega} c_{\omega} \text{ for some } q \in Q_M \}
$$
- this set is singleton $\iff$ $c$ is replicable in $M$
- if $c$ is not replicable, the adding it to the market with a price within arbitrage bounds will reduce $Q_M$ _(dimension decreases by one)_

<b>A Complete Market</b>

We consider two assets _(Arrow-Debreu Securities)_ in a two-states world:
- A <b>risky</b> asset $1$:
    - it can be purchased/sold ex-ante at $p_1$
    - ex-post it pays $1$ for $w = 1$ and zero otherwise 
- A <b>risky</b> asset $2$:
    - it can purchased/sold ex-ante at $p_2$
    - ex-post it pays $2$ for $w = 2$ and zero otherwise.

$z_A^1, z_B^1, z_A^2, z_B^2$denote the quantities of assets for agents $A,B$ _(they can be positive or negative depending on purchase/sell)_.

Ex-ante, each agent has zero initial wealth and a budget constraint:

$$
p_1 z_i^1 + p_2 z_i^2 = 0
$$

Ex-post, assets' pay-offs and income and realize and agent $i$ consumes $w_i^i + z_i^i$.

Therefore, agent $i$ solves the following optimization problem:

$$
\max_{z_i^1, z_i^2} U^i (w_i^1 + z_i^1, w_i^2+ z_i^2) \quad p_1 z_i^1 + p_2 z_i^2 = 0
$$

This problem generates the demand functions:

$$
z_i^2(w_i^1, w_i^2, p_1, p_2), \quad z_i^2(w_i^1,w_i^2, p_1,p_2)
$$

<b>Market Clearing</b>
<br/>

A market is balanced _(cleared)_ when demand equals supply for all assets (<b>Market Clearing Conditions</b>):

$$
z_A^1 + z_B^1 = 0\\
z_A^2 + z_B^2 = 0
$$

![No market clearing](/images/notes/financial-econ/no-market-clearing.png)

At equilibriu, we thenm have 2 equations in 2 unkwnowns:

$$
z_A^1(w_A^1, w_A^2, p_1, p_2) + z_B^1(w_B^1, w_B^2, p_1, p_2) = 0
z_A^2(w_A^1, w_A^2, p_1, p_2) + z_B^2(w_B^1, w_B^2, p_1, p_2) = 0
$$

Considering also the budget constraints, the previous two equations are then equal, so we have $1$ independent equation and $2$ unknowns.<br/>
$\implies$ <b>Equilibrium prices are determined up to a multiplicative scalar</b>.

## <a id="5"><b>5. Choice under uncertainty</b></a>

Consider $Z$ a set of possible consequences. Let $\Omega = \{ 1, 2, |\Omega| \}$ the set of possible states of the world and $L(\Omega)$ the set of lotteris with consequences in $Z$.

A lottery is a list of pairs $(x^{\omega}, \pi_{\omega})$ where $\pi_{\omega}$ gives the probability that $\omega$ will occur, and $x^{\omega}$ is the outcome.

_Example: $((0,0.5); (1,0.5))$ is the lottey that pays $0,1$ with equal probabilities._

A <b>compound lottery</b> is a lottery whose prizes are themselves lotteries.

<b>Mixture operation</b>

Consider two lotteries $L, L'$.<br/>
The <b>compound lottery</b> with $\lambda \in [0,1]$ corresponds to the lottery which consists in playing the first lottery with probability $\lambda$ and the second with probability $(1-\lambda)$.

The resulting lottery is denoted by $(\lambda L + (1-\lambda)L')$. The operation <b>+</b> is called a <b>mixture operation</b>.

_Example:_
$$
L = ((0,p);(1, 1-p))\\
L' = ((1,q); (2, 1-q))\\
\lambda L + (1-\lambda)L' = \Big( (0, \lambda p); (1; \lambda(1-p)+ (1-\lambda)q); (2, (1-\lambda)(1-q)) \Big)
$$

<div align = "center">
![Mixture](/images/notes/financial-econ/mixture.png)
</div>

---
<br/>

<b>Preference Relation</b>

We want to define a theory of preference defined over $L(Z)$.

A _relation of preferences_ $\ge$ on $L(Z)$ is a binary relation which is:

1.<b>Complete</b>
    - for any $L, L' \in L(Z)$, we have $L \ge L' $ or $L' \ge L$
2. <b>Transitive</b>
    - $L_1 \ge L_2$ and $L_2 \ge L_3 \implies L_1 \ge L_3$

<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Axioms of Expected Utility Theory</b>

<b>Von Neuman and Morgenstern</b> suggested to assume the following about the relation of preference:
- <b>Independence</b>: for all $L, L', L"$ and $\lambda \in (0,1)$:

$$
L \ge L' \iff \lambda L + (1 - \lambda)L" \ge \lambda L' + (1 - \lambda)L"
$$

- <b>Continuity</b>: if $L \ge L' \ge L"$, then there exists $\lambda \in [0,1]$ such that $L' \sim \lambda L + (1 - \lambda)L"$.

_Graphically, the independence axiom:_
<div align = "center">
![Mixture](/images/notes/financial-econ/ind-axiom.png)
</div>

---

<br/>
A relation of preferences is represented by a utility function $V: L(Z) \to \R$ if and only if: $L \ge L' \iff V(L) \ge V(L')$.

<b>Theorem (Von Neuman-Morgenstern)</b>

If $\ge$ is a preference relation on $L(Z)$ that satisfies the previous axioms, then there exists $u : Z \to \R$ such that:

$$
(x^{\omega}, \pi_{\omega}) \ge (\tilde x^{\omega}, \tilde \pi_{\omega}) \iff \sum_{\omega} \pi_{\omega} u (x^{\omega}) \ge \sum_{\omega} \tilde \pi_{\omega} u (\tilde x^{\omega})
$$

that can also be rewritten as: 
$$
\mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\omega}}[u(x^{\omega})] \ge \mathbb{E}_{\tilde \pi_{\omega}}[u(\tilde x^{\omega})]
$$

In thi case, we say that _the agent is said to have "VNM preferences"_.

A little note on terminology:
- $V: L(Z) \to \R$ is the utilility function _(expected utility function)_.
- $u: Z \to \R$ is just a function that appears when looking at the structure of $V$ _(utility index)_. Furthermore, for the rest of the chapters, we assume that$u$ is strictly <b>increasing</b> and <b>twice continuously differentiable</b>.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Risk Aversion</b>

We denote the average of lottery $L$ by $\mathbb{E}_[x] = \sum_{\omega} \pi_{\omega}x^{\omega}$.<br/>We represent with $\delta_{\mathbb{E}_[x]}$ the <b>degenerate lottery</b> that gives the expected payoff of $L$ with probability one.

An agent is said to be <b>risk averse </b>if and only if for all $L \in L(Z)$:

$$
\delta_{\mathbb{E}_[x]} \ge L
$$

An agent with VNM preferences is <b>risk averse</b> if and only if her utility index is concave: $u'\ge0, u"\le0$.


To prove it, we consider the _Jensen Inequality_: if a function $u$ is concave, then we have:

$$
\mathbb{E}_L[u(x)] \le u(\mathbb{E}_L[x])
$$

---

<br/>
_Recall:_ for any lottery $L$ we can define the <b>cumulative distribution function</b> $F_L: \R \to [0,1]$ as:

$$
F_L(x) = \mathbb{P}(L \le x)
$$

<b>Second-order Stochastic Dominance</b>

A lottery $L$ is said to second-order stochastically dominates $L' (\text{SOSD})$ if and only if: $\mathbb{E}_L[x] \ge \mathbb{E}_L'[x]$ and:

$$
\int_{-\infty}^y F_L(x)dx \le \int_{-\infty}^yF_{L'}(x)dx \quad \forall x
$$
_(Note that the first order stochastic dominace implies the second one)_

Consider $L_1, L_2$ with the same mean and with density functions $f_1(x), f_2(x)$. $L_2$ is said to be a <b>mean preserving spread </b> of $L_1$ if there exists an interval $I$ such tat:

f_2(x) \le f_1(x) \quad \forall x \in I

<b>Increases in Risk</b>

There are different ways to define an _increase in risk_:
- $L$ is riskier than $L'$ if $L' \text{ SOSD } L$ _($L'$ second ordder stochastically dominates $L$)_. 
- $L$ is riskier than $L'$ id $L$ is a _mean preserving_ spread of $L'$.
- _Adding a white noise:_ $L$ is riskier than $L'$ if $L = L' + \epsilon$, where $\mathbb{E}[\epsilon] = 0$. _(Note that $L, \epsilon$ do not neet to be independetly distriunted)_.

An agent with VNM preferences dislikes increases in risk if and only if her utility index $u$ is concave.

<br/>
<b>Quantifying Risk Aversion - Certainty Equivalent</b>

For a lottery $L$, its certainty equivalent is amount $e_L \in \R$ such that: $\delta_{e_L} \sim L$ or, in other terms:

$$
u(e_L) = \mathbb{E}_L[u(x)]
$$

_Intuition: $e_L$ is the amount of momey for which the individual is indifferent between playing $L$ and a fixed amount of momney_.
- For a concave utility _(risk averse agent):_ $\mathbb{E}_L[u(x)] - e_L > 0$
- For a linear utility _(risk neutral agent):_ $\mathbb{E}_L[u(x)] = e_L$
<br/>
<b>Risk Premium</b>

For any lottery $L$ with payoff $X$, define the **certainty equivalent** $e_L$ as:

$$u(e_L) = \mathbb{E}_L[u(X)]$$

The **risk premium** is then:

$$\pi_L = \mathbb{E}_L[X] - e_L$$

For a VNM agent, this is equivalent to:

$$u(\mathbb{E}_L[X]-\pi_L) = \mathbb{E}_L[u(X)]$$
- A risk neutral individual has zero risk premium
- A _(strictly)_ risk averge agent associates a positive risk premium to any _non-degenerate_ lottery.
<div align = "center">
![Risk Premium ](/images/notes/financial-econ/risk-premium.png)
</div>

<b>Absolute Risk Premium for small risks</b>

Consider $x \in \R$, $\epsilon$ small and the following lottery:

$$
L = x + \tilde \epsilon, \quad \mathbb{E}[\tilde \epsilon ] = 0
$$

Using Taylor expansions, we can show that the <b>(absolute) risk premium</b> $\pi_a$ when $\epsilon$ is small _(Arrow-Pratt approximation)_ is such :

$$
\pi_a \approx -\frac{1}{2} \frac{u''(x)}{u'(x)} \mathbb{E}[\tilde \epsilon^2]
$$

The <b>absolute ris aversion coefficient</b> $R_a(x)$ for wealth level $x$ is defined as:

$$
R_a(x) = -\frac{u''(x)}{u'(x)}
$$

For small risks of the form $x + \tilde \epsilon$:

$$
\pi_a \approx \frac{1}{2}R_a (x)\mathbb{E}[\tilde \epsilon ^2]
$$

_Note that $\mathbb{E}[\tilde \epsilon ^2]$ is the variance of the risk._

<b>Relative Risk premium for small risks</b>

Consider $x \in \R$ and the same lottery as before, $L + \tilde \epsilon$. <br/>We can define the <b>relative risk premium</b> $\pi_r$ by:

$$
(1 + \tilde \epsilon) x \sim (1 + \pi_r)x
$$

and, using the Taylor expansion when $\tilde \epsilon$ is small:

$$
\pi_r \approx - \frac{1}{2}\frac{x u''(x)}{u'(x)}\mathbb{E}[\tilde \epsilon ^2]
$$

The <b>relative risk aversion coefficient</b> $R_r(x)$ for wealth level $x$ is then define as:

$$
R_r(x) = -\frac{x u''(x)}{u'(x)} = x R_a(x)

$$

For a small risk of the form $(1 + \tilde \epsilon)x$:

$$
\pi_r \approx \frac{1}{2}R_r(x)\mathbb{E}[\tilde \epsilon ^2]
$$

<b>Comparing Risk Aversion</b>

For two agents $A, B$ all the following sentences are equivalent:
- $\forall L$ the certainty equivalent of $A$ is smaller or equal than the one of $B$.
- $\forall L$ the risk premium for $B$ is smaller or equal than the one for $A$
- $u_A$ is more concave than $u_B$: there exists and <b>increasing concave</b> functio $\phi$ such that: $u_A = \phi (u_B)$.
- $\forall x$ we have:

$$
-\frac{u''_A(x)}{u'_A(x)} \ge - \frac{u''_B(x)}{u'_B(x)}
$$
-$A$ is at least as risk averse as $B$

<b>Common Classes of VNM utility indices</b>
<br/>
- <b>Costant Absolute Return Aversion (CARA)</b>
$$
u_{\lambda}(x) = \frac{1 - e^{-\lambda x}}{\lambda}, \quad u_0 = x
$$
- <b>Constant Relative Risk Aversion (CRRA)</b>
$$
u_{\gamma}(x) = \frac{x^{1-\gamma}}{1-\gamma},\quad 

\forall \gamma >0, \gamma \neq 1\quad u_1 = \log(x)
$$
- <b>Hyperbolic Absolute Risk Aversion (HARA)</b>
$$
u(x) = \frac{\gamma}{1 - \gamma}(b + x/\gamma)^{1-\gamma}, \quad b \ge 0 \implies R_a = \frac{\gamma}{x + b \gamma} 

$$
- <b>Quadratic Utility Index</b>
$$
u(x) = x - \frac{\beta}{2}x^2, \quad R_a = \frac{\beta}{1 - \beta x}
$$

Note that for a quadratic utility index:
$$
\mathbb{E}[u(x)] = \mathbb{E}[x] - \frac{\beta}{2}\mathbb{E}[x^2] = \bar x - \frac{\beta}{2}\bar x^2 - \frac{\beta}{2}\text{Var}(x)
$$

then the utility of a lottery only depends on its mean and variance.

## <a id="6"><b>6. Demand for risk</b></a>

<b>Static Portfolio Choice - Setting Up the Problem</b>

Consider an agent with wealth $W$ who can invest at time zero $t_0$ in a risk-free asset with return $r_0$ and in a risky asset with random return $\tilde r$.

If the agent invests $\alpha$ in the risky assets, he gets in period $1$:

$$
(W - \alpha) r_0 + \alpha \tilde r = W r_0 + \alpha (\tilde r - r_0)
$$

with $\omega = Wr_0$ and $ \tilde R = \tilde r - r_0$, the agent's problem rewrites as:

$$
\max_{\alpha} V(\alpha) = \mathbb{E}[u(w + \alpha \tilde R)]
$$

<b>Positive Demand for Risky Asset</b>

Let's start deriving some properties:

$$
V'(\alpha) = \mathbb{E}[\tilde R u'(w + \alpha \tilde R)]
$$

$$
V''(\alpha) = \mathbb{E}[\tilde R^2 u''(w + \alpha \tilde R)]
$$

Then if the agent is risk-averse, $V$ is concave and therefore $V'$ is _non-increasing_, so we can conclude that:

- if $\mathbb{E}[\tilde r] > r_f \implies \mathbb{E}[\tilde R] > 0$: the agent should purchase some risky asset _(regardless of how much risk averse he migh be)_.
- if $\mathbb{E}[\tilde r] < r_f$: it is _never optimal_ to purchase the risky asset.
<br/>
<br/>
<b>Static Portfolio Choice - First Order Conditions</b>
<br/>
We derive the first order conditions to quantify how much the agent should invest.

$$
V'(\alpha^*) = 0 \implies \mathbb{E}[\tilde R u' (\omega \alpha^* \tilde R) r] = 0
$$

- If $\tilde R$ is always of the same sign $\implies$ there is _no interior solution_.
- Even if the agent is risk-averse and $\tilde R$ changes sign, there _may not be_ an interior solution.

In the following, we assume that there exists an interior solution and that $\mathbb{E}[\tilde R] > 0$, so that the solution is positive ($\alpha^* > 0$).

<b>Static Portfolio Choice - Small Risks</b>

We assume $\tilde R = \epsilon + \tilde R'$, with $\mathbb{E}[\tilde R'] =0$ and $\epsilon > 0$.<br/>
We denote by $\alpha(\epsilon)$ the solution to:

$$
\max_{\alpha} \mathbb{E}[ u(\omega (\epsilon + \tilde R') \alpha)]
$$

from which we can derive the <b>first-order condition</b>:

$$
\mathbb{E}[(\epsilon + \tilde R')u'(\omega + \alpha(\epsilon)\epsilon + \alpha (\epsilon)\tilde R')] = 0
$$

We know that $\alpha(0) = 0$, so using a Taylor Approximation, we expand $\alpha(\epsilon)$ around $\epsilon = 0$**

Since $\alpha(0) = 0$ _(at zero mean excess return, the agent doesn't buy the risky asset),_ the first-order Taylor expansion is:
$$\alpha(\epsilon) \approx \alpha(0) + \epsilon \alpha'(0) = \epsilon \alpha'(0)$$.

We then substitute into the wealth argument, so the final wealth is:

$$W(\epsilon) = \omega + \alpha(\epsilon)\epsilon + \alpha(\epsilon)\tilde R' = \omega + \alpha(\epsilon)(\epsilon + \tilde R')$$

Substituting $\alpha(\epsilon) \approx \epsilon \alpha'(0)$:

$$W(\epsilon) \approx \omega + \epsilon \alpha'(0)(\epsilon + \tilde R') = \omega + \epsilon^2 \alpha'(0) + \epsilon \alpha'(0) \tilde R'$$

We then expand $u'(W(\epsilon))$ around $\omega$, using $u'(x) \approx u'(\omega) + (x - \omega)u''(\omega)$:

$$u'(W(\epsilon)) \approx u'(\omega) + [W(\epsilon) - \omega] u''(\omega)$$
$$= u'(\omega) + [\epsilon^2 \alpha'(0) + \epsilon \alpha'(0) \tilde R'] u''(\omega)$$

The $\epsilon^2$ term is second-order in $\epsilon$, so we drop it:
$$u'(W(\epsilon)) \approx u'(\omega) + \epsilon \alpha'(0) \tilde R' u''(\omega)$$

Since $\tilde R = \epsilon + \tilde R'$, for small $\epsilon$ we can write this as:

$$u'(\omega + \alpha(\epsilon)\epsilon + \alpha(\epsilon)\tilde R') \approx u'(\omega) + \epsilon \alpha'(0) \tilde R u''(\omega)$$

If we plug it into the first-order condition, we obtain:

$$
\epsilon u'(\omega) + \epsilon \alpha' (0) u''(\omega) \mathbb{E}[\tilde R'^2] = 0
$$
$$
\implies \alpha'(0) = \frac{1}{\mathbb{E}[\tilde R'^2] R_a (\omega)}
$$

that finally gives:

$$
\alpha \approx \frac{\mathbb{E}[\tilde R]}{\mathbb{V}(\tilde R) R_a (\omega)}
$$

Then the share of wealth invested in the risky asset is:

$$
\frac{\alpha}{\omega} \approx \frac{\mathbb{E}[\tilde R]}{\mathbb{V}(\tilde R) R_r (\omega)}
$$

where $R_r(\omega) = - \omega \frac{u''(\omega)}{u'(\omega)}$ is the coefficient of relative risk aversion.

---
<br/>
<b>Static Portfolio Choice - Quadratic Utility Indices </b>

We assume now <b>quadratic utility index</b>, with $c = \omega + \alpha + \tilde R$, that is:

$$
u(c) = c - \frac{\beta}{2}c^2
$$

Under this scenario, the FOC writes as:

$$
(1 - \beta \omega) \mathbb{E}[\tilde R] - \alpha \beta \mathbb{E}[\tilde R^2]= 0
$$

that brings the following value for $\alpha$:

$$
\alpha = \frac{1- \beta \omega}{\beta}\frac{\mathbb{E}[\tilde R]}{\mathbb{E}[\tilde R^2]}
$$

_Intuition: the amount invested in the risky asset only depends on the mean and the variance of $\tilde R = \tilde r - r_0$._

---
<br/>
<b>Static Portfolio Choice - Hyperbolic Absolute Risk Aversion</b>

Consider now the HARA utility index, with $c = \omega + \alpha \tilde R$.


$$
u(c) = \frac{\gamma}{1 - \gamma} ( b + \frac{c}{\gamma})^{(1-\gamma)}
$$

Under this scenario, the FOC gives:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\tilde R (\omega + \alpha \tilde R + b \gamma)^{-\gamma}] = 0 \iff 
$$
$$
\iff \mathbb{E}[\tilde R (1 + \frac{\alpha}{(\omega / \gamma + b)\gamma})^{-\gamma}] = 0
$$

if we denote by $\alpha$ the solution of $ \mathbb{E}[\tilde R (1 + a \frac{\tilde R}{\gamma})^{-\gamma}] = 0$, we end up with:

$$
a = \frac{\alpha}{(\omega/\gamma + b)\gamma} \quad \implies \quad \alpha = a \left(\frac{\omega}{\gamma}+b\right)\gamma
$$

_Inutiotion behind corner cases:_
- <b>CARA </b> ($\gamma \to \infty$): $\alpha$ is independent of $\omega$.
- <b>CRRA</b> ($b = 0$): $\alpha$ is proportional to $\omega$.

<b>Static Portfolio Choice - The Impact of Risk Aversion</b>

<b>Proposition</b>

The demand for the risky asset decreases with risk aversion.

<b>Proof</b>

We consider $u_1 = \phi (u_2)$, with $\phi$ concave, then we assume that $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$ solve the following:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\tilde R u_1 (\omega + \alpha_1 \tilde R)] = 0\quad \mathbb{E}[\tilde R u_2 (\omega + \alpha_2 \tilde R)] = 0 
$$

that translates to $V'_1(\alpha_1) = V'_2(\alpha_2) = 0$. The concavity implies that:

$$
\forall \alpha \in [0,1], \quad \forall R \in \mathbb{R}: 
$$

$$
R \phi' (u_2 (\omega + \alpha R)) \le R \phi0 (u_2 (\omega))
$$

Therefore we can write:

$$
\begin{align*}
V'_1(\alpha_2) &= \mathbb{E}[{\tilde R u'_1 (\omega + \alpha_2 \tilde R)}\\

&= \mathbb{E}[{\tilde R u'_2 (\omega + \alpha_2 \tilde R)} \phi' (u_2 (\omega + \alpha_2 \tilde R))]\\
&\le \mathbb{E}[\tilde R u'_2(\omega + \alpha_2 \tilde R)\phi'(u_2(\omega))]\\
&= \phi'(u_2 (\omega))V'_2 (\alpha_2)//
&= 0

\end{align*}

$$

Thus, $V'_1(\alpha_2)\le 0 = V'(\alpha_1)$. Since $V$ concave, we have also that $\alpha_1 \le \alpha_2$.

<b>Wealth and Demand for Risk</b>

A utility index is said to exhibit decreasing absolute risk aversion <b>(DARA)</b> if and only if $R_a$ is a decreasing function of wealth.

<b>Proposition $I$</b>

The utility index $u$ exhinbits DARA if and only if for all $\tilde x$ the risk premium $\pi$ associated with the lottery $\omega + \tilde x$ is decraeasing in $\omega$.

Consider now an expected utility maximiizng investor with utility index $u$ and initial wealth $\omega$. Assume that there is one safe asset and one risky asset with higher expected return.

<b>Proposition $II$</b>

If the absolute risk aversion is decreasing _(increasing)_ then the dollar amount invested in the risky asset increases (decreases) with investor's wealth.

<b>Proposition $II$</b>

If the relative risk aversion is increasing _(decreasing)_, then the fration of wealth invested in the risky asset decreases _(increases)_ with wealth.

## <a id="7"><b>7. Risk sharing and insurance</b></a>

We consider a <b>single good</b> and two dates:
- $t_0$: where the contingent exchange contracts are signed 
- $t_1$: where the contracts are settled.

We now consider a probability space, $\Omega$, with $\omega \in \Omega$ is a state of the world.<br/>
$\pi_{\omega} \ge 0$ is the known probability distribution on $\Omega \implies$ if state of the world $\omega$ occurs, a total amount of $e^{\omega}$ resources is shared across agents.

There is a infite number of agents. They have NVM preferences and are risk averse. <br/>The utility of agent $i$ is denoted as $u_i$ and satisfites the _Inada Condition_:
$$
\lim_{c \to 0} u_i'(c) = + \infty
$$


<b>Optimal Allocation</b>

An allocation $c = (c_i^{\omega})_{1 \le i \le I}$ with $c_i^{\omega}\ge 0$ is the specification of a contingent consumption plan for all the individuals $i$ in each state of the world.

An allocation is <b>feasible</b> if in each state the total consumption equals the total resources:

$$
\forall \omega, \sum_{i = 1}^{I} c_i^{\omega} = e^{\omega}
$$

An allocation is <b>Pareto Optimal</b> if it is a _feasible_ and if there is no other feasible allocation $\tilde c$ such that, $\forall i \in \{1,... I\}$:

$$
\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[u_i(\tilde c_i)] \ge \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[u_i( c_i)]
$$

with at least one strict inequality.

---
<br/>
<b>Mutuality Principle</b>

<b>Proposition (Borhc)</b>

If all agents are strictly risk averse and have identical beliefs, then for any Pareto-optimal alllocation:

$$
e^{\omega} = e^{\omega'} \implies c_i^{\omega} = c_i^{\omega'}
$$
Let's then proceed with the following example.<br/>
Consider the matrix of state-contingent consumption, where lines corresponds to individuals and column to states of the world:



$$
c =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & 5 & 4 & 8 & 9 \\
2 & 5 & 3 & 7 & 8 \\
4 & 6 & 5 & 12 & 10
\end{bmatrix}
$$

In this case, the matrix is not a Pareto Optimal allocation.

The columns represent the aggregate resources by state:
$$
e = (8, 16, 12, 27, 27)
$$

State 4 and 5 have the same aggregate resources: $e^4 = e^5 = 27$, but the Borch condition is not satisfied: $c^4 = (8, 7, 12) \neq c^5 = (9, 8, 10)$.

---
<br/>
<b>Optimal allocation: Characterization</b>
<br/>
<b>Theorem</b>

A feasible allocation $c$ such that $c_i^{\omega} > 0$ is Pareto optimal $\iff$ there are nonnegative weights $(\lambda^i)_{1 \le i le I}$ and $(\mu^{\omega})_{\omega \in \Omega}$ such tat $\forall \omega \in \Omega$ and $\forall i$:

$$
\lambda^i u_i'(c_i^{\omega}) = \mu^{\omega}
$$


This condition can be restated as:
- the ratio of marginal utilities in a given state $\omega$ for 2 agents $i \neq j$ is independent of the state $\omega$:

$$
\frac{u'_i(c_i^{\omega})}{u_j'(c_j^{\omega})} = \frac{\lambda_j}{\lambda_i} 
$$

- the ratio of margina utilities in 2 given state $\omega, \omega'$ is the same across all agents:

$$
\frac{u_i'(c_i^{\omega})}{u_i'(c_i^{\omega_i})} = \frac{\mu^{\omega}}{\mu^{\omega'}}

$$

<b>Corollary</b>

Assume that all agents are risk averse. Then, for any Pareto-optimal allocation, $c$ and $e$ are comonotonic:

$$
e^{\omega} \ge e^{\omega'} \iff c_i^{\omega} \ge c_i^{\omega'}
$$

_Intuition: $e$ is about the aggregate risk (how much total output/income the economy has in each scenario, $c$ is about _risk sharing_, who beras how much risk across scenarios)_.

Since $\sum_{i = 1}^I c_i^{\omega} = e^{\omega}$, the variables $e^{\omega}, c_i^{\omega}$ are also comonotonic.

<b>Proof</b>

Assume that the allocation is Pareto optimal. Then $\lambda_i u_iì(c_i) = \mu^{\omega}$ and the ratio $u_i'(c_i^{\omega})/u_i'(c_i^{\omega'}) =\mu^{\omega}/\mu^{\omega'}$ is _independent_ of $i$. Hence $\forall i$ the cariables $c_i^{\omega}$ are _comonotonic_:

$$
c_i^{\omega} > c_i^{\omega'} \iff c_j^{\omega} \ge c_j^¶{\omega'}
$$

Let's proceed with an example. Consider the following matrix where lines correspond to individuals and columns to states of the world:

$$
c = \begin{bmatrix}
2 & 5 & 4 & 8 & 9\\
2 & 5 & 4 & 8 & 11\\
4 & 6 & 5 & 11 & 12
\end{bmatrix}
$$

In this case, we have resources equal to:

$$
e^{\omega} = \sum_i(c_i^{\omega}) = (8, 16, 13, 27, 32)
$$

To check if it is Pareto-optimal for risk averse individuals _(not necessarily with the same preferences)_, we check the _commonoticity with aggregate endowment/resources_: when $e^{\omega}$ is higher, also each $c_i^{\omega}$ should be _weakly higher_.

We order the states by $(e)$, $(1 < 3 < 2 < 4 < 5)$ and we see that _each row follows the same order_ for each agent, resulting then in a Pareto-optimal allocation:
- agent 1: $(2 < 4 < 5 < 8 < 11)$
- agent 2: $(2 < 4 < 5 < 8 < 9)$
- agent 3: $(4 < 5 < 6 < 11 < 12)$

If the agents have same preferences, we need to check the condition: 

$$
\frac{u'(c_1^{\omega})}{u'(c_2^{\omega})} = \text{costant across } \omega
$$
But in states 1–4, $(c_1^\omega=c_2^\omega)$, so the ratio must be $=1$.
Then in state 5 you would need $(u'(11)=u'(9))$, impossible with strictly decreasing $(u')$ (strictly risk-averse $(u)$)

---
<br/>
<b>Risk neutral agents should bear all the risk</b>

<b>Corollary</b>

Assume mthat there is a risk-neutral agent and that the allocation $(c_i)_{1 \le i \le I}$ is pareto-optimal. If agent $i$ is strictly risk-averse, then her consumption must be determnistic:

$$
\forall (\omega, \omega') \in \Omega^2, \quad c_i^{\omega} = c_i^{\omega'}
$$

_In other words: for a risk-averse agent, the consumption is independent of the state of the world $\omega$._

---

<br/>
<b>An example of Pareto-optimal allocation - CARA preferences</b>

Assume $u_i(x) = - e^{k_i x}$. Them we have:

$$
\frac{u_i'(c_i^{\omega})}{u_i'(c_i^{\omega'})} = e ^{-k_i (c_i^{\omega} - c_i^{\omega'})}
$$

For an allocatio to be optimal, the ratio has to be independent of $i$, that means:

$$
e ^{-k_i (c_i^{\omega} - c_i^{\omega'})} = f(\omega, \omega')
$$

that, if we assume $g(\omega, \omega') = \ln (f(\omega, \omega'))$, implies:

$$
-k_i (c_i^{\omega} - c_i^{\omega'}) = g(\omega,\omega') \implies  c_i^{\omega} = c_i^{\omega'} - \frac{g(\omega, \omega')}{k_i}
$$

If we denote by $\beta_i = \frac{1}{k_i}$ the <b>absolute risk tolerance</b> of $i$ and $\beta =\sum_i \beta_i$ the <b>aggregate absolute risk tolerance</b>, summing over $i$ and using the _feasibility condition_ $\sum_i c_i^\omega=e^\omega$ gives:

$$
e^{\omega} = e^{\omega'} - \beta g(\omega, \omega') \iff g(\omega, \omega') =\frac{ e^{\omega'}- e^{\omega}}{\beta}
$$

and thus 

$$
c_i^{\omega} = c_i^{\omega'} + \frac{\beta_i}{\beta} (e^{\omega}- e^{\omega'})
$$

Taking the expectation under the distribution of $\omega'$ gives:

$$
c_i^{\omega} = \mathbb{E}_{\pi} [c_i] + \frac{\beta_i}{\beta} (e^{\omega}- \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[e])
$$

_(Note that $\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[c_i^{\omega}] = c_i^{\omega}$, the value taken in state $\omega$ is not random)_.

- All Pareto-optima assume that the allocation is the same. Agent $i$ takes $\frac{\beta_i}{\beta}$ of the aggregate risk, where $\beta_i$ is, again, the <b>absolute risk tolerance</b>.
- Aggregate risk is therefore spread over all the agents, but the more risk averge agent take less risk.
- The difference between different Pareto-optimal equilibria is only reflected in the levels of expected consumption $\mathbb{E}_{\pi}{c_i}$.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Risk Sharing Equilibrium</b>

Suppose there are $I$ agents with strictly concave utilities, each with endowment $e^{\omega}_i$. Suppose that there' a <b>complete market</b> $a = (a_k^{\omega})_{1 \le k \le K}$.

An equilibrium is given by a <b>state price vector </b> $q = (q_{\omega})$ and a <b>consumption plan</b> $c = (c_i^{\omega})$ such that:
- $\sum_i c_i^{\omega} = \sum_i e^{\omega}_i $ for all $w$ _(scarcity constraint of feasibility)_ 
- each agent's consumption plan is optimal, i.e. solves:

$$
\max \mathbb{E}_{\pi} [u_i (c_i)] \text{  s.t. } \sum q_{\omega} c_i^{\omega} = \sum q_{\omega} e^{\omega}_i
$$


_Intuition: agents trade claims so that consumption is moved from states where they are relatively well off to states where they are relatively poorly off._ 

_Economically speaking, $p_{\omega}$ is the today-price of getting 1 unit of consumption only in state $\omega$ (market-insurance price of the future state)_.


If we denote by $\lambda_i$ the <b>Lagrange multiplier</b> associated with investor $i$'s budget constraint, the first order condition for the individual utility maximization is:

$$
\pi_{\omega} u_i'(c_i^{\omega}) = \lambda_i q_{\omega}
$$

_Intuition: at the optimum, the marginal utility of consumption in each state, weighted by its probability, equals the its price._

which implies that any equilibrium allocation is _necessarily_ Pareto-optimal.

<b>Risk Sharing Equilibrium - Theorem</b>

For any initial endowment $e^{\omega}_i$, there exists a <b>competitive equilibrium</b> supported by some prices $q_{\omega}$. This equilibrium is Pareto-Optimal.

_Intuition: if all claims can be traded, then a competitive equilibrium exists and it is efficient_.

_Financially speaking, this is the model behind insurance and asset pricing: markets aggregate beliefs and redistribute risk implementing the best feasible risk sharing given the initial resources and preferences_.

## <a id="8"><b>8. Mean variance analysis</b></a>

Mean-variance analysis was proposed independetly in 1952 by <a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Markowitz" target="_blank">Harry Markowitz</a>.<br/>For his research, Markowitz was awarded with the Nodel Price, along with <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_F._Sharpe" target="_blank">Sharpe</a>.

<a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6do_11UZNu4" target="_blank">Mean Variance Intuition in 2 minutes [Video] </a>

The main assumptions are that preferences over portfolios are:
- increasing with respect to expected return _(reward)_
- decreasing with respect to variance _(risK)_

The basic idea of the optimization is that, given returns, variance is  minimized and reciprocally, given variance, return is maimized.  This leaves <b>one free parameter</b>, that is the agent's risk aversion parameter, which determines their choice upon the risk/reward tradeoff.

_In other words, given some assets agents are assumed to maximize expected returns while keeping the variance at the desired level, or the opposite way, minimize the variance for a desired expected return._

But _why do we care for mean and variance only?_ Results can be shown to be _independent_ from higher moments in 2 specific cases:
- Agents have a <b>quadratic utility index</b>: $u(x) = x - \frac{\beta}{2} x^2$ with 

$$
\mathbb{E}[u(\tilde x)] = u ( \mathbb{E}[\tilde x]- \frac{\rho}{2}\text{Var}[\tilde x])
$$

- Agents have a <b>CARA</b> utility index and <b>returns are normally distributed</b>: $u(x) = -e^{\rho x}$:

$$
\mathbb{E}[u(\tilde x)] = \mathbb{E}[\tilde x] - \frac{\beta}{2}(\mathbb{E}[\tilde x]^2) - \frac{\beta}{2}\text{Var}[{\tilde x}]
$$


<b>Mean-variance analysis: the general setting </b>

We consider a probability space $\Omega$, $\omega \in \Omega$ is a state of the world with probability $\pi_{\omega}$.

Any random variable defined on $\Omega$ iss denoned with a tilde $\tilde y$.
- $\tilde y$ takes the value $y^{\omega}$ in a state $\omega$, hence: $\tilde y = (y^{\omega})$ 
- the expected value of $\tilde y$ is $\mathbb{E}[\tilde y] = \sum_{\omega} \pi_{\omega} y^{\omega}$.

We denote by $\tilde a_k = (a^{\omega}_k)$ the random payoff of asset $k$ and by $p_k$ its price 

Whem it exists, we shall denote by $a_0$ the safe asset, bringing a costant payoff $a_0^{\omega}$ in every state.

- By convention, the Latin subscript of $\tilde a_k$ denotes the risky assets $k \in \{ 1, ..., K\}$. When we mean all assets, including the riskless one, we use the Greek subscript $\tilde a_{\kappa}$.

We call $z = (z_{\kappa})$ the composition of an investor's portfolio and $w$ the initial wealth, _Note that short positions are possible, so we can have $z_{\kappa} < 0$ for some assets._

Moreover, we denote by:


- $\tilde{R}_k = (R_k^\omega)_{\omega \in \Omega}$: gross return of asset $k$, where $  R_k^\omega = \frac{a_k^\omega}{p_k}. $

- $R_k = \mathbb{E}_\pi[\tilde{R}_k]$: expected return of asset $k$.
 
  $$
  R = (R_1,\ldots,R_K)^\top, 
  \qquad
  \tilde{R} = (\tilde{R}_1,\ldots,\tilde{R}_K)^\top.
  $$

- $  \sigma_{kl}=   \operatorname{Cov}_\pi(\tilde{R}_k,\tilde{R}_l)= (  \mathbb{E}_\pi[\tilde{R}_k\tilde{R}_l] - R_kR_l)$ covariance between returns of assets $k$ and $l$.

- $  \Sigma = (\sigma_{kl})_{(k,l)\in\{1,\ldots,K\}^2} $ the $K \times K$ covariance matrix of asset returns.

- $   x_k = \frac{p_k z_k}{w}$   share of initial wealth invested in asset $k$.

<b>Expected return and variance of the portfolio</b>

The expected return of the portfolio is given by:

$$
\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde R_p] = x_1 R_1 + x_2 R_2 + ... + x_k R_k = \sum_k x_k R_k 
$$

Or, in matrix form as:

$$
\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde R_p] = x^T R
$$

The variance of the portfolio is given by:

$$
\begin{align*}
\operatorname{Var}{\pi}[\tilde R_p]
&= \mathbb{E}{\pi}[(\tilde R_p-\mathbb{E}{\pi}[\tilde R_p])^2] \\
&= \sum_{k,l=1}^{K} x_k x_l\sigma_{kl} \\
&= \sum_{k = 1}^K x_k^2 \sigma_k^2 + 2 \sum_{k < l} x_k x_l \sigma_{kl}\\
&= x^\top \Sigma x.
\end{align*}
$$

<b>Example with 2 stocks</b>

Assume an investor has allocated her wealth equally between two stocks, $x_1 = x_2 = 0,5$.

Suppose also that $R_1 = 0.1, R_2 = 0.05$, $\sigma^2_1 = 0.04$, $\sigma^2_2 = 0.01$ and $\rho_{12} = 0.5$.

The expected return of the portfolio is given by:

$$
\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde R_p] = x_1 R_1 + x_2 R_2 = 0.075
$$

and the variance by:

$$
\operatorname{Var}[\tilde R_p] = x_1^2 \sigma_{11}^2 + x_1 x_2 \sigma_{12} + x_2^2 \sigma_2^2 = 0.0175
$$

_Note that in the same formulation sometimes we use correlation coefficient $\rho_{12} = \frac{\sigma_{12}}{\sigma_1 \sigma_2}$_.

---
<br/>
<b>Minimum variance portfolio with two stocks</b> 

Given $R_1, R_2, \sigma_1, \sigma_2, \rho_{12} = \frac{\sigma_{12}}{\sigma_1 \sigma_2} < 1$, the investor choose $x_1$ to minimize the variance of the portfolio:

$$
\frac{1}{2} \sigma_p^2 = \min \frac{1}{2} (x_1^2 \sigma_1^2 + 2 x_1 x_2 \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \rho_{12} + x_2^2 \sigma_2^2) \text{ s.t } x_1 + x_2 = 1
$$

If we apply the first order condition:

$$
\frac{\partial \sigma^2_p}{\sigma x_1} = x_1 \sigma_1^2 (1 - x_1)\sigma^2_2 + ((1 - x_1)-x_1)\sigma_1 \sigma_2 \rho_{12} = 0 \implies
$$

$$
x_1^{MVP} = \frac{\sigma^2_2 - \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \rho_{12}}{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2 \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \rho_{12}}
$$

_(assuming $\sigma_1^2 \neq \sigma_2^2$ or $\rho_{12} \neq 1$)._

If we plug the solution into the formula for the variance of the portfolio we get:

$$
\sigma_p^{2, MVP} = \frac{\sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 ( 1 - \rho_{12}^2)}{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 - 2 \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \rho_{12}}
$$

We can then see two special cases:
- $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2, \rho_{12} = 0 \implies $: $x_1^{MVP} = 1/2, \quad \sigma_p^{2, MVP} = \sigma^2_1 / 2 < \sigma_1^2$
- $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2, \rho_{12} = -1 \implies $: $x_1^{MVP} = 1/2, \quad \sigma_p^{2, MVP} = 0$

and we can do the following remarks:
- with $K$ assets with identical variances and that are indepedent, the optimal minimum variance portfolio is such that the same amount is invested in each stock: $\sigma_p^{2, MVP} = \sigma^2_1 / k$. _This is the idea behind diversification_.
- with perfectly negative correlated stocks, we can obtain a portfolio with no risk.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Efficient frontier, no safe asset</b>

In the general case, the problem for the investor is to minimize the variance of the portfolio for a given expected return $m$.

The problem writes as:

$$
\min_{x} \frac{1}{2} \sum _{k,l} \sigma_{kl} x_k x_l \quad \text{ s.t. }\begin{cases}
\sum_k x_k \mathbb{E}[R_k] = m & \text{(target return)}\\
\sum_k x_k = 1 & \text{(budget)}
\end{cases}
$$

_(assuming no redundant asset)_

In matrix form:

$$
\min_{x} \frac{1}{2} x^T \Sigma x \quad \text{s.t.} \begin{cases}
x^T \mu = m \\
x^T \mathbf{1} = 1
\end{cases}
$$

where $\Sigma$ is the covariance matrix and $\mu = \mathbb{E}[R]$ is the vector of expected asset returns.

_(assuming no redundant asset)_

Let's now denote by $\lambda$ and $\nu$ the Lagrange multipliers for the constraints and write the Lagrangian:

$$
\mathcal{L}(x, \lambda, \nu) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{k,l} \sigma_{kl}x_k x_l + \lambda (m - \sum_k x_k \mathbb{E}[R_k]) + \nu (1 - \sum_k x_k)
$$

By FOC _(note: $x_j$ appears in the sum when $k=j$ and when $l=j$)_
$$
0 = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}(x, \lambda, \nu)}{\partial x_j} = \sum_l \sigma_{jl}x_l - \lambda \mathbb{E}[R_j] - \nu
$$

If we denote by $\Gamma = (\gamma^{kl})$ the inverse of the covariance matrix $\Sigma$:

$$
x_k = \lambda \sum_l \gamma^{kl} \mathbb{E}[R_l] + \nu \sum_l \gamma^{kl}
$$

If we plug this into the two constraints we get:

$$
\lambda\left(\sum_{k,l=1}^K \gamma^{kl}\mathbb{E}[R_k]\mathbb{E}[R_l]\right)
+\nu\left(\sum_{k,l=1}^K \gamma^{kl}\mathbb{E}[R_k]\right)=m,

$$

$$
\lambda\left(\sum_{k,l=1}^K \gamma^{kl}\mathbb{E}[R_l]\right)
+\nu\left(\sum_{k,l=1}^K \gamma^{kl}\right)=1.

$$

or, in compact notation:

$$
c:=\mathbb{E}[R]^\top\Gamma \mathbb{E}[R],\qquad
b:=\mathbb{E}[R]^\top\Gamma\mathbf{1}=\mathbf{1}^\top\Gamma \mathbb{E}[R],\qquad
d:=\mathbf{1}^\top\Gamma\mathbf{1},

$$
$$
\lambda c+\nu b=m,\qquad
\lambda b+\nu d=1.

$$

A solution for this system is given by:

$$
\lambda = \frac{dm - b}{\Delta}, \quad \nu = \frac{-bm +c}{\Delta}, \quad \Delta = dc - b^2
$$

Pre-multiplying the FOC of the problem by $x^T$ yields:

$$
\sigma_p^{2} = \lambda m + \nu \implies \sigma_p^{2} = \frac{dm^2 - 2mb + c}{\Delta}
$$

or, equivalently:

$$
\frac{\Delta}{d} \sigma_p^{2} - \left( m - \frac{b}{d}\right)^2 = \frac{\Delta}{d^2}
$$

</div>

<b>The Global Minimum Variance Portfolio</b>

The global minimum variance portfolio _(hedging portfolio)_ is the portfolio $x_G \in \mathbb{R}^K$ with the smallest variance. This portfolio (obtained with $\lambda = 0$) is given by:

$$
x_{G,k} = \frac{1}{d}\sum_l \gamma^{kl}
$$

It has expected return $m_G = b/d$ and risk $\sigma_G^2 = \frac{1}{d}$.

![GDP by country](/images/notes/financial-econ/ef.png)
</div>

_Note that portfolio on the efficient frontier with $m < b/d$ are dominated, since one can get higher returns with the same variance._

<br/>
<b>The Mutual Fund Theorem I (no safe asset)</b>

Given two frontier portfolios $x_A, x_B$, all efficient portfolios can be obtained by mixing $x_A, x_B$. Thus all agents seeking for frontier portfolios need only to invest in a combination of these two funds.

Every efficient portfolio is characterized by a scalar $\phi$ such that:
$$
x_k = \phi x_{A,k} + (1 - \phi)x_{B,k}
$$

So, given two portfolios with return $R_A, R_B$, $\phi$ alows to move along the efficient frontier:

$$
\phi R_A + (1 - \phi)R_B = m
$$

---
<br/>
<b>Efficient Portfolio with a Safe Asset </b>

We now consider the case of a portfolio $x$ with one safe asset $x_0$ and $K$ risky assets.

$$
x_0 = (x_1, ... x_K)^T
$$

The investor goal is to optimize:

$$
\frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 = \min \frac{1}{2} \sum_{kl} \sigma_{kl} x_k x_l
\\
\text{s.t.} \quad x_0 R_0 + \sum_k x_k R_k = m \\
x_0 + \sum_k x_k = 1
$$

The FOC are:

$$
\sum_k \sigma_{kl}x_k = \lambda R_l + \mu \\
0 = \lambda R_0 + \mu
$$

Subtracting the two equations removes $\mu$ and gives:

$$
\sum_k \sigma_{kl} x_k = \lambda (R_l - R_0)
$$

so the risky portfolio is chosen to match the vector of excess returns $(R_l - R_0)$.<br/>We then put $\Gamma = \Sigma^{-1}$ and calculate $\gamma$:

$$
x_k = \lambda \sum_l \gamma^{kl} (R_l - R_0)\\
x_0 = 1 - \sum_k x_k
$$

This means each <b>risky weight is proportional to a weighted combination of excess returns</b>, where the weights come from the inverse covariance matrix. _Intuition: assets with higher expected excess return get more weight, but assets are also penalized if they are highly risky or strongly correlated with other risky assets._

We can see that for any efficient portfolio, the risky-asset sub-portfolio is <b>proportional</b> to $\sum_l \gamma^{kl} (R_l - R_0)$.

We then define the <b>tangent portfolio</b> $x_T$ as the only efficient portfolio which does not use the safe assets, such that $x_{T,0} = 0$ and, $\forall k \in \{1 ... K \}$ we have:

$$
x_{T,k} = \frac{\sum_l \gamma^{kl}(R_l - R_0)}{\sum_{i,j}\gamma^{ij}(R_j - R_0)}
$$

Once we define the tangent portfolio and a risk-free asset exists, all efficient portfolios are built in the same way combining a common risky portfolio _(tangent portfolio)_ and some amount of the same asset.

So the tangent portfolio is the unique risky-only efficient portfolio. It is “special” because every other efficient portfolio has the same risky-asset mix, just scaled up or down.

In formulas, we write:

$$
x = \alpha y_0 + (1-\alpha)x_T
$$

where $y_0^T = (1, 0, .... 0)$ is the portfolio composed of the risk-free asset only and $x_T^T = (0, x_1.... x_K)$ is the tangent portfolio _(with no investment in the risk-free asset)_.

---

To choose $\alpha$, we consider the desired return and variance:

$$
m = \alpha R_0 + (1-\alpha)R_T = (1-\alpha)(R_T - R_0) + R_0
\\
\sigma = (1 - \alpha)\sigma_T \implies \\
m = \Big (\frac{R_T - R_0}{\sigma_T} \Big) \sigma + R_0
$$

---
<br/>
<b>The Mutual Fund Theorem II </b>

In the presence of a safe asset, efficient portfolios are a combination of the risk-free asset and the tangent portfolio. The exact combination is determined by $m$.

<b>Corollary</b>

All efficient portfolios are on a real line in the $(\sigma, m)$ plane and this line is called the <b>capital allocation line</b>.

With a combination of a safe portfolio and a risky portfolio, investors can improve and expand the set of opportunities available from the efficient frontier:
- all portfolios on the capital allocation line offer a better expected return than risky portfolios with the same volatility located on the EF.
- borrowing at the risk-free rate to get additional funds allows investors to seek higher expected returns _(higher $m$)_ and assume more risk _(higher $\sigma$)_.

<br/>
<b>Sharpe Ratio</b>

For any portfolio with expected return $m$ and standard deviation $\sigma$ we define the <b>Sharpe ratio</b> as:

$$
\text{Sharpe Ratio} = \frac{m - R_0}{\sigma}
$$

_Intuition: it quantifies the performance of a portfolio measuring the excess return per unit of risk (deviation)._ The slopes of the drawn lines are equal to the Sharpe Ratio of the portfolios located on the EF.

- the tangent portfolio has the <b>highest Sharpe ratio</b> among portfolios of only risky assets.
- all efficient portfolios have the same Sharpe ratio equaling the one of the tangent portfolio.
- the slope of the capital allocation line is equal to that Sharpe ratio.

## <a id="9"><b>9. CAPM</b></a>

The CAPM (<b>Capital Asset Pricing Model</b>) was independently introduced by Treynor, Sharpe, Lintner and Mossin in the 1960's.

The motivation of their works was to address criticisms of Markowitz's MVA, some of them were:
- MVA requires the full knowledge of the returns' correlation, which grows quadratically with the number of assets 
- even if robust estimation of $\Sigma$ are available, MVA makes global predictions ignoring the information present in the market.

The CAPM brings two key assumptions added to the Markowitz model:
- <b>complete agreement</b> on the joint distribution of asset returns 
- <b>borrowing and lending</b> at a risk-free rate available to all investors.

Under these assumptions, all investors see the same opportunity set and hold the same tangent portfolio of risky assets, which is therefore the <b>value-weight market portfolio</b>.

The CAPM rests on the notion of <b>market portfolio</b>, which is simply the aggregation of the economy's financial assets. Therefore, a fundamental assumption of the CAPM is that <b>the market portfolio is efficient</b> in the sense of Markowitz and this implies that all investors know the Capital Allocation Line which can be read in the market.

We now define the market portfolio as $z_M = (z_{M,0}, \ldots, z_{M,K})$ as the portfolio of all assets in the economy.

$\forall k \in (0, 1... K)$, the share of wealth in asset $k$ (<b>market weight</b>)is given by:

$$
x_{M,k} = \frac{p_k z_{M,k}}{\sum_{\kappa} p_{\kappa}z_{M, \kappa}}
$$

The market portfolio is assumed to be efficient, so, by the mutual fund theorem, we have:
$$
\sum_k \sigma_{kl}x_{M,k} = \lambda (R_l - R_0)
$$

<b>The gross return of the market portfolio</b> is determined by the weighted average of the asset returns as:

$$
\tilde R_M = \sum_{\kappa} x_{M,\kappa} \tilde R_{\kappa}
$$

If we now recall that $\sigma_{kl} = \operatorname{Cov}(\tilde R_k, \tilde R_l)$:

$$
\operatorname{Cov}_{\pi}(\tilde R_l, \tilde R_M) = \sum_k x_{M,k} \operatorname{Cov}(\tilde R_k, \tilde R_l) = \sum_k \sigma_{kl} x_{M,k}
$$

If we then put $\sum_k \sigma_{kl}x_{M,k} = \lambda (R_l - R_0)$ we get:

$$
\operatorname{Cov}(\tilde R_l, \tilde R_M) = \lambda (R_l - R_0)
$$

and multiplying both sides by $x_{M,l}$ and summing we get:

$$
\sum_l x_{M,l}\operatorname{Cov}(\tilde R_l, \tilde R_M) = \lambda (R_M - R_0)
$$

or, _in other terms_:

$$
\operatorname{Var}_{\pi} (\tilde R_M) = \lambda (R_M - R_0)
$$

if we substitute out $\lambda$ _(the Lagrange multiplier from the mean-variance optimization that links covariance to excess returns)_, we get the <b>very famous CAPM relation</b>:

$$
R_k = R_0 + \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(\tilde R_k, \tilde R_M)}{\operatorname{Var}(\tilde R_M)} (R_M - R_0) = R_0 + \beta_k (R_M - R_0)
$$

$$
\beta_k = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(\tilde R_k, \tilde R_M)}{\operatorname{Var}(\tilde R_M)}
$$

The CAPM tells us that the <b>risk premium associated to asset</b> $k$ is given by the product of the asset's _beta_ with the risk premium of the market portfolio.

- we call <b>idiosyncratic</b> a risk which has <b>zero correlation with the market</b>. According to CAPM, idiosyncratic risk has zero premium. _(This doesn't mean that this risk is liked by all investors, it just means that there is zero net supply/demand in the economy for that risk)_ 
- in equilibrium, no agent bears any idiosyncratic risk as every agent has a combination of the market portfolio and the safe asset 

On the other hand, we call <b>systematic</b> a risk which has correlation $=1$ to the market risk and cannot be avoided by investing in the market.
- the CAPM shows that, _for the same expected value_, assets which are <b>negatively correlated</b> with the market have a greater price _(and thus <b>lower expected returns</b>)_. $\implies$ there's  net demand for those assets in the economy as they insure the investors against systematic risk 
- assets which are <b>positively correlated</b> with the market are in net supply: people dislike them as they increase systematic risk, so they ask for an <b>higher risk premium</b>.

<b>Empirical Application - Tests on the cross-section of stock returns</b> 

Empirical strategy:
- choose a benchmark _(S&P500)_ 
- compute expected returns of each individual asset $k$
- compute all covariances between the asset and the index
- compute the variance of the index 
- compute $\beta_k = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(\tilde R_k, \tilde R_M)}{\operatorname{Var}(\tilde R_M)}$ 
- regress a cross-section of average asset returns on estimates of asset betas 

The CAPM should then predict:
- intercept will be the risk-free rate $R_0$ 
- coefficient on $\beta$ is the expected excess return of the market portfolio $R_M - R_0$

_(Each data point represents one asset)_

The regression line is called the <b>security market line</b>, its origin is the risk-free rate.
- any asset on the security line is fairly prices 
- any asset under the line is <b>overprice</b>
- any asset over the line is <b>underprice</b>

<b>Time-series regression tests</b>

According to CAPM, the expected value on an asset's excess return is _completely_ explaine by its expected CAPM risk premium $\beta_k (R_k - R_0)$.

This suggests estimating the following-time series regression:

$$
R_{kt} - R_{0t} = \alpha k + \beta_{kM}(R_{Mt - R_{0t}}) + \epsilon_{it}
$$

where the regression constant $\alpha_k$ is called <b>Jensen's alpha</b>$ for stock $k$.

- theory predicts $\hat \alpha_k = 0$, but empirical finance has spent the last 40 years looking for positive alpha 
    - those correspond to <b>underpriced assets</b> as the investors are expecting a greate return for the same beta.
    - these assets represent <b>arbitrage opportunities</b>.

<b>Empirical Findings </b>
- Positive and significant slope for the Security Market Line 
- The relation between beta and average return is much flatter than CAPM predicts 
- Other variables _(price ratios, size, momentum)_ are significant predicors of average returns $\implies$ <b>Fama and Frech 5 Factors Model</b>

The CAPM has indeed not ben an empirical sucess, raising questions about its use inm appications. Some possible explanations:
- <b>the market proxy problem</b>: 
    - empirical tests use proxies of the market portfolio 
    - the market portfolio at the center of the teory is not observable 
$\implies$ CAPM has never been really tested 
- the CAPM relies on <b>strong and unrealistic assumptions</b>:
 $\implies$ need to develop more sophisticated asset pricing models _(example_ <b>Consumption Based CAPM</b>)

## <a id="10"><b>10. Risk sharing and asset prices in a market equilibrium</b></a>


<b>The Consumption Based CAPM</b>

We consider a probability space $\Omega$, where $\omega in \Omega$ is a state of the world with probability $\pi_{\omega}$.

We denote by $\tilde y$ any random variabkle define on $\Omega$. We say that $\tilde y$ takes the value $y^{\omega}$ in state $\omega$ and its expected value if $\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde y] = \sum_{\omega} \pi_{\omega}y^{\omega}$.

We consider then $I$ agents living in two periods. For each agent $i$:
- at $t = 0$: <b>deterministic endowment </b>$e_i^0$ with consumption $c_i^0$
- at $t = 1$ <b>random endowment</b> $\tilde e_i = (e_i^{\omega})$ with consumption $\tilde c_i = (c_i^{\omega})$

We write $\tilde C_i = (c_i^0 \tilde c_i)$.

Agents are <b>expected utility maximizers</b> with additively sperable utility function:

$$
U_i (\tilde C_i) = u_i(c_i^0) + \delta \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[v_i(\tilde c_i)]

$$

where $\delta$ denotes the time preference.

We then consider a <b>market</b> with $K$ risky assets where $a_k = (a_k^{\omega})$ is the random payoff of asset $k$ with price $p_k$.<br/>There's also a risk-free asset $a_0$ yielding a costant payoff $a_0$ for every state.

We then define $r$ by: $(1 + r) = \frac{a_0}{p_0}$.


<b>Agents' optimization problem</b>

Agent $i$ maximizes his utility function, that means:

$$
\max_{z_i^{\kappa}} \{ U_i (\tilde C_i) = u_i (c_i^0) + \delta \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[v_i(\tilde c_i)] \}
$$

under the budget costraints:

$$
c_i^0 = e_i^0 - \frac{z_i^0}{1 + r} - \sum_k^K p_k z_i^k \\

c_i^{\omega} = e_i^{\omega} + z_i^0 + \sum_k^K z_i^K a_k^{\omega}
$$

_Intuition: the first costraint represents the current consumption as the initial endowment, minus the cost of investing $z_i^0$ in a risk-free asset (discounted by $r$) and the cost of investing $z_i^k$ for each asset $k$._ 

_The second budget costraint represents that future consumptio is equal to the sum of the future endowment, the return of the risk-free asset and the return from risky assets._

We can then substitute in the utility formula and we get:

$$
U_i = u_i \left( e_i^0 - \frac{z_i^0}{1 + r} - \sum_{k=1}^K p_k z_i^k \right) + \delta \mathbb{E}_{\pi} \left[ v_i \left( \tilde{e}_i + z_i^0 + \sum_{k=1}^K z_i^k \tilde{a}_k \right) \right].
$$

We apply the FOC as:

$$
\forall \kappa \in \{ 0 ... K\}, \quad \frac{\partial u_i}{\partial z_i^{\kappa}} = 0
$$

that brings to:

$$
u_i'(c_i^0) = (1 + r )\delta \mathbb{E}[v'_i (\tilde c_i)]\\
p_k u_i'(c_i^0) = \delta \mathbb{E}[\tilde a_k v_i'(\tilde c_i)]
$$

_Intuition: intertemporal margin rate of substitution, since it equates the marginal utility of current consumption with the discounted expected marginal utility of future consumption $\implies$ the he first equation describes the trade-off between current and future consumption, incorporating the risk-free interest rate and time preference_ 

_The second equation representsw the optimality condition for risky asset allocation, since it equates the marginal cost of investing in risky asset to the discounted expected marginal utility of the returns._

They can be combined as:

$$
\begin{align*}
(1 + r)p_k \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[v_i'(\tilde c_i)] &= \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde a_k v'_i(\tilde c_i)] \\
&= \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde a_k]\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[v_i'(\tilde c_i)] + \operatorname{Cov}_{\pi}(\tilde a_k, v_i'(\tilde c_i))
\end{align*}
$$

We now focus on the case $v'_i$ <b>linear</b>, that means: $v_i(c) = c - \alpha_i c^2 \implies v_i' (c) = 1 - 2\alpha_i c$.

We then get:

$$
\operatorname{Cov}_{\pi}(\tilde a_k, v_i'(\tilde c_i)) = - 2\alpha_i \operatorname{Cov}(\tilde a_k, \tilde c_i)
$$

If we plug the covariance in the FOC we obtain:

$$
\frac{1}{2 \alpha_i}\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[v_i'(\tilde c_i)] \Big[  \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde a_k] - (1 + r)p_k \Big] = \operatorname{Cov}_{\pi}(\tilde a_k, \tilde c_i)
$$

We now model the consumption as:

$$
\tilde c_i =\tilde e_i + z_i^0 + z_i' \tilde a
$$

we then denote by $\Sigma$ the variance-covarianc matrix of $\tilde a$ and we get, in matrix notation:

$$
\Sigma z_i = - \operatorname{Cov}(\tilde a, \tilde e_i) + \frac{\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[v_i'(\tilde c_i)]}{2 \alpha_i}[\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde a]- (1 + r)p]
$$

We then define by $T_i^a$ the <b>absolute risk tolerance </b> for investor $i$:

$$
T_i^a = - \frac{v_i'(c)}{v_i"(c)}
$$

If $v_i'$ is linear we have:
- $\mathbb{E_{\pi}}[v_i'(\tilde c_i)] = v_i'(\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde c_i])$
- $v_i" (c) = - 2 \alpha_i$

so the previous equation rewrites as:
$$
\Sigma z_i = -\text{Cov}_{\pi}(\tilde{a}, \tilde{e}_i) + T_i^a \left( \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde{c}_i] \right) \left\{ \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde{a}] - (1 + r)p \right\}.
$$

<b>Market Clearing</b>

The accounting balance is $\sum_i^I z_i = 0$, this implies that in all states of the world:

$$
\sum_i^I c_i^{\omega} = c_M^{\omega} = e_M^{\omega} = \sum_i^I c_i^{\omega}
$$

_Intuition: the total consumption in any state of the world cannot exceed the total resources (endowments) available in that state._

We now sum $\Sigma z_i$ over all individuals and we get:

$$
\Sigma \cdot \left( \sum_{i=1}^I z_i \right) = \sum_{i=1}^I \left[ T_i^a \left( \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde{c}_i] \right) \left( \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde{a}] - (1 + r)p \right) - \text{Cov}_{\pi}(\tilde{a}, \tilde{e}_i) \right].
$$

<b>Aggregate risk-tolerance</b>

We define by $T^a$ the <b>aggregate risk tolerance</b> as:

$$
T^a(c_M) = \sum_i^I T_i^a (c_i)
$$


Then if we use $\Sigma_i^I z_i = 0$ and $c_M = e_M$ in every state we get:

$$
0 = T^a (\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde e_M]) (\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde a]-(1 + r)p)- \operatorname{Cov}_{\pi}(\tilde a, \tilde e_M)
$$

If we isolate $p_k$ we obtain the <b>Consumption Based Capital Asset Pricing Model (CCAPM)</b>:

$$
p_k = \frac{1}{1 + r} \left\{ \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde{a}_k] - \frac{\text{Cov}_{\pi}(\tilde{e}_M, \tilde{a}_k)}{T^a \left( \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde{e}_M] \right)} \right\}.

$$

In the CCAPM we can decompose the price of a <b>risky security</b> as the sum of:

- the <b>discounted expected value of its payoff</b>
$$
\frac{\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde a_k]}{(1 + r)}
$$

- minus a <b>risk-premium</b> that is proportional to the correlation between the security's payoff and the aggregat risk:

$$
- \frac{\text{Cov}_{\pi}(\tilde{e}_M, \tilde{a}_k)}{T^a ( \mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde{e}_M] )} 
$$

_Important_: the CCAPM is <b>more general than the CAPM</b> $\implies$ it's explicitly derived from a market equilibrium.<br/>The CCAPM links the price of securities to <b>underlying movements </b> in the fundamental characteristics of the economy _(macroeconomic fluctuations in aggregate consumption or income)_.

<b>Interpretation of the CCAPM</b>

According to CCAPM, the expected return of an asset is equal to the risk-free rate if its payoff is _uncorrelated_ with the aggregate risk in the economy.

Like in the CAPM, the risk-premium can be <b>positive/negative</b> depending on the sign of the correlation:
- a positive correlation between the security and the aggregate risk $\tilde e_M$ <b>lowers the security price</b> 
    - note that the aggregate/total endowment in the economy represents the risk/uncertainty in the econommy $\implies$ when the economy is doing poorly, people value consumption more 
    - the security's payoff is high when the economy is doing well and the opposite $\implies$ the security is _less desiderable_ because it doesn't provide _insurance_ against bad states of the world $\implies$ the investors require <b>higher risk-premium</b> to hold this security
- a security that shows a negative correlation with the aggregate risk can be then used to <b>hedge against macroeconomic risk</b> $\implies$ the price of the security will be higher.

<b>The risk-free rate</b>

Under CARA and preferences and Gaussian risks, the risk free rate of interest:
- decreases with $\delta$ _(impatience effect)_ 
- increases with $\mathbb{E}_{\pi}[\tilde e_M]- e_M^0$ _(average endowment effect)_
- decreases with risk _(precautionary savings effect)_
