# <b><a id="1"></a>1. Introduction </b>
### <b> Why Not to Do Backtesting </b>

Contrary to popular belief, <b>backtesting is not a research tool</b>.  
Backtests can never prove that a strategy is a <b>true positive</b>.

Never develop a strategy solely through backtests.  
Strategies must be supported by <b>theory</b>, not only historical simulations.

Your theories must be <b>general</b> enough to explain particular cases, 
including extreme events (black swans).

## <b>Role of Machine Learning (ML)</b>

- ML helps <b>discover hidden variables</b>.
- ML itself does <b>not</b> reveal the functional relations between variables. Therefore:
  - Formulate a <b>theory</b> that binds the elements together.
  - <b>Test the theory</b> in different contexts (even where ML found no signal).

Once the theory has been tested, it should stand on its own; the <b>theory</b>, not the ML algorithm, should make predictions.

## <b>Uses of ML</b>

- <b>Existence</b> ŌĆö ML can indicate the presence of previously unknown relations by successfully predicting outcomes.
- <b>Importance</b> ŌĆö Feature-importance measures (e.g., Mean-Decrease Accuracy) reveal which variables matter more for model performance.
- <b>Causation</b> ŌĆö ML can support causal inference by comparing predictions.
- <b>Reductionism</b> ŌĆö Dimensionality reduction can reveal low-dimensional structure and clusters.
- <b>Retrieval</b> ŌĆö ML scans large datasets to find rare or interesting events.
- <b>Outlier Detection</b> ŌĆö ML identifies anomalies using learned patterns.

### <b>Types of Overfitting</b>

- <b>Train-set overfitting</b> ŌĆö model fits noise and signal in the training data (learns tiny irrelevant movements).
- <b>Test-set overfitting</b> ŌĆö repeatedly testing many models on the same test set and selecting the best-performing one.

A useful diagnostic is the **generalization gap**:
$$
\text{Generalization Gap } G = E_{\text{test}} - E_{\text{train}},
$$
where $E_{\text{train}}$ and $E_{\text{test}}$ are the expected errors (or loss) on training and test sets respectively. A large positive $G$ indicates overfitting.

# <b><a id="2"></a>2. Denoising and Detoning </b>
### <b> Covariance Matrix </b>

<b>Covariance matrices</b> are empirically obtained, so they contain a 
significant amount of <b>noise</b>.  
If used directly (e.g., for portfolio optimization), 
this noise can make results unstable.

<b>The Mar─ŹenkoŌĆōPastur Theorem</b>

The theorem tells us what the eigenvalues
 of a covariance (or correlation) matrix should look
  like if the data is completely random.
- If we compute the covariance matrix of asset returns, 
some of the eigenvalues <b> may just be noise. </b>
(random correlations that donŌĆÖt carry information).

Consider a matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times N}$ with:
  - $T$ observations (rows)
  - $N$ assets (columns)

Assume returns have mean $0$ and variance $\sigma^2$.
Compute the sample covariance matrix:

$$
C = \frac{1}{T}X^\top X
$$

Each eigenvalue $\lambda$ represents 
<b> how much variance is explained by a certain ŌĆ£directionŌĆØ </b>
(or factor) in the data.

If the data is purely random, the eigenvalues follow 
a known distribution: <b> the Mar─ŹenkoŌĆōPastur distribution.</b>

The theorem tells us that the eigenvalues will lie between two limits:
$$
    \lambda_{-} = \sigma^2 \big(1 - \sqrt{N/T}\big)^2, \quad

    \lambda_{+} = \sigma^2 \big(1 + \sqrt{N/T}\big)^2
$$


All eigenvalues between $\lambda_-$ and $\lambda_+$ are 
what we expect from random data, they are <b> just noise </b>. <br />
Eigenvalues <b>larger than </b> $\lambda_+$ (or smaller than $\lambda_-$) 
may represent true correlations or factors in the data.


The basic <b>Mar─ŹenkoŌĆōPastur theorem</b> 
assumes that <b>all eigenvalues are random</b>.  

In real financial data, this assumption is not valid ŌĆö some eigenvalues capture <b>true, non-random structure</b>, 
such as <b>market factor</b> (the first principal component, representing systematic market risk), <b>sector effects</b> or 
other common risk factors.

### <b>The Solution: Laloux Adjustment</b>

Laloux proposed an approach to <b>adjust</b> the Mar─ŹenkoŌĆōPastur model to account for the presence of <b>non-random eigenvalues</b>.

1. <b>Identify non-random eigenvalues</b>, typically the largest eigenvalues
 (e.g., the first one representing the <b>market mode</b>).

2. <b>Remove their contribution from the variance</b>  
   - Since these are not random, they should not inflate the estimated noise level.  
   - Adjust the variance:

   $$
   \sigma^2_{\text{adjusted}} = \sigma^2 \Bigg(1 - \frac{\lambda_{+}}{N}\Bigg)
   $$

3. <b>Recompute Mar─ŹenkoŌĆōPastur bounds</b>  
   - Use the adjusted variance to compute new limits:

   $$
   \lambda_{-} = \sigma^2_{\text{adjusted}} \Big(1 - \sqrt{\frac{N}{T}}\Big)^2,
   \qquad
   \lambda_{+} = \sigma^2_{\text{adjusted}} \Big(1 + \sqrt{\frac{N}{T}}\Big)^2
   $$

4. <b>Fit the theoretical PDF</b>  
   - Fit the Mar─ŹenkoŌĆōPastur distribution wit updated data to estimate how much variance is explained by random noise.


### <b> Denoising </b>

When you build a <b>correlation matrix </b> from asset returns, 
a lot of its eigenvalues come from noise.

If you use this noisy matrix directly (e.g. in portfolio optimization), 
the results are unstable and misleading.

So, the idea is to <b>denoise the matrix</b>: keep the 
information from the meaningful eigenvalues (the big ones 
above the Mar─ŹenkoŌĆōPastur threshold) and replace the noisy ones with something cleaner.

### <b>1. Decomposition of the denoised correlation matrix </b>

Start from the denoised correlation matrix $C_1$ with its eigen-decomposition:

$$
C_1 = W \Lambda W^\top,
$$

where $W=[w_1,\dots,w_N]$ (orthonormal eigenvectors) and $\Lambda=\mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1,\dots,\lambda_N)$.

Partition the eigenpairs into <b>market components</b>
(hopefully justone, typically the largest eigenvalue(s)) and <b>non-market (detoned) components</b>.
$$
W = [W_M \;|\; W_D],\qquad
$$
$$
\Lambda=\begin{bmatrix}\Lambda_M & 0\$$4pt] 0 & \Lambda_D\end{bmatrix},
$$
so that
$$
C_1 = W_M \Lambda_M W_M^\top \;+\; W_D \Lambda_D W_D^\top.
$$

### <b>2. Remove the market (detoning)</b>

Subtract the market part to get the detoned correlation matrix $C_2$:

$$
C_2 \;=\; C_1 \;-\; W_M \Lambda_M W_M^\top \;=\; W_D \Lambda_D W_D^\top .
$$

Because we removed at least one eigenvector, $C_2$ is singular (it has at least one zero eigenvalue).

Rescale to enforce unit diagonal if you need a correlation matrix: 
$$
C_2 \rightarrow \operatorname{diag}(C_2)^{-1/2}\,C_2\,\operatorname{diag}(C_2)^{-1/2}.
$$

### <b> 3. Portfolio optimization in the reduced (principal-component) space </b>

You cannot directly invert $C_2$. 
Instead <b>optimize on the non-zero</b> principal components
(the detoned subspace) and then map allocations back to the original assets.

Write a portfolio as a linear combination of the surviving eigenvectors:

$$
\omega \;=\; W_+ f,
$$

where $W_+$ is the matrix of eigenvectors that survived detoning
 (i.e. the columns of W_D), and f is the vector of allocations on those principal components.

Take the classic meanŌĆōvariance objective (risk aversion $\gamma$):

$$
\max_{\,\omega}\; \Big\{ \mu^\top \omega \;-\; \tfrac{\gamma}{2}\,\omega^\top C_1 \omega \Big\}.
$$

Substitute $\omega = W_+ f$. Using orthonormality and the block decomposition, the objective in $f$ becomes

$$
\max_{f}\; \Big\{ \mu^\top W_+ f \;-\; \tfrac{\gamma}{2}\, f^\top \Lambda_+ f \Big\},
$$
where $\Lambda_+$ is the diagonal matrix of the non-zero eigenvalues (the same as $\Lambda_D)$.

The first-order optimality condition yields the closed form

$$
f^\ast \;=\; \frac{1}{\gamma}\,\Lambda_+^{-1}\,W_+^\top \mu.
$$

Finally map back to original asset weights:

$$
{\;\omega^\ast \;=\; W_+ f^\ast \;=\; \frac{1}{\gamma}\,W_+ \Lambda_+^{-1} W_+^\top \,\mu\; \;}
$$

<b>Note:</b> because W_+ columns are orthonormal, $W_+^\top$ is the left-inverse of $W_+$. 
This expression avoids inverting the full (singular) $C_2$ and only requires inverting the diagonal $\Lambda_+$.

### <b>What is $f$?</b>

So $f_i$ tells you <b>how much you are investing in eigenportfolio i</b> (the eigenportfolio built from eigenvector $w_i$).

After detoning (removing market eigenvectors), we only keep the ŌĆ£survivingŌĆØ eigenvectors $W_+$.
Instead of trying to optimize in the full (possibly singular) asset space, we solve for $f$ in the reduced space:

$$
f^\ast = \frac{1}{\gamma} \Lambda_+^{-1} W_+^\top \mu
$$
Then we map back to original asset weights:
$$
\omega^\ast = W_+ f^\ast
$$
This way we only allocate risk to components that carry non-noise information.

### <b> Shrinkage </b>

<b>Shrinkage</b> is a technique to <b>improve the estimation of covariance (or correlation) matrices</b> when data is noisy or limited.

Empirical covariance matrices, estimated from historical returns, are often <b>unstable</b> or <b>ill-conditioned</b> 
(especially if you have many assets and few observations).
So, instead of using the raw covariance matrix $C_{\text{empirical}}$, combine it with a <b>target</b> $C_{\text{target}} $
that is more stable (like the identity matrix or an average correlation matrix).

Formally:
$$
C_{\text{shrunk}} = \alpha \, C_{\text{target}} + (1-\alpha) \, C_{\text{empirical}}
$$

$\alpha \in [0,1]$ = shrinkage intensity.
    - $\alpha = 0$ : no shrinkage (use raw covariance)
    - $\alpha = 1$ : full shrinkage (use only the target)

# <b><a id="3"></a>3.Distance Metrics </b>

### <b> Correlation </b>

Correlation $\rho[X,Y]$ measures 
<b> linear codependence </b> but is not a metric 
(fails nonnegativity and triangle inequality).

We therefore a metric that gives a geometry/topology to compare and cluster data, so define:
$$
d_\rho[X,Y] = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}(1 - \rho[X,Y])}
$$

$ d_\rho[X,Y] $ standardize X,Y into zero-mean, unit-variance vectors x,y.

If we look at the Euclidean distance:
    $$
    d[x,y] = \sqrt{\sum_{t=1}^T (x_t - y_t)^2}
    $$
it Expands to:
    $$
    d[x,y] = \sqrt{2T(1 - \rho[x,y])} = \sqrt{4T \, d_\rho[X,Y]}
    $$
    
So $d_\rho$ is proportional to a Euclidean distance, 
hence inherits metric properties.

$d_\rho \in [0,1]$ since $\rho \in [-1,1]$.

Positively correlated show small distance, negatively correlated 
 large distance.
<br /> This makes sense in <b>long-only portfolios</b>
 (negatively correlated assets act as diversifiers).

### <b> Absolute Correlation </b>

Sometimes, in long-short portfolios, 
we want <b>negatively correlated assets</b> to be considered similar.

So we can define <b> absolute correlation </b> by:

$$
d_{|\rho|}[X,Y] = \sqrt{1 - |\rho[X,Y]|}
$$

That is still a metric but highly negatively correlated 
assets have small distance, i.e. treated as ŌĆ£close.ŌĆØ

### <b> Problems of Correlation </b>
- correlation quantifies the <b>linear codependecy</b>of two assets
- itŌĆÖs highly<b> influenced by outliers </b>
- its application<b> beyond the multivariate Normal case </b> is questionable 
(Within the Gaussian world correlation is a clean, sufficient measure of dependence, outside it correlation can be unstable, incomplete, or misleading).

So better to introduce other metrics.

### <b> Entropy of a Discrete Random Variable </b>

Shannon entropy measures the average amount of
 information needed to describe the outcome of a random variable or the uncertainty or diversification 

For random variable $X$ with support $S_X$ and pmf $p(x)$:

$$
H(X) = - \sum_{x \in S_X} p(x) \, \log p(x)
$$

with the convention that:    $ 0 \log 0 := 0 $ (limit argument).

If event x has probability $p(x)$, its ŌĆ£surpriseŌĆØ is $\frac{1}{p(x)}$, so
 low probabilitybrings= high surprise.

<b> Entropy </b> can be interpreted as the expected surprise: it'sa
 the <b>average uncertainty</b> in  $X$.

When all probability mass is on a single outcome, there's no uncertainty and $H(x) = 0$.
The <b>Maximum entropy</b> is reached when $X$ is uniform over its support and has

$$
        
        H(X) = \log |S_X|
$$

### <b> Joint Entropy </b>

For random variables $X$, $Y$ with joint pmf %p(x,y)$:

$$
    
    H(X,Y) = - \sum_{x \in S_X} \sum_{y \in S_Y} p(x,y) \, \log p(x,y)
  $$  
It measures the <b>uncertainty of the pair</b> $(X,Y)$ considered together.

<b> Propertries</b>:

- $$ H(X,Y) = H(Y,X) $$
    
    
- $$    H(X,X) = H(X) $$
    
- $$   H(X,Y) \geq \max\{H(X), H(Y)\} $$
        
-  $$H(X,Y) \leq H(X) + H(Y)$$
    
(equality holds if $X$ and $Y$ are independent.)

### <b> Conditional Entropy </b>

The conditional entropy of $X$ given $Y$ is:

$$
H(X|Y) = \sum_{y \in S_Y} p(y) \sum_{x \in S_X} p(x|y) \, \log \frac{1}{p(x|y)}
= - \sum_{y \in S_Y} p(y) \sum_{x \in S_X} p(x|y) \log p(x|y)

$$

$H(X|Y)$ measures the remaining uncertainty about X once Y is known.

- Conditional entropy is always Ōēż entropy:
$$
H(X|Y) \le H(X)
$$
- Knowing $Y$ can never <b>increase</b>the uncertainty about X.
- Equality occurs if $X$ and $Y$ are independent ($Y$ gives no info about $X$).

- Entropy of a variable given itself**:
$$
H(X|X) = 0
$$
If you already know X, thereŌĆÖs no uncertainty left.

- Chain rule for entropy:


$$
H(X,Y) = H(Y) + H(X|Y) = H(X) + H(Y|X)
$$

Total uncertainty about $(X,Y)$
 can be decomposed into uncertainty of $Y$
  and residual uncertainty of $X$ after knowing $Y$.

### <b>KullbackŌĆōLeibler Divergence </b>

For two discrete probability distributions 
$p$ and $q$ defined on the same set $S_X$:

$$
D_{KL}(p \parallel q) = \sum_{x \in S_X} p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}
$$

- $p(x)$ = true probability of x
- $q(x)$ = reference or approximate probability of $x$
- Condition: if $q(x) = 0 \Rightarrow p(x) = 0$ to avoid division by zero

- KL divergence measures <b>how much</b> $p$ <b>diverges from</b> $q$.
- It quantifies the ŌĆ£extra informationŌĆØ required if we encode data using $q$ instead 
of the true distribution $p$.

<b> Propertries </b>
- $$ D_{KL}(p \parallel q) \ge 0 $$
Equality occurs <b>only when</b> $p = q$.
- $$ D_{KL}(p \parallel q) \neq D_{KL}(q \parallel p) $$
(Order matters, so KL divergence is <b>not a metric</b>)

- <b>Triangle inequality fails</b>, so  KL divergence does not satisfy all properties of a distance metric.

- If $$ q(x) = 1/|S_X|$$ (uniform distribution),$$ D_{KL}(p \parallel q)$$
 measures <b>how far</b> p is from uniform.


### <b> Cross Entropy </b>

For two discrete probability distributions $p$ (true distribution) and $q$
 (approximation or model) defined on the same set $S_X$:

$$
H(p, q) = - \sum_{x \in S_X} p(x) \log q(x)
$$

- $p(x)$ = true probability of outcome x
- $q(x)$ = probability assigned by a model or reference distribution
- It measure of uncertainty of $X$ when using $q$ instead of the true $p$.

<b>Entropy:</b> $$H(p) = -\sum p(x)\log p(x)$$ measures uncertainty using
 the true distribution.

<b>Cross-entropy</b>: $$H(p,q) = H(p) + D_{KL}(p \parallel q)$$
    - Cross-entropy is always <b>greater than or equal to true entropy</b>.
    - The difference $$H(p,q) - H(p) = D_{KL}(p \parallel q)$$ measures how ŌĆ£wrongŌĆØ $q$ is.

### <b> Mutual Information </b> 

Mutual information measures <b>how much knowing one variable reduces
 uncertainty about another</b>. 

$$
I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X|Y)
$$

- $H(X)$ = entropy of $X$ (uncertainty about $X$)
- $H(X|Y)$ = conditional entropy of $X$ given $Y$ 
Equivalently:

$$
I(X;Y) = \sum_{x \in S_X} \sum_{y \in S_Y} p(x,y) \log \frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)}
$$

<b> Propertries </b>

- <b>Non-negative</b>: $$I(X;Y) \ge 0$$
  $$MI = 0$$ if $X$ and $Y$ are independent (knowing Y gives no info about X)
- <b>Symmetric</b>:  $$I(X;Y) = I(Y;X) $$
- <b>Upper bound</b>: $$I(X;Y) \le \min\{H(X), H(Y)\} $$
- <b>Not a metric</b>: Does not satisfy triangle inequality
- <b>Grouping / decomposition</b>:
  $$ 
    I(X;Y;Z) = I(X;Y) + I((X,Y);Z)
  $$  

Mutual information can be expressed as a KL divergence:
$$
I(X;Y) = D_{KL}(p(x,y) \parallel p(x)p(y))
$$
It measures <b>>how far the joint distribution is from independence</b>.

# <b><a id="4"></a>4. Optimal Clustering </b>

Clustering groups objects (items) 
by similarity using their features. 
The goal is to produce clusters where <b>intra-cluster similarity is high</b>
 and <b>inter-cluster similarity is low</b>. 
 Clustering is a form of <b>unsupervised learning</b>
  because we do not provide labelled examples.



### <b> Proximity matrix </b>

A proximity matrix measures how "close" or "similar" 
objects in a dataset are to each other.

- For a dataset with $n$ elements, the proximity matrix $P$ 
is an $n \times n$ matrix.
- Each entry $P[i,j]$ encodes the closeness between object $i$ and object $j$.

Typical definitions:
- $P[i,j] = \text{similarity}(x_i, x_j)$ 
- $P[i,j] = \text{distance}(x_i, x_j)$


Note: A symmetric proximity matrix with zeros on the diagonal is common when using pairwise distances.

Two main families of clustering:

### <b>Hierarchical clustering </b>
- Produces a nested sequence of partitions (a dendrogram).
- Top: a single cluster containing all points. Bottom: each point alone.
- Two approaches:
  - <b>Agglomerative</b> (bottom-up): start with singletons and merge.
  - <b>Divisive</b> (top-down): start with one cluster and split.
- No need to specify $K$ up-front (but you choose a cut in the dendrogram to get $K$ clusters).

### <b>Partitional clustering </b>
- Partitions the dataset into $K$ disjoint clusters.
- Each object belongs to exactly one cluster.
- Example: <b>k-means</b>, <b>k-medoids</b>, <b>spectral clustering</b>.
- Often requires the number of clusters $K$ as input.


### <b>Practical issues</b>

- <b>Curse of dimensionality</b>: when the number of features 
is large relative to observations,
  distances become less informative and clustering can degrade. 
  Dimensionality reduction (PCA, t-SNE, UMAP) or feature selection helps.
- <b>Initialization randomness</b>: algorithms like 
k-means are sensitive to initial centroids. 



Because partitional methods require $K$, 
several methods exist to estimate a good $K$:

### <b> Elbow method </b>
- Compute within-cluster sum of squared errors (WCSS) for different $K$.
- Plot WCSS vs $K$. The "elbow" point (where marginal gain drops) is a reasonable $K$.

### <b>Silhouette analysis </b>
- Measures how well each point lies within its cluster compared to the nearest other cluster.

For an observation $i$:

- Let $a_i$ = average distance from $i$ to all other points **in the same cluster** (intra-cluster distance).
- Let $b_i$ = minimum over other clusters of the average distance from $i$ to points in that other cluster (nearest inter-cluster distance).

The <b>silhouette coefficient</b> for $i$ is
$$
S_i = \frac{b_i - a_i}{\max(a_i, b_i)}
$$

- $S_i \approx 1$: well clustered (close to own cluster, far from others).
- $S_i \approx 0$: on or near cluster boundary.
- $S_i < 0$: likely misclassified (closer to another cluster).

The <b>average silhouette score</b>
 across all points is a scalar summary 
 used to compare different $K$: higher is better.

### <b> Practical checklist for clustering </b>

1. <b>Preprocess </b>
   - Standardize / normalize features when distances are used.
   - Handle missing values.
   - Reduce dimensionality if needed.

2. <b>Choose distance / similarity </b>
   - Euclidean for continuous, cosine for directional data, Jaccard for binary sets, etc.

3. <b>Choose algorithm </b>
   - Hierarchical if you want a dendrogram and no fixed $K$.
   - K-means/k-medoids for large datasets with spherical-ish clusters.

4. <b>Select $K$</b>
   - Use elbow, silhouette, gap statistic, or domain knowledge.

5. <b>Validate </b>
   - Visual inspection (2D/3D embedding).
   - Cluster compactness and separation metrics (silhouette, DaviesŌĆōBouldin).
   - If possible, external validation against known labels.

Once we run a clustering algorithm 
(e.g., k-means), we still need to evaluate 
how good the clustering result is. 
This section introduces a quality measure and a procedure 
to improve clustering by selectively reclustering low-quality clusters.



### <b>Quality Measure $q$ </b>

Define the <b>overall clustering quality</b>:

$$
q = \frac{E[S_i]}{\sqrt{V[S_i]}}
$$

Where:

- $E[S_i]$ = mean of silhouette scores (average clustering quality).
- $V[S_i]$ = variance of silhouette scores (consistency across points).

Higher $q$ means better clustering (high average silhouette + low variability).
Low $q$ means that some clusters might be poorly formed or unstable.



### <b>Base Clustering Algorithm </b>

At the **base level**, the procedure works as follows:

1. <b>Input</b>
   - Start from the <b>observation (distance/similarity) matrix</b>.

2. <b>Outer Loop (over $K$)</b>
   - For $k = 2,3,\dots,N$:
     - Run k-means clustering with $k$ clusters.
     - Compute the quality measure $q$ from silhouette scores.
     - Store results for that $k$.

3. <b>Inner Loop (over initializations) </b>
   - Repeat the above with **different random initializations** of centroids.
   - This reduces sensitivity to random starts.

4. <b>Selection </b>
   - Choose the clustering result with the <b>highest quality</b> $q$.
   - This yields:
     - Optimal number of clusters $K$.
     - Best initialization (most stable clustering result).

Even after selecting the best $K$ and initialization, <b>some clusters
 can still be poor quality</b>.


### <b> Reclustering Low-Quality Clusters </b>

To refine the clustering, we <b>recluster only the bad clusters</b>.


1. <b>Start with Base Clustering Result </b>
   - We already have $K$ clusters from the base algorithm.
   - For each cluster $k$, compute its <b>quality score </b>
   $q_k$ (mean silhouette score of its members).

2. <b>Identify Poor-Quality Clusters </b>
   - Compute overall average quality:
     $$
     q = \frac{1}{K} \sum_{k=1}^{K} q_k
     $$
   - Identify clusters with $q_k < q$ ŌåÆ ŌĆ£below-average quality.ŌĆØ
   - Let $K_1$ = number of such clusters.

3.  <b> Decision Rule Based on $K_1$ </b>
   - If $K_1 \leq 1$:
     - No meaningful reclustering, so return base clustering.
   - If $K_1 \geq 2$:
     - <b>Recluster only those $K_1$ clusters </b>:
       - Build a <b>new observation matrix </b> for elements of these clusters.
       - <b>Rerun the base algorithm </b>
        (search over $K$ and initializations) on this reduced subset.

4. <b>Check Improvement </b>
   - Compare **average quality before vs. after reclustering**.
   - If quality improved:
     - Merge the unchanged good clusters with the newly reclustered clusters.
   - Else:
     - Keep the original base clustering result.

# <b><a id="5"></a>5. Financial Labels </b>

Supervised learning aims to predict an output $y$ given features $X$.

### <b> Types of Supervised Learning </b>

- <b>Regression problems</b>
  - Predict a continuous target (real numbers).
  - Examples drawn from infinite population (countable like integers or uncountable like reals).

- <b>Classification problems</b>
  - Predict a discrete label from a finite set (e.g., ${0, 1}$, ${bearish, bullish}$).

### <b> Fixed-Horizon Labeling </b>

We have a <b>feature matrix</b>:

- $X = \{X_i\}_{i=1}^{I}$ 
- For each observation $X_i$:
  - Let $t_{i,0}$ = index of bar where features are sampled.
  - Define a <b>fixed horizon</b> $h$.
  - Compute return over horizon $h$:

$$
r_{t_{i,0}; t_{i,1}} = \frac{p_{t_{i,1}}}{p_{t_{i,0}}} - 1, \quad \text{where } t_{i,1} = t_{i,0} + h
$$


Assign label $y_i \in \{-1, 0, 1\}$ based on return:

$$
y_i =
\begin{cases}
-1 & \text{if } r_{t_{i,0}; t_{i,1}} < -\tau \\
0  & \text{if } |r_{t_{i,0}; t_{i,1}}| \leq \tau \\
+1 & \text{if } r_{t_{i,0}; t_{i,1}} > \tau
\end{cases}
$$

Where:

- $\tau$ = constant <b>return threshold</b>.
- Time bars = bars sampled at regular time intervals.
- Result: <b>fixed time horizons</b> for all samples.

### <b> Concerns with Fixed-Horizon Labeling </b>

- Returns show intraday volatility patterns (open/close more volatile).
- Constant $\tau$ transfers seasonality into labels, label distribution becomes **non-stationary**.

<b>Solution:</b> use tick/volume/dollar bars instead of time bars or standardize returns using volatility estimate:

$$
z_{t_{i,0};t_{i,1}} = \frac{r_{t_{i,0}; t_{i,1}}}{\sigma_{t_{i,0}; t_{i,1}}}
$$

where $\sigma_{t_{i,0}; t_{i,1}}$ = predicted volatility.
Then apply labeling rule to $z$-scores.



### <b> Triple-Barrier Method </b>

Labels should represent **actual trading outcomes** ŌĆö profit, loss, or timeout.

This method sets <b>three barriers</b>:

1. Upper Horizontal Barrier: Profit-taking (success).
2. Lower Horizontal Barrier:  Stop-loss (failure).
3. Vertical Barrier: Maximum holding period (timeout).


### <b>Step-by-Step Procedure </b>

1. Start at $t_{i,0}$ (where features $X_i$ observed).
2. Monitor price path forward until one barrier is hit.
3. Assign label $y_i$:
   - If <b>upper barrier</b> reached first: $y_i = +1$.
   - If <b>lower barrier</b> reached first: $y_i = -1$.
   - If <b>vertical barrier </b> reached first:
     - Option 1:  $y_i = 0$ (neutral).
     - Option 2:  $y_i = \text{sgn}(r_{t_{i,0};t_{i,1}})$ (final return sign).

### <b> Trend Scanning Method </b>

Instead of setting a fixed horizon 
$h$, profit-taking, or stop-loss levels, 
<b>trend scanning</b> lets trends <b>run naturally</b> until they end.  

 Label each observation as part of:

- <b>Uptrend:</b> $y_t = +1$
- <b>Downtrend:</b> $y_t = -1$
- <b>No trend:</b> $y_t = 0$

### <b>Define a Trend </b>

Fit a <b>local linear regression</b>
 over a window of $L$ observations:

$$
x_{t+l} = \beta_0 + \beta_1 l + \varepsilon_{t+l}, \quad l = 0,1,\dots,L-1
$$

- $\beta_1$ = slope, direction & strength of trend.
- $\varepsilon_{t+l}$ = residuals (noise).


### <b> Test Statistical Significance </b>

Compute <b>t-statistic</b> of the slope:

$$
\hat{t}_{\beta_1} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_1}{\hat{\sigma}_{\hat{\beta}_1}}
$$

- Large $|\hat{t}_{\beta_1}|$: strong trend evidence.
- Near 0: no clear trend.


### <b> Assign Labels </b>

For each $t$:

$$
y_t =
\begin{cases}
+1 & \text{if } \hat{t}_{\beta_1} > \tau \quad \text{(Uptrend)} \\
-1 & \text{if } \hat{t}_{\beta_1} < -\tau \quad \text{(Downtrend)} \\
0 & \text{if } |\hat{t}_{\beta_1}| \le \tau \quad \text{(No trend)}
\end{cases}
$$

Where $\tau$ = critical t-value threshold.

### <b> Optimization over $L$ </b>

Because $L$ is unknown, try a <b>set of candidate window lengths</b>
 and pick $L$ that **maximizes** $|\hat{\beta}_1|$ (strongest trend evidence).

### <b> Meta-Labeling </b>

Meta-labeling is a **two-model approach**:

1. <b>Primary model</b>: predicts position direction (long/short).
2. <b>Secondary (meta) model</b>: predicts whether that position will be profitable (filtering false positives).

The goal is to reduce exposure to losing 
trades and size positions proportionally to predicted success probability.



### <b> Expected Value & Risk </b>

Let $p$ = probability of profit and $\pi$ = payoff magnitude.

The expected value is:


$$
\mu = p \pi + (1 - p)(-\pi) = \pi (2p - 1)
$$

- $p > 0.5 \Rightarrow \mu > 0$: positive expected profit.
- $p = 0.5 \Rightarrow \mu = 0$: no edge.


### <b> Expected Variance </b>

$$
\sigma^2 = 4 \pi^2 p(1-p)
$$

- Highest when $p = 0.5$ (most uncertain).
- Shrinks as $p \to 0$ or $1$ (certainty increases).


### <b> Sharpe Ratio </b> 

Standardize risk/return with Sharpe-like measure:

$$
z = \frac{\mu}{\sigma} =
\frac{\pi(2p-1)}{2\pi \sqrt{p(1-p)}} =
\frac{2p-1}{2\sqrt{p(1-p)}}
$$

- $z > 0$: attractive opportunity.
- $z < 0$: avoid trade.


### <b> Mapping to Position Size </b>

Convert $z$ to position size $m \in [-1, 1]$:

$$
m = 2 \, Z(z) - 1
$$

Where $Z(\cdot)$ = CDF of standard normal.

- $z = 0 \Rightarrow m = 0$ (no trade)
- Large $z > 0 \Rightarrow m \to 1$ (max long)
- Large $z < 0 \Rightarrow m \to -1$ (max short)


### <b> Meta-Labeling for Position Size with Multiple Classifiers </b>

Suppose we have $n$ meta-labeling classifiers:

$$
y_i \in \{0,1\}, \quad i=1,\dots,n
$$

Sum of predictions:

$$
\sum_{i=1}^n y_i \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)
$$

- Mean: $E[\sum y_i] = np$
- Variance: $Var[\sum y_i] = np(1-p)$

As $n \to \infty$, by <b>de MoivreŌĆōLaplace </b>:

$$
\frac{\sum y_i - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)
$$

Thus, average prediction:

$$
\hat{p} = \frac{1}{n}\sum y_i \sim N\Big(p, \frac{p(1-p)}{n}\Big)
$$



### <b> Hypothesis Test</b>

Null hypothesis: $H_0: p = 0.5$ (no predictive edge)

t-statistic:

$$
t = \frac{\hat{p} - 0.5}{\sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}}
$$

Follows Student-t distribution with $n-1$ d.o.f.



### <b> Mapping t to Bet Size </b>

Convert $t$ to bet size $m \in [-1, 1]$:

$$
m = 2 \, T_{n-1}(t) - 1
$$

Where $T_{n-1}(\cdot)$ = CDF of Student-t distribution.

- $t=0 \Rightarrow m=0$ (no bet)
- Large $t > 0 \Rightarrow m \to 1$ (max long)
- Large $t < 0 \Rightarrow m \to -1$ (max short)

In practical:

- $\hat{p} \to 1$: strong long conviction ŌåÆ large $m > 0$.
- $\hat{p} \to 0$: strong short conviction ŌåÆ large $m < 0$.
- $\hat{p} \approx 0.5$: no edge ŌåÆ $m \approx 0$ (no position).

# <b><a id="6"></a>6. Feature Importance Analysis</b>

### <b> P-Values (Statistical Significance) </b>

 Probability of observing the estimated coefficient 
 if the true <b> coefficient = 0 </b>.

It tests whether a<b> feature has any effect</b>, not its predictive power.

The p-value is a probability ŌĆö but not the probability that your hypothesis is true.
It answers a very specific question:

_ŌĆ£If the null hypothesis were true, how likely is 
it to observe a result as extreme (or more extreme) than what I 
actually observed?ŌĆØ_

<b> Limitations </b>
- False positives/negatives if model assumptions fail.
- Not robust with <b>multicollinearity</b> (correlated features).
- Measures statistical relevance, not:
  - Effect size
  - Predictive power
  - Causality

A low p-value doesn't mean feature is ŌĆ£importantŌĆØ,
it only means the effect is statistically detectable.



### <b> Mean Decrease Impurity (MDI) ŌĆö Tree-Based Models </b>

[A clear video explanation can be found here, credits to StatQuest](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_L39rN6gz7Y)


1. At each node pick feature $X_f$ and threshold $\tau$ to split:
   - Left: $X_f < \tau$
   - Right: $X_f \ge \tau$
2. Measure impurity with e.g.:
   - Entropy: $H(t) = -\sum_c p_c \log_2 p_c$
   - Gini: $G(t) = 1 - \sum_c p_c^2$
3. Information gain (impurity reduction) for a split:
   $$
   \Delta g(t,f) = i(t) - \frac{N(t_0)}{N(t)} i(t_0) - \frac{N(t_1)}{N(t)} i(t_1)
   $$
4. Feature importance (MDI) for feature $f$: sum of weighted $\Delta g(t,f)$ over all nodes where $f$ was used.


### <b>Mean Decrease Accuracy (MDA) </b>

This algorithm measures out-of-sample importance.

1. Fit model and compute cross-validated performance (baseline).
2. For each feature:
   - Shuffle that feature's values (breaks relationship with target).
   - Recalculate cross-validated performance.
3. $MDA$ = performance(before shuffle) ŌłÆ performance(after shuffle).

<b>Interpretation </b>
- Large positive MDA means that feature important.
- MDA near zero means that feature irrelevant.
- Negative MDA means feature damaging performance (model improved after shuffling).


### <b> Model Performance Metrics </b>



### <b>Negative Average Likelihood (NegAL) </b>
  $$
  \text{NegAL} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=1}^N \sum_{k=1}^K y_{n,k} \, p_{n,k}
  $$
  where $y_{n,k}=1$ if observation $n$ has true label $k$, else 0; $p_{n,k}$ is predicted prob.

<b>Range:</b>$0 \le \text{NegAL} \le 1$.  
<b>Higher =is better</b> (closer to 1 the model assigns higher probability to true labels).

### <b>Probability-Weighted Accuracy (PWA) </b>

  $$
  \text{PWA} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=1}^N y_n \, p_n
  $$
  where $y_n = 1$ if prediction correct else 0; $p_n = \max_k p_{n,k}$.

It weighs correct predictions by model confidence.


### <b> Feature Clustering for Importance </b>

With highly codependent features the individual importance unreliable,
so it's commonoto cluster features to analyze them better.

1. <b>Represent features in a metric space </b>
   - Options:
     - Correlation-based distances (linear dependencies).
     - Information-theoretic metrics (e.g., Variation of Information) to capture nonlinear redundancies.
2. <b>Cluster formation </b>
   - Apply an ONC (Optimal Number of Clusters) algorithm:
     - Finds number of clusters and assignments.
     - Partitional assignment: each feature belongs to exactly one cluster.
     - Within-cluster features share much information; across clusters share little.
3. <b>Optional residualization </b>
   - For cluster $k$, regress each feature $X_{n,i}\in D_k$ on features from earlier clusters $\cup_{l<k} D_l$:
     $$
     X_{n,i} = \alpha_i + \sum_{j\in \cup_{l<k} D_l} \beta_{i,j} X_{n,j} + \varepsilon_{n,i}
     $$
   - Replace $X_i$ with residuals $\hat\varepsilon_i$.
   - If regressors are too many, reduce dimensionality inside clusters.

<b>Clustered Importance</b>
- Apply **MDI** or **MDA** at the **cluster** level (not single-feature).
- Interpret which groups of features drive model performance.

# <b><a id="7"></a>7. Portfolio Construction</b>

Given $N$ assets the expected excess returns $\mu$ (vector $N\times 1$) and 
the covariance matrix $V$ ($N\times N$), the goal is to find portfolio
weights  $\omega$ that minimize <b>portfolio variance </b>.


### <b> Classical MeanŌĆōVariance Optimization (Variance Minimization) </b>


$$
\min_{\omega}\ \tfrac{1}{2}\,\omega^\top V \omega
\quad\text{s.t.}\quad
\omega^\top a = 1
$$

The vector $a$ encodes the portfolio constraint (e.g., fully invested: $a = \mathbf{1}_N$).

Lagrangian:

$$
\mathcal{L}\big(\tfrac12\omega, \lambda\big) = \tfrac12 \omega^\top V \omega - \lambda(\omega^\top a - 1)
$$


<b>Optimal weights: </b>

$$
{\ \omega^{*} = \frac{V^{-1} a}{a^\top V^{-1} a}\ }
$$


### <b>Useful Special Cases </b>

- <b>Equal weights under isotropic variance</b>: $a = \mathbf{1}_N,\ V = \sigma^2 I_N$, $\omega^* = \tfrac{1}{N}\mathbf{1}_N$                                                       
- <b>Inverse-variance </b>: $a = \mathbf{1}_N,\ V$ diagonal,  $\omega^*_n \propto \dfrac{1}{V_{n,n}}$                              
- <b>Minimum-variance</b>: $a = \mathbf{1}_N$, $\omega^* = \dfrac{V^{-1}\mathbf{1}_N}{\mathbf{1}_N^\top V^{-1}\mathbf{1}_N}$ 
- <b>Maximum Sharpe </b>: $a = \mu$, $\omega^* = \dfrac{V^{-1}\mu}{\mathbf{1}_N^\top V^{-1}\mu}$        


### <b>Numerical Stability & Conditioning Problem </b>

Let $C$ be the standardized correlation matrix of $V$. 
The solution needs to calculate on $V^{-1}$.

If assets are <b>highly correlated </b> ($|\rho|\approx 1$), then $C$ is **ill-conditioned**:
  - Condition number large means that $V^{-1}$ numerically unstable (small 
  errors in $V$ estimate produce large swings in $V^{-1}$).
We need Markowitz when correlations exist, but those same correlations make the solution unstable.

<b>Practical fixes </b>:
- Shrinkage estimators (LedoitŌĆōWolf)
- Factor models (lower-dimensional structure)
- Regularization: add $\delta I$ to $V$ before inversion ($V+\delta I$)
- Robust/resampled optimization, constraints on weights (box, turnover), or use of priors (BlackŌĆōLitterman)


### <b> Nested Clustered Optimization (NCO) </b>

THe goal is tos tabilize Markowitz by solving smaller, 
better-conditioned subproblems.


1. <b>Cluster the correlation matrix</b> to find groups of highly correlated assets.
2. <b>Intracluster optimization</b>:
   - For each cluster, compute intracluster weights using the denoised covariance (call it `cov1`).
   - Smaller cluster size means better-conditioned covariance estimates.
   - With very correlated cluster, the min-variance tends toward equal weights, so instability is limited.
3. <b>Intercluster optimization </b>:
   - Build reduced covariance (`cov2`) between clusters.
   - `cov2` is by construction close to diagonal; invertibility and conditioning are much improved.
4. <b>Combine</b>:
   - Final asset weights = (intra-cluster weights) ├Ś (inter-cluster weights).

<b> Why NCO helps </b>
- Dimensionality reduction: each inversion is on smaller matrices.
- Denoising at step 1 reduces noisy eigenvalues.
- Aggregation produces a near-diagonal inter-cluster covariance, improving stability.

# <b><a id="8"></a>8. Testing Set Overfitting </b>

### <b>Selection Bias under Multiple Testing (SBuMT) </b>

SBuMT occurs when a researcher tests many strategies on the same historical dataset 
and then reports only the best-performing one.

This process leads to <b>false discoveries </b>, strategies that look profitable 
in-sample but fail in live trading.

### <b>True vs. False Investment Strategies & Precision/Recall </b>


The total number of strategies is

$$
s = s_T + s_F
$$

where:
- $s_T$ = number of <b>true </b> strategies (positive expected return)
- $s_F$ = number of <b>false </b> strategies (zero or negative expected return)

The <b>Odds ratio</b> of true to false strategies is:

$$
\theta = \frac{s_T}{s_F}
$$

In finance, signal-to-noise ratio is usually very low, meaning most strategies are false.

So the number of true strategies is:

$$
s_T = \frac{\theta s}{1+\theta}
$$

and the number of false ones:
$$
s_F = \frac{s}{1+\theta}
$$

---

### <b>Statistical Errors </b>

- <b>False Positive Rate (Type I error):</b> $\alpha$
    - <b>False Positives (FP):</b> $FP = \alpha s_F$
    - <b>True Negatives (TN):</b> $TN = (1-\alpha)s_F$

- <b>False Negative Rate (Type II error):</b> $\beta$
    - <b>False Negatives (FN):</b> $FN = \beta s_T$
    - <b>True Positives (TP):</b> $TP = (1-\beta)s_T$


The <b>Precision (Positive Predictive Value)</b> is defined as:

$$
\text{precision} =
\frac{TP}{TP+FP}
= \frac{(1-\beta)s_T}{(1-\beta)s_T + \alpha s_F}
= \frac{(1-\beta)\theta}{(1-\beta)\theta+\alpha}
$$

and the <b>Recall (Sensitivity)</b> as:

$$
\text{recall} =
\frac{TP}{TP+FN}
= 1-\beta
$$



Precision strongly depends on odds ratio ╬Ė.

A discovered strategy is **more likely to be false than true** if:

$$
(1-\beta)\theta < \alpha
$$

---

### <b>Type $I$ Error Across Multiple Trials </b> 
_(False alarm: see a signal where none exists, identify a false positive)_

In a <b>single trial </b>:
    - $P$(Type I error) = $\alpha$
    - $P$(No Type I error) = $1-\alpha$

with <b>K independent trials</b>:
    - $P$(No Type I error in any trial): $(1-\alpha)^K$
    - $P$(At least one Type I error) = <b>Family-Wise Error Rate (FWER): </b>

$$
\alpha_K = 1 - (1-\alpha)^K
$$

FWER <b>increases with K </b>, the more strategies you test, 
the higher the chance of finding a false positive.



### <b>Type $II$ Error Across Multiple Trials </b> 
_(Missed discovery: fail to detect a true strategy)_

In a <b>single trial:</b>:
-  $P$(Type II error) = $\beta$

with <b>K trials</b>P(Missing all positives):

-  $P$(Type II error) $ =\beta_K = \beta^K $



As $K \uparrow$:
- $\alpha_K \uparrow$ (false positive risk grows)
- $\beta_K \downarrow$ (less likely to miss all true positives)


### <b>Precision & Recall Adjusted for Multiple Testing </b>

The <b>Adjusted Precision</b> can be defined as:

$$
\text{precision}_K =
\frac{(1-\beta^K)\theta}{(1-\beta^K)\theta + \alpha_K}
= \frac{(1-\beta^K)\theta}{(1-\beta^K)\theta + 1 - (1-\alpha)^K}
$$

and the <b>Adjusted Recall</b> as:

$$
\text{recall}_K = 1 - \beta^K
$$

As $K$ increases:
> - <b>Recall improves</b> (lower chance of missing all true strategies).
> - <b>Precision usually drops</b> (higher risk of including false positives).


### <b> The Sharpe Ratio Framework </b>


Given the strategy excess returns:

$$
r_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2), \quad t=1,\dots,T
$$

and the <b>Population (true) Sharpe Ratio:</b>

$$
SR = \frac{\mu}{\sigma}
$$

The <b>Sample (estimated) Sharpe Ratio</b> is defined as:

$$
\widehat{SR} = \frac{\bar{r}}{s_r}
$$

where $\bar{r}$ is sample mean return, $s_r$ sample standard deviation.

The distribution of Sample SR can be defined as following.

#### <b>Case 1: IID Normal Returns </b>


$$
\sqrt{T}\,(\widehat{SR} - SR)
\xrightarrow{d}
\mathcal{N}\!\left(0, \, 1 + \frac{SR^2}{2}\right)
$$

#### <b>Case 2: Non-Normal Returns </b>

Empirical evidence: hedge fund returns often exhibit:
- Negative skewness
- Positive excess kurtosis


$$
\sqrt{T}\,(\widehat{SR} - SR)
\xrightarrow{d}
\mathcal{N}\!\Bigl(0, \,
1 + \frac{SR^2}{2}\Bigl(1-\gamma_3 SR + \frac{\gamma_4-3}{4}SR^2\Bigr)
\Bigr)
$$

where:
- $\gamma_3$ = skewness
- $\gamma_4$ = kurtosis

Special case: for Normal returns ($\gamma_3=0,\ \gamma_4=3$), we recover the initial formula.



### <b>SBuMT and Maximum Sharpe Ratios </b>


Given  $K$ independent strategies with Sharpe ratios:

$$
SR_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0, V[SR_k]), \quad k = 1,\dots,K
$$

the <b>Expected Maximum of K Sharpe Ratios </b>

$$
\mathbb{E}\!\big[\max SR_k\big]
\approx
(1-\gamma)\, Z^{-1}\!\!\left(1 - \frac{1}{K}\right)
+ \gamma\, Z^{-1}\!\!\left(1 - \frac{1}{Ke}\right)
$$

where:
- $Z^{-1}(\cdot)$ = quantile function of standard Normal
- $\gamma \approx 0.5772$ = EulerŌĆōMascheroni constant
- $e \approx 2.718$

The more strategies you try ($K \uparrow$), the higher the <b>expected maximum Sharpe ratio by chance</b>.

So a discovered strategy is likely a **false positive** unless:

$$
\max_k SR_k \gg \mathbb{E}[\max_k SR_k]
$$


---

### <b>Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR) </b>

Adjusts Sharpe ratio for:
- Multiple testing ($K$)
- Sample size ($T$)
- Skewness ($\gamma_3$) and kurtosis ($\gamma_4$)


$$
DSR = \frac{\widehat{SR} - \mathbb{E}[\max_k SR_k]}{\sqrt{\text{Adjusted Variance}}}
$$

A <b>high DSR brings stronger evidence of true profitability</b>.
A low DSR means thatis likely a false discovery


### <b>Clustering Strategies & Cluster-Level Returns </b>


N strategies are grouped into $K \ll N$ **clusters** based on correlations, so
it's possible to reduces dimensionality for multiple testing bias.


The <b>aggregate return series</b> for each cluster is:

$$
S_{k,t} = \sum_{i \in C_k} w_{k,i} r_{i,t}
$$


Weights chosen to minimize cluster variance:

$$
w_k =
\frac{\Sigma_k^{-1} \mathbf{1}_k}{\mathbf{1}_k^{\top}\Sigma_k^{-1} \mathbf{1}_k}
$$

where:
- $\Sigma_k$ = covariance matrix of returns within cluster $k$
- $\mathbf{1}_k$ = vector of ones of appropriate size

Clustering <b>reduces effective number of trials $(K Ōē¬ N)$</b>:
- Lowers false discovery probability
- Produces a more realistic null distribution
- Improves **precision** of DSR-based significance testing
