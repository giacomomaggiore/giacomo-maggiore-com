### <a id="1.1"> <b>1.1 Cobweb Dynamics: a simple model for supply and demand</b> </a>


Consider Supply $(S)$, Demand $(D)$ coupled via the price $(P)$ with linear dependence.
- Demand $D$ responds to price immediately
- Supply $S$ responds with lag (production takes time).

$$
Q_t^D = D_t = \frac{a - P_t}{b}
\\
Q_t^S = S_t = \frac{P_{t-1}-c}{s}
$$

The system can converge, oscillate or diverge depending on how supply and demand are sensitive to price.

### <a id ="1.2"> <b>1.2 Basics of Epidemic Models</b> </a>

A simple <b>Susceptible-inftyected (SI)</b> model states:

$$
\dot S = - \beta \frac{SI}{N},\quad  \dot I = \beta \frac{SI}{N}
$$

where $\beta$ detnoes the inftyectious contacs per time unit, $I$ the inftyected, $S$ the susceptible (healty) individuals.
Note that, <b>at the final state</b>: everyone is inftyected.



Assuming that the population is closed and that recovery is not possible, the share $x(t)$ of the population inftyected increases following a <b>logistic growth</b>:

$$
x(t) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\beta(t - \mu)}}
$$

In the early phase: $\dot x \approx \beta x$ (almost a exponential growth), in the long run $x \implies 1$.

On the other hand, <b>Susceptible-inftyected-Removed (SIR)</b> model introduces the possibility of recovery or date, represented by $\gamma$.

$$
\dot S = - \beta \frac{SI}{N}, \quad \dot I = \beta \frac{SI}{N} - \gamma I, \quad  \dot R = \gamma I
$$

The inftyected people are then a _transient state_ and at the equilibrium _(the final stage)_ the disease cannot spread anymore.

$$

\\

z_{t+1} = q z_t (1 - z_t), \quad q = 1 + r
$$

### <a id ="1.3"> <b>1.3 Supply CHains as Rooted Trees</b> </a>


We can represent a chain as a graph $G = (V,E)$, with $V$ representing the set of verttices (notes) and $E \subseteq V \times V$ the set of edges, that can be directed or not: $(u,v) = \text{or} \neq (v,u)$.

Edges can be weighted ($w_{uv}$) and represent capacity, volume, lead time or reliability of a single link of the supply chain.

We done by $k_v^{in}$,$k_v^{in}$ the degrees (number of connections) of a node $v$ has in input (suppliers) and output (customers).
A <b>directed tree</b> models an isolated supply chain with source suppliers and root assembler.

In reality, firms have multiple interconnected suppliers and customers, leading to a <b>supply network</b>

<b>Dynamics of networks: how links form:</b>

- Networks evolve: entry, exit, mergers, rewiring.
- The simple mathematical rule behind is: $\mathbb{P}(u \to v) \propto k_v + \alpha$ _(the probability that a node $u$ connects to a new node $v$ is proportional to its number of degrees)_
- The outcome is a hevily-tailed degree with a few hubs and many small degrees.

<b>Dynamics of networks: how goods flow | Continuous Flow (deterministic)</b>

Let's denote by $x_v(t)$ the inventory at node $v$.

The flow balance is given by:

$$
\dot x = \sum_u f_{uv}(t) - \sum_w f_{vw}(t) + s_v(t) - d_v(t)
$$

Where $f_{uv}$ is the inftylow from suppliers, $f_{vw}$ the outflow to customers or other nodes, $s_v$ the production and $d_v$ the demand. 

In matrix form: $\dot X(t) = - LX(t)+ S(t) - D(t)$.

<b>Dynamics of networks: how goods flow | Markov Routing  (stochastic)</b>

We consider a transition matrix $P$ with entries $P_{uv} = \mathbb{P}(u \to v)$ and we denote by $p(t)$ the probability of good at each node.

We then have the following dynamics:
$$
p(t + 1) = p(t)P
$$

### <a id =".1"> <b>2.1 Growth Models: exponential and logistic</b> </a>

We initially study a simple growth dynamic: the compounding interest. <br/>
We consider a initial deposit $x_0$ and a nominal annual rate $r$, with interest credited every $\Delta t$.

$$
\begin{align*} 
x_0 &= x_0 \\ 
x_{\Delta t} &=  x_0 (1 +  r \Delta t)\\
x_{2 \Delta t} &=  x_{\Delta t} (1 +  r \Delta t)\\
... \\


x_{n \Delta t} &= x_0 (1 + r \Delta t)^n
\end{align*}
$$

with $t = n \Delta t$, we then obtain: $x(t) = x_0 (1 + r \Delta t)^{t / \Delta t}$.

As $t \to 0$, we can switch to continuous time:

$$
\frac {dx}{dt} = \dot x(t) = rx(t)
$$

The analytical solution of this simple ODE is obtained via separation of variables:

$$
\frac {dx}{dt} = rx \implies \frac{1}{x}dx = r dt \\

\int_{x_0}^{x} \frac{1}{x} dx = \int_{x_0}^{x} r dt \implies -\log(|x_0|) + \log(|x|) = rt
$$

and then, solving for x:

$$
x_(t) = e^{rt + \log(|x_0|)} = x_0 e^{rt}
$$

If we instead want to solve the initial value problem with a <b> numerical approximation</b> (Euler's method) we choose a step size $\Delta t$ and set up a time grid.

At each step, we obtain the gradient $f(x_n,t_n)$ at the current point and take a step $\Delta t$ in that direction. This leads to the Euler update rule:

$$
x_{n+1} = x_n + \Delta t f(x_n,t_n)
$$

In python, for example:

```python 
def euler(f, x0, t0, T, dt):
   t, x = [t0], [x0]
   while t[-1] < T:
       slope = f(x[-1], t[-1])
       x.append(x[-1] + dt * slope)
       t.append(t[-1] + dt)
return t, x

```

We now analyze a saturated growth model.
Let $x$ be the population size, $r$ the intrinsic growth rate, $K$ the carrying capacity.

We start from the <b>per-capita growth rate </b> (proportional growth)
$$
\frac{1}{x}\frac{dx}{dt} = r
$$

we then add a <b>limited resources </b> constraint: per-capita rate decreases with $x$.

$$
\frac{1}{x}\frac{dx}{dt} = r ( 1 - k/K) \implies \dot x = rx(1 - x/K)
$$

_The term $-x/K$ limits the exponential growth up to $K$._

The close form solution brings to:

$$
x(t) = \frac{K}{1 + \frac{K - x_0}{x_0}e^{-rt}}
$$

In the logistic growth, if we consider $f(x) = \frac{dx}{dt} = rx(1 - x/K) = rx - r/Kx^2$, we obtain two equilibria:
- $x^{eq}_1 = 0$: <b>unstable</b>. $(\frac{df}{dx}|x^{eq}_1 > 0)$
- $x^{eq}_2 = K$: <b>stable</b> $(\frac{df}{dx}|x^{eq}_1 < 0)$

The <b>MSY</b> (maximum sustainable yield) is obtained where $\frac{df}{dx} = 0$: $x^* = K/2$.

---



We now consider the <b>Allee Effect</b>, modelling the fact that, a low density, individuals struggle to grow.
The model then becomes:


$$
\frac{dx}{dt} = - xr (1 - x/K)(1 - x/A)
$$

where the term $(1 - x/A)$ models the _critical density_.
The growth rate is then:

$$
\dot x = - rx + (\frac{r}{A} + \frac{r}{K})x^2 - \frac{r}{AK}x^3
$$

In the Allee dynamics, there are three equilibrium:
- $0$ and $K$ are stable
- $A$ is an unstable equilibrium
Indeed, if $x(0) < A \implies$ the population declines to $0$, otherwise it grows to $K$.

### <a id ="2.2"> <b>2.2 Fishing/Harvesting Model</b> </a>

We now consider a <b>harvesting function </b>$h(x,e)$:
- $x$ represents the stock (fish population)
- $e$ represents the effort (boats, manpower)
- $h(x,e)$ represents the outflow of resources (catch per unit time)

Then we have:

$$
\dot x = f(x) - h(x,e), \quad f(x) = rx(1 - x/K)
$$

At the equilibrium:

$$
\dot x = 0, \quad f(x) = h(x,e)
$$

If we set $h(x,e) = h$, the equilbrium is given by $f(x) = h$.

Multiple cases possible:
- $h > f(x^*)$: above the MSY $\implies $ extinction.
- $h = f(x^*) \implies $ maximum yieldl, but not maximum risk.
- $0 < h < f(x^*) \implies $ two intersections:
    - the lower one is unstable, the upper one is stable.

If we instead consider the <b>harvesting function proportional</b> to effort: 

$$
h(x,e) = aex, \quad \dot x = rx(1 - r/K) - aex
$$

 the two equilibria are:
- $x = 0 $
- $x^* = K(1 - \frac{ae}{r})$ (positive and then stable only if $ae < r$)

### <a id ="2.3"> <b>2.3 Growth Models: supply and demand determine price</b> </a>

In this case, the harvesting function $h$ is not fixed, but market driven.
- demand is decreasing in price $p$
- supply is increasing in price $p$ and stock $x$.

$$
q^d = c_0 - c_1p, \quad q^s = b_1è + b_2x
$$

The market clearing is obtained as: $q^d = q^s = h^{eq}(x)$ and the equilibrium price $p^{eq}(x)$:

$$
p^{eq}(x) = \frac{c_0 - b_2x}{c_1 + b_1}
$$

The harvesting function becomes then a function of stock:

$$
h^{eq}(x) = q^s \big(  p^{eq},x  \big) = b_1 p^{eq}(x) + b_2 x 

h^{eq}(x) = \frac{c_0 b_1 + c_1 b_2 x}{c_1 + b_1}
$$

We can the model the stock dynaimics as:

$$
\dot x = f(x)- h^{eq}(x), \quad f(x) = rx(1 - x/K)
$$

We now consider the profit function $\pi$ of a firm, given by:

$$
\pi(e) = TR - TC
$$

with $TC = we$, $w$ wage effort; and revenue $TR = ph$, with $h = aex$.

From the previous section we know that:

If we plug the previous equilibrium $x^*$ in the profit function and we set $\frac{d\pi}{\d e} = 0$, we obtain:

$$
e^* = \frac{r}{2a} - \frac{wr}{2pa^2K}
$$

### <a id ="3.1"> <b>3.1 Common Source Diffusion</b> </a>

We start by analyzing a simple model with:
- $x(t) \in [0,1]$ representing the fraction adopted
- <b> only external push</b>
- No initial adopters: $x(0) = 0$.
- $p$ adoption hazard
- $(1 - x)$ share of non-adopters

$$
\dot x = p \times (1 - x)
$$

The analytical solution is:

$$
x(t) = 1 - e^{-pt}
$$

We clearly that this model is linear in $x$ (the logistic is instead quadratic) and it is <b>always concave </b>, with adoption slowing down as $x \to 1$.

If we instead want to find the time $t(x)$ to reach a target, we simply set:

$$
\begin{align*}
1 - e^{-pt} &= x \\
e^{-pt} &= 1 - x\\
- pt &= \ln(1-x)\\
t(x) &= -ln(1-x)/p

\end{align*}
$$

### <a id ="3.2"> <b>3.2 Bass Diffusion</b> </a>

In this model, each nonadopter is inftylyenced by twho channels: adoption (as in the commono source one) and <b>imitation or world of mouth</b>, expressed by the term $qx$, increasing in $x$:

$$
g(x) = p + qx
$$

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot x(t) &= g(x) \times (1 - x)\\
\dot x(t) &= (p + qx) \times (1 -x)
\end{align*}
$$

Two special cases in this model:
- $q = 0:$ commono source model
- $p = 0:$ pure imitation, needs $x(0)>0$ to start.

<div align = "center">
![Bass](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/bass.png)
</div>

However, note that the Bass Model requires <b>strong assumptions</b>:
- adoption only driven by direct persuasion ($p$ and $q$)
- homogeneous population, no spatial or network effects
- no threshold dynamics and no critical mass modeled
- no economic ingredients (prices, preferences etc etc)

and strong limitations too:
- at the final state: $x \to 1$ with no reversal
- the model cannot capture competition, substitution or churn.

### <a id ="3.3"> <b>3.3 SIRS Models</b> </a>

Bass is an example of <b>spreading</b> model, epidemic models (SIRS) are the canonical toolkit for this scenarios.

We start by analyzing a <b>SI model </b>:

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot s &= - \beta s i, \quad s + i = 1
\dot i &= \beta s i, \quad s = 1 - i

\end{align*}
$$

if we add a recovery rate $\gamma$ and inftyection ruation $1/\gamma$, we obtain a <b>SIR</b> model:

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot s &= - \beta s i
\dot i &= \beta s i - \gamma i
\dot r &= \gamma i

\end{align*}
$$

where $r$ is the recovery rate. We can define the <b>Basic Reproduction Number </b> $R_0$ as the number of inftyected from one person in a _fully_ subscettible population as:

$$
R_0 = \frac{\beta}{\gamma}
$$

In this case, we have an early growth if and only if $R_0 > 1$.

The effective reproduction number is then $R(t) = R_0 s(t)$. The spread slows down and eventually stops when:

$$
R_t = 1 \implies s = \frac{1}{R_0} \impies \approx r^* = 1 - \frac{1}{R_0}
$$

The intuition behind is that, once $r^*$ individuals are immune (recovered or vaccinated), each case generates less than one new case on average and inftyections dies out.

<div align = "center">
![Bass](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/sir.png)
</div>

Finally, a <b>SIRS</b> model introduces a waning immuunity that vanishes at rate $w$.

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot r &= \gamma i - w r\\
\dot s &= - \beta s i + wr\\
\dot i &= \beta si - \gamma i
\end{align*}
$$

The end state is <b>non trivial </b> and it is given by the vector $(s^*, i^*, r^*)$.

### <a id ="3.4"> <b>3.4 Complex Contagions on a graph: a formal setup</b> </a>

We consider a network $G = (V,E)$, with noted $v \in V$ and edges $(u,v) \in E$.

- each node $x_v \in \{0, 1\}$ ($0$ non-adopter, $1$ adopter)$ 
-  adoption threshold $\theta$
- neighborhood $N(v) = \{u : (u,v) \in E \}$

The threshold rules says:

$$
x_v(t+1) = \begin{cases} 
1, & \displaystyle\sum_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} x_u(t) \geq \theta, \\
x_v(t), & \textbf{otherwise},
\end{cases}
$$

_Intuition: node $v$ adopts if $\theta$ or more neighbors are adopters._

### <a id ="4.1"> <b>4.1 Coupled Dynamics: Lotka-Volterra Predator-Prey Model</b> </a>

Coupled dynamics are needed when two (or more) variables <b>evolve together</b>, leading to <b>cycles, attractors</b> and <b>repellors</b>. Common examples are predator and prey in _biology_, susceptible and inftyected in _epidemiology_, employment and wages in _economics_. In formulas, we can write:

$$
\dot x = f(x,y) \quad \dot y = g(x,y)
$$

The analysis of this systems comes from the <b>classification</b> of <b>fixed points</b> and <b>stability</b>, both analytically and numerically.

We start by analyzing the <b>Lotka-Volterra Predator-Prey Model </b>, representing the interaction between two species:
- $x(t)$: preys (hares)
- $y(t)$: predators (lynx)

In isolation we know that:
- prey reproduces exponentially, $\dot x = \alpha x$
- predators die out: $\dot y = - \gamma y$.

But, with interaction:
- encounters remove prey at rate $\beta xy$
- encounters yield predators growth at rate $\delta xy$.

resulting in the following coupled system:

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot x &= \alpha x - \beta xy \\
\dot y &= \delta xy - \gamma y

\end{align*}
$$

<div align = "center">
![Lotka](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/lotka.png)
</div>

We now show the procedure determine <b>fixed points</b>.
We start by setting the following to find the equilibrium (fixed points $x_k^*, y_k^*)$:
$$
\dot x = f(x,y),   \quad \dot y = g(x,y)\\
f(x,y) = 0, \quad g(x,y) = 0 \implies (x_k^*, y_k^*)
$$

In the case of the LV model we have:

$$ 
\begin{align*}
\dot x &= \alpha x - \beta xy = x (\alpha - \beta y)\\
\dot y &= \delta xy - \gamma y = y (\delta x - \gamma)\\

\end{align*}

\\
\begin{cases}
    x (\alpha - \beta y) = 0 \\
    y (\delta x - \gamma) = 0
\end{cases}
$$

resultin in:
$$
(x^*, y^*) = (0,0) \text{or} \quad (x^*, y^*) = (\gamma / \delta, \alpha / \beta)
$$

In general, the number of roots will depend on $f$ and $g$:
- <b>Zero</b>: no equilibria
- <b>One</b>: unique equilbrium
- <b>Finite number $> 1 </b>: several isolated equilibria
- <b>inftyinitely many</b>: whole x-axis equilibria

_Note that, in higher dimensions, understanding stability and trajectories become more complicated that in the simple 1D case._

<div align = "center">
![Lotka](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/lotka2.png)
</div>

To visualize the trajectory of the system, a common approach is to <b>numerically solve the ODEs</b> with initial condition $(x_0,y_0)$ chosen and then run a series of simulations.
From the plot, we should then be able to clearly see if the trajectories are <b>converging</b>, <b>diverging</b> or <b>staying</b> in rotation around the fixed points.

<div align = "center">
![Lotka](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/lotka3.png)
</div>

Then, at a fixed point, a <b>nonlinar system </b> can be approximated by a linear one.
Define <b>perturbations $u, v$</b> as:

$$
u := x - x^*, \quad v := y - y^*
$$

and, thanks to Taylor expansions, we end up in the dynamics for small perturbations:

$$

\left.
\begin{aligned}
    \dot{u} &= u \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\bigg|_{(x^\star, y^\star)} + v \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\bigg|_{(x^\star, y^\star)} + \mathcal{O}(u^2, v^2, uv) \\
    \dot{v} &= \underbrace{u \frac{\partial g}{\partial x}\bigg|_{(x^\star, y^\star)} + v \frac{\partial g}{\partial y}\bigg|_{(x^\star, y^\star)}}_{\text{ linear}} + \underbrace{\mathcal{O}(u^2, v^2, uv)}_{\text{higher order terms}}
\end{aligned}
\right\}
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\begin{bmatrix}
    \dot{u} \\
    \dot{v}
\end{bmatrix}
\approx
\underbrace{
\begin{bmatrix}
    \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \\[1em]
    \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial g}{\partial y}
\end{bmatrix}_{(x^\star, y^\star)}
}_{\text{Jacobian}}
\begin{bmatrix}
    u \\
    v
\end{bmatrix}

$$

The <b>eigenvalues of Jacobian </b> tell us whether <b>perturbations grow, decay or oscillate</b>.
In the 1D case, we had that:

$$
\delta \dot x = \delta \partial x(t) \implies \delta x(t) = \delta x(0)e^{\lambda t}
$$
- if $\lambda <0$: perturbations decay (<b>stable</b>)
- if $\lambda>0$: perturbations grow (<b>unstable</b>)

The same applies to the 2D case. We consider $z(t) = p e^{\lambda t}$, where $p$ is a costant vector.
<br/>Then we plug $\dot z = J z$, resulting in:

$$
\lambda p e ^{\lambda t} = J p e^{\lambda t} \implies Jp = \lambda p
$$

We can clearly see that the <b>eigenvalues of J</b> describe how small perturbations evolve.

Recall that to compute the eigenvalues of $J$ we can simply write and solve the <b>characteristic polynomial</b>:

$$
\det(J - \lambda I) = \lambda^2 - \tau \lambda + \Delta 
\\
\tau := \operatorname{Tr}(J) = f_x + g_y

\Delta := \det(J) = f_x g_y - f_y g_x

$$
leading to the following solution:
$$
\\
\lambda_{1,2} = \frac{\tau \pm \sqrt(\tau^2 - 4 \Delta)}{2}

$$

<b>The classification of the fixed points</b> can be done analytically depending on the characteristic polynomial:

- _(a)_: <b>Saddle</b>: $\Delta < 0$. $\lambda_i$ are real and have opposite single
- _(b,c)_: <b>Stable and Unstable Nodes</b>: $\Delta > 0$ and $\tau^2 \ge 4 \Delta$ (stable with $\tau <0$).
    Also known as attractors and repellors, $\lambda_i$ are real and have the same sign.
- _(d,e)_: <b>Stable and Unstable Spiral</b>: $\tau \neq 0$ and $\tau^2 < 4 \Delta$ (stable with $\tau <0$). Also known as attractive focus and repulsive focus, $\lambda_i$ are complex.
- _(f)_: <b>Center</b>: $\tau = 0 $ and $\Delta > 0$. $\lambda_i$ are complex with zero real part.

![Lotka](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/lotka4.png)
</div>

<b>Example: Stable/Unstable Spirals or Center in the Lotka-Volterra Model</b>.

We consider the following model:

$$

f(x, y) = \dot{x} = \alpha x - \beta xy, \quad g(x, y) = \dot{y} = \delta xy - \gamma y

$$

Explicitily computing the Jacobian:

$$

J(x, y) = \begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \\
\frac{\partial g}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial g}{\partial y}
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
\alpha - \beta y & -\beta x \\
\delta y & \delta x - \gamma
\end{bmatrix}

$$

we then find fixed points:

$$

(x^\star, y^\star) \in \left\{ (0, 0), \left( \frac{\gamma}{\delta}, \frac{\alpha}{\beta} \right) \right\}


$$
and we evaluate them at:
$$

J(0,0) = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha & 0 \\ 0 & -\gamma \end{bmatrix}, \quad \tau = \alpha - \gamma, \quad \Delta = -\alpha\gamma < 0
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \lambda_{1,2} = \{ \alpha, -\gamma \} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \textbf{saddle}


$$

$$
J\left( \frac{\gamma}{\delta}, \frac{\alpha}{\beta} \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -\frac{\beta\gamma}{\delta} \\ \frac{\delta\alpha}{\beta} & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \tau = 0, \quad \Delta = \alpha\gamma > 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \lambda_{1,2} = \pm i\sqrt{\alpha\gamma} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{\textbf{center}}

$$

### <a id ="4.2"> <b>4.2 Coupled Dynamics: Goodwin's Model (1967), Cycles in Employment and Wages</b> </a>

This model comes from a simple observation: <b>economies often show recurrent cycles</b>. Booms cause high employment, so workers gain bargaining power, then wages rise, wage share of outpus increases as well. As a consequence, protits fall, investment slows and growth stalls.
<br/>. Employment then falls, weakening workers' position, wages stagnate or declinde, profits recover and growth resumes.

<b>Goodwin's idea</b> is to model class struggle dynamics with similar equations used for predator-prey cycles.

We then consider two state variables:
- $q_t$: employment ratio (jobs over the population) - _analogy with the prey population_
- $w_t$: wage share (workers' share of output) - _analogy with the predator population_

The <b>qualitative</b> cycle logics says that:
- $q \uparrow \implies$ bargaining power $\uparrow \implies w \uparrow$
- $w \uparrow \implies$ profits $\downarrow \implies$ investment $\downarrow \implies q \downarrow$
- $q \downarrow \implies$ wage pressure $\downarrow \implies w \downarrow \implies$ profits $\uparrow \implies q \uparrow$

Formally, if we consider the following variables:
- $q_t = l_t / n_t$ employment ratio
- $w_t = w_t l_t / Y_t = w_t / a_t$ wage share
- $Y_t$ total output (GDP), with capital-output ratoi $\sigma$ such that: $\dot Y_t = \frac{1-w_t}{\sigma}Y_t$
- $k_t$ capital stock (invested at: $\dot k_t = (1 - w_t)Y_t$)
- $l_t$ employed labor
- $n_t$ total labor force, growing at rate $\beta$.
- $a_t = Y_t / l_t$ labor productivity, growing a rate $\alpha$.
- $w_t$ real wage, following the Philipps Curve with: $\frac{\dot w_t}{w_t} = - \gamma + \kappa q_t$

and parameters $\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \kappa, \sigma$.

if we derive the employment ratio and the wage share, we obtain:

$$
\frac{\dot q_t}{q_t} = \frac{\dot l_t}{l_t}- \frac{\dot n_t}{n_t} = \frac{\dot Y_t}{Y_t} - \frac{\dot a_t}{a_t} - \beta = \frac{1 - w_t}{\sigma} - \beta
$$

$$
\frac{\dot w_t}{w_t} = \frac{\dot w_t}{w_t} - \frac{\dot a_t}{a_t} = (- \gamma + \kappa q_t) - \alpha
$$

Putting toghether, we have the <b>Goodwmin's System</b>:

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot q_t &= \big( \frac{1}{\sigma} - \alpha - \beta \big)q_t - \frac{1}{\sigma}w_t q_t\\
\dot w_t &= \kappa q_t w_t - (\gamma + \alpha) w_t 

\end{align*}
$$

Simplifying, we get:
$$
\dot q_t = A q_t - B w_t q_t, \quad \dot w_t = B q_t w_t - C w_t\\

\dot q = q (A - Bw), \quad \dot w = (Dq - C)
$$

The intuition, very similar to the previous predator-prey model, is that _workers and capitalists_ are two interacting groups. Workers indeed seek higher wages, capitalists seek higher profits.
These opposing objectives create a self-regulaasting cycle, neither side "wins" permanently but they co-move in economic cycles.

From an economic point of view:
- <b>Growth-recessions cycles are endogenous</b> without external shocks
- <b>Booms</b> (high $q$) are the beginning of slowdowns (rising $w$) and viceversa
- The neutral center is not robust, small modifications destroy cycles.

### <a id ="5.1"> <b>5.1 Discrete Dynamics: Cobweb Plot</b> </a>

There are several reason behind why we should <b>discretetime dynamics</b>:
- decision timing: firms and individuals set prices/output quarterly or at fixed intervals
- institutional calendars: central banks and governments meet on fixed dates
- contracts and billing: loans, rent and utilities are paid monthly or similarly
- observed data: GDP, monthly unemployments, annual reports etc etc...

To start with discrete time dynamics, we define the <b>difference equation</b>, representing how the next state depends on the previous:

$$
x_{t + 1} = f(x_t) \quad \text{\textit{update rule}}
$$

The evolution if then given by the repeated application of $f$:

$$
\begin{align*}

x_1 &= f(x_0)\\
x_2 &= f((f_0))\\
...\\
x_t &= f^{(t)}(x_0)
\end{align*}
$$

To visualize this, we usually use <b>Cobweb Plot</b>, showing the evolution of the discrete rule.
- Start with a initial value $x_0$.
- Move vertically till the blue line representing $f(x)$ and find $f(x_0)$.
- Move horizontally to the diagonal $x = y$ and find $x_1$.
- Repeat with $x_1$ and iterate.

<div align = "center">
![Cobweb](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/cobweb.png)
</div>

Each update defined a sequence:
$$
x_{t +1} = f(x) \implies X = \{ x_0, x_1, x_2 ...\}
$$

when $f(x)$ crosses $ y = x$ we have <b>fixed points</b> $x^* = f(x^*)$.

_Note that the sequence may cycle set of points (periodi orbits) or may be aperiodic (chaotic) deterministic, but not repeating._

To determine the <b>fixed points</b>, we have to find $z_t^*$ such that $z_{t+1} = f(z_t)$.

We then consider, as usual, the deviation from the fixed point:

$$
z_{t+1} - z^* = f(z_t) - f(z^*)
$$

and we linearize around $z^*$:

$$
f(z_t) \approx f(z^*) + f'(z^*)(z_t - z^*) \implies
\implies z_{t+1} - z^* \approx f'(z^*) (z_t - z^*)
$$

if we then iterate $t$ times we obtain:

$$
z_t - z^* \approx [f'(z^*)]^t (z_0 - z^*)
$$

To determine if it is stable or not, we simply look at:
- $|f'(z^*)| < 1 \implies:$ attracting
- $|f'(z^*)| < 1 \implies:$ repelling
- $0 < f'(z^*)< 1 \implies:$ monotone
- $-1 < f'(z^*)< 0 \implies:$ alternating

### <a id ="5.2"> <b>5.2 Discrete Dynamics: Saturated (Logistic) Growth</b> </a>

We previously analyzed the continuous-time logistic growth as:

$$
\dot x = rx(1 -r) = rx - rx^2
$$

if we apply the Euler discretization as:

$$
\frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{x_{t + \Delta t} - x_t}{\Delta t}
$$

we can then write:

$$
x_{t+1}- x_t = rx_t (1 - x_t) \implies
\begin{align*}

x_{t+1} &= x_t + rx_t(1 -x_t)\\
&= (1 + r)x_t - rx_t^2\\
&= (1 + r)x_t (1 - \frac{r}{r+1}x_t)

\end{align*}
$$

if we define $z_t = x_t \frac{r}{1+r}$:

$$
\begin{align*}
z_{t+1} &= (1 + r)z_t (1 - z_t)\\
&= R z_t (1 - z_t)

\end{align*}
$$

We now determine <b>fixed points</b>:

$$
f'(z) = R(1 - 2Z), \quad z^* = \frac{R -1}{R}
\\

f'(z^*) = 2 - R

$$

we now appòy the stability condition:
- if $1 < R < 3$ then $|2 - R| < 1$ and the <b>fixed points attract </b>
- at $R = 3: f'(z^*) = - 1$: <b>flip bifurcation</b> (fixed points becomes repelling: stable 2-cycles).

_Intuition: As long as the growth rate $R$ is between 1 and 3, the population settles down to $z^*$. At exactly $R=3$, the slope becomes $-1$. This is the "tipping point": The system can no longer settle at a single number._

In the second period, if $R > 3$, the population starts jumping back and forth between two values, $z_1$ and $z_2$, every period.
The period-2 points solve $f(f(z)) = z$, resulting in:

$$
z_{1,2} = \frac{R + 1 \pm \sqrt(R -3 )(R +1)}{2R} \quad \text{for} R > 3
$$
As in the first cycle, f the k-cycle, we obtain stability is reached when: $|(f^{(k)})'(z^*)| < 1$, that, for the 2-cycle rewrites as:

$$
3 < R < 1 + \sqrt(6) \approx 3.44949
$$

At $R = 1 + \sqrt(6)$ the2-cycle does another flip _(stable 4-cycle, the population repeats a pattern of 4 different values)_

For many periods, we now consider $R_n$ the value of $R$ where a $2^n$-cycle first appears:

$$
\begin{align*}
R_1 &= 3.000\\
R_2 &= 3.449\\
R_3 &= 3.544\\
...\\


R_{\infty} &= 3.569
\end{align*}
$$

The sequence converges geometrically to $R_{\infty} = 3.569$

We then define the <b>Feigenbaum constant</b> as:

$$
\delta = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{R_{n+1}-R_n}{R_{n+2} - R_{n+1}} \approx 4.6692
$$

resulting in the <b>Scaling Law</b> for $R_n$:

$$
R_n \approx R_{infty} - c \delta ^{-n}, \quad c_{logistic} \approx 2.632
$$

<div align = "center">
![Cobweb](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/bifurcation.png)
</div>

- For $R_{infty} < R < 4$ we can see a <b>mixture of order and chaos</b>: periodic windows reappe within chaotic bands.
- At $R = 4$ we see <b>fully developed chaos</b>.

This is called <b>deterministic chaos</b>: once $x_0$ and $r$ are fixed, each step is predetermoined, but long term behavior becomes <b>unpredictable</b>, because tiny differences in $x_0$ grow rapidly.

A deterministc system is then called <b>chaotic</b> when it shows:
- <b>aperiodic long-run behavior</b> (no fixed points or repeating patterns)
- <b>determinism</b>
- <b>sensitivity to initial conditions</b>

Note that, as we've seen, <b>deterministic </b> $\neq$ <b>predictable</b>. In nonlinear systems, tiny errors or deviations can explode over time, therefore long term forecasts lose meaning.

### <a id="6.1"> <b>6.1 Endogenous Cycles</b> </a>

In the economy, markets are _rarely_ isolated (producers can switch between goods, farmers can sell domestically or abroad, energy and manufacturing costs increase etc, etc...)
We start now from the simple Cobweb Model but allow producers to <b>switch markets</b>.

Each market $i \in \{ A, B\}$ has a ficed supplier share $W_i$, with $W_A = 1 - W_B$.

$$
D_{i,t} = \frac{a_i - P_{i,t}}{b_i}, \quad S_{i, t} = \frac{P_{i,t-1}- c_i}{d_i}
$$

if we set the market clearing conditin $D_i = W_i S_i$:

$$
\frac{a_i - P_{i,t}}{b_i} = \frac{P_{i,t}- c_i}{d_i}
$$

then we can find the <b>Price-Update Rule</b>:

$$
P_{i,t} = a_i + \frac{b_i W_i c_i}{d_i} - {b_i W_i}{d_i} P_{i, t-1} = \alpha_i - \beta_i P_{i, t-1}
$$

At the equilibrium:

$$
P_i^* = \frac{a_i d_i + b_i c_i W_i}{d_i + b_i W_i}
\\
|\beta_i! = | W_i \frac{b_i}{w_i}| < 1

$$

_Note that larger $W_i \implies $ faster response to equilibrium and then less stability._

We now consider <b>costs and profits</b> in each market.
Each supplier earn $\pi_i$, depending on price and total quantity:

$$
\pi_i = P_{i,t}S_{i,t} - C_i(S_{i,t})
$$

assuming that cost increases with ouput: 

$$
C_i(S_{i,t}) = g_i S_i + e_i S_{i,t}^2, \quad e_i > 0
$$

we result in the following profit funtion:

$$
\pi_t = P_{i,t}S_{i,t}- (g_i S_i + e_i S_{i,t}^2)
$$

Now we can see the coupled system as a <b>sequence of events</b>:

- The suppliers produce $W_{i,t} (\fra{P_{i, t-1} - g_i}{d_i})$, with $W_{i,t}$ is the probability of choosing a market depending on the profits:
$$
W_{i,t} = \frac{1}{1 + \exp(- f \times (\pi_{A, t-1} - \pi_{B, t-1}))}
$$

where  $f \ge 0$ is the _intensity of choice_ (rationality).
- Prices adjust to clear each market:
$$
P_{i,t} = a_i + \frac{b_i W_i c_i}{d_i} - {b_i W_i}{d_i} P_{i, t-1} 
$$
- Profits are realized
- Suppliers update market choice for the next period

To understand if it is chaos or just complex, we can perform a <b>FFT test</b>.<br/>
A time series can be indeed seen as a <b>mix of pure waves</b>. We then apply the _Fourier Transform_ to understand how much of each frequency is present.

Plotting then $|X(\text{frequency})|$ gives a spectrum: 
- peaks in the spectrum represents cycles (_not chaotic)_
- no clear peaks in the spectrum implies that there's not a dominant rhythm.

In general, we can conclude about endogenous cycles that:
- <b>Volatility is structural</b> and it is amplified by a large supplier share in the market, faster switching intensity $f$ and excessive supply response (low $d$).
- Cycles create <b>deadweight loss</b>: mis-times investment and wasted inventory/resources.
- Coupling transmits fluctuations across markets.

### <a id="6.2"> <b>6.2 Endogenous Cycles: the Business Cycle </b> </a>

We now model a 2-player model with:
- Household that can either consume or save: $Y = C + S$
- Capicalists that can either consume or invest: $Y = C + I$
- Savings fund investments: $S \to I$ via banks
- At equilibrium: $S = I$.

Starting from the setup we have:
- Households consume $C$ and save $S$.
- Firms plan investment $I^{p}$ based on the expecteded consumption $C^{e}$
- in reality, $C^{e} \neq C^{acqual}$.
- The gap shows up as:

$$
\Delta \text{Inv.} = C^{e} - C^{acqual} = - (I - S)
$$

- The firm reacts:
    - If $\Delta \text{Inv.} > 0 \implies$: overproduction, cut output
    - If $\Delta \text{Inv.} > 0 \implies$: underproduction, expand output
- Leading to the dynamic rule:

$$
\dot Y = \alpha (I(y) - S(Y)), \quad \alpha > 0
$$

we can then incur in two scenarios:
- if $S$ has a steeper slope than $I$ (at the right of the equilibrium $\dot Y < 0$ and at the left $\dot Y > 0$): <b>stable equilibrium</b>.
- if $I$ has a steeper slope than $S$ (at the right of the equilibrium $\dot Y > 0$ and at the left $\dot Y < 0$): <b>unstable equilibrium</b>.

<div align = "center">
![Business Cycles](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/business_cycle.png)
</div>

So far, we treader investment and savings as a function of the output, in reality, they also depend on the capital stock $K$, that accumulates through investment and declines thorugh depreciation, reflecting <b>past investment decisions</b>. We can then write (<b>Kaldor Model </b>):
$$
\dot Y = \alpha (I(Y,K)- S(Y,K )),      \quad \dot K = I(Y,K) - \delta K
$$

In this model, we have 3 equilibria: two are stable (A,C) and one unstable (B)

</div>

When equilibrium C vanishses, the economy collapes to a lower activity state (A), where investment is very low and capital erodes slowly.
Then, only when capital falls far enough, new opportunities emerge and the system can move back to C.
