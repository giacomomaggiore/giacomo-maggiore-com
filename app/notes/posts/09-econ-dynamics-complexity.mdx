### <a id="1.1"> <b>1.1 Cobweb Dynamics: a simple model for supply and demand</b> </a>


Consider Supply $(S)$, Demand $(D)$ coupled via the price $(P)$ with linear dependence.
- Demand $D$ responds to price immediately
- Supply $S$ responds with lag _(production takes time)_.

$$
Q_t^D = D_t = \frac{a - P_t}{b}
\\
Q_t^S = S_t = \frac{P_{t-1}-c}{s}
$$

The system can <b>converge</b>, <b>oscillate</b> or<b> diverge</b> depending on how supply and demand are sensitive to price.

### <a id="1.2"> <b>1.2 Basics of Epidemic Models</b> </a>

A simple <b>Susceptible-infected (SI)</b> model states:

$$
\dot S = - \beta \frac{SI}{N},\quad  \dot I = \beta \frac{SI}{N}
$$

where $\beta$ denotes the inftected contacts per time unit, $I$ the infected, $S$ the susceptible _(healty)_ individuals.
Note that, <b>at the final state everyone is infected.</b>


Assuming that the population is closed and that recovery is not possible, the share $x(t)$ of the population infected increases following a <b>logistic growth</b>:

$$
x(t) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-\beta(t - \mu)}}
$$

In the early phase: $\dot x \approx \beta x$ _(almost a exponential growth)_, in the long run $x \implies 1$.

On the other hand, <b>Susceptible-infected-Removed (SIR)</b> model introduces the possibility of recovery, represented by $\gamma$.

$$
\dot S = - \beta \frac{SI}{N}, \quad \dot I = \beta \frac{SI}{N} - \gamma I, \quad  \dot R = \gamma I
$$

The infected people are a <b>transient state</b> and at the equilibrium _(the final stage)_ the disease cannot spread anymore.

<div align = "center">
![SIR](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/sir.png)
</div>

In _discrete time_, the previous expressions become:

$$
x_{t+1} = x_t + rx_t (1 - x_t)
\\

z_{t+1} = q z_t (1 - z_t), \quad q = 1 + r
$$

### <a id="1.3"> <b>1.3 Supply CHains as Rooted Trees</b> </a>


We can represent a chain as a graph $G = (V,E)$, with $V$ representing the set of verttices (notes) and $E \subseteq V \times V$ the set of edges, that can be directed or not: $(u,v) = \text{or} \neq (v,u)$.

Edges can be <b>weighted </b>($w_{uv}$) and represent capacity, volume, lead time or reliability of a single link of the supply chain.

We denote by $k_v^{in}$,$k_v^{out}$ the <b>degrees</b> _(number of connections)_ of a node $v$ has in input _(suppliers)_ and output _(customers)_.
A <b>directed tree</b> models an isolated supply chain with source suppliers and root assembler.

In reality, firms have multiple interconnected suppliers and customers, leading to a <b>supply network</b>

<b>Dynamics of networks: how links form:</b>

- Networks evolve: _entry, exit, mergers, rewiring._
- The simple mathematical rule behind is: 
$$\mathbb{P}(u \to v) \propto k_v + \alpha$$ 
_(the probability that a node $u$ connects to a new node $v$ is proportional to its number of degrees)_
- The outcome is a _heavily-tailed degree_ with a few hubs and many small degrees.

<br/>
<b>Dynamics of networks: how goods flow | Continuous Flow (deterministic)</b>

Let's denote by $x_v(t)$ the inventory at node $v$.

The flow balance is given by:

$$
\dot x = \sum_u f_{uv}(t) - \sum_w f_{vw}(t) + s_v(t) - d_v(t)
$$

Where $f_{uv}$ is the _inflow_ from suppliers, $f_{vw}$ the _outflow_ to customers or other nodes, $s_v$ the production and $d_v$ the demand. 

In matrix form: $\dot X(t) = - LX(t)+ S(t) - D(t)$.

<b>Dynamics of networks: how goods flow | Markov Routing  (stochastic)</b>

We consider a transition matrix $P$ with entries $P_{uv} = \mathbb{P}(u \to v)$ and we denote by $p(t)$ the probability of good at each node.

We then have the following dynamics:
$$
p(t + 1) = p(t)P
$$

### <a id="2.1"> <b>2.1 Growth Models: exponential and logistic</b> </a>

We initially study a simple growth dynamic: <b>the compounding interest.</b> <br/>
We consider a initial deposit $x_0$ and a nominal annual rate $r$, with interest credited every $\Delta t$.

$$
\begin{align*} 
x_0 &= x_0 \\ 
x_{\Delta t} &=  x_0 (1 +  r \Delta t)\\
x_{2 \Delta t} &=  x_{\Delta t} (1 +  r \Delta t)\\
... \\


x_{n \Delta t} &= x_0 (1 + r \Delta t)^n
\end{align*}
$$

with $t = n \Delta t$, we then obtain: $x(t) = x_0 (1 + r \Delta t)^{t / \Delta t}$.

As $t \to 0$, we can switch to continuous time:

$$
\frac {dx}{dt} = \dot x(t) = rx(t)
$$

The analytical solution of this simple ODE is obtained _via separation of variables:_

$$
\frac {dx}{dt} = rx \implies \frac{1}{x}dx = r dt \\

\int_{x_0}^{x} \frac{1}{x} dx = \int_{x_0}^{x} r dt \implies -\log(|x_0|) + \log(|x|) = rt
$$

and then, solving for x:

$$
x_(t) = e^{rt + \log(|x_0|)} = x_0 e^{rt}
$$

---

If we instead want to solve the initial value problem with a <b>numerical approximation</b> _(Euler's method)_ we choose a step size $\Delta t$ and set up a time grid.

At each step, we obtain the gradient $f(x_n,t_n)$ at the current point and take a step $\Delta t$ in that direction. This leads to the Euler update rule:

$$
x_{n+1} = x_n + \Delta t f(x_n,t_n)
$$

In python, for example:

```python 
def euler(f, x0, t0, T, dt):
   t, x = [t0], [x0]
   while t[-1] < T:
       slope = f(x[-1], t[-1])
       x.append(x[-1] + dt * slope)
       t.append(t[-1] + dt)
return t, x

```
---

We now analyze a <b> saturated growth model.</b>
Let $x$ be the population size, $r$ the intrinsic growth rate, $K$ the carrying capacity.

We start from the <b>per-capita growth rate </b> _(proportional growth)_:
$$
\frac{1}{x}\frac{dx}{dt} = r
$$

We then add a <b>limited resources </b> constraint: per-capita rate decreases with $x$.

$$
\frac{1}{x}\frac{dx}{dt} = r ( 1 - x/K) \implies \dot x = rx(1 - x/K)
$$

_Intuition: the term $-x/K$ limits the exponential growth up to $K$._

The close form solution brings to:

$$
x(t) = \frac{K}{1 + \frac{K - x_0}{x_0}e^{-rt}}
$$

In the logistic growth, if we consider $f(x) = \frac{dx}{dt} = rx(1 - x/K) = rx - r/Kx^2$, we obtain two equilibria:
- $x^{eq}_1 = 0$: <b>unstable</b>. $(\frac{df}{dx}|x^{eq}_1 > 0)$
- $x^{eq}_2 = K$: <b>stable</b> $(\frac{df}{dx}|x^{eq}_1 < 0)$

The <b>MSY (maximum sustainable yield)</b> is obtained where $\frac{df}{dx} = 0 \implies$ $x^* = K/2$.

<a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=aP4YXOo-Uko" target="_blank">The Logistic Growth Differential Equation [VIDEO] </a>

---



We now consider the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allee_effect" target="_blank"><b>Allee Effect</b></a>, modelling the fact that, a low density, <b>individuals struggle to grow.</b>
The model then becomes:


$$
\frac{dx}{dt} = - xr (1 - x/K)(1 - x/A)
$$

where the term $(1 - x/A)$ models the <b>critical density</b>.
The growth rate is then:

$$
\dot x = - rx + (\frac{r}{A} + \frac{r}{K})x^2 - \frac{r}{AK}x^3
$$

In the Allee dynamics, there are <b>three equilibrium:</b>
- $0$ and $K$ are stable
- $A$ is an <b>unstable equilibrium</b>

Indeed, if $x(0) < A \implies$ the population declines to $0$, otherwise it grows to $K$.

### <a id="2.2"> <b>2.2 Fishing/Harvesting Model</b> </a>

We now consider a <b>harvesting function </b>$h(x,e)$, representing the rate at which a resource is removed from the population.:
- $x$ represents the <b>stock</b>_(fish population)_
- $e$ represents the<b> effort</b> _(boats, manpower)_
- $h(x,e)$ represents the <b>outflow of resources</b> (catch per unit time)

Then we have:

$$
\dot x = f(x) - h(x,e), \quad f(x) = rx(1 - x/K)
$$

At the equilibrium:

$$
\dot x = 0, \quad f(x) = h(x,e)
$$

If we set $h(x,e) = h$, the equilbrium is given by $f(x) = h$.

Multiple cases possible:
- $h > f(x^*)$: above the MSY $\implies $ extinction.
- $h = f(x^*) \implies $ maximum yieldl, but not maximum risk.
- $0 < h < f(x^*) \implies $ two intersections:
    - the lower one is unstable, the upper one is stable.

If we instead consider the <b>harvesting function proportional</b> to effor and resources _(intuition: If the stock $x$ or $e$ drops, your harvest $h$ naturally drops - assuming effort the other stays the same- preventing accidental extinction better than a fixed quota because the "drain" slows down automatically as the population decreases)_: 

$$
h(x,e) = aex, \quad \dot x = rx(1 - r/K) - aex
$$

 the two equilibria are:
- $x = 0 $
- $x^* = K(1 - \frac{ae}{r})$ (positive and then stable only if $ae < r$)

### <a id="2.3"> <b>2.3 Growth Models: supply and demand determine price</b> </a>

In this case, the harvesting function $h$ is not fixed, but market driven.
- demand is decreasing in price $p$
- supply is increasing in price $p$ and stock $x$.

$$
q^d = c_0 - c_1p, \quad q^s = b_1p + b_2x
$$

The market clearing is obtained as: $q^d = q^s = h^{eq}(x)$ and the equilibrium price $p^{eq}(x)$:

$$
p^{eq}(x) = \frac{c_0 - b_2x}{c_1 + b_1}
$$

The harvesting function becomes then a function of stock:

$$
h^{eq}(x) = q^s \big(  p^{eq},x  \big) = b_1 p^{eq}(x) + b_2  h^{eq}(x) = \frac{c_0 b_1 + c_1 b_2 x}{c_1 + b_1}
$$

Then, since the stock is "_consumed_" by supply, that is equal to the harvesting function, We can the model the stock dynaimics as:

$$
\dot x = f(x)- h^{eq}(x), \quad f(x) = rx(1 - x/K)
$$

---

We now consider the profit function $\pi$ of a firm, given by:

$$
\pi(e) = TR - TC
$$

with $TC = we$, $w$ wage effort; and revenue $TR = ph$, with $h = aex$.

If we plug the previous equilibrium $x^*$ in the profit function and we set $\frac{d\pi}{d e} = 0$, we obtain:

$$
e^* = \frac{r}{2a} - \frac{wr}{2pa^2K}
$$

<div align = "center">
![Logistic](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/profit_effort.png)
</div>

- market entry as long as $\pi > 0, \implies e$ increases
- $e = e_2 \implies TR(e_2)> TC(e_2)$: excess profit, entry
- $e_2 < e < e^*$: profit further increases
- $e^* < e < e_1$: proif decreasing but still $\pi > 0$.
- $e = e_1$: no profit, not entry.

### <a id="3.1"> <b>3.1 Common Source Diffusion</b> </a>

We start by analyzing a simple model with:
- $x(t) \in [0,1]$ representing the fraction adopted
- <b> only external push</b>
- No initial adopters: $x(0) = 0$.
- $p$ adoption hazard
- $(1 - x)$ share of non-adopters

$$
\dot x = p \times (1 - x)
$$

The analytical solution is:

$$
x(t) = 1 - e^{-pt}
$$

We clearly that this model is linear in $x$ (the logistic is instead quadratic) and it is <b>always concave</b>, with adoption slowing down as $x \to 1$.

If we instead want to find the time $t(x)$ to reach a target, we simply set:

$$
\begin{align*}
1 - e^{-pt} &= x \\
e^{-pt} &= 1 - x\\
- pt &= \ln(1-x)\\
t(x) &= -\ln(1-x)/p

\end{align*}
$$

### <a id="3.2"> <b>3.2 Bass Diffusion</b> </a>

In this model, each nonadopter is influenced by twho channels:<b> adoption</b> (as in the commono source one) and <b>imitation or world of mouth</b>, expressed by the term $qx$, increasing in $x$:

$$
g(x) = p + qx
$$

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot x(t) &= g(x) \times (1 - x)\\
\dot x(t) &= (p + qx) \times (1 -x)
\end{align*}
$$

Two special cases in this model:
- $q = 0:$ commono source model
- $p = 0:$ pure imitation, needs $x(0)>0$ to start.

<div align = "center">
![Bass](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/bass.png)
</div>

However, note that the Bass Model requires <b>strong assumptions</b>:
- adoption only driven by direct persuasion ($p$ and $q$)
- homogeneous population, no spatial or network effects
- no threshold dynamics and no critical mass modeled
- no economic ingredients (prices, preferences etc etc)

and strong limitations too:
- at the final state: $x \to 1$ with no reversal
- the model cannot capture competition, substitution or churn.

### <a id="3.3"> <b>3.3 SIRS Models</b> </a>

Bass is an example of <b>spreading</b> model, epidemic models (SIRS) are the canonical toolkit for this scenarios.

We start by analyzing a <b>SI model </b>:

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot s &= - \beta s i, \quad s + i = 1\\
\dot i &= \beta s i, \quad s = 1 - i

\end{align*}
$$

if we add a recovery rate $\gamma$ and infection duration $1/\gamma$, we obtain a <b>SIR</b> model:

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot s &= - \beta s     \\
\dot i &= \beta s i - \gamma i\\
\dot r &= \gamma i

\end{align*}
$$

where $r$ is the recovery rate. We can define the <b>Basic Reproduction Number </b> $R_0$ as the number of infected from one person in a _fully_ subscettible population as:

$$
R_0 = \frac{\beta}{\gamma}
$$

In this case, we have an early growth if and only if $R_0 > 1$.

The effective reproduction number is then $R(t) = R_0 s(t)$. The spread slows down and eventually stops when:

$$
R_t = 1 \implies s = \frac{1}{R_0} \implies \approx r^* = 1 - \frac{1}{R_0}
$$

(since $s + i + r = 1$ and $i \to 0$).

_Intuition: once $r^*$ individuals are immune (recovered or vaccinated), each case generates less than one new case on average and infections dies out._

<div align = "center">
![Bass](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/sir.png)
</div>

Finally, a <b>SIRS</b> model introduces a waning immuunity that vanishes at rate $w$.

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot r &= \gamma i - w r\\
\dot s &= - \beta s i + wr\\
\dot i &= \beta si - \gamma i
\end{align*}
$$

The end state is <b>non trivial </b> and it is given by the vector $(s^*, i^*, r^*)$.

### <a id="3.4"> <b>3.4 Complex Contagions on a graph: a formal setup</b> </a>

We consider a network $G = (V,E)$, with noted $v \in V$ and edges $(u,v) \in E$.

- each node $x_v \in \{0, 1\}$ ($0$ non-adopter, $1$ adopter)$
-  adoption threshold $\theta$
- neighborhood $N(v) = \{u : (u,v) \in E \}$

The threshold rules says:

$$
x_v(t+1) = \begin{cases} 
1, & \displaystyle\sum_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} x_u(t) \geq \theta, \\
x_v(t), & \textbf{otherwise},
\end{cases}
$$

_Intuition: node $v$ adopts if $\theta$ or more neighbors are adopters._

### <a id="4.1"> <b>4.1 Coupled Dynamics: Lotka-Volterra Predator-Prey Model</b> </a>

Coupled dynamics are needed when two (or more) variables <b>evolve together</b>, leading to <b>cycles, attractors</b> and <b>repellors</b>. Common examples are predator and prey in _biology_, susceptible and infected in _epidemiology_, employment and wages in _economics_. In formulas, we can write:

$$
\dot x = f(x,y) \quad \dot y = g(x,y)
$$

The analysis of this systems comes from the <b>classification</b> of <b>fixed points</b> and <b>stability</b>, both analytically and numerically.

We start by analyzing the <b>Lotka-Volterra Predator-Prey Model</b>, representing the interaction between two species:
- $x(t)$: <b>preys (hares)</b>
- $y(t)$: <b>predators (lynx)</b>

<a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=DDEvlLa9z_U" target="_blank"> Predator-Prey Population Models || Lotka-Volterra Equations [Video]</a>

In isolation we know that:
- prey reproduces exponentially, $\dot x = \alpha x$
- predators die out: $\dot y = - \gamma y$.

But, if together in the same ecosystem:
- interaction <b>remove prey</b> at rate $\beta xy$
- interaction yields<b> predators growth </b>at rate $\delta xy$.

resulting in the following coupled system:

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot x &= \alpha x - \beta xy \\
\dot y &= \delta xy - \gamma y

\end{align*}
$$

<div align = "center">
![Lotka](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/lotka.png)
</div>

We now show the procedure determine <b>fixed points</b>.
We start by setting the following to find the equilibrium (fixed points $x_k^*, y_k^*)$:
$$
\dot x = f(x,y),   \quad \dot y = g(x,y)\\
f(x,y) = 0, \quad g(x,y) = 0 \implies (x_k^*, y_k^*)
$$

In the case of the LV model we have:

$$ 
\begin{align*}
\dot x &= \alpha x - \beta xy = x (\alpha - \beta y)\\
\dot y &= \delta xy - \gamma y = y (\delta x - \gamma)\\

\end{align*}

\\
\begin{cases}
    x (\alpha - \beta y) = 0 \\
    y (\delta x - \gamma) = 0
\end{cases}
$$

resultig in:
$$
(x^*, y^*) = (0,0)\quad \text{or} \quad (x^*, y^*) = (\gamma / \delta, \alpha / \beta)
$$

In general, the number of roots will depend on $f$ and $g$:
- <b>Zero</b>: no equilibria
- <b>One</b>: unique equilbrium
- <b>Finite number $> 1 $</b>: several isolated equilibria
- <b>inftyinitely many</b>: whole x-axis equilibria

_Note that, in higher dimensions, understanding stability and trajectories become more complicated that in the simple 1D case._

<div align = "center">
![Lotka](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/lotka2.png)
</div>

To visualize the trajectory of the system, a common approach is to <b>numerically solve the ODEs</b> with initial condition $(x_0,y_0)$ chosen and then run a series of simulations.
From the plot, we should then be able to clearly see if the trajectories are <b>converging</b>, <b>diverging</b> or <b>staying</b> in rotation around the fixed points.

<div align = "center">
![Lotka](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/lotka3.png)
</div>

Then, at a fixed point, a <b>non-linear system </b> can be approximated by a linear one.
Define <b>perturbations $u, v$</b> as:

$$
u := x - x^*, \quad v := y - y^*
$$

and, thanks to Taylor expansions, we end up in the dynamics for small perturbations:

$$

\left.
\begin{aligned}
    \dot{u} &= u \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\bigg|_{(x^\star, y^\star)} + v \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\bigg|_{(x^\star, y^\star)} + \mathcal{O}(u^2, v^2, uv) \\
    \dot{v} &= \underbrace{u \frac{\partial g}{\partial x}\bigg|_{(x^\star, y^\star)} + v \frac{\partial g}{\partial y}\bigg|_{(x^\star, y^\star)}}_{\text{ linear}} + \underbrace{\mathcal{O}(u^2, v^2, uv)}_{\text{higher order terms}}
\end{aligned}
\right\}
\quad \Longrightarrow \quad
\begin{bmatrix}
    \dot{u} \\
    \dot{v}
\end{bmatrix}
\approx
\underbrace{
\begin{bmatrix}
    \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \\[1em]
    \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial g}{\partial y}
\end{bmatrix}_{(x^\star, y^\star)}
}_{\text{Jacobian}}
\begin{bmatrix}
    u \\
    v
\end{bmatrix}

$$

The <b>eigenvalues of Jacobian </b> tell us whether <b>perturbations grow, decay or oscillate</b>.
In the 1D case, we had that:

$$
\delta \dot x = \delta \partial x(t) \implies \delta x(t) = \delta x(0)e^{\lambda t}
$$
- if $\lambda <0$: perturbations decay (<b>stable</b>)
- if $\lambda>0$: perturbations grow (<b>unstable</b>)

The same applies to the 2D case. We consider $z(t) = p e^{\lambda t}$, where $p$ is a costant vector.
<br/>Then we plug $\dot z = J z$, resulting in:

$$
\lambda p e ^{\lambda t} = J p e^{\lambda t} \implies Jp = \lambda p
$$

We can clearly see that the <b>eigenvalues of $J$</b> describe how small perturbations evolve.

Recall that to compute the eigenvalues of $J$ we can simply write and solve the <b>characteristic polynomial</b>:

$$
\det(J - \lambda I) = \lambda^2 - \tau \lambda + \Delta 
\\
\tau := \operatorname{Tr}(J) = f_x + g_y

\Delta := \det(J) = f_x g_y - f_y g_x

$$
leading to the following solution:
$$
\\
\lambda_{1,2} = \frac{\tau \pm \sqrt(\tau^2 - 4 \Delta)}{2}

$$

<b>The classification of the fixed points</b> can be done analytically depending on the characteristic polynomial:

- _(a)_: <b>Saddle</b>: $\Delta < 0$. $\lambda_i$ are real and have opposite single
- _(b,c)_: <b>Stable and Unstable Nodes</b>: $\Delta > 0$ and $\tau^2 \ge 4 \Delta$ (stable with $\tau <0$).
    Also known as attractors and repellors, $\lambda_i$ are real and have the same sign.
- _(d,e)_: <b>Stable and Unstable Spiral</b>: $\tau \neq 0$ and $\tau^2 < 4 \Delta$ (stable with $\tau <0$). Also known as attractive focus and repulsive focus, $\lambda_i$ are complex.
- _(f)_: <b>Center</b>: $\tau = 0 $ and $\Delta > 0$. $\lambda_i$ are complex with zero real part.

</div>

<b>Example: Stable/Unstable Spirals or Center in the Lotka-Volterra Model</b>.

We consider the following model:

$$

f(x, y) = \dot{x} = \alpha x - \beta xy, \quad g(x, y) = \dot{y} = \delta xy - \gamma y

$$

Explicitily computing the Jacobian:

$$

J(x, y) = \begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \\
\frac{\partial g}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial g}{\partial y}
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}
\alpha - \beta y & -\beta x \\
\delta y & \delta x - \gamma
\end{bmatrix}

$$

we then find fixed points:

$$

(x^\star, y^\star) \in \left\{ (0, 0), \left( \frac{\gamma}{\delta}, \frac{\alpha}{\beta} \right) \right\}


$$
and we evaluate them at:
$$

J(0,0) = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha & 0 \\ 0 & -\gamma \end{bmatrix}, \quad \tau = \alpha - \gamma, \quad \Delta = -\alpha\gamma < 0
$$

$$
\Rightarrow \quad \lambda_{1,2} = \{ \alpha, -\gamma \} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \textbf{saddle}


$$

$$
J\left( \frac{\gamma}{\delta}, \frac{\alpha}{\beta} \right) = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -\frac{\beta\gamma}{\delta} \\ \frac{\delta\alpha}{\beta} & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \tau = 0, \quad \Delta = \alpha\gamma > 0
$$
$$
\Rightarrow \quad \lambda_{1,2} = \pm i\sqrt{\alpha\gamma} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \text{\textbf{center}}

$$

### <a id="4.2"> <b>4.2 Coupled Dynamics: Goodwin's Model (1967), Cycles in Employment and Wages</b> </a>

<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodwin_model_(economics)" target="_blank">The Goodwin's Model [Wikipedia]</a>

This model comes from a simple observation: <b>economies often show recurrent cycles</b>.<br/> Booms cause high employment, so workers gain bargaining power, then wages rise, wage share of outpus increases as well. As a consequence, profits fall, investment slows and growth stalls.
<br/>Employment then falls, weakening workers' position, wages stagnate or declinde, profits recover and growth resumes.

<b>Goodwin's idea</b> is to model class struggle dynamics with similar equations used for predator-prey cycles.

We then consider two state variables:
- $q_t$: <b>employment ratio</b> (jobs over the population) - _analogy with the prey population_
- $w_t$: <b>wage share</b> (workers' share of output) - _analogy with the predator population_

The <b>qualitative</b> cycle logics says that:
- $q \uparrow \implies$ bargaining power $\uparrow \implies w \uparrow$
- $w \uparrow \implies$ profits $\downarrow \implies$ investment $\downarrow \implies q \downarrow$
- $q \downarrow \implies$ wage pressure $\downarrow \implies w \downarrow \implies$ profits $\uparrow \implies q \uparrow$

Formally, if we consider the following variables:
- $q_t = l_t / n_t$ employment ratio
- $w_t = w_t l_t / Y_t = w_t / a_t$ wage share
- $Y_t$ total output (GDP), with capital-output ratoi $\sigma$ such that: $\dot Y_t = \frac{1-w_t}{\sigma}Y_t$
- $k_t$ capital stock (invested at: $\dot k_t = (1 - w_t)Y_t$)
- $l_t$ employed labor
- $n_t$ total labor force, growing at rate $\beta$.
- $a_t = Y_t / l_t$ labor productivity, growing a rate $\alpha$.
- $w_t$ real wage, following the Philipps Curve with: $\frac{\dot w_t}{w_t} = - \gamma + \kappa q_t$

and parameters $\alpha, \beta, \gamma, \kappa, \sigma$.

We then differentiate the employment ratio and the wage share:

$$
\frac{\dot q_t}{q_t} = \frac{\dot l_t}{l_t}- \frac{\dot n_t}{n_t} = \frac{\dot Y_t}{Y_t} - \frac{\dot a_t}{a_t} - \beta = \frac{1 - w_t}{\sigma} - \beta
$$

$$
\frac{\dot w_t}{w_t} = \frac{\dot w_t}{w_t} - \frac{\dot a_t}{a_t} = (- \gamma + \kappa q_t) - \alpha
$$

Putting toghether, we have the <b>Goodwmin's System</b>:

$$
\begin{align*}
\dot q_t &= \big( \frac{1}{\sigma} - \alpha - \beta \big)q_t - \frac{1}{\sigma}w_t q_t\\
\dot w_t &= \kappa q_t w_t - (\gamma + \alpha) w_t 

\end{align*}
$$

Simplifying, we get:
$$
\dot q_t = A q_t - B w_t q_t, \quad \dot w_t = B q_t w_t - C w_t\\

\dot q = q (A - Bw), \quad \dot w = (Dq - C)
$$

The intuition, very similar to the previous predator-prey model, is that _workers and capitalists_ are two interacting groups. Workers indeed seek higher wages, capitalists seek higher profits.
These opposing objectives create a <b>self-regulating cycle</b>, neither side "wins" permanently but they co-move in economic cycles.

From an economic point of view:
- <b>Growth-recessions cycles are endogenous</b> without external shocks
- <b>Booms</b> (high $q$) are the beginning of slowdowns (rising $w$) and viceversa
- The neutral center is not robust, small modifications destroy cycles.

### <a id="5.1"> <b>5.1 Discrete Dynamics: Cobweb Plot</b> </a>

There are several reason behind why we should <b>discrete time dynamics</b>:
- decision timing: firms and individuals set prices/output quarterly or at fixed intervals
- institutional calendars: central banks and governments meet on fixed dates
- contracts and billing: loans, rent and utilities are paid monthly or similarly
- observed data: GDP, monthly unemployments, annual reports etc etc...

To start with discrete time dynamics, we define the <b>difference equation</b>, representing how the next state depends on the previous:

$$
x_{t + 1} = f(x_t) \quad \text{{update rule}}
$$

The evolution if then given by the repeated application of $f$:

$$
\begin{align*}

x_1 &= f(x_0)\\
x_2 &= f((f_0))\\
...\\
x_t &= f^{(t)}(x_0)
\end{align*}
$$

To visualize this, we usually use <b>Cobweb Plot</b>, showing the evolution of the discrete rule.
- Start with a initial value $x_0$.
- Move vertically till the blue line representing $f(x)$ and find $f(x_0)$.
- Move horizontally to the diagonal $x = y$ and find $x_1$.
- Repeat with $x_1$ and iterate.

<div align = "center">
![Cobweb](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/cobweb.png)
</div>

Each update defined a sequence:
$$
x_{t +1} = f(x) \implies X = \{ x_0, x_1, x_2 ...\}
$$

when $f(x)$ crosses $ y = x$ we have <b>fixed points</b> $x^* = f(x^*)$.

_Note that the sequence may cycle set of points (periodi orbits) or may be aperiodic (chaotic) deterministic, but not repeating._

To determine the <b>fixed points</b>, we have to find $z_t^*$ such that $z_{t+1} = f(z_t)$.

We then consider, as usual, the deviation from the fixed point:

$$
z_{t+1} - z^* = f(z_t) - f(z^*)
$$

and we linearize around $z^*$:

$$
f(z_t) \approx f(z^*) + f'(z^*)(z_t - z^*) 
\implies z_{t+1} - z^* \approx f'(z^*) (z_t - z^*)
$$

if we then iterate $t$ times we obtain:

$$
z_t - z^* \approx [f'(z^*)]^t (z_0 - z^*)
$$

To determine if it is stable or not, we simply look at:
- $|f'(z^*)| < 1 \implies:$ attracting
- $|f'(z^*)| > 1 \implies:$ repelling
- $0 < f'(z^*)< 1 \implies:$ monotone
- $-1 < f'(z^*)< 0 \implies:$ alternating

### <a id="5.2"> <b>5.2 Discrete Dynamics: Saturated (Logistic) Growth</b> </a>

We previously analyzed the continuous-time logistic growth as:

$$
\dot x = rx(1 -r) = rx - rx^2
$$

if we apply the Euler discretization as:

$$
\frac{dx}{dt} = \frac{x_{t + \Delta t} - x_t}{\Delta t}
$$

we can then write:

$$
x_{t+1}- x_t = rx_t (1 - x_t) \implies
\begin{align*}

x_{t+1} &= x_t + rx_t(1 -x_t)\\
&= (1 + r)x_t - rx_t^2\\
&= (1 + r)x_t (1 - \frac{r}{r+1}x_t)

\end{align*}
$$

if we define $z_t = x_t \frac{r}{1+r}$:

$$
\begin{align*}
z_{t+1} &= (1 + r)z_t (1 - z_t)\\
&= R z_t (1 - z_t)

\end{align*}
$$

We now determine <b>fixed points</b>:

$$
f'(z) = R(1 - 2Z), \quad z^* = \frac{R -1}{R}
\\

f'(z^*) = 2 - R

$$

we now apply the stability condition on the first derivative $f'(z^*)$:
- if $1 < R < 3$ then $|2 - R| < 1$ and the <b>fixed points attract </b>
- at $R = 3: f'(z^*) = - 1$: <b>flip bifurcation</b> (fixed points becomes repelling: stable 2-cycles).

_Intuition: As long as the growth rate $R$ is between 1 and 3, the population settles down to $z^*$. At exactly $R=3$, the slope becomes $-1$. This is the "tipping point": The system can no longer settle at a single number._

In the second period, if $R > 3$, the population starts jumping back and forth between two values, $z_1$ and $z_2$, every period.
The period-2 points solve $f(f(z)) = z$, resulting in:

$$
z_{1,2} = \frac{R + 1 \pm \sqrt(R -3 )(R +1)}{2R} \quad \text{for } R > 3
$$
As in the first cycle, in  the $k$-cycle, we obtain stability is reached when: $|(f^{(k)})'(z^*)| < 1$, that, for the $2$-cycle rewrites as:

$$
3 < R < 1 + \sqrt(6) \approx 3.44949
$$

At $R = 1 + \sqrt(6)$ the $2$-cycle does another flip _(stable 4-cycle, the population repeats a pattern of 4 different values)_

For many periods, we now consider $R_n$ the value of $R$ where a $2^n$-cycle first appears:

$$
\begin{align*}
R_1 &= 3.000\\
R_2 &= 3.449\\
R_3 &= 3.544\\
...\\


R_{\infty} &= 3.569
\end{align*}
$$

The sequence <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_series" target="_blank"> converges geometrically</a> to $R_{\infty} = 3.569$

We then define the <b>Feigenbaum constant</b> as:

$$
\delta = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{R_{n+1}-R_n}{R_{n+2} - R_{n+1}} \approx 4.6692
$$

resulting in the <b>Scaling Law</b> for $R_n$:

$$
R_n \approx R_{infty} - c \delta ^{-n}, \quad c_{logistic} \approx 2.632
$$

<a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Costanti_di_Feigenbaum" target="_blank">More on the Feigenbaum Constants [Wikipedia]</a>

<div align = "center">
![Cobweb](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/bifurcation.png)
</div>

- For $R_{\infity} < R < 4$ we can see a <b>mixture of order and chaos</b>: periodic windows reappe within chaotic bands.
- At $R = 4$ we see <b>fully developed chaos</b>.

This is called <b>deterministic chaos</b>: once $x_0$ and $r$ are fixed, each step is predetermined, but long term behavior becomes <b>unpredictable</b>, because tiny differences in $x_0$ grow rapidly.

A deterministc system is then called <b>chaotic</b> when it shows:
- <b>aperiodic long-run behavior</b> (no fixed points or repeating patterns)
- <b>determinism</b>
- <b>sensitivity to initial conditions</b>

Note that, as we've seen, <b>deterministic </b> $\neq$ <b>predictable</b>. In _non-linear systems_, tiny errors or deviations can explode over time, therefore long term forecasts lose meaning.

### <a id="6.1"> <b>6.1 Endogenous Cycles</b> </a>

In the economy, markets are _rarely_ isolated (producers can switch between goods, farmers can sell domestically or abroad, energy and manufacturing costs increase etc, etc...)
We start now from the simple Cobweb Model but allow producers to <b>switch markets</b>.

Each market $i \in \{ A, B\}$ has a ficed supplier share $W_i$, with $W_A = 1 - W_B$.

$$
D_{i,t} = \frac{a_i - P_{i,t}}{b_i}, \quad S_{i, t} = \frac{P_{i,t-1}- c_i}{d_i}
$$

if we set the market clearing conditin $D_i = W_i S_i$:

$$
\frac{a_i - P_{i,t}}{b_i} = \frac{P_{i,t}- c_i}{d_i}
$$

then we can find the <b>Price-Update Rule</b>:

$$
P_{i,t} = a_i + \frac{b_i W_i c_i}{d_i} - {b_i W_i}{d_i} P_{i, t-1} = \alpha_i - \beta_i P_{i, t-1}
$$

At the equilibrium:

$$
P_i^* = \frac{a_i d_i + b_i c_i W_i}{d_i + b_i W_i}
\\
|\beta_i! = | W_i \frac{b_i}{w_i}| < 1

$$

_Note that larger $W_i \implies $ faster response to equilibrium and then less stability._

We now consider <b>costs and profits</b> in each market.
Each supplier earn $\pi_i$, depending on price and total quantity:

$$
\pi_i = P_{i,t}S_{i,t} - C_i(S_{i,t})
$$

assuming that cost increases with ouput: 

$$
C_i(S_{i,t}) = g_i S_i + e_i S_{i,t}^2, \quad e_i > 0
$$

we result in the following profit funtion:

$$
\pi_t = P_{i,t}S_{i,t}- (g_i S_i + e_i S_{i,t}^2)
$$

Now we can see the coupled system as a <b>sequence of events</b>:

- The suppliers produce $W_{i,t} (\fra{P_{i, t-1} - g_i}{d_i})$, with $W_{i,t}$ is the probability of choosing a market depending on the profits:
$$
W_{i,t} = \frac{1}{1 + \exp(- f \times (\pi_{A, t-1} - \pi_{B, t-1}))}
$$

where  $f \ge 0$ is the _intensity of choice_ (rationality).
- Prices adjust to clear each market:
$$
P_{i,t} = a_i + \frac{b_i W_i c_i}{d_i} - {b_i W_i}{d_i} P_{i, t-1} 
$$
- Profits are realized
- Suppliers update market choice for the next period

To understand if it is chaos or just complex, we can perform a <b>FFT test</b>.<br/>
A time series can be indeed seen as a <b>mix of pure waves</b>. We then apply the _Fourier Transform_ to understand how much of each frequency is present.

Plotting then $|X(\text{frequency})|$ gives a spectrum: 
- peaks in the spectrum represents cycles (_not chaotic)_
- no clear peaks in the spectrum implies that there's not a dominant rhythm.

In general, we can conclude about endogenous cycles that:
- <b>Volatility is structural</b> and it is amplified by a large supplier share in the market, faster switching intensity $f$ and excessive supply response (low $d$).
- Cycles create <b>deadweight loss</b>: mis-times investment and wasted inventory/resources.
- Coupling transmits fluctuations across markets.

### <a id="6.2"> <b>6.2 Endogenous Cycles: Kaldor Model (Business Cycle) </b> </a>

We now model a 2-player model with:
- Household that can either consume or save: $Y = C + S$
- Capicalists that can either consume or invest: $Y = C + I$
- Savings fund investments: $S \to I$ via banks
- At equilibrium: $S = I$.

Starting from the setup we have:
- Households consume $C$ and save $S$.
- Firms plan investment $I^{p}$ based on the expecteded consumption $C^{e}$
- in reality, $C^{e} \neq C^{acqual}$.
- The gap shows up as:

$$
\Delta \text{Inv.} = C^{e} - C^{acqual} = - (I - S)
$$

- The firm reacts:
    - If $\Delta \text{Inv.} > 0 \implies$: overproduction, cut output
    - If $\Delta \text{Inv.} > 0 \implies$: underproduction, expand output
- Leading to the dynamic rule:

$$
\dot Y = \alpha (I(y) - S(Y)), \quad \alpha > 0
$$

we can then incur in two scenarios:
- if $S$ has a steeper slope than $I$ (at the right of the equilibrium $\dot Y < 0$ and at the left $\dot Y > 0$): <b>stable equilibrium</b>.
- if $I$ has a steeper slope than $S$ (at the right of the equilibrium $\dot Y > 0$ and at the left $\dot Y < 0$): <b>unstable equilibrium</b>.

<div align = "center">
![Business Cycles](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/business_cycle.png)
</div>

So far, we treaded investment and savings as a function of the output, in reality, they also depend on the capital stock $K$, that accumulates through investment and declines thorugh depreciation, reflecting <b>past investment decisions</b>. We can then write (<b>Kaldor Model </b>):
$$
\dot Y = \alpha (I(Y,K)- S(Y,K )),      
$$

In this model, if we assume that $K$ is set externally, we have 3 equilibria: two are stable (A,C) and one unstable (B)

<div align = "center">
![Business Cycles](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/kaldor.png)
</div>

$K$ then introduces a <b>hysteresis cycle</b>: the equilibrium state is path dependent.

- an economy starts on the high $Y$ branch (high income).
- as $K$ increases, the economy moves to the right along the top red line. 
- eventually, it reaches the "knee" or the edge of the cliff.
    - if $K$ increases past this point, the economy crashes down to the low $Y$ branch.
- once it falls to the bottom level, it remain stuck in the low-income state 
- to get back to the high-income state, the economy jas to decrease $K$  to move way to the left on the graph and to find the other "knee" where the system jumps up.

<div align = "center">
![Business Cycles](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/hysteresis2.png)
</div>

_Note that, since no equilibrium satisfies $\dot Y = \dot K = 0$, the economy follows a closed cycle._

<div align = "center">
![Business Cycles](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/hysteresis.png)
</div>


If we then allow $K$ to have its own dyncamics, we have the <b>full Kaldor Model</b>.
$$
\dot K = I(Y,K) - \delta K
$$

The economy then movesd around the <b>unstable equilibrium</b>: the interaction of fast $Y$ and slow $K$ creates endogenous cycles. _Note that $\alpha$ represents the speed of the economy, while $\delta$ the speed of capital and they both shape the curvature of the cycles._

### <a id="7.1"> <b>7.1 Introduction to Networks </b> </a>

A network (graph) is tuple $G = (V,E)$:
- $V$: the set of nodes (vertices)
- $E \subseteq V \times V$: set of links (edges)
- $V \times V$: all possible ordered pairs

We use the <b>direction convention</b>:
$$
(i, j) \in E \implies \text{link points fromm i to j}
$$

A network can be stored as a <b>adjacency matrix A</b>:

$$
A_{ij} = 
\begin{cases}
1 \quad \text{if link }(i,j) \in E \\
0 \quad \text{otherwise}

\end{cases}
$$

- Rows represent the <b>sources (outgoing link)</b>
- Column represent the <b>targets (incoming links)</b>

A network is <b>directed </b>if $A_{ij} = A_{ji}\quad \forall i,j$

In a <b>weighted network </b> links have a numerical strength. A function $w : E \to \R$ assigns a weight to each link and the adjacency matrix becomes real-valued.

A link of the form $(i,i)$ is called <b>self-loopz</b> and it appears in the diagonal entry $A_{ii}$ _(economically, it represents the self-dependence or internal feedback)_.

A <b>node's degree </b> is the number of links connected to it _(economically: they represent connectivity of a node)_. Also, in a directed network, we can distinguish:
- <b>indegree</b> $d_{in}(i)$: number of incoming links $(j,i)$
- <b>outdegree</b> $d_{out}(i)$: number of incoming links $(i,j)$

In a weighted network, a <b>node's strength</b> sums the weights of its connections:

$$
w_{in}(in) = \sum_j w(j,i), \quad w_{out}(in) = \sum_j w(i,j)
$$

In a unweighted network, a node's strength is a simple in-and-out degree count.

A <b>path between nodes</b> $v$ and $w$ is a sequence $(p_0, p_1, p_2... p_l)$ such that:
- $p_0 = v, p_l = w$
- each pair $(p_i, p_{i+1}) \in E$

The path length is, _of course_, the number of links.

The <b>distance </b> between two nodes is the length of the shortest path connecting them. If no path exists: $\text{dist}(v,w) = \infty$.

The <b>network diameter</b> is the longest shortest path in the network.

$$
\text{diag}(G) = \max_{(v,w) \in V \times V} \text{dist}(v,w)
$$

The <b>average shortest path length </b> measures typical separation.

$$
<l> = \frac{1}{|V|^2} \sum_{(v,w) \in V \times V} \text{dist}(v,w)
$$

A network is <b>connected</b> if $\text{dist}(v,w)$ for all $$v,w \in V$.

<b>Connected components</b> are connected subgraphs $G' = (V', E') \subseteq G$.

The <b>largest component </b> is the <b>giant connected component (GCC) </b> if $|V'| / |V| \approx 1$.

<b>Degree centrality</b> of anode is the number of its direct links _(economically it measures the connectivity as exposure/access to partners, lenders, clients or other nodes)_1

<b>Betweeness centrality</b> counts how often node $i$ lioes on the shortest paths between others:

$$
C_B(i)= \sum_{s \neq i, t \neq i}n_i (s,t)
$$

where %n_i(s,t)$ is the shortest path $s \to t$ through $i$. In the _normalized form_, we also write:

$$
C_B(i)= \sum_{s \neq i, t \neq i}n_i (s,t) / N_{st}
$$

where $N_{st}$ is the total number of shortest shortest paths $s \to t$ through $i$.

### <a id="8.1"> <b>8.1 Diffusion on Networks</b> </a>

We define diffusion as flow across links (in the two nodes case). Suppose we have two noes $i,j$ connected by one link: the flow is then proportional to the difference between two states.
We can then write an ODEs for the nodes:

$$
\frac{dx_i}{dt} = C(x_j - x_i), \quad \frac{dx_j}{dt} = C(x_i - x_j)
$$

with the <b>conservation constraint</b>: $\frac{d}{dt}(x_i + x_j) = 0$ and parameter $C$ with unit $\text{time}^-1$.

The previous ODEs bring the <b>convergence to stationarity</b>:

$$
x_i(t) - x_j(t) = (x_i(0)- x_j(0))e^{-2Ct}
$$

If we generalize this to a graph and define an ODE for every node:

$$
\dot x_i = C \sum_{j} A_{ij}(x_j - x_i)
$$

with the conservation contraint: $\frac{d}{dt}\sum_i x_i = 0$ (average preserved).

---

From the aggregate network, we can study the node-level dynamics. Consider a node $i$:

$$
x_i = \underbrace{g(x_i)}_\text{local adoption} + \underbrace{C \sum_{j} A_{ij}(x_j - x_i)}_\text{diffusion between links}
$$

_(For example, we could choose $g(\dot)$ modelling a Bass diffusion and then the rest to model the network)_

---

To study better the behavior of the network, we proceed by computing the <b>Laplacian Matrix</b>.<br/>
Consider degrees $k_i = \sum_j A_{ij}$ and $K = \text{diag}(k_1,k_2... k_n)$.

The Laplacian Matrix is  ${\mathcal{L}} = K - A$ and we can then rewrite the ODEs compactly:

$$
\dot x = - C {\mathcal{L}} x
$$

We now proceed with the <b> spectral decomposition of the Laplacian Matrix</b>.
_Recall: the spectral decomposition of a generic diagonalisable matrix $S$ is $S= U^{-1} D_{\lambda} U$, where $U$ is the eigenvector matrix and $D_{\lambda}$ the eigenvalues diagonal matrix._

Define $\{w_i\}$ as the eigenvectors, forming an orthonormal basis. Then we write: ${\mathcal{L}} w_i = \omega_i w_i $ with $0 - \omega_1 < \omega_2 ...$.

Define then the projections $\alpha_i(0) := w_i^T x(0)$ (_intuition: $\alpha_i$ are the time-dependent coefficients showing how "strong" each pattern/eigenvector is at time $t$)_ and then:

$$
x(0) = \sum_i \alpha_i(0)w_i, \quad \alpha_1(0) = 0
$$

Now plug $x(t) = \sum_i \alpha_i (t)w_i$  into $\dot x = - C {\mathcal{L}} x$. Since $w_i$ is an eigenvector, multiplying it by the matrix $\mathcal{L}$ is simple: $\mathcal{L} w_i = \omega_i w_i$, resulting in:

$$
\sum_i \dot{\alpha}_i(t) w_i = \sum_i (-C \omega_i \alpha_i(t)) w_i
$$

Because eigenvectors are linearly independent, we can splits the big matrix problem into $N$  separate problems:

$$
\dot{\alpha}_i(t) = -C \omega_i \alpha_i(t)
$$

This is the <b>decoupling </b> of the ODE: each $\alpha_i$ evolves _indepedently_ and we can solve each one, resulting in:

$$
\alpha_i(t) = \alpha_i(0) e^{-C \omega_i t}
$$

and, finally, in:

$$
x(t) = \sum_i \alpha_i(0) e^{-C \omega_i t} w_i
$$

- the term with the smallest eigenvalue may have $e^0 = 1$ and therefore this one does not decay, representing the <b>final equilibrium state</b> _( average value across the network)_
- the terms with large eigenvalues decay very fast. 
- the terms with small eigenvalues decay slowly.


Now we want a <b>single number</b> to measure how far we are from stationarity.
- we start by computing the average: $\bar x = \mathbb{1}^T x / n$
- we compute the per-node deviation: $d_i(t) = x_i(t) - \bar x$.
- we compute the <b>network deviation</b> and track it over time: 
$$
D(t) = \frac{1}{2} || z(t)||^2 = \frac{1}{2} \sum_i (x_i(t)- \bar x)^2
$$

In fact, after some algebraic manipulation, we can see that $D(t)$ decays with rate $2C\omega_2$. 

So, if we consider a <b>connected network</b>, the second-smallest eigenvalue $\omega_2$ is called the <b>algebraic connectivity</b> (also known as <b>Fiedler Vector</b>) and determines how fast or slow diffusion is. The smaller $\omega_2$, the harder is to cross the network _(capturing how well-connected a network is)_.

If we now consider a network with <b>two disconnected components</b>:
- each block in the matrix ${\mathcal{L}}$ is another Laplacina Matrix
- each has a trivial eigenvalue $0$ with eigenvectors $(1,1,1)$ and $(1,1,1,1)$ (depending on the component size)

Therefore, ${\mathcal{L}}$ has <b>two zero eigenvalues</b>, so $\omega_2 = 0$ and the network is disconnected.



If we consider then the <b>deviations</b> $z = x - \bar x$, we can simply derive that deviations follow the same linear system:
$$
\dot z = - C \mathbb{1} z
$$

and, therefore, face the same dynamics:
$$
z = \sum^n_{i = 1} \alpha_i (0) e^{-C\omega_i t}w_i
$$

of course, as before, approach to stationarity is determined by eigenvalues of $\mathbb{1}$. _Note that, however, $z(t) \to 0$ (it's mean free!)_.

### <a id="9.1"> <b>9.1 Community Detection: Dirichlet Energy, Rayleigh Quotient and Network Bisection</b> </a>

Considering what we said in the previous chapter, we can go on studying networks.
We start by defining the <b>energy od a single edge </b> $(i,j)$ for a node state $x$ as:

$$
\epsilon_{ij}[x] = A_{ij} |x_i - x_j|^2
$$

- the energy _penalizes disagreement_ across a link: it's zero if neighbors agree.
- it is large on weak links where values "jump" between nodes.

If we then sum over all edges, we obtain the <b>Dirichlet Graph Energy</b>:

$$
\epsilon[x] = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{i,j} A_{ij} |x_i - x_j|^2 = \frac{1}{2} x^T  {\mathcal{L}} x
$$

Then, for any non-zero node, we can define the <b>Rayleigh Quotient</b> ${\mathcal{R}}[v]$, representing how well the node $v$ fits the network or how uneven values are across linked regions _(smooth profiles show small ${\mathcal{R}}$)

$$
{\mathcal{R}}[v] = \frac{v^T {\mathcal{L}} v}{v^T v} = \frac{\sum_{i,j} A_{ij} |x_i - x_j|^2}{\sum_i v_i^2}
$$

_At the numerator we have the total edge energy, large if neighbors differ a lot, at the denominator a scale normalization._

Also, we know that:

$$
\omega_2 = \min_{v \neq 0} {\mathcal{R}}[v], \quad w_2 = \argmin {\mathcal{R}}[v]

$$

_intuition: $w_2$ is the vector that minimizes the Rayleigh quotient_.

Now, to identify communities within a network, consider the following two sets:

$$
S^+ = \{ i: w_2(i) > 0\}, \quad S^- = \{ i: w_2(i) < 0\}
$$

_intuition: $w_2$ minimizes tension across edges, leaning to a smooth split across the weakest connection._

We can then apply this procedure recursively to find finer communities. Common stopping criteria are: max depth and min size.
_Note that bisection using the fiedler vector cannot detect more than two communities at once_.

![SIR](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/fiedler.png)
</div>

<b>Example: Spectral bisection algorithm in Python</b>:

```python 
def spectral_bisection(G):
   L = laplacian(G) # L = K - A
   w2 = second_eigenvector(L) # Fiedler vector
   Spos = {i for i in G.nodes if w2[i] > 0}
   Sneg = set(G.nodes) - Spos
   return Spos, Sneg
def recursive_spectral(G, depth=0, max_depth=3):
   if depth == max_depth or len(G) < 3:
       return [set(G.nodes)]
   Spos, Sneg = spectral_bisection(G)
   return (recursive_spectral(G.subgraph(Spos), depth+1, max_depth)
         + recursive_spectral(G.subgraph(Sneg), depth+1, max_depth))
```

### <a id="9.2"> <b>9.2 Network Eigenvector Centralities</b> </a>

We saw in the last chapters that <b>degree counts links</b>, but not all links are equal. Indeed, a connection to an _important node_ should matter more than one to an unimportant node.
_Inutition: each node's importante should depend oon the importance of its neighbors_.

$$
x_i \propto \sum_j A_{ij} x_j
$$

To compute then the centrality $x_i$ of a node, we can start with a guress and repeatedly update it using the network structure.
- we start with initial centrality $x^0$ (all equal to one)
- we sum all the neighbots centralities with $\kappa$ const. of proportionality.

$$
x_i = \kappa ^{-1} \sum x_j
$$

or, _equivalently:

$$
x_i = \kappa ^{-1} \sum_j A_{ij} x_j = \kappa^{-1} A x
$$

This reduced to the <b>eigenvector equation </b>:

$$
Ax = \kappa x
$$

with the <b>leading eigenvector </b> $x$ giving the <b>eigenvector centrality</b>.

- we repeat this procedure until the vector of centralities $x_i$ converges.

_Intuition: high centrality comes from many neighbors (quantity) and/or a few but very central neighbors (quality). Also, note that eigenvector centrality works best for connected, undirected networks_.

Also, note that <b>root nodes</b> have no incoming edgedm, so it gets $x_i = 0$, by consequence, any node that is pointed to _only by_ the root also gets $x_i = 0$.
_(the zero propagates backward through the graph: nodes reachable only from roots inherit zero centrality)_.

### <a id="9.3"> <b>9.3 Katz and PageRank Centralities</b> </a>

To fix the previous problem of the propagation of the zero, <b> Katz Centrality</b> fives each node a small $\beta > 0$, so importance can start _everywhere_:

$$
x_i = \alpha \sum_j A_{ij} x_j + \beta \iff x = \beta (I - \alpha A)^{-1} \mathbb{1}
$$

_Intuition: $\beta$ adds ecogenous centrality._
Note: choose $\alpha < 1 / \kappa_1$ to ensure convergence.

The problem in this model is that an <b>high centrality</b> node can pass large score to many neighbors.
To fix this, the <b>PageRank algorithm</b> dilutes a node's contribution by its out-degree:

$$
x_i = \alpha \sum_j A_{ij} \frac{x_j}{k_j^{out}} + \beta
$$

_PageRank it was firstly introducted by Google to rank web pages, widely used because it downweights mega-directories that link to millions. Google famously used $\alpha \approx 0.85$.

In matrix form, the PageRank model rewrites as:

$$
x = (I - \alpha A D^{-1})^{-1} + \mathbb{1}B, \quad D = \text{diag} ( \max (k_j^{out}, 1))
$$

note that:
- $\beta$ does not affect the results (just scaling)
- $\alpha$ must be smaller than the largest eigenvalue of $AD^{-1}$.

### <a id="10.1"> <b>10.1 Strategic Network Formation</b> </a>

Given a formed network, we now model how agents can influence and modify the network.
Agents can be representes as noded on a network $G = (N, E)$ with a utility $u_i(G)$ based on their position:
$$
u_i(G) = \text{benefits from G}-\text{costs}
$$

The social welfare is then:

$$
W(G) = \sum_i u_i(G)
$$

A network $G^*$ is <b>socially efficient </b> if $G^* = \argmax_G W(G)$. (_intuition: no other feasible network yields higher total welfare)_.

<b>Pairwise stability</b> occurs when:
- no agent wants to cut an existing link
- no agent pairs wants to add a new link

### <a id="10.2"> <b>10.2 The Connections Model (1996)</b> </a>

In this model, agents are nodes in an undirected network, links give access to information and benefits come from both direct and indirect neighbours, with the following utility:

$$
u_i(G) = \sum_{j \neq i} \delta^{d_{ig}(G)} - cd_i
$$

where $d_{ij}$ is the shortest-path disstance between $i,j$, $\delta^{d_{ig}(G)} = 0$ is $j$ is not reachable, $\delta \in (0,1) represents then the benefit decay per step and each link costs $c$.

We now analyze the welfare of a <b>complete graph $K_n$</b>, where every node is linked to all others.
For node $i$, we have the following utility:

$$
\begin{align*}
u_i(K_n) &= \sum_{j \neq i} \delta^{d_{ig}} - cd_i \\
&= \sum_{j \neq i} \delta^{-1} - c (n-1)\\
&= (n-1)\delta - c(n-1)\\
&= (n-1)(\delta - c)

\end{align*}
$$

resulting in the following total welfare:
$$
W(K_n) = n (n-1)(\delta - c)
$$

A similar approach can be applied to the <b>Star Graph</b> $S_n$:

$$
u_0(Sn) = (n-1)(\delta - c) \quad \text{(center)}

\\

W(S_n) = (n-1)[2 \delta + (n-2)\delta^2 - 2c]
$$

We can now compare when $S_n$ is better than the empty network:

$$
W(S_n) > 0 \iff 2 \delta + (n-2)\delta^2 - 2c > 0 \iff c < \delta + \frac{n - 2}{2} \delta^2
$$

and when $K_n$ is better than $S_n$:

$$
\begin{align*}
W(K_n) &> W(S_n) \\
n(\delta - c) &> 2 \delta + (n-2)\delta^2 - 2\\
0 &> (n-2)(c + \delta^2 - \delta)\\
c &< \delta - \delta^2, \quad n > 2


\end{align*}
$$

![welfare](/images/notes/econ-din-compl/welfare.png)
</div>

In these models, we often incurr in <b>externalities</b>: actions that change other's utility but whose effects the decision kamer does not internalize.

<b>Positive externalities </b> include new links make others better (shorter paths) but, since agents do not take these benefits into account, the result is <b>under connected </b> network _(too few links in equilibrium)_.

<b>Negative externalitiea</b> include that links can harm others _(diluiting resources), resulting in a <b>over connected </b> network.

To compensate externalities, we can introduce <b>side payments </b> in the moel to share $W(G)$.

The idea is that, if a link increases $W(G)$, endpoints can transfer payoffs, so losers can be compe sated by winners:

$$
(\pi_1(G), \pi_2(G)...)\quad  \text{with}\quad \sum_i \pi_i(G) = W(G)
$$

### <a id="10.3"> <b>10.3 The Co-Author Model</b> </a>

Nodes represent researchers, links are co-autorships.
- A collaboration $ij$ creates value for both but...
    - each researcher has limited time
    - a collaborator with many co-authors is less focused
- adding a link reduced efficiency for _all other projects_.
- <b>Negative externality</b>: when a researcher takes another project with someone else, it reduces the value of existing collaborations.

Let $d_i$ the number of co-authors (degree) of researcher $i$:

$$
u_i(G) =
\begin{cases}
\sum_j (\frac{1}{d_i}+ \frac{1}{d_j} \frac{1}{d_i d_j}), \quad d_i > 0
0   \quad d_i = 0
\end{cases}
$$

We can clearly see that, if $i$ increases $d_i$: every one of $i$'s projects becomes less valuable.

In this specific model, welfare is maximized when each collaboratoin has 2 _fully focused researchers_:
$$
d_i = 1 \quad \forall i
$$

The <b>efficient network</b> (maximum welfare) is a set of disjoint pairs (for even $n$).

### <a id="11.1"> <b>11.1 Dynamics of Strategic Network Formation</b> </a>

Knowing which networks are efficient or stable doesn't tewll us which networks actually emerge.
Networks grow through sequence of local decisions, creating a <b>dynamic system</b> where path-dependence can arise.

In the last section, we have seen that several networks are stable, but we assumed the cost per link <b>constant</b>.
In reality, maintaining more links becomes more expensive: to account for this we introduce <b>quadratic costs </b> $ck_i^2$.

$$
u_i(G) = \delta_{j \neq i} \delta^{d_{ij}} - c k^2_i
$$

the marginal cost of adding one link is then:
$$
\Delta C_i = c [(k_i + 1)^2 - k_i^2] = c (2k_i +1)  \ge c
$$

_Intuition: first links are cheap, link costs grow with degree._

Under linear costs, we have seen <b>star-like</b> or core-periphery structures. With <b>quadratic costs</b> highest degrees are much smaller and networks look more <b>"egalitarian"</b> (no super hubs).
This happen because, with high marginal costs, they stop adding links earlier.

<a href="https://www.verginer.eu/teaching/spatial_network.html" target="_blank">To simulate the network formation game, please use this very cool tool by Luca Verginer! </a>

We consider now a potential network with $n$ nodes, with $M = \binom{n}{2}$ possible links and $2^M$ possible networks.

In this scenario, <b>pairwise-stable </b> networks are a tiny subset. 
<br/>Our process is a slow, myopic search in a gigantic space: therefore, we chanfe one link at time with the following rule:
- pick a random pair
- add the link if both gain
- delete the link if one gains

In simulation, this long process shows up as:
- <b>long transients</b> with very slow convergence, if at all
- <b>oscillations</b> with the number of links and welfare fluctuate.

With $n$ large, also the number of constraints to reach pairwise-stability grow <b>superlinearly</b> in $n (n^2)$.

_Key takeaway: in large economic networks, the <b>ongoing dynamic</b> is more important the the (unreachable) equilibrium_.

### <a id="11.2"> <b>11.2 Spatial Connections Model</b> </a>

So far, networks lived in an abstract place, but in reality and in many economic networks, agents have a <b>location</b>.
Empirically, we see:
- <b>strong local connectivity</b> (public transports)
- a few <b>long range link</b> (airports)

We now try to extend the connection models to take into account to model:
- easier access to nearby agents
- more expensive long-range links

We then consider than eachn ode $i$ has a position $x_i \in \R$ and $\phi(i,j)$ is the distance between $i,j$, then:

$$
u_i(G) = \sum_{i \neq j} \delta^{d_{ig} + \phi(i,j)} -ck_i
$$

_Intuition: geography acts as an extra hops in the network, increasing the effective distance exponent._
- Closer neighbours have smaller exponent and larger benefits
- distand nodes have small benefits unless we create shortcuts.

In the simple case, with two isolated nodes $i,j$, link forms if:
$$
\delta^{1 + \phi(i,j)} -c \ge 0 \iff \delta^{1 + \phi(i,j)} \ge c \\

\iff \phi(i,j) \le \frac{\log(c)}{\log(\delta)} -1 = r^*
$$

$r^*$ is also defined as the <b>critical distance </b>.
