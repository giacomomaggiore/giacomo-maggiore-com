### <b>Tendinitis and giant slalom </b>

A few days ago, an annoying pain appeared in my Achilles tendon, probably the result of a slight mechanical overload from running and cycling training.

No big deal, I told myself, I'll just rest in the gym to stretch for a few days. But as I was walking home in a terrible mood, figuring out when I could run again, I remembered the riddle with which <a target ="_blank" href="https://luca-dellanna.com/">Luca Dellanna</a> begins his book <a href="https://luca-dellanna.com/books/ergodicity"><b>"Ergodicity"</b></a>.

<Quote>
My cousin is a very good skier and participates in the giant slalom world championship.

During each run, he has a 20% chance of falling and breaking his leg but, once he reaches the finish line, he has a 20% chance of winning against his opponents. 

How many races does he expect to win?
</Quote>

The most immediate answer: <b>2 </b> (20% win rate over 10 races).

The correct answer: about <b>0.71 </b>.

The reason is easily understood from the table below (credits to the author Luca Dell’Anna), while the mathematical proof is at the end of the article.

This little story serves to introduce the central concept of this article: in theory, <b>performance determines success</b>: the best skier overall wins. In practice, <b>survival is a necessary condition for performance</b>: the best skier _among all those able to compete_ wins.

### <b> I promise these are the only integrals for today </b>

Returning to my injury, the underlying principle is very similar. 
Assuming, for the sake of argument, that an injury is irreversible _(once the damage is done, I can no longer heal and recover)_, my ability to run can be represented as an <b>absorbing Markov chain</b> with two states:

- <b>State 1 (Transient):</b> I am healthy and able to run.
- <b>State 0 (Absorbing):</b> I am injured.

Every day I train, I have a probability $p$ of remaining in state 1 and a probability $(1-p)$ of getting injured and moving to state 0. Once state 0 is reached, I stay there forever.

What has just been described perfectly represents, _exactly like the cousin's story_,  a <b>non-ergodic process</b>. 

Now let's step away from my tendinitis for a moment and try to better define the concept of ergodicity. To do so, however, we must first define two concepts.

- The <b>time average</b> is what you get by observing *a single* process over a long time _(e.g., one person playing the same game repeatedly)._
- The <b>space (or ensemble) average</b> is what you get by observing *many copies* of the process at a single instant _(e.g., 100 people playing the same game at the same time)._

<b> In an ergodic process, these two averages coincide. In a non-ergodic one, they do not. </b>

In formulas, a process is ergodic if: 

$$
\lim_{T \to \infty} \frac{1}{T} \int_0^T f(x(t)) \, dt = \int_{\Omega} f(x) \, d\mu(x)
$$

_Alternative definitions are proposed in the appendix._

### <b> Inside the casino </b>

To clarify the point, let's use the <s>widely overused</s> casino example.

<b>Classic Roulette</b>, where you bet on red or black with an equal probability of winning (ignoring zero for simplicity), is <b> an ergodic game. </b>

A person who plays 100 consecutive times _(time average)_, expects to win about 50 times. Likewise, from 100 people spinning the roulette wheel at the same time _(space average)_, we expect about 50 people to be happy with their winnings. The results match.

<b>Russian Roulette</b>, on the other hand, where you point a revolver with one bullet in six chambers at your head, is not an ergodic game.

If one person plays 100 consecutive times, despite initially having a 5/6 probability of survival, they are killed on the first wrong click _(zero survival rate)._

If 100 people play once, we instead expect about 83 people (5/6 of 100) to survive _(a survival rate of, precisely, 83%)_.

Since <b> 0% $\neq$ 83%</b>, we conclude that Russian roulette is a non-ergodic process.

### <b>Infinity is just for philosophers and mathematicians </b>

Moving away from these two extreme examples, we immediately notice that ergodicity is not a constant value, but rather changes based on the time horizon $T$ over which we evaluate it and, generally, as $T$ increases, the ergodicity of a phenomenon also decreases.

Consequently, since we make decisions over finite and more-or-less near time horizons _(we leave the limit as $T \to \infty$ to mathematicians and philosophers)_, we can place every action on an <b>ergodicity spectrum</b>, based on how reversible or irreversible the risks are over the considered horizon.

If we considered a sufficiently long $T$, in fact, any process would become non-ergodic: just think of the fact that, _adopting an apocalyptic view of life_, every action we take includes a minimal possibility of dying and, consequently, of not being able to repeat an experiment infinitely. 

(One of the few <s>almost</s> ergodic activities that comes to mind is rolling dice in an isolated room without external interventions: excluding sudden death, one could go on rolling the dice for a very long time).

### <b> There is no religion anymore: all churches are insured against fire</b>

The study of ergodicity introduces the concept of irreversibility and, consequently, it feels natural to broaden today's discussion to a closely related concept: <b>uncertainty management. </b>

To do this, let's distinguish risks based on two properties: reversibility and insurability.

- A risk is<b> reversible</b> if, in a finite and reasonable time, the loss can be remedied. A risk is <b>irreversible </b>if the consequences are drastic enough to make the loss relevant in the long term or to make recovery impractical.
- A risk is <b>manageable </b>if it is possible to buy insurance that reimburses us in case of loss,<b> unmanageable</b> otherwise.

Here too, since these are not always binary properties, it would be more correct to construct a Cartesian plane where the two axes represent these two properties as **continuous variables**:

- <b>$X$-axis - Manageability </b>:  minus infinity represents the <b>totally unmanageable</b> risk _(a life regret, a nuclear war)_, and the opposite extreme the <b>totally</b> insurable risk _(like minor damage 100% covered by a policy)._
- <b>$Y$-axis - Reversibility </b>: minus infinity represents the <b>totally irreversible</b> risk _(death, in extremis)_, the upper extreme a <b>totally reversible</b> risk _(missing the metro on the way home)._ 

Consequently, instead of being placed in one of the four quadrants listed above, every phenomenon in our life becomes a point $(x, y)$ on the risk plane. 

Furthermore, according to what was said earlier, <b>non-ergodic processes are the domain of irreversible risks </b> (Quadrants 3 and 4) or, equally, all points with $y < 0$.

### <b> Stretch and keep cash liquid</b>

After briefly classifying uncertainty, the question spontaneously arises as to how to <b>manage, embrace</b>, and <b>avoid</b> the different types of risk.

To answer this, let's return to the tendon.

Optimal athletic preparation should not maximize the benefits of <b>every single workout</b>, but rather <b>minimize the irreversible and unmanageable risk</b> _(Quadrant 3)_ of tendinitis _(feel free to substitute tendinitis with any other sports injury)_ or, in other words, <b>maximize the probability</b> of still being able to train in the long run.

This behavior, also called the <b>redundancy principle</b>, leads us to act in an apparently <b>sub-optimal</b> way in the short term, accepting inferior results, in order to maximize future survival. 

In sports, this means dedicating time to stretching _(apparently "useless" and terribly boring)_, taking an extra rest day, and not pushing every session to the limit.

In personal finance, this means maintaining a liquid emergency fund _(with zero return)_, accepting a lower average portfolio return, but protecting oneself from the risk of ruin (insolvency).

The goal of the redundancy principle is therefore, after classifying every phenomenon as a point $(x,y)$ on the plane, to <b>neutralize the risks we are not willing to take</b>, regardless of how <b>infinitesimal the probability</b> associated with it is.

To delve deeper into the next topic, however, we have to bring in surgeons and extreme sports.

### <b> Bungee Jumping, Surgeons, and Skin in The Game</b>

Bungee jumping has historically caused $1$ death for every $500,000$ jumps. A very low rate. The problem is that for that poor desperate person whose rope breaks, statistics don't matter: <b>100% of the bad luck falls on them irreversibly.</b>

The same goes for surgery. We believe in modern medicine because, _on average_, it saves lives. But when the surgeon messes up an operation, the patient doesn't console themselves by thinking about how many healed smiles leave the hospital every day.

These two examples, deliberately extreme, serve to clarify an important lesson: in the presence of non-ergodic phenomena (or, in other words, in the face of irreversible risks), <b>the very concept of classical probability and arithmetic mean lose their meaning</b> for the individual.

When an individual faces an <b>irreversible and unmanageable</b> loss due to an event $X$, their individual utility (happiness) is irremediably compromised. This is true regardless of the fact that, _on average_, event $X$ increases the utility (happiness) of the population. 
Since it is a loss with no chance of recovery and no insurance, <b>the average benefit enjoyed by others becomes irrelevant to them.</b>

Generalizing, one could also conclude that <b>the survival of a system rarely coincides with the survival of its individuals</b> and that, often, the system has no incentive for the survival of its members. On the contrary, <b>the replaceability of individuals makes the system ergodic, antifragile, or capable of evolving</b> _(depending on the author you read: Dell’Anna, Taleb, or Darwin)._

The situation changes radically if the loss is <b>manageable</b>. In that case, risk-sharing or transfer mechanisms _(p2p, insurance policies)_ can protect the individual, allowing them to still benefit from a potential increase in their utility.

The surgeon's example, moreover, highlights the problem of <b>"skin in the game"</b> _(as Taleb defines it)_. In many situations, the entity making the decision _(example: a politician)_ <b>does not coincide with the entity upon which the irreversible negative consequences fall</b> _(example: the citizens of a country)_. For the hospital system, a death caused by avoidable errors is one number among millions of patients _(ergodic situation)_. For the unfortunate patient, on the contrary, it is a 100% ruin event _(non-ergodic phenomenon)_. 

The solution to this misalignment problem suggests acting in contexts where those who make decisions _(friends, politicians, companies, etc. etc.)_ <b>coincide with those who pay the consequences of bad choices</b> but, due to obvious real-world dynamics, such behavior is almost never possible.

### <b> Always look up when walking down the street</b>

This entire discussion on risk, if taken to the extreme, would also lead one to never leave the house for fear of a ceramic pot falling on their head.
The truth is that our brain constantly performs a cost-benefit analysis, evaluating, more or less rationally, the probabilities, benefits, and utility of every situation we expose ourselves to. 

As a general and purely theoretical rule, our risk aversion should be:

- <b>Infinite</b> for risks in Quadrant 3 _(irreversible and uninsurable)_.
- <b>High</b> for Quadrant 4 _(irreversible but insurable)_, with a concave utility function
- <b>Low</b> for Quadrant 1 and 2 _(reversible risks)_, with linear utility functions _(if we are purely interested in the expected value)_ or convex _(if there is an asymmetry in our favor, see <a target="_blank" href="https://www.investopedia.com/articles/investing/013114/barbell-investment-strategy.asp">Taleb's barbell strategy for more on this</a>)_.

### <b> Find the right friends</b>

The last risks I would like to address are the most subjective, difficult to insure, and often irreversible: <b>those related to human capital and relationships.</b>

Regarding the former, the greatest risk is not a realized loss, but a <b>potential loss</b>, namely the failure to exploit growth opportunities.

The most trivial example comes from my university experience.

In a grading system from 0 to 30 _(with 18 being a pass, I know: the italian grading system is strange...)_, it is much more useful to get two 23s on two exams than a 30 and a 17, even though the arithmetic average is lower in the first scenario.

- Case $A$: $23 + 23$  $\to$ Average $23$ _(the university path continues)_
- Case $B$: $30 + 17$ $\to$ Average $23.5$ _(the failure introduces a high cost of failure: extra time, effort, and stress)_

The discussion becomes more complex with <b>relationships</b>, as emotions and interpersonal dynamics come into play. I will just say that, of all risks, ending an important relationship is perhaps <b>the most impactful, irreversible, and least insurable event</b> of our lives.

In this case, however, not feeling up to the task of an in-depth discussion as before, I will limit myself to proposing, _rather hastily_, two possible solutions:

- apply the same logic as before: <b>prefer "ruin minimization"</b> (avoiding irreparable breakups, lies, betrayals...) to "average maximization" (being right most of the time, getting along at all costs...).
- <b>have friends who are not easily offended</b>

Mine, _fortunately_, not only fall into this category, but _(more importantly)_ they put up with me <s>even</s> when I'm in a bad mood because of my tendon pain. <b>Thanks! </b>

### <b> Appendix</b>
#### <b>A.1 - Why does my cousin expect to win only 0.71 races? </b>

Let's define the variables for a single race $i$:

- $Y_i$: My cousin finishes race $i$ without falling
- $X_i$: My cousin wins race $i$
- $S_i$: My cousin has survived (not broken his leg) until the start of race $i$

From the text, we get:

$$
P(Y_i = 1) = 1 - 0.2 = 0.8
$$

$$
P(X_i=1 \mid Y_i=1) = 0.2
$$

since the probability of winning is conditional on having reached the finish line (event $Y_i=1$).


Therefore, the absolute probability of winning race $i$, assuming he started (event $S_i$), is:
$$
P(\text{Win Race } i \mid S_i) = P(X_i=1 \mid Y_i=1) \cdot P(Y_i=1)
$$

$$
P(\text{Win Race } i \mid S_i) = 0.2 \cdot 0.8 = \mathbf{0.16}
$$

Every time he is at the starting gate, my cousin therefore has a 16% chance of winning and an 80% chance of being able to participate in the next race.

Furthermore, the probability of getting to participate in race $i$ (event $S_i$) depends on not having fallen in any of the $i-1$ previous races. Consequently:

$$
P(S_i) = (0.8)^{i-1}
$$

We can therefore write that the absolute probability of winning race $i$ _(seen from the start of the championship)_ is the probability of surviving until that race ($P(S_i)$) multiplied by the probability of winning it once there (0.16).

$$
P(X_i = 1) = P(\text{Win Race } i \mid S_i) \cdot P(S_i)
$$

$$
P(X_i = 1) = 0.16 \cdot (0.8)^{i-1}
$$

The total number of expected wins, $\mathbb{E}[V]$, is the sum of the probabilities of winning each single race, from $i=1$ to $i=10$.

$$
\mathbb{E}[V] = \sum_{i=1}^{10} P(X_i = 1) = \sum_{i=1}^{10} 0.16 \cdot (0.8)^{i-1}
$$

which is nothing more than a geometric series with first term $a = 0.16$ and ratio $r = 0.8$.

$$
\mathbb{E}[V] = a \cdot \left( \frac{1 - r^n}{1 - r} \right) = 0.16 \cdot \left( \frac{1 - 0.8^{10}}{1 - 0.8} \right)
$$

$$
\mathbb{E}[V] = 0.16 \cdot \left( \frac{1 - 0.8^{10}}{0.2} \right)
$$

$$
\mathbb{E}[V] = 0.8 \cdot (1 - 0.8^{10})
$$

$$
\mathbb{E}[V] \approx 0.8 \cdot (1 - 0.1074) \approx 0.8 \cdot 0.8926
$$

$$\mathbb{E}[V] \approx \mathbf{0.714}
$$

#### <b>A.2 - Alternative Definitions of Ergodicity? </b>

<Quote>
An observable event is called ergodic if its expectation value is constant in time and its time average converges to this value with probability one. <small> _[Ole Peters, Alexander Adamou]_ </small>
</Quote>

<Quote>
Ergodicity holds if, more or less, every time you compute a statistical measure (in space or in time), you find the same result. 

It means that the randomness is "well-distributed".

Another way to see it is that, when there is ergodicity, doing $N$ random experiments in parallel gives the same result as doing $N$ experiments in series.
</Quote>
