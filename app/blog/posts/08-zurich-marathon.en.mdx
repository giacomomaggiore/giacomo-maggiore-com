Sunday, April 12, two years after my last race, <a href="https://www.strava.com/activities/18076036329" target="_blank"> I ran my second marathon.</a><br/>I would like to say that standing on the starting line was the conclusion of a long journey of preparation and sacrifices that lasted months; the truth is that, _just over a month ago,_ encouraged by my uni's friends and hyped by the idea of running my <b>"home" marathon</b>, I decided to sign up at the last minute.

Consequently, _despite already running about seventy km a week,_ I didn't have time to follow a "canonical" preparation: in the last 2 years, I have only run 14 high-intensity workouts and, in the last month of preparation, <a href="https://www.strava.com/activities/17897509343" target="_blank">two long runs at marathon pace.</a>

Nonetheless, beyond being very satisfied with these <b>42.195 km</b>, I think these moments were a period of great growth: during this time, in addition to university, work, and social life <s>(split between Milan and Zurich)</s>, an further source of stress was added that pushed me to make several reflections on the matter, listed below in no particular order.

### <b>Dependent and Independent Variables</b>

Precisely estimating the final time of a marathon is very complex: besides not knowing all the factors that influence performance on race day, we cannot even quantify the impact of many of them. In short, <b>defining a function that links input variables to the finish time is impossible.</b>

What is more reasonable to do, _at least in my experience_, is to focus on the variables under our control that have a <b>greater marginal impact</b> on the result. I cannot decide the temperature, the wind, or the humidity on the starting line; I cannot decide how many people will run at my pace, the unforeseen events along the route, or how much cheering I will receive along the 42 km. Similarly, I don't think it makes sense, _at least at amateur levels where all optimization problems must account for the constraints imposed by daily life,_ to focus on the grams of salt to take the night before, the weight of the racing gear, or other low-impact factors.

On the contrary, the three levers I feel I have full control over, especially in the pre-race week, are, in order of importance: <b>nutrition</b>, <b>training volume management</b>, and <b>sleep.</b>

### <b>Eat Pasta Run Fasta</b>

When I <a href="https://www.strava.com/activities/11125450756" target="_blank"> ran the Milan marathon</a> two years ago, although I knew the guidelines on pre- and intra-workout carbohydrate intake, living with my family meant I didn't have full control over it. For this reason, _besides eating a bit more pasta al pesto the night before,_ I didn't optimize this aspect well.

Since living alone, I've realized how much what I eat the day before drastically impacts performance. Consequently, <b>measuring and controlling the grams of carbohydrates</b> in the days before and during prolonged endurance activities immediately became a habit without great extra costs or effort.

In the days preceding the Zurich marathon, I didn't change my total caloric intake much; rather, I reduced fats to a minimum in favor of carbohydrates _(without reducing fiber)_ and maintained a diet <b>based on a few simple ingredients</b>: vegetables, complex carbohydrates, very little added sugar, no fried or processed foods, and very little fruit.<br/>The night before the marathon, _after testing the same meal twice during training "long runs",_ I had dinner with 200g of rice, 400g of vegetables, 140g of oats, and 400g of white yogurt: <b>approximately 290 grams of net carbohydrates 12 hours before the race.</b>

The next morning, following an already established routine, I had breakfast with 3 cereal bars of 25g, 5 tablespoons of sugar, and a cup of instant barley _(the only liquids of the morning)_, avoiding weighing down my stomach or overdoing caffeine and liquids. Finally, during the marathon, I took <a href="https://guenergy.com/collections/gels?srsltid=AfmBOoqCEVbUz0u-48DtKo_Sq8aw5VZWo70trkMzcGjsZHrY4GLzaOz4" target="_blank"> 8 gels of 25g of carbs each</a>, for a total of <b>about 70g of carbohydrates per hour spread over 2h 50'.</b>

I didn't bend over backward to optimize micronutrients or meal timing to the milligram; I preferred to follow a simple plan: <b>very few sources, tracked carbohydrates, and consolidated routines.</b> Even though my nutrition wasn't perfectly aligned with guidelines _(I think I can push higher with pre-race carbs),_ I had full control of the situation, aware of how my body would react.

### <b>Sleep is as beautiful as it is overrated</b>

Secondly, regarding volume management in the preceding weeks _(the famous tapering!)_, guidelines suggest cutting the total mileage by about 25% _(in the penultimate week)_ and then by 40-50% _(in the last)_, while maintaining a few intensity sessions to _"keep the legs turning."_ In my peak week, I ran 95 km; in the first taper week 64 km and, excluding the marathon itself, only 46 km in the seven days preceding the race; noticing, as the marathon approached, <b>a very pleasant sense of lightness in my legs.</b>

I put sleep last because, compared to the other two levers, I believe it has a much more limited potential for positive impact. Getting enough sleep in the two or three preceding days is a necessary condition to feel rested, yet, from my experience, I am convinced that increasing the duration of rest beyond a certain threshold _(in my case 7-8 hours a night)_ <b>does not correlate positively with performance.</b>

Regardless of individual data or what the guidelines suggest, I think the most important thing is to adopt an intentional and especially analytical approach, whose precision increases exponentially as it converges toward race day and the <b>margin of error tends asymptotically to zero</b> as you approach the starting line.

### <b>An Imperfect Science</b>

From the previous paragraph, it clearly emerges how the marathon is a <b>very imperfect science</b>: many data points are at play, _both quantitative and qualitative,_ and their interpretation is almost never objective. I believe, in fact, that much more analytical skill is required than one might think, both to give meaning to the numbers we read on Strava or the Garmin app, and to learn to listen to one's body.

As mentioned before, estimating, and therefore deciding in advance, your race pace is the result of a <b>cross-analysis</b> of previous workouts, fatigue levels, temperature, elevation, course layout, and many other factors we often don't control. Although it might seem easy, a tiny upward error is enough to ruin a race: starting even just a few seconds per kilometer too fast risks making the impact with "the wall" much more violent. 

The most you can do on race day is <b>pure execution:</b> leave as little as possible to chance and constantly monitor your status, checking your pace at short and regular intervals _(always use the manual lap, never rely on the watch's GPS!)_, keeping an eye on your heart rate and staying attuned to your feelings. Even in this case, it’s not rocket science: these are all habits accessible at zero cost even to us amateurs, without requiring any extra layer of complexity.

### <b>The only allowed form of doping</b>

The marathon, however, is not just about numbers, data, and carbohydrates; all of that represents only the foundation for a <b>much larger celebration</b>. I like to divide the 42 km into a first part _(up to about the 30th-33rd km)_ of apparent calm and control, during which you run just below your threshold, enjoying the route, the music, and the cheering, trying to maintain as composed a form as possible without getting mentally drained.

The goal of this first phase is to reach the 10 km mark ready for what everyone calls the "real" race. Arriving there still feeling energetic and ready to suffer in this final phase is <b>the greatest privilege a runner can have</b>: it is the proof that everything, _up to that point,_ went smoothly, and the moment when, _in a brutally simple way,_ <b>everything starts to suck just a little bit more.</b>

I believe that one of the most beautiful sensations to experience in a marathon is the coexistence, _growing in intensity,_ of contrasting feelings in the final kilometers: on one hand, feeling the body still capable of pushing but <b>aching in every single part</b>; on the other, visualizing the finish line in advance, _often too far in advance._ In my experience, this is also the moment when cheering becomes a <b>source of exogenous doping</b>: running through live music, hearing your name shouted by total strangers, reading witty signs, and high-fiving children, _besides causing a constant thrill of emotion,_ translates into a literal push for your legs.

### <b>Wrong incentives with positive results</b>

Beyond the unique emotion felt by those tackling every kilometer of the race, major European and world marathons have become <b>mainstream cultural events</b> that often involve the entire citizenship, public entities, and local businesses.

Just as happens in most high-attendance sporting events, the Zurich marathon brought together on a Sunday morning _(despite Switzerland usually being a ghost nation on Sundays, with everything closed and no one around)_ countless families and friends cheering, many volunteers making it all possible, numerous food trucks along the route and, _no less importantly,_ many companies, mostly local, sponsoring the event in search of visibility.

Obviously, behind all this mobilization often lie <b>monetary incentives born of capitalism</b>: companies primarily have an economic interest in being present; people, on the other hand, are quite happy to receive a Garmin-branded cookie, a Lindt chocolate, or mints from the cantonal bank as a gift, only to then spend 25 francs on a cheeseburger and leave their email and personal data in exchange for promotional offers.<br/>While I would like to declare myself against this exasperation of capitalism and <b>preach a return to a more essential version</b> of this sport, _where the only focus is on the pure athletic gesture,_ I won't deny that I don't mind this way of practicing sport at all, where running mixes with much more and becomes the occasion for a great party.

### <b>We are becoming slower</b>

Starting from the premise that running is good for people, I am overall happy that this sport has become <b>increasingly accessible and practiced by the masses</b>, even if often at the expense of average athletic performance. Data from the New York Marathon, _probably the most historical and famous to the general public,_ is the perfect example of this trend.

_From 1970 to 2024,_ the number of participants has increased drastically, going from fewer than 100 finishers in the first edition to <b>55,495 runners</b> who crossed the finish line in 2024. The average performance, on the contrary, <b>has worsened:</b> the average finish time rose from 3h 26' in 1971 to 4h 48' for women and 4h 18' for men in 2024, demonstrating a great <b>democratization</b> of the event, making it accessible no longer just to a sporting elite, but to the mass population. 

_(One should, however, also consider the improvement in performance due to technological evolution and training methods, the analysis of which is postponed to an indefinite future)._

### <b>Marathon runners like to spend</b>

Even from an economic point of view, the world of running has undergone massive growth. Sticking with the New York Marathon, in 2024 the event generated an economic impact of <b>692 million dollars</b> for the city, marking a 139% increase compared to the 2019 edition. If we analyze the entire year, activities organized by the <a href="https://www.nyrr.org/" target="_blank">NYRR</a> brought nearly 1 billion dollars into the local economy: a figure comparable to the combined impact of all home games of the New York Yankees and the New York Mets.

This phenomenon is not limited to the "Big Apple": for cities hosting major marathons, these sporting events are true money-making machines capable of attracting people, capital, and consumption, proving that their reach extends, very easily, <b>well beyond the pure sporting sphere.</b>



That said, I conclude with two very low-level thoughts:

- I wish for you to <b>dream, prepare</b>, and <b>run</b> a marathon
- I admit I missed the marathon prep: this spring I held back a bit... <b>in autumn we do it again, even better!</b>
