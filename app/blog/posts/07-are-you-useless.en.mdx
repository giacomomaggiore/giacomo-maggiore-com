### <b>Polimi, ETH & Statale</b>

I was lucky enough to grow up with the awareness that my passions coincided, _at least for the most part_, with skills that the job market rewarded and that, more broadly, <b>society considered useful.</b>

Since I was a child, I always showed an interest in mathematics, physics, and technology, discovering the world of coding on my own and ending up, _inevitably_, studying <a href="https://giacomomaggiore.com/blog/01-polimi-recap" target="_blank"> Engineering at the Politecnico di Milano. </a>
<br/>Later, becoming passionate about finance and economics in my spare time, <b>I moved to the dark side</b> by starting a master's in <a href="https://giacomomaggiore.com/blog/02-one-month-eth" target="_blank"> Management and Economics at ETH.</a>

Only now, as I get closer to leaving the happy status of a student and after numerous discussions with different perspectives and hopes, do many doubts arise. <br/>More specifically, my reflections come from long debates held with two wise women who, _fortunately_, have a great influence on my life _(my mother and my girlfriend, Giulia)_ and who, ironically, also share,  with some time delay, the same course of study: <b>both studied philosophy.</b>

The central theme of our conversations, and consequently of these lines, can be summarized as: <b>“How and in what way should society tell you how useless you are?”</b>

### <b>Separated at Birth </b>

During my _(almost)_ 20 years of study, there has always been a constant: I knew that what I was studying coincided with something “useful” and, consequently, following society’s most cliché and overused advice, <b>I “followed my passions.”</b>

Now imagine a twin brother of mine who, starting from the same situation and equal boundary conditions _(same intelligence, stupidity, and access to opportunities)_, shows interest in different aspects of knowledge _(read: philosophy, epistemology, ethics)_ and, following the same advice I did, ends up studying humanities.

It can reasonably be expected that, regardless of the graduation grade or the effort put into studying during the academic path, one of the two will have an easier entry into the world of work, a better-paid position, and a <b>higher level of professional satisfaction.</b>

I am also convinced that, since there is much more demand for STEM figures on the market and consequently a higher probability for them to be hired in a similar sector, holding a job position more or less aligned with what they studied (which consequently <b>“values”</b> a student’s academic path) leads to greater levels of satisfaction.

On the contrary, following a humanities path with great passion but ending up, due to low demand, doing something _NOT_ related to one's studies, can be much <b>more frustrating and demotivating. </b> _(And by this, I don't just mean the classic stereotype of the philosopher preparing fries at <small>* insert American fast-food name *</small>, but all those graduates who, not knowing what career to pursue, end up taking the first job they find, partly as a fallback, partly out of the necessity to make a living)._

### <b>Almost in the Relegation Zone</b>

Strongly influenced by my background, at this moment in my life, I feel I can answer the same question with a very convinced: <b>“maybe.”</b>

Mine is the experience of a twenty-two-year-old who, _excluding a brief stint of wanting to become an astronaut_, has always had interests in the "humanities" field and followed a "humanities" school path, culminating in the choice of Philosophy as a university faculty.

The _“do what you love and things will follow”_ or _“follow your passions”_ are therefore narratives that I have been and still am quite familiar with, and which have seen me direct a wide spectrum of feelings toward them: from <b>trust</b> that it was true; to <b>disillusionment</b> that it was an excessive romanticism that, however, deviates from reality; to “maybe.”

Now, in this very delicate phase where “do what you like” must inevitably take on an increasingly concrete form, I find myself thinking that, _however appropriately warned by the stereotypes about studying Philosophy_, this narrative should have been accompanied by a <b>good dose of pragmatism.</b>

Due to the <b>strong skill mismatch between supply and demand</b>, a large portion of non-STEM graduates ends up, out of necessity, occupying generic positions not designed for their course of study, where the latter is not valued in the slightest. 

On the contrary, <b>only the brightest</b> _(and luckiest!)_ <b>percentile</b> of humanities graduates ends up having an intentional career path that is especially connected to their background, without having to adapt and accept compromises.

### <b>Philosophy has this use: it serves to console us for its uselessness.</b>

The evolution of the simple question _“and after graduation?”_ has become: _“in today's world, do spaces exist for figures with an education not oriented toward a practice?”_
<br/>In a way of conceiving education as increasingly necessary and <b>hyper-specialized</b>, is there a need for figures who, in fact, <b>don't know how to do anything useful?</b> <br/>How much room should be left for reinterpretation? And how much does this reinventing oneself lead to compromises where one's profile and experience are not valued?


Starting from these realities and the central question posed by Giulia, I have broken down the problem into two main points, much more related than they might seem:
- In what way and at what stage of growth should society _(and who, within society)_ give us realistic perspectives on what awaits us after our studies?
- Is following your passions always a good idea?

### <b>Bad Advice</b>

Reflecting in hindsight on my academic path, only a few times(_probably too late and never in a clear way)_, was I given a realistic picture of the skills that the job market rewards _(or is expected to reward)_. 

The little awareness I have gained over the years comes, almost exclusively, from the wise advice _(and the right impositions)_ given by my parents, who always <a href="https://www.amazon.it/Nudge-spinta-gentile-Thaler-Sunstein/dp/8807898616/ref=asc_df_8807898616?mcid=77982c7f599736cea902664856b521d4&tag=googshopit-21&linkCode=df0&hvadid=700815031569&hvpos=&hvnetw=g&hvrand=17444712298387689593&hvpone=&hvptwo=&hvqmt=&hvdev=c&hvdvcmdl=&hvlocint=&hvlocphy=1003297&hvtargid=pla-2274743115029&psc=1&hvocijid=17444712298387689593-8807898616-&hvexpln=0" target="_blank"> “gently pushed”</a> me in a more or less successful direction.

The primary cause of all this is that the teachers we grow up with _(who are also the people best suited to shape our mindset)_ are, by necessity, <b>incapable and therefore ineffective</b> (almost never through their own fault) at doing all this.

Firstly, the (Italian) school system does not provide a path aimed at providing awareness of the post-academic world and, _only more strictly_, professional integration.

Secondly, and probably much more importantly, teachers, _while being much more cultured and prepared in their field than I could ever hope to be in my life,_ have likely spent most of their careers within the walls of a school, remaining <b>not very permeable</b> to how the job market has evolved, especially in recent years.

Although all the possible answers to my questions are not binary but rather fall on a spectrum between <b>romanticism and brutal honesty</b>, I remain convinced that, at least in Italy, we remain too idealistic about it.

I still think it's part of everyone's growth process to discover how to be useful to society _(or, more concretely, in which sector to professionalize and look for work)_; however, most students (except for the lucky ones who receive input in other contexts) <b>lack the tools and sufficient initiative</b> to do so. This is why schools, especially through figures outside the purely academic world, should provide better guidance through these uncertainties.

My answer to the second question is strongly influenced by a book I read a few years ago but whose thesis, fortunately, often resonates within me: <a href="https://www.amazon.com/Good-They-Cant-Ignore-You/dp/1455509124" target="_blank"><b>So Good They Can’t Ignore You</b> by Cal Newport.</a>

The book's main thesis is that following your passions, _besides being misleading_, leads nowhere. On the contrary, competence and a sense of impact are what <b>positively correlate with a satisfying future</b> (and career). The advice _“follow your passions"_ thus translates to _“find what you are good at, specialize, and look for a job in that vertical.”_ 

In fact, I am convinced that being recognized and valued, from both a human and professional perspective, for one's skill, competence, or knowledge (even if it doesn't correspond to our first and greatest passion) is much more satisfying than seeing one's effort poorly appreciated by society, _regardless of how passionate one might be about it._

### <b>Italy is a country of the old</b>

Trying instead to look for a historical reason why the idea of _“do what you love”_ is so widespread (and starting from the premise that this statement is addressed to a segment of people who have the privilege of being able to try), it strikes me as the <b>child of a generation</b> (_that of our parents_) which witnessed the proliferation of a multitude of professional roles and fields of study that didn't even exist a few years prior.

The worldview of a generation that had been taught to follow fairly linear tracks, and which, in a very short time, saw things take shape that they couldn't even imagine existing, was: _“do what you love and somehow you'll be able to market it."_ 

However, the evolution of this idea has completely removed the active component and it has become a rather short-sighted and limited reading of the world, split between _“do what you love and wait”_ and _“sacrifice yourself and do something you don't like but that will be useful.”_



### <b>You have to like the right things</b>

Is it possible to reach a synthesis?

The “maybe” arises from observing _(certainly not an epochal discovery)_ that doing what you love is good advice if you like the right things: the ones the job market demands from you. Otherwise, you face the inevitable reckoning: having done something you like and not feeling valued in the very same world that told you to do what you love.

If it is not universally applicable, and starting from the assumption that not everyone likes the same things and that individuals themselves experience an evolution in what they enjoy, <b>is this a narrative that still makes sense to carry forward?</b>

I like to think the answer isn't a simple “no” _(which would presumably be resolved by merely changing direction from “do what you love” to “do what is useful”)_ but rather working toward a world that rediscovers the activity of choice and is ready to welcome those who find a way to adapt their skills and their education.

Society <b>should not tell you that you are useless</b>, because that would be <b>as limiting as</b> telling you that somehow you will be useful; instead, it should give you the tools to ask yourself _(and possibly answer the question)_: if my experience has led me to be shaped this way, <b>where do I fit in?</b>

This drive for action is omitted in most paths that churn out <b>perfect profiles designed to fill spaces</b>, but we have learned that, _in most cases_, this penalizes in the long run all those who witness the insertion of a new variable into their framework _(cf. the advent of AI)._

### <b>The Master's degree is useless</b>

Graduates in literary-humanistic disciplines in Italy see an employment rate of $\approx$ 62%, with no difference between Bachelor's and Master's graduates _(61.9% Master's and 62.6% Bachelor's, source: AlmaLaurea 2025)_ one year after graduation; not exactly a rosy outlook.

If it is true that working is the way to <b>confirm our place in the world</b> _(something widely studied by those of us with a “humanistic” background!)_, along with the effort involved _(which should signify the will to be there)_, it is inevitable to conclude that digging through the "least worst" of compromises (also in light of the skills mismatch) has <b>repercussions on the way we conceive ourselves in the world</b>, on how we experience personal and professional satisfaction, and on staying satisfied and proud of the goals achieved and the education received.

In conclusion, I believe that the thing that needs to be stimulated most of all is the <b>active search for where my profile can fit</b> and, _in the attempt to find an answer_, <b>integrating and refining where necessary.</b> The presence (and don't get me wrong... I recognize its importance) of figures who are increasingly trained in increasingly specific sectors penalizes those who have not followed a path with a very clear practical implication. Perhaps still a bit too much on the wave of illusion _(and maintaining a rather optimistic attitude)_, I believe that at the same time, this can be an advantage for those who, being more fluid, <b>find more spaces in which to adapt.</b>

The fact remains that prioritizing something you are good at over something you like always works for those who like the right things (also because we usually become good at the things we like). What to do with this baggage of almost useless skills (in the strict sense of the term) is to try to read where there is truly a need for someone and try to become that person by building on what exists, but not remaining anchored solely to what we like.

The initial _“maybe”_ therefore derives from the awareness that this is an optimistic vision of the world _(maybe in a year, flipping fries, I'll change my mind!)_, but one that has <b>inevitably reckoned with the fact that what we like is not enough.</b>
