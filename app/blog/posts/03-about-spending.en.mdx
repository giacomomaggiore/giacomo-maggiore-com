_Necessary premise: in this article I might appear more extreme and hyper-rational than I actually am. I’m still a human being, <s>unfortunately</s> I too give in to material temptations and often find myself spending with pleasure. <b>Enjoy!</b>_


### <b> Spending money is an Art </b>

This is how Morgan Housel writes in the opening lines of his latest book 
<a href = "https://www.amazon.com/Art-Spending-Money-Simple-Choices/dp/0593716620" target = "_blank"> <i>The Art of Spending Money</i> </a>
 but, as much as I truly appreciate the author’s books and <a href ="https://collabfund.com/blog/" target ="_blank">blog</a>, 
I can’t help but disagree with this statement.

Spending is above all <b>a habit</b>, one of the most deep-rooted and personal habits we have, which started when we were kids, when at age $X$ we asked for the first time $Y$ euros from relative $Z$ to buy product $W$.

(In my case: $X = 8$, $Y = 1.5$, $Z = “mom”$, $W = “ice cream”$).

Like all daily behaviors, the way we spend says a lot about us and, although several variables are beyond our control, many people try to set rules, limits, goals and, more generally, build a narrative around money.

And since I’m convinced that personal finance contributes to a person’s mental health (<a href = "https://youtu.be/HeNCLsFNVmc?si=qE5z3nYKQlfDixms&t=3440" target = "_blank"><b>cit Mr. Rip</b>, live from 2025-05-20</a>), I think it’s worth exploring how such behaviors arise and how much rationality lies behind them, even though—especially in Italy—there’s still a lot of fear, hypocrisy, and discomfort in discussing and opening up about these topics.

Since I can’t and don’t want to conduct a comparative analysis of the Italian/world/Swiss population, I’ll simply present, break down, and analyze my own beliefs and habits related to money.

### <b> Limited functions and partial derivatives </b>

In recent years, as I’ve become increasingly independent and started earning 
_(not enough to support myself: thanks Parent 1 and Parent 2 for the rent in Zurich)_, 
my relationship with money has changed a lot, influenced by people, books, and family stories.

I’ve always defined myself as <b>frugal</b> and <b>minimalist</b> (one could write
an entire article about the difference between these two adjectives but, for 
the purposes of this reflection, they’ll be treated as synonyms): I like 
<b>simple and linear</b> things, I have few clothes, 
keep an essential furnishing style, eat a diet based on few ingredients, and practice
mostly “free” or very low-cost hobbies. <br />
I have no online subscriptions, I detest luxury, and I have no expensive vices.

All perfect? <s>Almost</s>.

Frugality is one of the values I consider most important, but like everything else, 
when taken to the extreme it stops being that and becomes <b>just a perversion</b>. 

For years, I actually viewed the act of spending as something negative, 
regardless of 
what was being bought and, although it’s a spectrum
(spending for primary needs is less “negative” than wasting money on useless impulsive purchases), 
I’ve never liked spending money.

In mathematical terms, for me the goal has always been to minimize the 
spending function $f$:

$$
f(w, \alpha_1 x_1 \dots \alpha_n x_n), 
$$

where $w$ represents our wealth, $x_1... x_n$ our needs and desires, the coefficients $\alpha_1 ... \alpha_n$ the weights (importances) we assign to each $x_i$, and $f$ quantifies our outgoing cash flow.

Secondly, my second goal (especially for the future when, optimistically,
$w$ will grow) has always been to nullify 
the partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $w$:

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial w} = 0
$$

that is, to prevent my spending habits from depending on my wealth and, 
in particular, to avoid having my expenses increase as my wealth increases.

As for the first objective, it’s a tragicomic daily struggle:
$f$ is in fact bounded below by the necessary daily expenses ($f \geq K \ge 0$) and undergoes 
local oscillations on occasions such as events, unforeseen circumstances, and occasional pleasure purchases.

Regarding the second constraint, I think I’ve reached a good level of awareness
and maturity such that an extra $x$ euros in my wallet don’t significantly change my expenses,
as I can now easily identify which expenses bring me value (or not).

Although I’ve always been comfortable with this way of interpreting frugality,
I’ve always done it because I found pleasure in <b>NOT</b> spending and saw value in that behavior,
not because there was an ultimate goal or something I was saving for.

### <b> In the Balkans with Nick Magiulli </b>

In recent months, however (fortunately), some behaviors have begun to 
change, especially after moving 
<a href="https://giacomomaggiore.com/blog/02-one-month-eth" target = "_blank">to live alone in Switzerland</a> and after consuming,
over the past few years, dozens of contents between books, articles, and videos (the full list
can be found at the end of the article).

Last summer during <s>one of the most beautiful trips of my life</s>
— a road trip through Greece, Albania, Montenegro, and Bosnia — I read <i>
<a href="https://www.amazon.com/Wealth-Ladder-Proven-Strategies-Financial/dp/0593854039" target = "_blank">The Wealth Ladder</a></i>, 
the latest book by <a href = "https://ofdollarsanddata.com/" target = " _blank">Nick Magiulli</a> and, even though that vacation wasn’t exactly the ideal setting
to reflect and discuss money, wealth, and investments, I appreciated the content enough 
to include some lessons in this article.

The main takeaway from this table is that, regardless of the situation
one finds themselves in, any spending habit should be adapted and evaluated based on one’s wealth,
to the point that Nick Magiulli suggests:

<Quote>
You can spend up to $0.01\%$ of your net worth each day 
without compromising your wealth growth.
</Quote>

Although such advice is <b>too general and simplistic</b>, 
the core concept is clear:
below a very small threshold relative to your wealth, it’s not worth
stressing too much about whether or not to make a purchase.

I actually believe that whenever money represents a good opportunity to 
eliminate stress or unnecessary effort from our days (by _"unnecessary”_
I mean something that brings no benefit to anyone, not even 
ourselves), it makes sense to spend it without overthinking.

As you can easily guess, this advice contrasts with my initial thesis,
since it no longer resolves an absolute minimization problem but imposes a different constraint:

$$
\sum _{j \in same \space day}f_j(w, \alpha_1 x_1 ...   \alpha_n x_n,) = 0.01 \% w
$$

Personally, I’m still in an intermediate phase: I’m lucky to 
have a side job that provides me with a few hundred euros a month and, as mentioned earlier, a family that covers most of my expenses.

The question I ask myself almost every day is therefore:

<Quote> 
How should I approach every expense? Does it really make sense to minimize 
everything down to the last Euro/Swiss Franc?”
</Quote>

Unfortunately—or fortunately, depending on your point of view—<b>for now the answer is yes</b>.

If there’s no significant benefit, especially in the long term, I prefer 
to trim the expense to the last decimal digit, even though one franc more or less equals 
<b>about two minutes of my work</b> (considering my current hourly wage).

Behind this choice, in fact, there are no motivations related to 
the need to save or have money for something else, but rather a 
value-based choice to assign as much importance as possible to frugality, supported by the fact that I 
find "pleasure" in <b>NOT</b> spending money.

_Note: the case just presented mainly concerns cash outflows for 
my “survival” and everything that happens in daily life.
There are some categories of spending (health, education, 
some moments of social life...) for which this reasoning becomes irrelevant and stupid._

### <b> Do you really need that bed? </b>

An interesting counter-thesis, which I actually quite agree with, 
argues that only by reducing expenses to the minimum and adopting an extremely frugal lifestyle can we truly understand how little we need to be happy.

Often, in fact, we take for granted that many of the expenses we make have 
real value just because we’ve gotten used to making them and spending money on them. 
<b> It would be enough to give them up </b> to 
discover that, in reality, we need much less than we think.

As fascinating as this view is to me, I remain puzzled by its social consequences.

Living in a more or less consumerist community (depending on the country or the people 
around us), <b> spending is often a necessary condition </b> to participate in 
community life.

Although it’s purely rational when following this theory,
I don’t think it’s reasonable to push minimalism to the extremes just to 
understand how little we “need” to be happy, ending up living on the 
margins of society.
However, I am convinced that, for most people
and for limited periods, adopting a lifestyle aimed at <b> eliminating the superfluous </b>
can bring greater awareness and mental clarity about the way
we spend money.

### <b> At 40 you’ll be too old </b>

If Magiulli proposed a strictly parametric way of acting, focusing on saving in 
relation to one’s wealth, <a href ="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Perkins_(businessman)" target ="_blank"> Bill Perkins</a>,  
author of <a href ="https://www.diewithzerobook.com/welcome" target = "_blank">Die With Zero</a>, shows how and, above all, 
when it makes sense to spend money.

The main thesis of the book, at times banal, is that the financial goal of 
our lives is not to maximize wealth, but rather to <b>maximize the value 
we gain from meaningful experiences</b> and, to achieve this,
the <b>timing</b> with which we spend and save
plays a fundamental role, since not 
all experiences are available or enjoyable at every stage of life. 

Perkins introduces a <b>spending curve</b> and suggests modulating one’s spending function 
also according to age (thus adding an additional parameter $t$ to represent age): spending becomes much
more reasonable when we can derive the 
maximum physical and emotional benefit, often in the first decades of life, which is why, 
especially at a young age, the last goal of our spending habits should 
not be saving.

According to Perkins:

$$
\frac{\partial (f_{experiences}(w, \alpha_1 x_1 \dots \alpha_n x_n, t))}{\partial t} \leq 0
$$

for $t > \bar{t}$, where $f_{experiences}$ quantifies our expenses in experiences 
and $\bar{t}$ is the age of maturity when we have enough awareness to 
spend and intentionally appreciate the experiences we pay for.

In non-mathematical terms: all else being equal, our spending on experiences should
decrease with aging.

Although I totally agree that maximizing the value of our 
broker account is a <s>bullshit</s> short-sighted idea, I’m also convinced that most 
valuable experiences we can live don’t require billionaire capital and, 
beyond a certain threshold, the benefits from spending more money are null.

Formally, if $u(x)$ is the utility of an expense $x$ in experiences, for $x > x_{threshold}$
we can assume:
$$
u’(x) \leq 0 \quad \text{or} \quad u(x) \approx \text{constant}
$$
that is, the marginal benefits we gain after a certain spending threshold are null or, 
for a niche of the population, <b>decreasing</b>, in cases where spending more money ruins or 
makes an experience less authentic.

As much as I appreciate the author’s thesis, since it adopts a 
less rigorous approach and is more focused on maximizing our well-being, <b>I think the initial assumption
is wrong</b>: why should spending more automatically improve 
our lives?

I don’t think there’s a causal link between wealth $w$ and happiness beyond a certain threshold $w_{threshold}$ or, in any case,
even if there were, our well-being would depend on so many other variables that it would make
$w$ almost irrelevant.

To close the loop, although I didn’t share a quote from Morgan Housel at the beginning,
I’m convinced these concepts are masterfully expressed by the American author and investor;
which is why, in addition to recommending <a href="https://www.morganhousel.com/" target ="_blank">The Psychology of Money</a>, it feels natural to mention him again here
(strictly in English since I can’t find a proper translation for _rich_ and 
_wealthy_).

<Quote>
“If you are rich, you have money in the bank that
 allows you to buy the stuff you want.

If you are wealthy, you have a level of control
 over what that money does to your personality, your 
 freedom, your desires, ambitions, 
 morals, friendships, and mental health.”
</Quote>

Just like minimalism and frugality, I’d love to write an entire article about the differences
between _rich_ and _wealthy_ and expand on Morgan Housel’s theses, but for today, I’ll just define myself as 
<b> _wealthy_ much more than I am _rich_</b>, hoping that what I’ve just shared can offer
some thought-provoking insights and anecdotes.
