### Due Premise: In the article, I risk appearing more extreme and hyper-rational than I actually am. I remain a human being, *unfortunately* I too succumb to material temptations and it often happens that I spend with pleasure. **Enjoy!**


### **Spending Money is an Art**

This is how Morgan Housel writes in the opening lines of his latest book  
<a href = "https://www.amazon.com/Art-Spending-Money-Simple-Choices/dp/0593716620" target = "_blank"> <i>The Art of Spending Money</i> </a>  
but, as much as I greatly appreciate the author's books and <a href ="https://collabfund.com/blog/" target ="_blank">blog</a>,  
I can only disagree with this statement.

Spending is above all **a habit**, one of the most ingrained and personal habits we have, started as children, when at $X$ years old we asked for the first time for $Y$ euros from relative $Z$ to buy product $W$.

(In my case: $X = 1.5$, $Y = 1.5$, $Z = “mom”$, $W = “ice cream”$).

Like all daily behaviors, the way we spend says a lot about us and, despite various variables escaping our control, many try to set rules, limits, goals and, more generally, to craft a narrative around money.

And, since I'm convinced that personal finance contributes to a person's mental health (<a href = "https://youtu.be/HeNCLsFNVmc?si=qE5z3nYKQlfDixms&t=3440" target = "_blank"><b>cit Mr. Rip</b>, live from 2025-05-20</a>), I think it's worth investigating how such behaviors arise and how much reasonableness lies behind them, despite, especially in Italy, there still being so much fear, hypocrisy, and discomfort in addressing and opening up about these topics.

Not being able and willing to do a comparative analysis of the Italian/world/Swiss population, I will limit myself to exposing, breaking down, and analyzing my own beliefs and habits related to money.

### **Limited Functions and Partial Derivatives**

In recent years, becoming increasingly independent and starting to earn  
_(not enough to support myself: thanks Parent 1 and Parent 2 for the rent in Zurich)_,  
my relationship with money has changed a lot, influenced by people, books, and family stories.

I've always defined myself as **frugal** and **minimalist** (one could write an entire article about the difference between these two adjectives but, for the purposes of this reasoning, they will be treated as synonyms): I like things that are **simple and linear**, I have few clothes,  
I maintain essential furnishings, a diet based on few ingredients, and I practice hobbies that are mostly "free" or very low-cost. <br />  
I have no online subscriptions, I detest luxury, and I have no expensive vices.

All perfect? *Almost*.

Frugality is a value I appreciate, but, like all things,  
when taken to the extreme, it stops being such and becomes **just a perversion**. 

For years, in fact, I considered the act of spending as something negative,  
regardless of what one is buying and, although it was a spectrum  
(spending on primary necessities is less “negative” than throwing money into an unnecessary impulse purchase),  
it has always remained something against my values.

In mathematical terms, for me the goal has always been to minimize the spending function $f$:

$$
f(w, \alpha_1 x_1 \dots \alpha_n x_n), 
$$

where $w$ represents our net worth, $x_1... x_n$ are our needs and desires, the coefficients $\alpha_1 ... \alpha_n$ the weights (importances) we assign to each $x_i$ and $f$ quantifies  
our cash outflow.

Secondarily, my second goal (especially for the future when, optimistically  
$w$ will grow) has always been to nullify  
the partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $w$:

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial w} = 0
$$

that is, to avoid my spending habits depending on my net worth and,  
in particular, my expenses increasing with my net worth.

Regarding the first goal, it's a tragicomic constant daily struggle:  
$f$ is indeed bounded below by necessary daily expenses ($f \geq K \ge 0$) and undergoes  
local oscillations on occasions of events, unforeseen circumstances, and occasional pleasure purchases.

Regarding the second constraint, I think I've reached a good level of awareness  
and maturity such that $X$ euros more in my wallet don't significantly change my outflows,  
now knowing how to identify the expenses from which I derive (or not) value.

Although I've always been comfortable with this way of understanding frugality,  
I've always done it because I took pleasure in **NOT** spending and saw value in this behavior,  
not so much because there was an ultimate goal or something I needed to save for.

### **In the Balkans with Nick Magiulli**

In recent months, however (fortunately), some behaviors have begun to change, especially after moving  
<a href="https://giacomomaggiore.com/blog/02-one-month-eth" target = "_blank"> to live alone in Switzerland </a> and having consumed,  
in recent years, dozens of contents between books, articles, and videos (the complete list  
is available at the end of the article).

Last summer during <s>one of the most beautiful trips of my life</s>  
a road trip through Greece, Albania, Montenegro, and Bosnia, I read <i>  
<a href="https://www.amazon.com/Wealth-Ladder-Proven-Strategies-Financial/dp/0593854039" target = "_blank">The Wealth Ladder</a></i>,  
the latest book by <a href = "https://ofdollarsanddata.com/" target = " _blank">Nick Magiulli</a> and, although that vacation didn't represent the most suitable atmosphere

Reducing the author's theses to the bare minimum, Magiulli classifies  
people (or family units) **by net worth levels** and  
suggests spending habits and investment goals based on the cluster of belonging.

| Level | Net Worth (EUR) | Description |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|
| 1     | < 10,000        | Live paycheck to paycheck, every expense counts. Priority: build an emergency fund and increase income. |
| 2     | 10,000 - 100,000| You can afford to go grocery shopping without stress. Start investing small amounts and reduce debts. |
| 3     | 100,000 - 1,000,000 | You can eat out or indulge in small luxuries. Focus: regular investments and capital growth. |
| 4     | 1,000,000 - 10,000,000 | Travel and experiences become accessible. You have financial stability and can make freer decisions. |
| 5     | 10,000,000 - 100,000,000 | You can buy the house of your dreams and live without economic constraints. Asset management and estate planning become central. |
| 6     | > 100,000,000   | Wealth is used to create impact, donate, influence, and build legacy. Asset management is strategic and philanthropic. |

<small> <i> This classification is U.S.-centric and may not be representative of the European situation due to very different purchasing power, salaries, and welfare levels between nations. </i></small>

The main thesis extracted from this table is that, regardless of the situation  
one is in, any spending habits should be adapted and evaluated based on one's net worth,  
to the point that Nick Magiulli advises:

> Puoi spendere ogni giorno fino allo $0,01%$ del tuo patrimonio netto 
> senza compromettere la crescita della ricchezza.

Nonostante consigli come questi, <b> troppo generalisti e semplicativi</b>, 
lascino il tempo che trovino; il concetto centrale è chiaro:
 sotto una soglia molto piccola rispetto al patrimonio non vale la
 pena stressarsi troppo per decidere se effettuare una spesa o meno.

Penso infatti che ogni volta in cui i soldi rappresentano una buona occasione per 
eliminare uno stress o una fatica inutile dalle nostre giornate (con _"inutile”_
intendo qualcosa che non porta alcun beneficio a nessuno, nemmeno 
a noi) abbia senso spenderli senza troppi problemi.

Come si può facilmente intuire, questo consiglio si pone in contrapposizione
con la mia tesi iniziale, dal momento che non si rivolve più un problema di mininizzazione
assoluto ma si impone un vincolo diverso:

$$
\sum _{j \in stesso \space giorno}f_j(w, \alpha_1 x_1 ...   \alpha_n x_n,) = 0.01 \% w
$$

Personalmente, mi trovo ancora in una fase intermedia: ho la fortuna di 
avere un side job che mi garantisce qualche centinaio di euro al mese e, come 
anticipato, una famiglia che copre la maggior parte delle spese.

La domanda che mi pongo quasi ogni giorno è quindi:

> How should I approach every expense? Does it really make sense to minimize  
> everything down to the last Euro or Swiss Franc?

Unfortunately, or fortunately, depending on the point of view, **for now the answer is yes**.
