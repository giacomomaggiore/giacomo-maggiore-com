### <b>Swedish <s>Roller</s> Mountains</b>

At the end of September 2024, I left for my <b>exchange in Sweden</b>, and the six months that followed were a real rollercoaster of emotions and new experiences: some good, some bad, but overall, surprising.  
Without going into too much detail:

- I lived alone for the first time in a foreign country  
- I met people with values and habits completely different from mine  
- I discovered a new study method, far different from the traditional structure of Politecnico di Milano and Italian high school  
- I experienced the most serious sports injury of my life, which kept me away from training for several months  

_A quick note on that last point: as trivial as it may sound, for someone like me, who for years has lived partly around running, that injury had a huge impact on everything else._

A year later, even though those months were extremely volatile in terms of personal satisfaction, happiness, and hope for the future, I can see <b>an overall positive trend</b> that has led me to be in Zurich right now.

### <b>The transition to the Dark Side of the Force</b>

After three years in <a href="https://www.polimi.it/formazione/corsi-di-laurea/dettaglio-corso/ingegneria-dellautomazione" target="_blank">Automation Engineering</a>, during which, despite greatly enjoying the mathematical complexity and analytical approach,  I was sure I didn’t want to work with controllers, circuits or robotic arms;  I no longer study engineering.

Today, my focus is on <b>economics, technology management</b>, and <b>especially mathematical models applied to risk management, markets, and macroeconomics,</b> with some exposure to quantitative finance and banking.

Quite a mix, I know.

Yet, the ability to choose exactly which topics to explore, in my case, those with a quantitative and analytical approach, with a focus on the financial sector, has opened a completely new perspective, since I study almost exclusively what I’m passionate about and what, at least ideally, could become part of my future career.

As already mentioned <a href="http://giacomomaggiore.com/blog/01-polimi-recap" target="_blank">in this previous article</a>, being passionate about a subject doesn’t reduce the total study hours required, but makes them much more enjoyable.

### <b>Poisson-Bernoulli vs Numerical Methods</b>

Starting this year, I’ll be studying subjects that are less quantitative compared to my three years at Polimi, <a href="https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1-NsC5fvQB3IZ8dbyv28LKerWm3BDS3wSQFUg-YDvHIk/edit?usp=sharing" target="_blank"> (I’m attaching my study plan here for the curious)</a>.

I consider this normal: as you progress academically, <b>you solve fewer equations and work more on abstraction.</b>

However, some subjects, especially those more related to the Management and Technology aspects of my Master, still make me a bit uncomfortable due to their non-strictly mathematical or engineering nature.

I admit that, at first, I felt <s>(and still feel)</s> a sense of inferiority toward these disciplines.

Only by interacting with classmates and attending the courses I realized how <b>excelling and differentiating yourself in a more qualitative subject can be as difficult as doing so in a quantitative one.</b>

In a purely mathematical or engineering subject, <b>the grade can be decomposed into a sum of random variables</b>, where each variable is a Bernoulli trial: correct (1) with probability $p_i$, or wrong (0) with probability $(1 - p_i)$.

$$
X_i \sim Bernoulli(p_i)
$$

The overall evaluation of knowledge thus follows exactly a Poisson-Binomial distribution with parameters $ n $ and $ \{p_i\}_{i=1}^n $.

$$
Y = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i \sim Poisson-Bernoulli(p_i)
$$

with expected value:

$$
E[Y] = \sum_{i=1}^n p_i
$$

and probability distribution:

$$
P(Y = k) = \sum_{\substack{A \subseteq \{1, \dots, n\} \\ |A| = k}} \left( \prod_{i \in A} p_i \right) \left( \prod_{j \notin A} (1 - p_j) \right)
$$

<b>Quantifying and maximizing your knowledge becomes linear and relatively simple:</b> just maximize $ E[X]$ by improving the individual $ p_i $, i.e., your ability to solve a specific class of problems or exercises.

In contrast, in a more qualitative subject, there is no direct formula to quantify knowledge. It’s like facing an unknown function:

$$
f(t, x_1, x_2 ... x_n)
$$

whose algebraic form and variable nature are unknown (except for $t$, purely theoretical study time)  

The only way to gather information about $f$ is through simulations and iterative numerical methods.


Moreover, this function <b>has a plateau</b> for $t > \bar{t} > 0$, where $\bar{t}$ represents a threshold value.  

Formally, we could express this condition as:

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} = 0 \quad \text{for} \quad t > \bar{t}
$$

Beyond $\bar{t}$, the partial derivative of knowledge with respect to study time vanishes; from this point onward, something else is needed (<b>discussion, a case study, direct experience</b>), often <b>non-standardizable and non-replicable</b> in a purely university context.

All this to say that, despite maintaining a bias toward scientific subjects, I’m starting to think that obtaining a hedge in more qualitative subjects can be equally complex, especially for those like me and my classmates with an almost exclusively engineering background.

### <b>Inside or Outside the Gaussian?</b>

The last difference, probably the strongest, that I noticed between the two universities concerns the people.

I preface this reflection by saying that <b>I don’t want to appear classist or spit in the dish I ate from</b>; I still consider Polimi (and Milan in general) <b>one of the best places to grow, study, and develop</b>.

However, the first impression at ETH is that <b>the level of both professors and students is extremely high.</b>

Before starting classes, my roommate — a longtime friend and also a Poli alumnus — and I jokingly said:

<Quote> Idiots are everywhere. </Quote>

<b>One month later, however, I’m starting to reconsider.</b>

Assume that student intelligence (whose definition is left to the reader) is distributed according to a normal (Gaussian) distribution, with skewness and kurtosis varying depending on context.

$$
f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \, e^{ -\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} }
$$

where $x$ represents the “intelligence” level of an individual student (regardless of definition), $\mu$ is the mean value (the group’s average intelligence), and $\sigma$ is the standard deviation.

At ETH, however, this distribution, which we’ll call $\tilde{f}$, is slightly different.

Firstly: 

$$
\tilde{f}(x) = 0  \quad \forall x < \bar{x}, 
$$

meaning the distribution is truncated on the left at a threshold value $\bar{x}$. 

In less formal terms, for intelligence levels $x$ below the threshold $\bar{x}$, <b>the probability of finding a student is practically zero.</b>

Furthermore:

$$
\tilde{f}(x) = {f}(x - \Delta C)
$$

meaning <b>the entire distribution is shifted toward higher intelligence values.</b>

Integrating $\tilde{f}$ so that it represents a probability distribution gives:

$$
\tilde{f}(x) =
\begin{cases}
0, & x < \bar{x} + \Delta C \\
\dfrac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi} \left[ 1 - \Phi\left( \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\sigma} \right) \right]} \, 
e^( -\frac{(x - \Delta C - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}), & x \ge \bar{x} + \Delta C
\end{cases}
$$
