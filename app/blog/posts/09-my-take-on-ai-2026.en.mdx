Since 2022, interest in the term _“AI”_ has increased by 2500% <a href="https://trends.google.com/explore?q=ai&date=now%201-d&geo=CH" target="_blank">_(Google Trend)_ </a> but, even though this topic is very close to my heart, I have always preferred not to speak out about it—partly because I didn't feel I had much relevant to add, and partly because, both on LinkedIn and in the _*nome di quotidiano di paese*_, <b>the signal-to-noise ratio is already high enough.</b>

Only recently, however, after a few interesting discussions and insights—and also to try and pin down my opinion to see if and how it evolves over the coming ~~months~~ years—I thought it would be a good idea to put my concerns and hopes regarding artificial intelligence into writing.

### <b>Give me a lever long enough and a fulcrum on which to place it, and I shall move the world _(Archimede)_</b>

Using AI means, at least in the activities I am involved in, <b>leveraging one's time and effort</b>; in other words, AI is a tool for amplifying our output. 

Even though I sometimes miss banging my head against intricate but very low-level problems _(like writing Python functions by hand, searching for hidden information, or wasting time solving equations or integrals)_, I am convinced that current AI models make projects accessible and feasible that were previously unthinkable. They don't just allow us to stop wasting time on low-level problems; they allow us to work at a <b>higher level of abstraction</b>, focusing more on design and the desired output.

My concern in this regard is that, by having "almost infinite" computational resources available at a very low cost, we risk failing to perform a cost/benefit analysis of a project _(since the costs are almost zero)_. We end up starting or implementing things _"just for the sake of it"_ that aren't always useful or that have <b>diminishing marginal utility</b> (it often happens, for example, that we develop a prototype, an idea, or write something and only later ask ourselves how useful and relevant it actually is).

The same perplexity applies to the recent trend of wanting to build "agents" for every activity: <b>do we really need to automate everything?</b> How often is this justified by a qualitatively superior output compared to a human one, and how often is it <b>pure show-off</b> or a reflection of mental laziness? 

Having a powerful tool at our disposal does not imply having to exploit it in every circumstance: I am convinced that, _in many cases still_, using GenAI is an <b>overkill</b> and an unnecessary push dictated by the trend of the moment. With the necessary skills, _old-school_ purely <b>algorithmic</b> solutions or, _even "worse"_, purely <b>human-based</b> solutions are still qualitatively superior.

### <b>If you concentrate and look closely, everything will clarify itself _(Haruki Murakami)_</b>

My biggest fear is not that AI will take away our jobs, but rather that it will take away our <b>ability to concentrate</b> on a complex problem for long periods of time. Building something, solving structured problems, and writing elaborate texts are all activities that require time and mental effort _(which is why there isn't a text generated from scratch by AI on this blog yet)_. 

However, thanks to the use of more or less complex AI solutions _(from the free version of an LLM to expensive ad-hoc agents)_, all of this often translates into prompts of a few lines, written with imprecise grammar and syntax and without much reflection behind them, hoping that the model somehow "understands" our intentions and spits out something relevant.

We lose the entire part of problem analysis and decomposition, the deep dive, and the associated effort, ending up—in most tasks—being satisfied with an instant output but <b>settling for a mediocre result</b> or, worse, one that doesn't belong to us or doesn't reflect the nature of the problem in detail. 

For this reason as well, I don't feel comfortable comparing a request to an LLM to a Google search: while the former is instantaneous and purely "passive," a search engine requires <b>choosing, verifying, and analyzing</b> every source.

I don't think, however, that this is the only way to interact with AI. It is indeed still possible to complement _(and be helped by)_ your work with GPT, Gemini, or Grok: entering a state of <b>deep work</b> together with the model, having a clear macroscopic problem and breaking it down into smaller tasks to delegate, brainstorming, researching relevant information faster and, above all, constantly stress-testing your own convictions thanks to their enormous analytical capacity.

I have noticed that, in many cases, the primary goal is no longer to obtain the highest quality output, but rather to <b>maximize the result/effort ratio</b> or, even worse, to <b>minimize the effort exerted</b>: goals for which, _unfortunately_, LLMs are perfectly suited.

Furthermore, in <b>activities where the ends are path-dependent</b> _(primarily studying)_, the use of AI often proves <b>counterproductive</b>. Although I don't want to seem opposed to AI in education _(and I use Gemini myself to help me study)_, in activities where real mental effort is required, I still try to use it as little as possible. <br/>There are tasks for which I am quite happy to save time _(translating and correcting emails, formatting texts, retrieving specific information online)_ and activities where I am happy to have <b>"sub-optimal" productivity</b> for the sake of spending a few extra hours on them _(which is why I am grateful to have completed most of my education before <a href="https://github.com/openai/gpt-2" target="_blank">dell’uscita di GPT-2 nel 2019</a> and why I am fully committed to taking notes "manually," even though Gemini could do it in a few seconds starting from my slides)._

Even though solving integrals, manually writing Python code, and extracting signal from complex texts are now activities easily delegated to an AI, I am convinced that the process through which one arrives at the result is highly formative and allows us to activate parts of our brain _(if necessary, I am ready to be proven wrong by neuroscience)_ that are fundamental for the development of problem-solving skills.

AI is, in fact, removing from our lives—_in a progressive and often subtle way_—all those occasions where mental effort and making choices are necessary. Even if in some cases this effort may be an end in itself without a direct positive outcome, I don't like the idea of a daily life <b>where thinking about problems is no longer required</b>, where we no longer have to run a thousand calculations to evaluate alternatives or iterate several times on the same solution before reaching a convincing result.

### <b>Homework, theorems, and moldy cheese</b>

What worries me most about all this is the long-term impact on primary and secondary education. AI and education are traveling at <b>speeds that are not even remotely comparable</b>: while on one hand <a href="https://llm-stats.com/llm-updates" target="_blank"> ogni pochi mesi viene rilasciato un nuovo modello più performante </a> and capable of tackling long and complex problems, teaching methods have unfortunately lagged behind, caught unprepared by this revolution (_80% of US students report using AI at school, but only 50% of institutions have rules regarding it and only 6% of these rules are clear, <a href="https://hai.stanford.edu/ai-index/2026-ai-index-report/education" target="_blank"> fonte Staford AI report</a>_). In fact, I am convinced that, at least until the final years of high school, a frontier model _(2026)_ is capable of <b>outperforming even the most brilliant students.</b>

The most obvious _(and disastrous)_ consequence is that every student is perfectly aware that any problem assigned at school is easily solvable in a few clicks and, potentially, every difficulty can be "bypassed" with a photo of the assignment and a short prompt. Added to this is the fact that no method is being taught to use these new tools, which, given their enormous potential, require at least a basic level of knowledge to be used effectively.

However, I find that, _despite being very bearish regarding the short-term relationship between education and AI,_ it cannot be denied that in this field AI remains a revolutionary resource, <b>at least as much as the invention of the printing press or Wikipedia.</b> Indeed, I am amazed by how incredible it is to have access to <s>(almost)</s> unlimited knowledge instantaneously, in the ways I prefer and at all desired levels of "superficiality" or depth _(from asking to explain the proof of a mathematical theorem step-by-step to asking what to do when Parmigiano is moldy)._

### <b>Zurigo has more AI-agents than inhabitants</b>

Although this resource is, _at least for me,_ accessible at zero cost and effort, I am well aware that <b>I am in a bubble.</b> For a few months now I have been living in Zurigo, <a href="https://deeptechnation.ch/dtn-news/ai-in-switzerland-stanford-data-confirms-world-leading-talent-density/" target="_blank">la città con la più grande densità di talento in ambito AI </a> but, regardless of that, I spend my days in a very young, favorable context accustomed to technological progress, and I happily work in <a href="https://www.zurich.com/" target="_blank">un'azienda</a> that _(despite being as far as possible from a startup run by twenty-year-olds in t-shirts and Patagonia)_ is proudly pushing hard on the adoption of these tools.

I am aware, however, that the world travels on very different tracks: even among people of my generation, the use and knowledge of LLMs remain very limited, adoption in educational environments is often not even considered _(let alone accepted)_, and not all companies are looking for ways to best exploit this technology. Moreover, its spread strictly depends on the reference sector: software engineering has certainly been the most revolutionized field and, _in general,_ <b>all STEM subjects</b> have strongly felt the <s>positive</s> effects. Not all "intellectual" jobs, however _(clearly leaving aside bricklayers and cooks who, at least for the moment, are safe!)_, are affected in the same way and, consequently, those working in those fields are much less involved and touched by this revolution.

### <b>OpenAI is still a non-profit</b>

My final concern, however, regards another bubble: <b>the one linked to the economic and financial nature</b> behind this trend. I do not question the capabilities of Claude Opus 4.7 or GPT-5; rather, I remain skeptical about how economically sustainable all this is, both for the companies behind the large models and for the third-party companies investing in their adoption and, _ultimately_, for us final consumers given that, at the moment, this entire great revolution is based on <b>companies that are leaking from all sides.</b>

<b>OpenAI</b>, despite its 900 million users _(of which only 5% are paying!)_, <a href="https://finance.yahoo.com/news/openais-own-forecast-predicts-14-150445813.html" target="_blank">prevede di chiudere il 2026 in rosso con 14 miliardi di dollari di perdite _(Yahoo Finance)_</a> and, again according to estimates, increasing its liabilities until 2029. The forecasts for <b>Anthropic</b>, which is more oriented toward an enterprise model than B2C, <a href="https://finance.yahoo.com/news/anthropic-projects-70b-revenue-2028-164854310.html" target="_blank">sono leggermente migliori </a> _(with a positive cash flow predicted as early as 2028)_ but still, at least for now, they remain in the red. A similar game is being played by <b>xAI</b> _(Musk's company behind Grok)_ which, <a href="https://finance.yahoo.com/news/musks-xai-posts-net-quarterly-003131361.html" target="_blank">secondo gli analisti, brucia più di un miliardo a trimestre.</a>

_As Gemini itself suggests to me when I ask it to extract some "bad taste" jokes from this situation_, Gen-AI thus resembles the greatest <b>socialism experiment in Silicon Valley:</b> the rich put up the money and the people use the models to avoid reading books and to work less.

Jokes aside, the mechanisms of <b>circular financing</b> between tech providers and AI scale-ups represent another cause for concern. The pattern—<i>now validated multiple times by various Big Tech companies</i>—involves a hardware/software infrastructure provider (read NVIDIA for graphics cards, Microsoft for Cloud infrastructure) providing capital to an external company (read OpenAI) which, with those same funds, buys services or products from its own financier, <b>inflating financial and growth metrics on both sides.</b>

Despite all these cracks, growth expectations, investments in Private Equity and Credit (markets that are anything but transparent and liquid—another potentially alarming characteristic!) toward these entities and, above all, the valuations of chip providers (NVIDIA first and foremost, the "shovel seller" in the gold rush) are at all-time highs. All this promises nothing but a <b>brilliant future</b>, yet outlines expectations very different from the current reality and actual cash flows.

On the other hand, I wonder how much final users are actually willing to pay for a premium subscription: most people <b>do not need Claude Code or Gemini Pro</b> and, <i>consequently</i>, are not willing to pay dozens of dollars a month. At the same time, however, the models accessible for free maintain a significant cost—both for one-off development and for maintenance—which, <i>sooner or later</i>, <b>someone will have to bear.</b>

That said, I don't think (and I don't hope) that the AI bubble will burst as violently as it did during the dot-com crisis in the early 2000s <s>(famous last words!)</s>; I believe (and perhaps hope!) that this phenomenon will downsize, both in terms of hype and in purely economic terms, eventually leading us to <i>"gently"</i> adjust to this new daily reality. So, whether we like it or not, <b>we better learn to live with it!</b>
