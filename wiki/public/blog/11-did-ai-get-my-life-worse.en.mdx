_Necessary premise: in this article I am deliberately playing devil's advocate: despite the tragicomic tone, I do believe that the revolution in question has brought many more benefits than anything else, with at least $n$ positive externalities for every harm mentioned._

### <b>An exercise in style</b>

Last week I read a comment on YouTube asking, provocatively, _"how has your life gotten worse since AI came along?"._
The live answer was immediate and interesting but the host did not have the opportunity to go deeper into the topic, which is why, _mostly as an exercise in style,_ I tried to give myself some answers.

I will not deny, however, that it took me a few days to come up with some ideas: at first, all the conclusions I reached were actually mostly positive things presented in the wrong way; a bit like when we are asked _"what is our worst flaw"_ and we answer with something we actually consider a virtue. 

Secondly, I wanted to give answers tied as much as possible to my past experience, as a child/teenager <b>who grew up during the AI-free internet boom</b>, and to my present experience, as a guy/student living through this revolution in a <b>passive but fairly conscious</b> way.

As a result, interacting daily with Claude, GPT and [...] and living right in the middle of the bubble <a href="https://giacomomaggiore.com/blog/09-my-take-on-ai-2026" target="_blank">(in Zurich there are more AI agents than human beings!)</a>, every token consumed has inevitably made me reflect. <br/>That said, I am still not sure I have clear ideas about it; in fact, I suspect that right now, _since this is, all in all, a gradual transition,_ <b>we are underestimating the impact</b> it will have on our lives in the long term, and we are no longer able to remember what it was like to study, work and, in general, interact with information before GPT was launched  at the end of 2022.

### <b>An orthogonal basis is enough</b>

I have always admired anyone able to value the concept of <b>"enough"</b>. Especially when it comes to "material" goods, in fact, I like to think that, even assuming a world of infinite resources _(time, money)_, there is an <b>upper bound</b> to what we need and what we can aspire to. 
By contrast, I would never have thought of applying the same limit to everything surrounding information, content and thought.

Most likely, I was wrong. Having available, _at negligible prices,_ AI models capable of improving and surprising us with every training run, I feel we are at the point where all of this is too much or, at the very least, too much for the speed at which it is currently moving, <b>to the point that it gives us no time to react, reflect and adapt.</b>

Although this feeling of "too much" and inadequacy is reflected at different levels of granularity in our lives _(from the chambers of national governments to the amount of bullshit generated with AI and spread on Instagram)_, what worries me most _(and what I think has in some way worsened my daily life)_ is the feeling of having, or at least being able to have, <b>everything I want at my fingertips</b>, often redundantly.

Secondly, the Internet, despite already being a place where filtering noise from signal had become increasingly difficult, has recently undergone a general process of <a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enshittification" target="_blank">enshittification </a>because of how easy it has become to publish content.

In short, <b>it is all too much.</b>

From a content perspective, I like to think that almost every AI output is a <b>linear combination</b> of something "original" _(whose definition is left to the reader)_, which is why I do not need $n+1$ pieces of content if there are already $n$ linearly independent ones capable of containing the sum of human knowledge.

Secondly, the "too much" I am talking about, at least in my experience, often <b>negatively correlates with quality.</b>

Until a few years ago _(when it was not possible to generate thousands of lines of code or an ultra-realistic photomontage in a few seconds!)_, the creation (publication) of information was only the tip of the iceberg of a <b>much more complex iterative process</b>: before doing anything, _since it required time (effort)_, research was carried out, followed by execution, evaluation and continuous questioning and, _only in extremis,_ the synthesis of something.
Today, by contrast, it seems to me that the opposite behavior prevails: first we produce and, _rarely,_ only afterwards, after the public's reaction, do we actually evaluate the quality of what we have done.

Secondly, the too much I am talking about is also reflected in the speed with which we produce and consume information: although I often happen to do it, <b>I hate the idea of accelerating an intellectual process with AI</b>, whether it is writing an article _(something I am still resisting!)_, solving an equation or, _worst of all,_ taking the time to read an article or a book. 

All of this only feeds <b>a dog chasing its own tail:</b> we are able to produce more, we produce more and, _inevitably,_ we are incentivized to consume more, faster and in a more distracted way.

### <b>The Tragedy of Commons</b>

The beast mentioned above also brings a second problem, one that involves society at both the collective and individual level.

Let us assume a world _(not that unrealistic)_ in which everyone has access to AI but where, for the long-term good of humanity, <b>it would be better to limit its use</b> or, taking it to the extreme, stop using it.
<br/>Let us also assume that adopting AI, _at least in the short term,_ <b>brings competitive advantages</b> to those who use it. 

In this context, therefore, the <b>dominant strategy</b> for every individual is to make full use of the technology, knowing that everyone else will do the same.
<br/>By contrast, if people cooperated and agreed to limit (block) its use, the individual would be <b>incentivized to deviate</b> and start using it, driven by the competitive advantage they would obtain.

The point is that, _unfortunately,_ this prisoner's dilemma affects many different realities: first of all, as a student, I am aware that carrying out a project from start to finish without Claude Code would lead me, _perhaps over a longer period of time,_ to a greater understanding of the topic.<br/>
At the same time, I am also aware that with that tool I can reach a better output more quickly and, since I expect the rest of the class to use it in the same way, <b>I am incentivized to use it so as not to look bad in comparison.</b>

In the same way, a scenario in which governments (or companies) around the world agree to stop developing/using more powerful AI models is unrealistic, since each individual actor would have the incentive to <b>collude in order to exploit first mover advantages.</b>

All this to arrive at the conclusion that, _even in cases where one does not want to use AI,_ doing so is often a <b>choice more or less dictated by context:</b> society has now set increasingly higher standards, forcing everyone to <b>run faster and faster just to maintain the status quo </b>.

### <b>Control freak tendencies</b>

Using AI very often means not only having a second voice capable of helping us, but often structurally modifying the way we think, design and work, and all of this translates, _at least in my experience,_ into a <b>(partial/total) loss of control.</b>
<br/>By this I mean that AI often relieves us of design decisions or more or less important daily choices, spares us some tasks and, _even more relevantly,_ allows us to <b>not have to understand 100% of what we are doing.</b>

_As inefficient as it often is,_ I am still fascinated by the idea of having a purely deterministic digital world in which everything somehow "depends" on me _(example: I like the idea of having full ownership of this website, without relying on vibe coding or AI website builders)_ or, similarly, I like the idea of conducting research, a reading or a deep dive without falling back on an explanation from Gemini.

Moreover, the technology around us had already been evolving, _even before the arrival of AI,_ in this direction: if at the dawn of computing it was reasonable to host your own email or build a home NAS, now it seems <b>almost impossible to free yourself from service providers</b> _(more or less transparent and more or less AI-intensive)._ 

Until recently, _however,_ this trend had been <b>confined to tech/software;</b> my concern is that, in a world where Gemini can make me a training plan, draft my weekly menu or my shopping list, <b>our dependence on these models will progressively increase,</b> exposing us to all the risks of centralizing information and control implicit in these dynamics.

In the same way, _although I obviously recognize the positive potential behind delegation,_ I wonder to what extent I am willing to lose control of my life or, _conversely,_ how much I am willing to "limit" my potential in order to remain 100% in command of it.

This loss of control is reflected, _perhaps more evidently,_ especially in learning: although I recognize the usefulness and convenience of being able to ask _anything at any time in any tone,_ by doing so we are indirectly <b>losing mastery over our process of reasoning and inference</b> and, _honestly,_ it is something that worries me in the long run.

Still connected to the topic of learning _(which, as you may have understood, is <s>the only thing that matters </s> fairly relevant at this stage of my life),_ I feel that <b>incentives are changing considerably</b> and all of this risks making us lose sight of the bigger picture. Why should a student struggle to solve an equation or learn to write code when, presumably,<b> they will always have an LLM available that can do it? </b>
Why should a student make the effort to understand a topic in depth when, at the slightest moment of confusion or doubt, there will be an LLM able to answer _"explain this concept to me as if I were a 3-year-old"_? 

Here too, however, the same problem as before arises: in a world where everyone uses AI to compete, why should I, _while aware that it is not ideal for my intellectual development,_ accept <b>falling behind </b> and abstain from this competition; especially when the grade and the resulting comparison with others are often decisive for accessing new opportunities, better universities and jobs?

### <b>We are all less sensitive</b>

The last major theme I would like to raise concerns <b> our ability to be surprised:</b> for a few years now, we have gradually "normalized" and accepted AI's great capabilities, to the point of almost completely saturating our capacity for wonder.

If only 5 years ago we had imagined a future in which a computer was able to replicate _(and often overperform)_ human reasoning across multiple sets of problems, many would have been incredulous. _By contrast,_ it seems to me that we now almost take for granted that Claude 4.6 can recreate Minecraft from scratch or win the mathematics olympiad.

At the same time, this insensitivity to the extraordinary that we feel toward AI inevitably also reflects onto human work: <b>we are in fact no longer able to recognize and value something that is not extraordinary </b> since, _in most cases,_ with the help of Claude and a few dollars of tokens, one could reach a superior output.

On the other hand, <b>some errors or imperfections are no longer tolerated in the slightest</b>, as they are easily "fixable by AI" and, _although I greatly appreciate the grammar check I have Gemini do for every document I write,_  in some contexts <b>avoiding the error was precisely what elevated</b>, _in a positive sense,_ our work from the masses.

I will not deny that there is also a sense of <b>personal frustration</b> in seeing AI surpass me from multiple points of view and, _having based much of my education on technical, analytical and problem-solving-oriented skills,_ sometimes I think I have lost a competitive advantage that now, precisely thanks to LLMs, <b>is available to everyone.</b>

_(On this point, this disappointment of mine is deliberately exaggerated: I am in fact convinced that, although I may have lost "the competitive advantage of knowing how to solve $y' = g(x)h(y)$", the engineering education I received remains a competitive advantage even when AI is able to model and solve most problems)._

All of this becomes much more problematic among younger age groups: I perfectly remember <b>the enthusiasm I felt when writing my first for loop in Python</b>, displaying the first HTML page locally or, _very simply_, the satisfaction of reaching the end of a proof without any help, being surprised by the beauty of the result or by my <b>level of engagement in the activity.</b>
Now, if anyone can do much more complex things in a fraction of a second without the same level of involvement and concentration, I wonder where we are taking <b>the performance bar, </b> how far we are willing to raise it and, above all, <b>what 15-year-olds today tinker with.</b>

That said, far be it from me to be anti-progress or to wish for a return to a world with the abacus or Commodore 64. 

In fact, I do not deny AI's potential and I continue to be positively amazed by everything that can be done, to the point of considering AI an excellent <b>tool for leveraging one's time and skills</b>... I just think that, _before relying on and immersing ourselves completely in this revolution,_ it would be wise to make a few considerations that go beyond simple assessments of short-term productivity and output!
