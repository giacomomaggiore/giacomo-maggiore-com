## <a id="0"><b>Introduction</b> </a>


<Quote>

“Every decision made in a business has financial implications, and any decision that involves the use of
money is a corporate financial decision. Defined broadly, everything that a business does fits under
the rubric of corporate finance. It is, in fact, unfortunate that we even call the subject corporate
finance, because it suggests to many observers a focus on how large corporations make financial
decisions and seems to exclude small and private businesses from its purview. A more appropriate title
for this discipline would be Business Finance, because the basic principles remain the same, whether
one looks at large, publicly traded firms or small, privately run businesses. All businesses have to invest
their resources wisely, find the right kind and mix of financing to fund these investments, and return cash
to the owners if there are not enough good investments.” 

(Damodaran, 2025)

</Quote>

<a href="https://pages.stern.nyu.edu/~adamodar/" target="_blank">It's worth spending some time on Damodaran's website: a lot of (free) lectures, books and papers!</a>.

<b>First Principles - maximizing the value of a business</b>
<br/>
1. <b>The investment Decision</b>
    - the <b>hurdle rate</b> _(minimum rate of return a company or investor requires to approve an investment)_ should reflect the riskiness of the investment
    - the <b>return</b> should reflect the magnitude and the timing of the _cash flows_
2. <b>The Financing Decision</b>
    - the <b>optimal mix of debt and equity</b> that maximizes firm's value
    - the <b>right kind</b> of debt matches the tenor of the firm's assets 
3. <b>The Dividend Decision</b> _(if the firm cannot find a investment that meets its minimum acceptance rate, return the cash to the owners)_
    - <b>How much cash </b> you can return depends on the current and potential opportunities 
    - <b>How you choose</b> to return the cash to the owners will depend on whether they prefer _dividends_ or _buybacks_

_Corporate Finance is not a perfect science!_
<br/>It applies mathematical models and statistics but interprets and tests them in a real-world economic and behavioral contexts.

Recall that the finance goals of the corporation are, from the <b>stockholders' point of view</b>:
- maximize current wealth
- transform wealth into most desirable time pattern of consumption 
- manage risk characteristics of the chosen consumption plan 

But... _how does cash flow between financial markets and the firm's operations?_

<div align = "center">
![GDP by country](/images/notes/a-finance/cash-market.png)
</div>

Recall that, _in presence of such cash-flows,_ the <b>Net Present Value (NPV)</b> is defined as:

$$
\begin{align*}
\text{PV} &= \sum_{t = 1}^{t = T} \frac{c_t}{(1 + r)^t}\\

\text{NPV} &= c_0 + \text{PV}\\
&= c_0 +\sum_{t = 1}^{t = T} \frac{c_t}{(1 + r)^t}


\end{align*}
$$

- $\text{NPV}$ depends only on the <b>forecasted cash flows</b> and the <b>opportunity cost of capital</b> $r$
- $\text{NPV}(A+B) = \text{NPV}(A) + \text{NPV}(B)$

## <a id="1"><b>1. Agency Problems and Corporate Governance</b> </a>

The <b>agency problem</b> is defined as a conflict of interest inherent in any relationship when one party is expected to act in another's best interests.

In particular, <b>the principal-agent problem </b> is a conflict of interest that occurs when an agent _(manager)_ authorized to act on behalf of a principal _(owner)_ pursues their own self-interests rather than maximizing the principal's goals

<b>Agency costs</b> reduce firm value due to agency problems:
- value lost because managers do not maximize the value of the firm 
- costs of monitoring managers and intervening when problems arise.

Principals agency problems come from:
- not put in sufficient efforts
- use cash on _perks_ and _private benefits_
- over-invest in search of power and prestige 
- be reluctant to take risks or take too many risks
- focus on short-term results at the expense of long-term value 

In the corporate world, <b>Boards of Directors</b> exist to solve this problem.<br/>In particular, _in the US and the UK,_ boards are composed as follows:
- One single board with outside non-executive directors and inside executive directors _(CEO, CFO)_ to provide the board with necessary expertise and information about the firm.
- The majority of the directors has to be independent according to NYSE and NASDAQ _(in practise, around 85% are independent)_  to ensure that the people monitoring the managers aren't also friends with them or financially dependent on them.
- It should not be too big to generate a <b>free-rider </b> problem  _(when those who benefit from resources, public goods and common pool resources do not pay for them)_
- It should not be too small to be unable to handle the complexities.
- Directors should not serve too many boards.
- Annual elections are done for a share of the board, some directors are not changed yearly.

On the other hand, shareholders can also play a strong role in monitoring the firm with three by:
- <b>Voting</b>
    - <b>Binding</b> versus <b>Advisory</b> votes
    - Promoting <b>proxy fight</b> _(aggressive move where an activist shareholder tries to replace the current board by persuading other shareholders to use their "proxy" votes and vote on behalf of another.)_
    - <b>Dual-Class Equity</b> _(when some shares, usually held by founders, have more voting power (example: 10x, 100x) of "normal" shares)._
- <b>Engagement</b>
- <b>Governance through exit from the company</b>

<b>Auditors</b> monitor the firm ensuring consistency with <b>generally accepted accounting principles</b> (GAAP):
- in case of no problems: document certifying that the financial statements fairly represent the company's financial conditions 
- in case of problems:
    1. <b>Qualified opinion</b>: accounts have not fully acted according to GAAP
    2. <b>Adverse opinion</b>: accounts violate many GAAP rules.

<br/>
<a href="https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/adverseopinion.asp" target="_blank">More on the adverse opinion [Investopedia]</a>

Moreover, <b>lenders</b> usually track company's asset. <b>Takeover</b> involves funding the acquisition of a target company, typically through cash, debt, equity issuance, or a mix of these methods when assets are not being used efficiently.

## <a id="2"><b>2. Stakeholder Capitalism and Responsible Business</b> </a>

<b>Stakeholders</b> are individuals, groups, or organizations with a vested interest in, or who are affected by, the actions, decisions, and success of a project, company, or organization
- employees 
- customers
- suppliers
- local and regional communities
- the environment 
- the government 

<br/>
<b>The Case for Shareholder Capitalism</b>


<a href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milton_Friedman" target="_blank">Milton Friedman</a> in 1970 with his article _"The Social Responsibility of Business is to increase its profits"_ rejected <b>stakeholder capitalism</b> and argued that a company’s only responsibility is to its shareholders:

<Quote>

“.....there is one and only one social responsibility of business—to use its
resources and engage in activities designed to increase its profits so long as it
stays within the rules of the game, which is to say, engages in open and free
competition without deception or fraud.”

</Quote>

Friedman's argument is based on three points:
- maximizing shareholder value requires investing in stakeholders and taking them seriously
- government policies ensure companies will engage in socially responsible behavior 
- maximizing shareholder value gives shareholders maximum freedom to support the social objectives they care about.

<Quote>
The discussions of the “social responsibilities of business” are notable for their analytical looseness and lack of rigor. What does it mean to say that “business” has responsibilities? Only people can have responsibilities. A corporation is an artificial person and in this sense may have artificial responsibilities, but “business” as a whole cannot be said to have responsibilities, even in this vague sense. The first step toward clarity in examining the doctrine of the social responsibility of business is to ask precisely what it implies for whom.

<br/>(quote from the article)
</Quote>

<a href="https://www.nytimes.com/1970/09/13/archives/a-friedman-doctrine-the-social-responsibility-of-business-is-to.html" target="_blank">Full article here</a>.

With that, Friedman also pointed out that it is in the companies' self interest <b>to invest in their local community</b> and proposed that all future cash flows should be included into the NPV calculation, _including those arising through stakeholders._

According to this view, since prosperity depends on profits and externalities and externalities are usually addressed by laws and government, <b>the market cannot police itself regarding social harm</b> $\implies$ _that is the job of the state._
<br/>In other words, <b>the government sets the _"rules of the game."_,</b> then the company plays to win _(maximize profit)_ within those rules.



By contrast, <b>"Enlightened Shareholder Value" (ESV) </b> is a managerial objective phrase used, for example, in the UK Companies Act 2006. Under ESV directors are required to promote the success of the company for the benefit of its members (shareholders) while considering a list of factors _(long-term consequences, employees, suppliers, customers, community, environment, and the company's  ability to innovate)._

ESV therefore treats stakeholder and sustainability considerations as factors that can affect long-term shareholder value and so deserve attention in management decisions.

Practical implications:
- ESV keeps a clear, shareholder-centred objective (promote the company's success) but recognises that long-run shareholder value can be improved by attending to stakeholder interests and environmental risks.
- Economic tools _(NPV, risk-adjusted discounting)_ are central for investment valuation, but under ESV managers should also incorporate longer horizons, material non-market impacts and risk mitigation benefits that affect expected shareholder returns.
- ESV helps justify some social and environmental investments when they are expected to preserve/increase long-term value 
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>The Case for Stakeholder Capitalism</b>

Violations of Friedman's assumptions may justify stakeholder capitalism:
- _Assumption 1_: <b>well functioning governments</b>
    - government may be influenced by lobbying
    - elections are not very frequent
    - regulation cannot regulate qualitative issues effectively _(negative externalities are not priced!)_
- _Assumption 2_: <b>companies have no comparative advantage in serving society</b> 
    - companies have comparative advantages in activities they control 
    - companies have comparative advantages in expertise 
- _Assumption 3_: <b>investing in stakeholders is _instrumental_ and _functional_ to increase profits</b>
  - it's impossible to calculate the return of investing in stakeholders

_In reality_, investing in stakeholders splits the pie in _short-term_ <b>but grows it in the _long-term_ </b> in ways that could not be
predicted _(uncertainty, NPV calculation,..)_ and, at the end, <b>both shareholders and stakeholders are better off than before</b>.

The <b>potential advantage </b> of stakeholder capitalism is that it allows managers to pursue stakeholder interest even if doing so can't be justified by an NPV calculation.<br/>However, this causes:
- no clear rule to replace NPV
- <b>accountability to everyone means accountability to no one.</b>

Moreover, there's arbitrariness at least regarding two dimensions:
- <b>weighting</b>: how much _shareholders_ vs _stakeholders_ value
- <b>comparability</b>: how to measure shareholder value _(USD)_ vs stakeholder value _(salary, happiness, environment etc...)_
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Responsible Business</b>

Responsible business lies at the middle between shareholder and stakeholder capitalism.
- It creates value for shareholders by creating value for society _(with an explicit duty to shareholders)_ 
- While <b>Corporate Social Responsibility</b> relates to non-core activities _(donations, philanthropy)_, <b>responsible business is about the core activities of a firm.</b>
- <b>Guiding Principle</b>: create value for society and _at least_ don't reduce shareholder value significantly.
    - <b>Principle of Multiplication</b>: does an investment in a stakeholder generate a greater benefit?
    - <b>Principle of Comparative Advantage</b>: can the company deliver more value through an activity than others?
    - <b>Principle of Materiality</b>: are the stakeholders an objective of the company's business?

_Generally speaking_, some firms tie CEO and management's pay to ESG targets, so the company is more aligned and motivated to hit them. _(In 2023, 76% of S&P500 firms linked executive pay to at least one ESG target)_.

However, the CEO may focus only on the metric and miss the point. Secondly, it's an <b>extrinsic</b>, _not intrinsic,_ motivation: CEOs focus on ESG target because they earn a bonus, rather than care really about the environment.

To ensure that companies are being run responsibly, <b>ESG rating agencies</b> provide an overall assessment of a company's ESG performance, similar to how credit agencies apply a rating on creditworthiness, however:
- <b>ESG ratings are inconsistent.</b> _The correlation between ratings of two different agencies are, on average, 0.28-0.71; compare do 0.9 for credit ratings._
  - 38% is caused by the use of different attributes 
  - 56% is due to measurement 
  - 6% is due to weight: different providers place different importance on individual indexes/components of the rating.

## <a id="3"><b>3. Credit Risk and the Value of Corporate Debt</b> </a>

The price of a bond s the present value of all cash flows generated by the bond _(coupons and face value at maturity $T$)_ discounted at the required rate of return $r$.

$$
PV = \sum_i \frac{\text{Coupon}_i}{(1 + r)^i} + \frac{\text{Face Value}}{(1 + r)^T}
$$

Note that the price of _long-term_ bonds is<b> more sensitive</b> to changes in the interest rate than the price of short-term bonds.

<div align = "center">
![GDP by country](/images/notes/a-finance/sensitivity.png)
</div>

<b>Expected vs Promised Yield</b>

However, it is very important to distinguish between <b>promised</b> and <b>expected yields</b>.


Suppose Giacomo GmbH offers a 5% 1-year note, issued at par ($1000 CHF$), with a risk-free rate $5%$.
<br/>Consider also that, unfortunately, Giacomo has a $20%$ chance of default and only pay $500 CHF$.



The <b>Present Value of the Note </b> is then:

$$
PV = \frac{(1000 + 50) CHF}{1.05} = 1000 CHF
$$

However, considering the risk of default, we can calculate the <b>expected payoff</b> of the note as:

$$
\text{Expected Payoff} = (1050 CHF) \times 0.8 + (500 CHF) \times 0.2 = 940 CHF
$$

We then discount the expected payoff to obtain the present value of the bond:

$$
PV_{default} = \frac{940 CHF}{1.05} = 895 CHF
$$

If we consider the <b>face value promised by Giacomo</b> (hence excluding the possibility of default), we obtain the <b>Promised Yield</b>:

$$
\text{Promised Yield} = \frac{1050 CHF}{895 CHF} - 1= 17.3% 
$$


Note that, even if the promised yield is so high, the <b>expected yield </b> is always 5%:

$$
\text{Expected Yield} = \frac{940 CHF}{895 CHF} - 1 = 5%
$$

---


$AAA$ bonds by Moody's are the highest-grade bonds and are issued only by a few _blue-chips_ companies.
<br/>Historically, the <b>promised yield</b> on these bonds has been about $1%$ higher than the yield on 10-Year Treasuries.
<br/>On the other hand, $BAA$ bonds are rated _three so called notches lower_, and the yield is historically $2%$ higher.

<b>Rate of Return</b>

The total return on a bond investment over a specific period, accounting for both the coupons received and the change in the bond's market price.

$$
\text{RoR} = \frac{\text{Coupon} + (P_{t+1} - P_t)}{P_t}

$$


<b>Yield to Maturity (YTM)</b>

YTM is the internal rate of return (IRR) earned by an investor who buys the bond today at the market price and holds it until maturity, assuming that _all coupons are reinvested at the same rate._

$$

P = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{C}{(1 + \text{YTM})^t} + \frac{FV}{(1 + \text{YTM})^T}
$$

<b>Macaulay Duration</b>

Duration is the weighted average of the times to each of the cash payments. It is the primary tool for estimating a bond's sensitivity to interest rate changes.

$$
D = \sum_{t=1}^{T} \frac{t \times PV(C_t)}{P}
$$

Often we use <b>Modified Duration</b> to calculate the actual percentage change in price for a 1% change in yield ($y$):

$$
\Delta P \% \approx - \frac{D}{1+y} \times \Delta y

$$


<b>Convexity</b>

Duration is a linear approximation of the interest rate and the price, convexity takes into account the second order relation, especially important for bigger interest rate changes.

$$
\text{Convexity} = \frac{1}{P \times (1+y)^2} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \left[ \frac{C_t}{(1+y)^t} \times (t^2 + t) \right]
$$

_Intuition:_ A bond with higher convexity drops less when rates rise and gains more when rates fall compared to a bond with lower convexity.

<b>Credit Default Swaps</b>

It is possible to insure corporate bonds with financial instrument called <b>Credit Default Swap</b> (CDS) (Warren Buffet call them _"financial weapons of mass destruction"_)

CDS are financial derivatives acting as an insurance against the risk of a borrower defaulting on debt (bonds). The buyer makes regular premium payments to a seller, who agrees to compensate them if a specific credit event, _like bankruptcy or insolvency, occurs._ 

CDS are traded over-the-counter (OTC) and their premiums are quoted in basis points, reflecting the market’s view on the risk of default. 

Note that a buyer does not have to own the underlying (the debt, bonds), he can just buy a CDS simply betting that the borrower’s credit health will decline or that they will default _(speculation)_. In this case, they are called <b>"naked" Credit Default Swaps</b>.

Regarding naked CDS, especially after 2008-crisis, CDS are heavily regulated. _For example_, in the EU, buying naked CDS on government bonds is NOT allowed to avoid speculation attacks on countries.

_Example_ during the Credit Suisse Crisis, CDS of that bank went incredibly high.

<b>Valuing the Option to default</b>

The main difference between corporate and treasury bonds is that the company has the option to default, whereas the government supposedly doesn't.

_Consider the following example:_

Giacomo's GmbH borrowed $ 50 CHF$ per share but then the firm faced hard times and market value of its assets fell to $30 CHF$. <br/>
Giacomo's Bond and stock prices fell respectively to $27 CHF$ and $3 CHF$.

If Giacomo's debt were _due_ and _payable now_, the firm could not pay the $50 CHF$ originally borrowed, it would <b>default </b> leaving bondholders with assets worth USD 30 and shareholders with nothing.

However... the reason the stock has a market value of $3 CHF£ is that the debt is _NOT_ due immediately, but one year from now.

But, Giacomo is not compelled to repay the debt at maturity, why?

- <b>If the value of assets $< 50 CHF$</b>: the value of the assets is less than it owes to the bondholders. Therefore, it will exercise its option to default on the debt and the debtholders will receive the assets.

So, Giacomo's GmbH issued a safe bond, but at the same time acquired an<b> option to sell the firm’s assets </b> to the bondholders for the amount of the debt. The exercise price is $50 CHF$ for this bond, e.g., the face value of the bond
 
- <b>If the value of assets $ > 50 CHF$:</b> Giacomo will not exercise the option to default.

In general terms, we can say:

$$
\text{Value of a Bond} = \text{Value of Default-Free Bond} - \text{Value of Default Put}
$$

_Intuition_: the <b>Default Put </b> represents the value of the _"limited liability"_ granted to the company's owners.

_For example_, if we assume returns on firm's assets $60%$, risk free-rate $10%$ and a current value of assets $30 CHF$, exercise price of default option $50 CHF$, we get:

From the Black Scholes model, we get that the value of the default put is $18.6 CHF$ and:

$$
\text{Value of a Bond} = \frac{50}{1.10} - 18.6
$$

Instead of comparing different ratio, a common way of evaluating the possibility of default is merging them in a single index: <b>The Altman's Z Score</b>, computed as:

$$
Z = 1.2 x_1 + 1.4x_2 + 3.3x_3 + 0.6 x_5 + x_5
$$

where:
- $x_1:$ working capital / total assets
- $x_2:$ retained earnigs / total assets
- $x_3:$ EBIT / total assets
- $x_4:$ market value of equity / total liabilities 
- $x_5:$ sales / total assets

_Common interpretations_:
- $Z  > 2.99$: <b>safe zone</b>
- $1.81 < Z < 2.99$: <b>grey zone </b>
- $Z < 1.81$: <b>red zone</b>

## <a id="4"><b>4. The Many Different Kinds of Debt</b> </a>

<div align = "center">
![Yield Spreads](/images/notes/a-finance/corporate-debt.png)
</div>

Before we start, some <b>bond terminology</b>:
- <b>indenture/trust deed</b>: between the company and a trustee that represents the bond-holders. The trustee must see that the terms of the indenture are respected and look after the bond-holders in case of default. _(usually a big commercial bank that monitors and acts in case of default)_ 
- <b>registered bond</b>: the company keeps records of the owners, interest and principal are paid directly to the bondholders _(the big majority of bonds traded today)_
- <b>bearer bond</b>: the company doesn't keep records of the owners, who have to physically cut off a coupon and present it to a bank to receive the interest payment
- <b>accrued interest</b>: amount of accumulated interest since the last coupon payment 
- <b>coupon</b>: annual/semi-annual interest paid on a bond 
- <b>debentures (=bonds)</b>: long term unsecured issues on debt 
- <b>mortgage bonds</b>: long term secured debt, often containing claims against a specific property 
- <b>collateral trust bonds</b>: bonds secured by common stocks of other securities from the borrower 
- <b>equipment trust certificate</b>: secured debt generally used to finance railroad equipment
- <b>asset-backed securities (ABS)</b>: sale of cash-flows derived directly from a set of bundled assets
- <b>mortgage-backed securities</b>: set of mortgage loans sold, owners receive a portion of mortgage payments _(off-balance sheet)_.
- <b>foreign bond</b>: bonds sold to local investors in another country's bond market 
- <b>yankee bond</b>: bonds sold publicly by a foreign company in the US 
- <b>samurai</b>: bonds sold by a foreign firm in Japan 
- <b>bulldog</b>: bonds sold by a foreign firm in the UK 
- <b>eurobond</b>: bond denominated in one country's currency but marketed internationally 
- <b>global bonds</b>: very large bond issues marketed internationally and on individual domestic markets.

A <b>callable bond</b> allows the issuer to call back _(repay)_ the debt before maturity, valuable to reduce the company's leverage, it often happens when interest rates fall.
<br/>A <b>puttable (retractable) bond </b> allows the investor to be repaid for the debt, it is a protective covenant for the investor, it often happens when interest rates increase.

A <b>sinking fund</b> is a fund established to retire debt before maturity.

<b>Bond covenants</b>

Since investors know that there's risk of default, covenants are used to limit the company default position, for example by:
- <b>debt ratios</b>: limits of further borrowing _(senior debt limits senior borrowing, junior debt limits senior and junior borrowing)_.
- <b>Security: negative pledge</b>: the company promises not to give any of its assets to create a security interest for other lenders, unless the current bondholders are given an equal share of that security.
- <b>Leasing </b> (similar to secured borrowing)
- <b>Dividends</b>

<b>The Bond-Warrant Package</b>

Instead of selling convertible bonds, firms sometimes sell a package of straight bonds and warrants. Warrants are simply long-term <b>call options</b> that give the investor the right to buy equity.
<br/>Warrants are usually issued privately, can be detached, exercised for cash and are usually taxed differently. _Note also that warrants can be issued on their own_.

<b>Bond innovations</b>
- <b>Catastrophe (CAT) bonds</b>: payments are reduced in the event of a specified natural disaster 
- <b>Contingent convertibles (CoCos)</b>: bonds that convert automatically into equity as the value of the company falls 
- <b>Equity-linked bonds</b>: payments are linked to the performance of a stock market index 
- <b>Longevity bonds</b>: payments are reduced or eliminated if there's a fall in mortality rates 
- <b>Mortality bonds</b>: payments are reduced or eliminated if there's a jump in mortality rates 
- <b>Pay-in-kind bonds (PKIs)</b>: issuer can choose to make interest payments in cash or in more bonds with same face value 
- <b>Credit-linked bonds</b>: coupon rate changes with the company's rating

- <b>Set-up bonds</b>: coupon payments increase over time.

## <a id="5"><b>5. Leasing</b> </a>

A <b>lease</b> is a rental agreement that involves a series of fixed payments from the lessee _(user)_ to the lessor _(owner)_. 
- <b>Operating lease</b>: short-term, cancelable. _(Historically, these were "off-balance sheet," but modern accounting standards (like IFRS 16) now require to be recognized as liabilities.)_
- <b>Financial lease</b>: long-term, non cancelable. _(Often, at the end of the term, the lessee often has the option to buy the asset)_.

Some more possibilities in leasing include:
- Rental lease (full service): the lessor handles everything, including maintenance, insurance and taxes.
- Net lease: the opposite of full service.
- Direct lease: arrangement where the lessee identifies an asset, and the lessor _(often a bank or leasing company)_ buys it from the manufacturer just to lease it to the lessee. 
- Sale and leaseback: A company sells an asset they already own (like a headquarters building) to a buyer and immediately leases it back in order to unlock some capital _(liquidity)_ while still using the asset.
- Leveraged lease: a three-party transaction involving a lessee, a lessor, and a lender. The lessor borrows a significant portion of the asset's cost (usually $60\%$ to $80\%$) to purchase it.

PROs of leasing:
- short-term leases are often convenient
- maintenance is provided
- standardization leads to lower costs 
- tax shields/advantages can be used 
- leasing preserves capital

CONs of leasing:
- leasing avoids capital expenditures control 
- leases may be off-balance sheet financing 

<b>Lease treatment in bankruptcy</b>

_What happens if a bankrupt lessee takes a lease? What happens if the lease is rejected?_ 

If the lease is confirmed, the lessee continues to use the asset and must sustain the payments. If the lease is rejected, the asset is returned to the lessor. <br/>
If the value of the returned asset is not enough to cover the remaining payments, the lessor's loss becomes an unsecured claim on the bankrupt firm.

<b>Operating Leases </b>

The Equivalent Annual Cost of an asset is the rental payment sufficient to cover the present value of all the costs of owning and operating an asset. The rental payments are hypothetical, _(just a way of converting a present value to an annual cost)_. In the leasing business the payments are real. 

If a business/firm needs an asset:
- buy it if the equivalent annual cost of ownership and operating is less than the best lease rate 
- if it plans to use it for extended period, usually annual cost of ownership and operating is less than the leasing rate.
There are two corner cases here operating lease make even sense despite the _higher cost_:
- the lessor is able to manage the asset more efficiently/at less expense than the lessee 
- operating leases often contain useful options

Suppose that, in the past _(when leaes were off-balance sheet)_, a company needed one asset. It has two main options:

- <b>Lower Leverage:</b> Debt-to-Equity ratios look lower

According to actual accounting standards however, most leases  _(even operating ones)_ must now be recorded on the balance sheet as a _Right-of-Use (ROU)_ Asset and a corresponding _Lease Liability._

## <a id="6"><b>6. Managing Risk, Options</b> </a>

<b>Why manage risk</b>

Reason why risk reduction does NOT add value:
- hedging is a zero sum game 
- investors can hedge themself $\to$ they have a do-it yourself alternative 
<br/>
<b>Insurance</b>

Most businesses face the risk of events that can cause financial stress to them (both financial or business-related risks). The cost and risk of a loss can be shared by others who share the same risk $\to$ the firm can transfer the risk to an insurance company.

<b>Catastrophe bonds _(CAT bonds)_ </b>allow insurers to transfer risk to bondholders by selling bonds whose cash flow payments depend on the level of insurable losses NOT occurring.<br/> <a href="https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/catastrophebond.asp" target="_blank">More on CAT bonds here [Investopedia]</a>.
- on some CAT bonds (<b>Indemnity Trigger</b>), payments are reduced if claims against the issuer exceed a limit  $\to$ the problem is that the insurance company, knowing this, may try to underwrite more counterparties to gain additional premia, therefore creating a problem of _moral hazard_ for the insurance company _(since it has less incentives to be careful)_
- in some other cases (<b>Index Trigger</b>), payments are reduced if claims against the _entire industry_ exceed a limit $\to$ partial solution to the _moral hazard_ problem

<b>Understanding options: terminology</b> 
<br/>
- <b>Derivatives</b>: _any financial contract derived from another_ 
- <b>Option</b>: _gives the holder the right to buy/sell a security in the future at specified price_ 
- <b>Option Premium</b>: _price paid for the option above the price of the underlying_ 
- <b>Intrinsic Value</b>: _difference between the strike price and the security price_ 
- <b>Time Premium</b>: _value of the option above the intrinsic value_ 
- <b>Exercise Price</b> (Strike): _price at which the underlying can be bought/sold_ 
- <b>Expiration Date</b> (Maturity): _last date on which the option can be exercised_ 
- <b>American Option</b>: _can be exercised any time_
- <b>European Option</b>: _can be exercised only on the expiration_

<br/>

---
<br/>
<b>The Put-Call Parity</b>

- a call option gives the right to buy the underlying.
- a put option gives the right to sell the underlying.

_For an European Option:_ 

$$
\text{C} + PV(K) = \text{P} + \text{S} 
$$

- $\text{C}$: premium of the call option
- $\text{P}$ premium of the put option
- $PV(K)$: present value of the strike price 
- $\text{S}$: underlying price

_The idea behind the put-call parity equation is that the two following portfolio must have the same value, otherwise we end up with an arbitrage opportunity_:
- <b>Portfolio 1</b>: $\text{Call} + \text{ Money to buy the asset at }K,T$
- <b>Portfolio 2</b>: $\text{Put} + \text{ Asset}$

In case this equation doesn't hold, there's of course an _arbitrage opportunity_ and, to capture a profit, the following strategies can be used:
- $C + PV(K) > S + P$:
    - <b>Reversal (the Call is overpriced)</b>:
    - The "synthetic" stock position (the call and the cash) is worth more than the actual stock and the put.
    - <b>Strategy</b> Sell the Call, Borrow the present value of the strike price, Buy the Put, and Buy the Stock.
   
- $C + PV(K) < S + P$: 
    - <b>Conversion (the Put is overpriced)</b>:
    - <b>Strategy:</b> Buy the Call, Lend (invest) the present value of the strike price, Sell the Put (Write it), and Short the Stock.
    
<br/>
_Intuition: given the put-call parity formula $\text{C} + PV(K) = \text{P} + \text{S}$ we short the "cheapest" side and we go long on the "expensive" one._

<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Option Strategies: The Protective Put (Buying Insurance)</b>

A protective put is the combination of: 
- a long position in the underlying 
- a long put on the same underlying 

The put _acts as insurance_ if the asset falls below $K$, the puts pays off and limits the loss. If the asset price rises, the put expires worthless $\implies$ the cost of the strategy is the put premium.

_In corporate finance_: a finance that owns a large equity stake in a partner company or a fund with equity exposure, may buy puts to protect the downside of its balance sheet without selling the asset_ $\implies$ the protective put is the _simplest real-world option strategy (structurally identical to an insurance)_

</div>

<b>The Covered Call (Generating income from an asset)</b>

A covered call combines:
- a long position in the underlying 
- a short call on the same underlying 

By selling the call, the investor collects the premium _immediately_. In exchange, they give up any gains of the asset above $K$:
- if the asset stays $<K$ the investor keeps both the asset and the premium 
- if the asset rises  $> K$ the investor sells at $K$ missing the upside

_A firm that has agreed to sell an asset at a fixed future price has implicitly written a covered call_. 

_The covered call structure also appears in a callable bond:_ the issuer holds the right to redeem early _(a call on the bond)_ which caps the investor's upside in a falling-rate (favorable) environment.

![Options](/images/notes/a-finance/covered-call.png)
</div>

<b>The Straddle (Betting on volatility)</b>

The straddle combines:
- a long call at strike $K$ 
- a long put at strike $K$ with the same maturity 

The strategy profits if the underlying moves _significantly_ in either direction and loses if the underlying stays close to $K$ $\implies$ the investor is buying volatility.

<br/>
<b>Options can be replicated</b>

The fundamental insight behind option pricing and hedging is that, _at every moment_, the risk of an option position can be <b>offset by taking an appropriate position in the underlying</b>. By continuously adjusting the hedge, the option seller can neutralise their exposure to movements, leaving them with only the <b>time decay</b> f the option, which they collect as a profit.

$\implies$ _the option seller does not simply absorb the risk and hope for the best._

To build a replicating strategy, the central concept is <b>Delta</b> ($\Delta$) that measures how much the option price changes when the underlying price changes by one unit:

$$
\Delta = \frac{\partial \text{Option Price}}{\partial S}
$$

For a call, $\Delta \in [0,1]$.
- ATM call has $\Delta \approx 0.5$ 
- ITM call has $\Delta \approx 1$ 
- OTM call has $\Delta \approx 0$ 

For a put. $\Delta \in [-1,0]$ because the value decreases as the price of the underlying decreases:
- ATM call has $\Delta \approx -0.5$ 
- ITM call has $\Delta \approx -1$ 
- OTM call has $\Delta \approx 0$ 

---
<br/>
<b>Delta Hedging</b>

To understand how delta hedging works, let's consider a bank <b>selling a call to a client</b> $\implies$ the bank is <b>short on the call</b> _(exposed to losses if the stock rises)_.

To hedge this, the bank buys $\Delta$ shares of the underlying for each option sold.

<div align = "center">
![Options](/images/notes/a-finance/delta-h.png)
</div>

For all the other cases, the logic is identical: for every option sold/purchases, the bank must buy $\Delta$ shares of the underlying. However, note that $\Delta$ is negative for a put and positive for a call, so the bank either shorts/goes long on the <b>underlying</b>.


_At every moment,_ the bank is holding <b>as many shares as needed to match the option's sensitivity</b>.

Therefore, the option premium is the <b>fair price of a dynamic hedging strategy</b>.

_Intuition: option sellers are not irrational, they run a "manufacturing business":_ 
- they take in raw materials _(premium)_ 
- they run a production process _(delta hedging strategy)_ 
- they deliver a product _(option payoff)_ 

This also explains why <b>volatility is so critical to option pricing</b>: higher volatility $\implies$ higher frequency of rebalancing $\implies$ more expensive replication strategy $\implies$ higher premium regardless of the direction.

<b>The limits of the hedge: Gamma risk</b>

Delta hedging works well if the trader can rebalance _continuously_. In practise, rebalancing occurs at _discrete intervals_, leaving the trader exposed to <b>gamma risk</b>, the risk that the stock jumps between rebalancing intervals and the delta estimation becomes wrong.

Therefore we say that a delta-hedged portfolio is <b>delta-neutral</b>, it doesn't gain or lose value from _small moves_ in $S$, but it still exposed to <b>gamma</b> _(large moves)_, <b>theta</b> _(time decay)_ and <b>vega</b> _(volatility changes)_.

---
<br/>
<b>Example: Future and Options for Jack Mining Company</b>

<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simmer_and_Jack#:~:text=Simmer%20and%20Jack%20Mines%20Ltd,at%20the%20Johannesburg%20Stock%20Exchange." target="_blank">Jack Mining Company</a> is worried about _short-term volatility_ in revenues.<br/>
Gold currently sells for USD $1300$/ounce, but the price is volatile and could fall to $1220$ or rise to $1380$ in the next month. Jack will sell $1000$ ounces to the market in the same period.

1. If Jack remains <b>unhedged</b> (Market Exposure), he remains fully exposed to the "spot" price.
$$
\text{Revenue} = S \times Q
$$

(Where $S$ is the spot price and $Q$ is the quantity of 1,000 ounces) 

2. If Jack hedges his position with <b>futures</b> (Price Lock), he eliminates all uncertainty by pre-selling their gold at a fixed price, ensuring a guaranteed revenue regardless of the future price.

$$
\text{Revenue} = F \times Q
$$
(Where $F$ is the locked-in futures price of $1,310)

- in this case, Jack enters a <b>short position</b> on futures _(obligation to sell the ounces at a specified price)_.

3. If Jack hedges with <b>option</b>, he has an "insurance" that sets a minimum sale price _(strike $K$)_ while allowing them to benefit from higher market prices, but they must subtract the cost of the insurance _(premium)_ from their total.
$$
\text{Revenue} = (\max(S, K) - P) \times Q
$$
(Where $K$ is the strike price of $1,300$ and $P$ is the premium of $110 per ounce)

Generally speaking, a business has:
- <b>Business risk</b> affecting fixed and variable costs and revenues $\to$ operating leverage
- <b>Financial Risk</b> due to IR, FX, commodity price changes etc...

<b>Trading and Pricing Financial Futures Contracts</b>

The basic relationship between futures prices and spot prices for equities:
$$
F_t = S_0 \times (1 + r_f - y)^t
$$
- $F_t$: futures price on contract of $t$ length 
- $S_0$: spot price _(today)_ 
- $r_f$: risk-free rate 
- $y$: dividend yield

For commodities, the basic relationship becomes:
$$
F_t = S_0 \times (1 + r_f +\text{storage costs}-\text{convenience yield} y)^t
\\
\text{NCY} = \text{convenience yield }-\text{storage cost} = \text{Net Convenience Yield}
$$

- The <b>net convenience yield</b> is generally positive and future prices are generally _below spot prices_. _(This dynamic is called <b>backwardation</b>).
- Sometimes storage prices are high and there's plenty of commodities available, so future prices are above spot prices. _(This dynamic is called <b>contango</b>).

_Note that the convenience yield is the implicit, non-monetary benefit of physically holding a commodity compared to owning a futures contract (example: avoiding shortages or ensuring production)._

Consider also that the holder of a future contract <b>misses out</b> any dividend or interest payment from the underlying but he does not have to pay for storage costs.

<b>Interest Rate Risk</b>

A <b>forward interest rate</b> is a rate for a loan starting in the future. To price it, we use the following formula:
$$
f_{n,m} = \left[ \frac{(1 + r_m)^m}{(1 + r_n)^n} \right]^{\frac{1}{m-n}} - 1
$$

where $r_n$ is the current rate for a $n$-year term and the same applies for $m$.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Interest Rate Swaps</b>

Some future cash flows are fixed, others may vary depending on the interest rates, rates of exchanges or prices of commodities. In a <b>swap contract</b>, one counterparty exchanges a _fixed set of cash_ for a _floating set of cash flow_ and the second counterparty takes the opposite position.

A swap uses a Notional Principal ($L$). <br/>
This amount is never exchanged between parties; it is simply the "dummy number" to calculate interest.

- <b>Fixed Rate Payer</b>: Pays an agreed and constant interest rate. They are essentially "shorting" interest rates _(betting they will go up)_.
- <b>Floating Rate Payer:</b> Pays a variable market rate _(like SOFR)_. They are "long" interest rates _(betting they will stay low)_.

In practice, only the difference between the two interest amounts is paid _(this reduces credit risk for both parties)_.

- Scenario A _(Market Rate < Fixed Rate):_ the Fixed Payer pays the difference.
- Scenario B (Market Rate > Fixed Rate): the Floating Payer pays the difference.
- Scenario C (Market Rate = Fixed Rate): no money moves _("Wash" scenario)_

When a swap is created, its Net Present Value (NPV) is $0$. The fixed rate is mathematically _"calibrated"_ with discount factor $Z$ so that the expected value of the floating payments equals the fixed payments today.
$$
r_{\text{fixed}} = \frac{1 - Z_n}{\sum_{t=1}^{n} Z_t}
$$

To calculate the current value value of an existing swap that changes to a new $r^*$:
$$
V_{\text{swap}} = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{(r_{\text{fixed}} - r_{\text{floating\_expected}}) \times L}{(1 + r^*)^t}
$$

A swap curve _(also called the "LIBOR curve/SOFR curve/EURIBOR curve)_ is the graphical representation of the relationship between swap rates and different maturities (tenors).
It's a line that shows you the "fixed rate" the market expects to pay for a swap lasting different maturities.

<br/>

<b>Currency Swaps</b>

A currency swap is a contract where two parties exchange cash flows in different currencies over time.

It can be decomposed in:
- a spot FX transaction today
- plus a series of interest payments
- plus a reverse FX transaction at maturity

<b>Heding Interest Rate Risk</b>

When a business receive cash flows that depends on interest rate, the firm can manage its risk by buying a bond and matching duration. In this way, exposure to small interest rate changes is _zero_.

---

In general, if we want to hedge against changes in the value of asset $A$ _(oil)_ using an offsetting sale of asset $B$ _(example: oil futures)_, we use the following formula:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\Delta A] = \alpha + \delta \times (\Delta B)
$$
- $\delta$: sensitivity of $A$ to change in value of $B$ _(hedge ratio)_ 
- $\alpha$: unexpected drift/changes

## <a id="7"><b>7. International Financial Management</b> </a>

<b>The Forward Premium/Discount</b>

The forward-premium measures whether the market expects a currency to be more expensive or cheaper in the future compared to right now. It is represented by the <b>annualized  rate</b>, defined as:

$$
\text{Annualized Rate} = \left( \frac{\text{Spot}}{\text{Forward}} - 1 \right) \times \frac{12}{\text{Months}}
$$
- If the result is Positive (+): the currency is at a <b>Forward Premium</b> _(It is expected to become more expensive)._
- If the result is Negative (-): the currency is at a <b>Forward Discount</b> _(It is expected to become cheaper)._ 

<b>Basic relationships: The International Fisher Effect and the Interest Rate Parity</b>

In general, these relationships are known as the <b>International Fisher Effect (IFE)</b> and <b>Interest Rate Parity (IRP)</b>, that describe the equilibrium between a home/domestic currency ($H$) and a foreign currency ($F$).

The <b>Fisher Effect</b> represents the relationship between <b>nominal interest rates</b> ($r$) and <b>expected inflation </b>($i$). In an  _efficient market_, the ratio of nominal interest rates equals the ratio of expected inflation:

$$
\frac{1 + r_H}{1 + r_F} = \frac{1 + \mathbb{E}(i_H)}{1 + \mathb{E}(i_F)}
$$

The <b>Interest Parity Equation</b>  shows that the difference between the Spot rate ($S$) and the Forward rate ($F$) should offset the difference in interest rates between the two countries to prevent arbitrage:

$$
\frac{1 + r_H}{1 + r_F} = \frac{F_{H/F}}{S_{H/F}}
$$

We can then add a final equation, the <b>Purchasing Power Parity</b>: it explains that the expected change in the exchange rate is driven by the difference in inflation rates between the two countries:

$$
\frac{1 + E(i_H)}{1 + E(i_F)} = \frac{\mathb{E}(S_{H/F})}{S_{H/F}}
$$

If combine them all, we get the  the <b> International Finance Linkages </b> _("Four-Way Equivalence")_: 

$$
\frac{1 + r_H}{1 + r_F} = \frac{1 + \mathb{E}(i_H)}{1 + \mathb{E}(i_F)} = \frac{F_{H/F}}{S_{H/F}} = \frac{\mathb{E}(S_{H/F})}{S_{H/F}}
$$

---
<br/>
<b>The Cost of Capital for International Investments</b>

When calculating the cost of capital in an international context, we need to adjust local valuation to account for foreign exchange risk, country risk, and differing inflation rates.

To obtain the required return for a project, the <b>Capital Asset Pricing Model </b> (CAPM) is often the starting point: 
$$

\text{Required Return} = \text{Risk-Free Rate}_{Swiss} + (\beta \times \text{Market Risk Premium})
$$

To discount the expected cash flows in _another currency_, we convert the cost of capital from $CHF$ to second currency _($USD$ in the following example)

$$
(1 + \text{dollar return}) = (1 + \text{Swiss return}) \times \frac{(1 + \text{interest rate}_{US})}{(1 + \text{interest rate}_{Swiss})}
$$

_Note that this formula (or method) doesn't take into account business risk!_(every country is exposed to risk, but some parts of the world are _more vulnerable, see the below table)_.

\text{Cash ratio} = \frac{\text{Cash} + \text{Marketable securities}}{\text{Current liabilities}}

## <a id="9"><b>9. Financial Planning, Working Capital Management</b> </a>

Capital requirements are very different depending on whether a company operates in the financial sector or not.

For most non-financial businesses, capital requirements refer to the total financial resources needed to operate and can be decomposed into:
- <b>Fixed Capital</b>: Money needed for long-term assets _(like machinery, buildings, or vehicles ...)_
- <b>Working Capital:</b> The cash needed for day-to-day operations _(paying salaries, buying inventory, and covering rent ...)_
- <b>Statutory Minimum Capital:</b> In many countries, you cannot legally register a _"Limited"_ company without proving you have a certain amount of money in advance.

In contrast, for financial businesses, capital requirements represent a strict law. Regulators _(the Federal Reserve or the ECB)_ mandate that banks hold a specific percentage of their assets as "capital" (equity) rather than debt.

<b>Financial Forecasting</b>

We start with the distinction between profit and cash:
- <b>accrual accounting</b> recognizes revenue when earned and expenses when incurred, it represents _profitability_.
- <b>cash flow accounting</b> tracks the actual money flow, it represents _liquidity_.

Any financial manager must ensure that the company has enough cash to support its operations:

$$
\text{External Capital Required} = \Delta \text{NCF} + \Delta \text{Fixed Assets }+ \text{Dividends} + \text{CF Operations}
$$

Cash can be affected by:
- <b>Sources</b>: net income, depreciation _(since it is a non cash-expense, is usually subtracted to the revenues to calculate net income even if there was no actual cash outflow)_, new debt, sale of assets, increase in payables _(meaning that the company is still keeping cash/delaying the payments)_.
- <b>Uses</b>: dividends, CAPEX, stock repurchase, increase in inventory/receivables

Note that a company with positive net income can need cash due to rapid expansion and inventory needs.

_Generally speaking_, there are 4 steps to build a robust cash budget:
- forecast sources 
- forecast uses 
- calculate the <b>net flow</b> 
- evaluate and adjust.

---
<br/>
<b>Short-term Financing and Tactical Liquidity</b>

After identifying when a company needs money, it has to understand where to get it. <br/>We start by analyzing two specific mechanisms for short-term financing:
- <b>bank loans</b>: very accessible and regulated for stable firms. 
- <b>stretching payables</b> (delaying payments to suppliers with an interest cost).

_Note that_ stretching payments is rarely free. It can solve _immediate liquidity crisis_, but it carries a high implicit cost, making it more expensive than a bank loan and risking to damage the company's creditworthiness with suppliers.

On the other side, the riskiness of debt financing is given by its <b>average maturity</b> $\implies$ the longer the average maturity, the lower the refinancing risk since the company _"locks in" the money for a longer period. With shorter maturities, a company is more exposed to tiny changes in the interest rate, 
<br/>Short maturities also imply that a company will have to repay back its debt sooner or more frequently, resulting bigger cash outflows/inflows.

Therefore, lengthening the maturity profile in low interest rate periods reduces interest rate expenses in high interest periods. However, long maturities are not always available in the market _(especially for lower rated companies)_.


Once a plan is drafted, it is usually stressed using financial ratios with two important metrics:
- <b>Current Ratio</b>: $\text{Current Assets} / \text{Current Liabilities}$, measuring the company's ability to pay off all its short-term liabilities using short-term assets.
- <b>Quick Ratio</b>: $\text{Cash + Marketable Securities + Receivables} / \text{Current Liabilities}$, measuring the "quickest" assets that can be turned into cash almost instantly.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Company valuation</b>

Cash flow is also very useful to determine how much a company is worth _(now and in the future)_, discounting its free cash flows.

$$
\text{FCF} = \text{Operating Cash Flow} - \text{Investment in NWC} - \text{Investment in Fixed Assets}
$$

However, when discounting, note that models are only as good as their assumptions:
- many models assume costs always stay at a fixed % of sales, in reality fixed costs may also vary independently of sales volume
- most models generate accounting numbers _(net income)_ but investors care more about cash flows.
- including too many _(and unnecessary)_ info can distract from the important decisions.
<br/>
---
<br/>
<b>Working Capital and Treasury Optimization</b>

<b>Net Working Capital</b> is the difference between current assets and liabilities, it represents the cushion of liquidity a firm needs to stay operational _(it varies depending on the industry: Pharmaceutical also show very high NWC $\approx 40$%, utilities very low $\le 10$%)_.

Similarly, the <b>Operating Cycle</b> defined the time from the purchase of raw materials to the collection of cash from the sale of finished goods.

$$
\text{Operating Cycle} = \text{Inventory Period} + \text{Receivables Period}
$$

And, _lastly_, <b>Cash cycle</b> represents the time during which the company's cash is actually tied up:

$$
\text{Cash Cycle} = \text{Inventory Period} + \text{Receivables Period} - \text{Payables Period}
$$



_Example: U.S. manufacturing firms in 2023 had an average cash cycle of 65 days $\implies$ they had to wait over 9 weeks to get their money back after paying for materials._

_On the other hand, for retail and grocery industries the typical cash cycle is short or negative, since companies use negative working capital as a source of free financing by selling inventory before paying suppliers_.

To shorten the cash cycle, a firm should:
- balance the cost of holding inventory _(carrying costs)_ against the cost of ordering more frequently 
- define clear <b>Terms of Sale</b> to manage receivables credits. 
    - _(Example: 2/10 net 30 means that the customer gets a 2% discount if they pay within 10 days, otherwise the full amount due in 30 days)_ 
    - In case of the need of immediate cash, a firm can sell its account receivables to a financial institution _(a "factor")_ that collects the debt for a fee.

Furthermore, note that cash <b>earns zero interest</b>, therefore treasury managers use "sweep" programs to move excess cash into <b>Money Market Investments</b> to earn a return while still maintaining liquidity. <br/>Some common investments vehicles are:
- Commercial Paper (CP): _unsecured short-term promissory notes issued by large firms_ 
- Repurchase agreements (Repos): _selling securities with an agreement to buy them back in order to receive cash_ 
- Banker's acceptance (BAs): _a "demand to pay" that has been guaranteed by a bank_.

## <a id="13"><b>13. Financial Crises and Regulatory</b> </a>

<b>Why regulate the banks? </b>

Banks play a special role in the economy _(combination of payments, lending, deposit taking etc...)_, so the impact of banking crises can be huge and lead to <b>permanent GDP losses</b>. For this reason, there's a lot of support from the public sector to promote financial stability _(deposit insurance, lender of last resort)_.

The primary metric is of course <b>capital</b>. <br/>We define the <b>common equity (net assets) of any company</b> as the difference between its assets and liabilities. Common equity can be understood as the shareholders's residual claim on the assets the company, after taking account of the claims of other funders.

With positive common equity, depositors and other funders can be comfortable that assets are sufficient to cover their claims and _"will be happy"_ to continue funding the bank.

Common equity is also considered the <b>first form of funding to bear a loss</b>, as it does it _instantaneously_.

<b>Subordinated debt </b> _(subordinated loan, subordinated bond, or junior debt)_ is a type of debt that ranks below other debts in terms of claims on a company's assets.<br/>In the event of a liquidation or bankruptcy, subordinated debt holders are paid only after the senior debt holders have been fully repaid.



<b>Hybrid capital</b> is a form of subordinate debt that shares some features of common share:
- it is usually perpetual _(no maturity date)_ 
- it enables the bank to cancel interest payments _(similar to cancelling dividend payments on common shares)_ 

<b>Liquidity</b> (funding liquidity) can be defined as having cash when you need it to meet repayments or to provide business to clients:
- can be generated from the asset side of the balance sheet by selling assets 
- can be generated from the liability side by raising funds through additional borrowing _(it does not change the size of the balance sheet)_ 

An asset is considered <b>liquid</b> if it can be rapidly converted into cash with <b>no loss of value</b> $\implies$ it means _typically_ that there's a deep and active market where the asset can be traded.

<b>Regulatory metrics: Capital</b>

There are two key metrics for capital:

$$
\text{Leverage Ratio Requirement} = \frac{\text{Capital}}{\text{Leverage Ratio Exposure}} \ge 3 \%
$$

The <b>leverage ratio exposure </b> is a measure of the bank size _(= total assets + off-balance sheet items converted into on-balance sheet equivalents)_ but it doesn't discriminate between the riskiness of assets.
$$
\text{Risk-based Capital Requirement} = \frac{\text{Capital}}{\text{Risk-weighted assets}} \ge 8\%
$$

<b>Risk-weighted assets (RWA)</b> are the nominal exposure converted into risk-weighted exposure using standardised risk-weights set by regulations or banks' internal models.

## <a id="14"><b>Debt Restructuring</b> </a>

Financial distress is one of the most _misunderstood concepts_ in corporate finance since the instinct it to treat a <b>distressed firm</b> as a <b>failing business</b>.

The first and most important analysis in any restructuring situation is to distinguish between two fundamentally different conditions:
- <b>economic distress</b>: occurs when a firm's business model is broken and the firm cannot generate enough cash from operations to cover its cost, _regardless of how its balance sheet is structured_.
- <b>financial distress</b>: occurs when a good business is burdened by a inefficient capital structure, often cashflows are positive or recoverable, but the firm cannot meet its debt obligations.

<b>Costs of financial distress and Optimal leverage</b>

Costs of distress can be separated in <b>direct costs</b> _(lawyers, restructuring advisors, investment bankers, accountants etc...)_ and <b>indirect costs</b> _(customer defection, suppliers,  employee leave, management distraction, loss of competitive competition)_ 

This brings the <b>fundamental economic rationale</b> for why firms do not borrow as much as possible. The <b>trade-off theory</b> of capital structure indeed says:

$$
\text{Optimal Leverage} = \text{PV}(\text{tax shield}) - \text{PV}(\text{distress costs})
$$

<b>The Merton model of a firm</b>

The Merton model establishes that:
- <b>equity</b> is a call option on a firm assets:
  - if the firm ha asset value $V_t$ and debt face value $D$, then:
  - equity gets $\max(V_t-D,0)$ _(equity has the same pauyoff of a call option on the assets with strike price $D \implies$ if assets are worth more, shareholders keep the upside, otherwise they walk away and lose only their equity)_ 
- <b>risky-debt</b> embeds a short put _(the bondholder is not holding a clean  risk-free, becuase oin case of default they don't receive the promided $D$)_:
  - debt gets $\min (V_t, D)$

<b>The Agency Problems in distress: 3 conflicts</b>
<br/>

1. <b>Risk Shifting (asset substitution)</b>: when a firm is near insolvency, shareholders hold a deep OTM call since the equity has minimal intrinsic value. In this case, may prefer a negative-NPM risky projects over positive-NPM safe projects, because the option structure give them upside <b>of the risky bet</b> _(while creditors bear the downside)_ 
<br/>
2. <b>Debt Overhang and underinvestment</b>: a firm burdene with excessive debt may reject positive-NPM investments because the gains accrue primarily to creditors thatn shareholders. _When a firm is insolvent_, any new investment essentially services the existing debt first $\implies$  if shareholders must contribute to the investment capital but creditors get most of the returns, shareholders will refuse, _even when the investment has positive NPV and would benefit everyone in aggregate._ 
<br/>
3. <b>Reluctance to recapitalise (debt dilution)</b>: when a distressed firm needs new equity to survive existing shareholders are reluctant to contribute because new equity injection primarily benefits exiting creditors rather than new equity providers $\implies$ New equity providers are _implicitly_ subsidising old creditors. They will only contribute if existing debt is written down first.

<b>Valuation in distress and the Fulcrum Security</b>

The central valuation question in any restructuring is: _"is the firm worth more alive or dead?"_ 

To understand this, we firstly define:
- <b>going-convern value (GCV)</b>: the present value of the firm's future cash flows on operating business assuming a successful restructuring. 
- <b>liquidation value (LV)</b>: the net proceeds from selling assets immediately, typically at a forced-sale discount. 

_Generally speaking_, if $\text{GCV} > \text{LV}$: <b>restructure</b>.

The excess ($\text{GCV} - \text{LV}$) is the <b>restructuring premium</b>.

Once the going-concern enterprise value (EV) is estimated, it is distributed to claimants according to the priority waterfall:

</div>

The <b>fulcrum security</b> is then identified as the class of debt _(or, rarely, equity)_ that is <b>only partially recovered</b> in the restructuring. It is the <b>pivot point</b> of the enterprise value waterfall: all senior claims are paid in full, all junior claims receive nothing, the fulcrum class is _partially recovered_. 
- the fulcrum holders are the <b>only class whose vote genuinely matters</b> 
- the fulcrum holders typically receive equity in the restructured firm. 
- <b>all negotiation effectively converges</b> on what the fulcrum class receives and on what terms.

<b>Restructuring instruments: Debt-for-equity Swaps</b>

A <b>debt-for-equity swap</b> is an exchange in which creditors give up their debt claims in return for an <b>equity stake</b> in the restructured firm $\implies$ the creditors's role shifts from _fixed-income_ to _residua-claim_ ownner.

Let $(P, c, T_n)$ denote the debt characteristics _(principal, coupon maturity)_. The share price before restructuring is $S_-$. Creditors hold debt with bankruptcy value $V_B$.

If the offer is rejected:
- Credit holders get: $V_B$
- Equity holders get $0$

If the offer is accdepted:
- Credit holders get: $\theta \times S_-$
- Equity holders get $(1 - \theta) \times S_-$

Creditors <b>accept the swap</b> if and only if:

$$
\theta \times S_- \ge V_B
$$

where $\theta$ is the equity fraction should compensate the creditors for the forward value of lost cashflows.

$$
\sum_{i=\tau}^{T_n} Pc\left(1 + r(\tau, T_n-i)\right)^{T_n-i}
+
P\left(1 + r(\tau, T_n)\right)^{T_n-\tau}
\le
\theta \times S_{-}
$$

<b>Restructuring instruments: Warrants</b>

Warrants serve as <b>sweetener</b>: creditors accept worse debt terms in exchange of warrants that provie <b>conditional upside exposure</b> if the restructured firm recover. Warrants are particularly useful when:
- the firm is in moderate distress 
- creditors believe in the upside story but are reluctant to become equity holders immediately 
- management wants to keep control of the company _while still compensating creditors for accepting reduced claims_ 
- the tax shield on remaining debt is valuable and should be preserved.

To design a warrant, we consider the following parameters::
- $K$ strike price, set above the current distressed price 
- $N_w$: number of warrants _(More warrants means more potential upside transferred to creditors, but also more dilution for existing shareholders if exercised)_ 
- <b>Coverage ratio</b>: warrants issued per unit of face value surrendered in the haircut _(it links concession to compensation: bigger haircut usually requires higher coverage)_
- $T$ expiration 
- <b>Exercise style</b>: usually European (only at expiration) to simplify administration

A <b>warrant</b> is then modelled as a <b>call on news shares</b>, so the dilution effect must be incorporated, we consider:
- $S_0$ current share price 
- $K$ strike
- $T$ expiry date
- $\sigma$ equity volatility
- £r$ risk-free rate 
- $n$ existing shares outstanding 
- $N_w$ warrants to be issued 

The <b>diluition factor </b> is:

$$
q = \frac{n}{n + N_w}
$$

and the <b>diluition-adjusted warrant price</b> is:

$$
W = q \times C_{BS} (S_0, K, T, \sigma, r)
$$

where $C_{BS}$ is the standard Black-Scholes call price formula.

<b>Restructuring instruments: Haircuts</b>
<br/>
A haircut is a restructuring tool in which creditors accept a<b> reduction in the face value, coupon, or present value </b>of their claims to restore debt sustainability. It is typically negotiated when the going-concern value is above liquidation value: _creditors take an immediate loss, but improve expected recovery by keeping the firm_. 

In simple terms, if original claim is $D_0$, the new claim is $D_1$ and the haircut is:

$$
\text{Haircut} = 1 - D_1/D_0
$$

<b>Restructuring instruments: PIK Notes</b>
<br/>
PIK notes _(Payment-in-Kind notes)_ allow a distressed firm to preserve near-term cash by paying interest with additional debt securities instead of cash coupons.<br/>
They can stabilize liquidity during a turnaround, but they also <b>compound leverage over time,</b> so they are usually negotiated with higher pricing and used only when there is a credible path to future deleveraging or refinancing.
